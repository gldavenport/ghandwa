## Overview

Ghandwa is positioned as a conservative western centum branch of PIE alongside Proto-Germanic, Proto-Italic, and Proto-Celtic. Its phonological profile — voiced fricatives from breathy-voiced stops, *a*-colored syllabic resonant vocalism — is a designed combination of features attested individually across WIE daughters. This document surveys proposals by historical linguists for other early IE branches or substrate layers with divergent phonological profiles, as typological precedents and as resources for the Crotonian borrowing framework.

The proposals below range from well-supported (Temematic, Lusitanian) to speculative (Ausonian, Nordwestblock as Italic split-off). They share a common methodological pattern: centum branch, unusual treatment of voiced aspirates, geographic position in the western or central European zone, visible only through substrate effects in the attested daughters.

## 1. Temematic (Holzer 1989)

**Proposed by:** Georg Holzer  
**Citation:** Holzer, Georg. 1989. *Entlehnungen aus einer bisher unbekannten indogermanischen Sprache im Urslavischen und Urbaltischen*. Wien: Österreichische Akademie der Wissenschaften.

**What it is:** A proposed centum IE substrate layer visible in Balto-Slavic vocabulary — ~45 etymologies in Slavic, 34 of which also appear in Baltic. Named "Temematic" by Holzer.

**Key phonological features:**
- Centum (palatal series merged with plain velars)
- Voiced aspirates → voiceless stops (same as Crotonian rule 4: Dʰ → T)
- Not satemized

**Diagnostic examples:**
- Com. Sl. \*proso 'millet' ← Temematic reflex of \*bʰr̥so- (PIE \*bʰers- 'burst, bud') — aspirate devoiced
- Com. Sl. \*trǫtŭ 'drone' ← Temematic \*tron- < PIE \*dʰrōn- — aspirate devoiced

**Reception:** Positively reviewed by Kortlandt (2003) and Matasović (2013: 77–82). Garnier & Sagot cite it as the most methodologically rigorous precursor to Crotonian.

**Relevance to Ghandwa:** Closest structural parallel to Crotonian as a borrowing-source language. The devoicing of aspirates (Temematic and Crotonian both have Dʰ → T) is the primary divergence from Ghandwa (which has Dʰ → voiced fricatives). A Temematic-type layer in the Ghandwa contact zone would produce similar doublet signatures.

**Further reading:**
- Kortlandt, Frederik. 2003. "An Indo-European substratum in Slavic?" In *Languages in Prehistoric Europe*, ed. Bammesberger & Vennemann. Heidelberg: Winter, 253–260.
- Matasović, Ranko. 2013. "Substratum words in Balto-Slavic." *Filologija* 60: 75–102.

## 2. Ausonian (Szemerényi 1991)

**Proposed by:** Oswald Szemerényi  
**Citation:** Szemerényi, Oswald. 1991. "The development of the Indo-European mediae aspiratae in Latin and Italic." In *Scripta minora: Selected essays in Indo-European, Greek and Latin*, ed. Hooker & Considine. Innsbruck: Institut für Sprachwissenschaft, 628–693.

**What it is:** A proposed centum IE substrate layer in Italic specifically. Thinner evidential base than Temematic (~3 core examples). Named "Ausonian" after the ancient Ausonians of southern Italy.

**Key phonological features:**
- Centum
- Voiced aspirates → voiceless stops (same as Temematic and Crotonian)
- PIE \*b (rare) possibly present

**Diagnostic examples:**
- Lat. *Alpēs* 'Alps' ← \*h₁albʰ-éi̯-es 'white mountains' (PIE \*h₁al-bʰ- 'white')
- Lat. *Tiberis* 'Tiber' ← \*dʰubʰ-rí- 'ravine' (PIE \*dʰebʰ- 'deep') — Garnier adopts this via Crotonian \*túβri-
- Lat. *Rutulī* ← PIE \*h₁rudʰ-ró- 'red'

**Reception:** Less positively received than Temematic; the evidential base is slim. Garnier & Sagot engage with it but propose Crotonian as a more systematic account of the same data.

**Relevance to Ghandwa:** Ausonian represents the same contact scenario as Crotonian (centum branch in contact with proto-Italic speakers in Italy), but without Garnier's systematic rule inventory. For Ghandwa purposes Crotonian supersedes Ausonian as a borrowing model.

## 3. Old European Hydronymy (Krahe 1962)

**Proposed by:** Hans Krahe  
**Citation:** Krahe, Hans. 1962. "Die Struktur der alteuropäischen Hydronymie." *Akademie der Wissenschaften und der Literatur, Abhandlungen der geistes- und sozialwissenschaftlichen Klasse* 5: 285–339.  
Also: Krahe, Hans. 1964. *Unsere ältesten Flussnamen*. Wiesbaden: Harrassowitz.

**What it is:** Not a single proposed language but a stratum: IE substrate vocabulary visible in river names across Europe (from the British Isles to the Baltic, from Scandinavia to northern Italy), predating all known named IE branches. Krahe called this stratum "Old European" (*Alteuropäisch*).

**Key features:** The hydronyms show a consistent set of IE roots and suffixes but cannot be assigned to any known branch. They appear to reflect an early undifferentiated IE layer that preceded the Celtic/Italic/Germanic/Baltic split.

**Reception:** Broadly accepted as a real phenomenon; the interpretation is debated. Subsequent scholars (Schmid, Kitson) have refined the corpus and some have proposed it reflects a late PIE dialect continuum rather than a single proto-language.

**Relevance to Ghandwa:** Provides the strongest real-world support for the idea that the western IE branch zone was more diverse than the surviving major branches suggest. The Ghandwa territory would plausibly overlap with the Old European hydronymy zone. River names in the Ghandwa corpus could be modeled on Krahe's material.

**Further reading:**
- Kitson, P. R. 1996. "British and European river-names." *Transactions of the Philological Society* 94: 73–118.
- Schmid, Wolfgang P. 1968. *Alteuropäisch und Indogermanisch*. Mainz: Akademie der Wissenschaften.

## 4. Nordwestblock (Kuhn 1962)

**Proposed by:** Hans Kuhn  
**Citation:** Kuhn, Hans. 1962. "Vor- und frühgermanische Ortsnamen in Norddeutschland und den Niederlanden." *Westfälische Forschungen* 15: 7–63.

**What it is:** A proposed substrate layer in NW continental Europe (roughly modern Belgium, Netherlands, NW Germany) visible in place names, personal names, and some vocabulary that fits neither Celtic, Germanic, nor Italic neatly. Kuhn called it the "Nordwestblock."

**Key features:** The material is consistent with IE but resists assignment to any known branch. The phonology is unclear because the evidence is primarily onomastic.

**Interpretations:**
- Kuhn himself: para-IE or non-IE
- Vennemann: pre-IE Atlantic substrate (controversial, largely rejected)
- Others: early split-off from the Italic branch before the major Italic innovations, or an independent western centum branch — this is the reading most relevant to Ghandwa

**Reception:** The existence of a substrate layer is accepted; its IE status and relationship to the known branches is debated. The Italic split-off hypothesis is one of the live positions.

**Relevance to Ghandwa:** If Nordwestblock is a very early Italic split-off or an independent western centum branch, it occupies precisely the slot Ghandwa fictionalizes. The geographic zone (NW continental Europe) is also consistent with a language that would have been in contact with proto-Germanic and proto-Celtic speakers.

**Further reading:**
- Kuhn, Hans. 1962. (as above)
- Schrijver, Peter. 1997. "Animal, vegetable and mineral: Some Western European substratum words." In *Sound Law and Analogy*, ed. Lubotsky. Amsterdam: Rodopi, 293–316.
- Looijenga, Tineke & Quak, Arend, eds. 1996. *Frisian Runes and Neighbouring Traditions*. Amsterdam: Rodopi. (contains relevant onomastic discussion)

## 5. Lusitanian

**Status:** Actually attested (fragmentarily); not a proposal  
**Key sources:**  
- Prósper, Blanca María. 2002. *Lenguas y religiones prerromanas del occidente de la Península Ibérica*. Salamanca: Universidad de Salamanca.  
- Villar, Francisco & Prósper, Blanca María. 2005. *Vascos, celtas e indoeuropeos: genes y lenguas*. Salamanca: Universidad de Salamanca.  
- Eska, Joseph F. 2008. "The emergence of the Celtic languages." In *The Celtic Languages*, 2nd ed., ed. Ball & Müller. London: Routledge.

**What it is:** A fragmentarily attested IE language of western Iberia (modern Portugal/western Spain), visible in inscriptions, theonyms, and personal names from the Roman period. Does not fit Celtic or Italic neatly despite being clearly IE and centum.

**Key phonological features:**
- Centum
- Preserves PIE \*p (unlike Celtic)
- Labiovelars appear to be preserved (unlike Celtic \*kʷ → p)
- Aspiration treatment unclear due to limited data

**Reception:** Widely accepted as a real language distinct from Celtic and Italic. Its exact branching position is debated — some treat it as an early Italic split-off, others as an independent western centum branch.

**Relevance to Ghandwa:** The most important real-world data point for the proposition that the western IE branch zone was more diverse than the surviving major branches suggest. Lusitanian is the ghost of exactly the kind of language Ghandwa fictionalizes. Its preservation of \*p and apparent labiovelar retention align with Ghandwa's defining features.

## 6. Crotonian (Garnier & Sagot 2017)

**Proposed by:** Romain Garnier, Benoît Sagot  
**Citation:** Garnier, Romain & Benoît Sagot. 2017. "A shared substrate between Greek and Italic." *Indogermanische Forschungen* 122(1): 29–60. DOI: 10.1515/if-2017-0002. HAL: hal-01621467.

**Follow-up:** Garnier, Romain & Benoît Sagot. 2018. "New results on a centum substratum in Greek: the Lydian connection." Paper presented at the International Colloquium on Loanwords and Substrata in Indo-European languages, June 2018, Limoges. HAL: hal-01798979. [Speculative: suggests Crotonian may be a pre-stage of Lydian. Not a committed position.]

**What it is:** A proposed centum IE language, named "Crotonian" after the Greek colony Κρότων (Crotone, Calabria), hypothesized as a substrate/adstrate donor to both Common Greek and Common Italic. ~100 Greek and ~20 Latin/Proto-Romance etymologies proposed.

**Phonological rule inventory** (Table 1, chronologically ordered):

| # | Rule | Notation |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Centum treatment | \*Ḱ → K |
| 2 | Laryngeal vocalism | CHC → CaC (short *a*) |
| 3 | Aspirate + liquid | \*bʰR → \*bR |
| 4 | Aspirate devoicing | (#)Dʰ → (#)T |
| 5 | Liquid metathesis | CVRC → CRVC |
| 6 | Schwa epenthesis | \*#(s)klV- → \*#(s)kəlV- |
| 7 | Verner-like lenition/fortition | P → Pː (post-stress); P → B (pre-stress) |
| 8 | Systematic barytonesis | stress retraction |
| 9 | Nasal anticipation | VPNV → VNBV |
| 10 | Resyllabification | \*Cr̥C → \*CurC |
| 11 | Word-initial treatments | \*#pr → \*#br; \*#tr → \*#Tr (fortis dental, nature unclear) |
| 12 | Monophthongization | \*ow → \*ū |

**Divergences from Ghandwa** (rules producing different surface forms from the same PIE preform):

| Feature | Crotonian | Ghandwa |
|---|---|---|
| Voiced aspirates | Dʰ → T (devoiced voiceless) | Dʰ → β/ð/ɣ/ɣʷ (voiced fricatives) |
| Aspirate + liquid | \*bʰR → \*bR | \*bʰR → \*βR |
| Syllabic resonants | \*Cr̥C → \*CurC (u-vocalism) | \*Cr̥C → \*CarC (a-vocalism) |
| Stress | systematic barytonesis | PIE accent preserved |
| Liquid metathesis | CVRC → CRVC | none |
| Verner-like alternation | yes, stress-conditioned | no |
| Nasal anticipation | VPNV → VNBV | none |

**Key note on rule 11:** The asymmetry between \*#pr- → \*#br- (voicing) and \*#tr- → \*#Tr- (fortition) is typologically unexplained by Garnier. The \*T notation is a cover symbol for a fortis dental of uncertain phonetic realization; the evidence base is thin (three θρ- examples in Greek). For the Crotonian transformer, \*#tr- → \*#Tr- should be flagged as low-confidence.

**Relevance to Ghandwa:** Primary source for the Crotonian borrowing generator (planned: `tools/pie-2-crotonian.jsx`). The rule inventory is implementable as an ordered transformer. Doublet pairs in the lexicon — inherited Ghandwa form vs. Crotonian loanword — are identifiable by systematic contrasts: *β/ð/ɣ* vs. *t/p/k*, *-ar-* vs. *-ur-* from syllabic resonants. The `provenance` column handles this: inherited forms tagged `PIE`; Crotonian loanwords tagged `Crotonian`.
