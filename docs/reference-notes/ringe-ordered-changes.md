# Ringe 2017 Chapter 3: Simplified Stages of Proto-Germanic Development

## 0. Orientation

Ringe’s Chapter 3 does not give one simple numbered chronology of all Proto-Germanic developments. The chapter presents a mixture of sound changes, ordered dependencies, morphological restructuring, and broader historical interpretation.

This outline presents the material in a simplified staged format, with no examples.

---

# 1. Early Phonological Cleanup

## 1.1 Laryngeal Loss and Vowel Effects

- 
Word-initial laryngeals before consonants are lost.
- Postvocalic tautosyllabic laryngeals are lost with compensatory vowel lengthening.
- Laryngeals between nonsyllabic segments become schwa.
- Prevocalic laryngeals are lost after relevant coloring effects.
- Laryngeal coloring leaves phonemic vowel differences after laryngeals disappear.
- Vowels in hiatus contract.
- Early contractions can yield overlong or trimoric vowels.
- Final *-ō develops an overlong or trimoric outcome.
- Stressed initial schwa becomes *a.
- Noninitial schwa is later lost in many consonantal environments.
- Osthoff’s Law shortens long vowels before sonorant + consonant.

## 1.2 Syllabic Sonorants

- PIE syllabic sonorants are resolved as *uR.
- Older ablaut relations are disrupted by the replacement of zero-grade sonorant vocalism with *uR sequences.
- Clusters created or exposed by this change undergo simplification or assimilation.

## 1.3 Sonorant and Nasal Cluster Adjustments

- *ln > *ll.
- *nw > *nn.
- Nasal + stop clusters assimilate in place.
- *m > *n before coronal stops.
- Word-final *m > *n.

## 1.4 Early Sibilant and Stop Cluster Treatment

- Dental stop + *s + dental stop clusters simplify toward *ss.
- *ss is later simplified in some heavy environments.

---

# 2. Dorsal Reorganization

## 2.1 Palatovelars and Plain Velars

- PIE palatovelars merge with plain velars.

## 2.2 Velar + *w and Labiovelars

- Plain velar + *w sequences merge with inherited labiovelars.

## 2.3 Labiovelar Delabialization

- Labiovelars are delabialized next to *u.
- Labiovelars are delabialized before *t.

## 2.4 Environment-Specific Outcomes of *gʷ

- *gʷ is retained after a homorganic nasal.
- *gʷ becomes *w intervocalically or before sonorants.
- *gʷ becomes a plain dorsal in some palatal or high-vowel environments.
- Word-initial *gʷʰ develops exceptionally toward *b.

## 2.5 Interpretive Constraint

- Ringe rejects deriving all apparent labial outcomes from a single general regular change caused by adjacent *w.

---

# 3. Obstruent Restructuring

## 3.1 Grimm’s Law

- PIE voiceless stops become voiceless fricatives.
- PIE voiced stops become voiceless stops.
- PIE breathy-voiced stops become voiced fricatives.

## 3.2 Cluster-Conditioned Complications

- Grimm’s Law is blocked or modified after obstruents, especially after *s.
- Stops before *s or *t undergo special devoicing or fricativizing developments.
- Labial and dorsal stops in such clusters can converge on fricative outcomes regardless of original stop series.

## 3.3 Voiced Fricative Developments

- Voiced fricatives become stops after homorganic nasals.
- Voiced fricatives develop stop allophones in some environments:
  - word-initially for some segments
  - after homorganic nasals
  - after certain liquids
  - after voiced sibilants

## 3.4 Kluge’s Law

- Ringe does not accept Kluge’s Law as a regular Proto-Germanic sound law.

---

# 4. Verner’s Law and Stress Reorganization

## 4.1 Verner’s Law

- Voiceless fricatives produced by Grimm’s Law are voiced when:
  - they occur in a voiced environment
  - the immediately preceding syllable was unaccented

## 4.2 Extension to *s

- Inherited *s undergoes the same voicing rule.

## 4.3 Chronological Implication

- Verner’s Law must precede the fixation of Germanic initial stress.

## 4.4 Fixation of Initial Stress

- Inherited mobile or contrastive PIE accent is lost.
- Proto-Germanic develops fixed initial stress.
- Verner alternations survive as morphophonological residues after the original conditioning accent disappears.

---

# 5. Final-Syllable Reduction and Word-Final Changes

## 5.1 Final Vowel Loss

- Word-final nonhigh short vowels are lost in polysyllables.
- High *i is more resistant to apocope.

## 5.2 Glide Loss with Final Vowels

- In some environments, preceding *j or *w is lost along with the final vowel.

## 5.3 Final Nasal Loss

- Word-final *n is lost in polysyllables.
- The preceding vowel is nasalized.

## 5.4 Final Coronal Stop Loss

- Final coronal stops are lost in unstressed polysyllables.

## 5.5 Morphological Consequences

- Case endings are reduced.
- Ending syncretism increases.
- Later analogical restructuring is encouraged.

---

# 6. Sievers’ Law, Glides, and Unstressed Vowels

## 6.1 Sievers-Type Developments

- Inherited alternation between *j and *ij is preserved.
- Sievers’ Law is reapplied after new heavy syllables arise.
- A converse Sievers development turns *i into *j after light syllables in some environments.
- Initial sonorant-glide alternations are mostly resolved in favor of nonsyllabic clusters.
- Word-initial *CuV sequences tend toward *CwV.

## 6.2 Unstressed Vowel Developments

- Unstressed tautosyllabic *ew becomes *ow before consonants.
- Unstressed *e becomes *i except before *r.
- Before *r, unstressed *e probably becomes *a.
- Stressed *e is raised to *i when a high front vocalic element follows in the same or next syllable.
- *ey becomes *ī.

## 6.3 *j-Loss Subtypes

- *j is lost before *i.
- *j is lost between vowels except after *i.
- *əj develops differently depending on following vowel quality.
- Loss of *j creates new vowel hiatus.
- New hiatus is later resolved by contraction.

## 6.4 *w-Loss Subtypes

- *w is lost between round vowels in restricted environments.
- Word-initial *CuV can become *CwV.
- Ringe does not treat all apparent *w-loss or *w-induced labialization as regular.

## 6.5 Schwa Loss and Contraction

- Surviving schwa is lost in many noninitial environments.
- Schwa loss creates new consonant clusters.
- Schwa loss creates new vowel hiatus.
- Late contractions of vowels in hiatus occur after glide loss and schwa loss.
- These contractions are chronologically distinct from earlier laryngeal-conditioned contractions.

---

# 7. Later Vowel-System Restructuring

## 7.1 Short Back Vowels

- Short nonhigh back vowels merge.
- *o > *a.

## 7.2 Long Back Vowels

- Long nonhigh back vowels first merge as a low long vowel.
- The long low vowel is then rounded or raised to Proto-Germanic *ō.

## 7.3 Overlong Vowels

- Overlong vowels participate in the broader restructuring of the vowel system.

## 7.4 Nasalized Vowels

- Nasalized vowels develop in connection with final nasal loss.
- Some nasalized long vowels undergo later lowering or rounding.

## 7.5 High-Vowel Conditioning

- *e is raised to *i before nasal + consonant.

## 7.6 Vowels before *h

- Vowels are lengthened or nasalized before *h in some environments.

## 7.7 Final Diphthongal Development

- Final *əi becomes *ai.

## 7.8 Systemic Result

- The late vowel changes help produce the recognizable Proto-Germanic vowel inventory.

---

# 8. Verbal Restructuring

## 8.1 Systemic Shift

- PIE aspect-based stem organization is replaced by a tense-based system.
- The inherited contrast between present, aorist, and perfect is reorganized.
- Most verbs are reduced to two finite stems:
  - present
  - past

## 8.2 Loss of the Aorist

- The PIE aorist indicative is lost.

## 8.3 Reorganization of the Perfect

- The PIE perfect is split into two Germanic systems:
  - strong pasts
  - preterite-present verbs

## 8.4 Preterite-Presents

- The old perfect’s stative value survives mainly in the preterite-present verbs.

## 8.5 Loss of the Imperfect

- The imperfect is almost entirely lost.
- A residual imperfect form becomes the basis of the Germanic weak past.

## 8.6 Weak Past Formation

- Derived presents acquire past stems through the new weak-past formation.
- The weak past develops from a periphrastic construction involving:
  - a participial form
  - a form of “do”

## 8.7 Middle and Passive

- The old middle or non-active system is narrowed toward a true passive.
- Proto-Germanic lacks a full inherited synthetic past passive.
- Passive past meaning is expressed periphrastically.
- Past passive participles become integrated into the verbal system.

---

# 9. Nominal and Adjectival Restructuring

## 9.1 Nouns

- PIE stem classes continue but are heavily remodeled.
- Ablaut alternations in nominal paradigms are reduced or leveled.
- Endings are reshaped by:
  - sound change
  - analogy
  - syncretism
- Case distinctions are reduced.
- The instrumental is largely lost or merged.
- The vocative mostly collapses with the nominative.
- The dual is largely lost outside restricted pronominal and verbal relics.
- Gender distinctions are reduced in parts of the plural oblique system.

## 9.2 Adjectives

- Proto-Germanic develops the strong/weak adjective contrast.
- Strong adjectives are based on inherited adjective inflection remodeled with pronominal endings.
- Weak adjectives are newly organized as n-stem inflection.
- The strong/weak adjective contrast is a major Germanic innovation.

## 9.3 Pronouns

- Pronouns preserve archaisms.
- Pronouns undergo extensive analogical remodeling.
- Pronominal endings spread into adjective inflection.

## 9.4 Numerals

- Numerals undergo analogical reshaping.
- Some numerals are influenced by adjacent numerals.

---

# 10. Syntax and Lexicon

## 10.1 Syntax

- Ringe is cautious about reconstructing detailed Proto-Germanic syntax.
- Older clause-final finite-verb tendencies are likely inherited.
- Later Germanic main-clause verb-second syntax should not be projected back as a full Proto-Germanic rule.
- Subordinate-clause verb-final tendencies are more plausibly conservative.
- Syntax is much less securely recoverable than phonology or morphology.

## 10.2 Lexicon

- Lexical change is not presented as a single systematic transformation.
- Major lexical categories include:
  - inherited words retained with semantic drift
  - inherited words lost or replaced
  - new Germanic formations
  - borrowings
  - items of uncertain etymology

## 10.3 Overall Character of Chapter 3

- Chapter 3 is primarily a reconstruction of phonological and morphological development.
- It is not a full lexical history of Proto-Germanic.
