Candidate changes for Western (A), Central (B), and Eastern (C) at Stage 2 and later.
All six were evaluated as potential Late Common Ghandwa (Stage 1) changes and rejected
in favor of daughter-level treatment. Daughter assignments are tentative where noted;
open items require a dedicated session per daughter.

This document is a working list. When a candidate is fully assigned and implemented,
move its content into ch5-03 and delete the entry here.

---

## Provenance and status codes

| Code | Meaning |
|---|---|
| LCG-carry | Free variation or naturalistic trend during LCG period; daughters systematize it independently |
| Dark-Ages | No LCG precedent; innovation arises per daughter during Stage 2 |
| Open | Daughter assignment undecided |
| Tentative | Assignment proposed but not committed |
| Decided | Assigned; awaiting implementation or already in pipeline |

## Candidate 1 — Labiovelar delabialization before front vowels

**Rule:** kʷ, gʷ → k, g / _ {e, i}

**Provenance:** LCG-carry

**Motivation:** Rounding feature of labiovelar and fronting feature of following vowel are
phonetically incompatible. Delabialization in this environment is near-universal across
IE daughters; pressure was presumably present in late spoken Ghandwa before the collapse.

**Typological parallel:** Greek, Latin, Slavic, Balto-Slavic, Indo-Iranian all show this
or a parallel. No IE branch preserves kʷe/kʷi contrasting with ke/ki.

**Daughter differentiation notes:**

- **Western (A):** Already delabializes kʷ → k and gʷ → g across the board at Stage 2.
  The front-vowel environment is a proper subset of A's general rule; no additional rule
  needed. This candidate is vacuous for A.
- **Central (B):** Labiovelar fate in B is "conditioned reduction" (ch5-03 matrix).
  Delabialization before front vowels is the most natural and defensible condition.
  Strong candidate for B as the primary or sole labiovelar rule, with back-vowel and
  word-final environments handled separately (or left as kʷ/gʷ).
- **Eastern (C):** Already develarizes kʷ → p and gʷ → b everywhere at Stage 2.
  The front-vowel environment produces a labial stop in C regardless; this candidate
  does not change C's output. Vacuous for C.

**Assignment:** Tentative — relevant primarily to Central (B). The B labiovelar ruleset
(currently flagged as "conditioned environments, unspecified") should evaluate this as its
primary condition before specifying others.

**Open questions:**
- Does B also delabiavelize before back vowels in some environments, or is the front-vowel
  rule the extent of B's labiovelar system?
- Does the labiovelar delabialization feed any of B's voiced fricative rules (e.g., if gʷ
  → g / _{e,i}, the resulting g is then a plain velar; does B's Stage 2 treat it the same
  as inherited plain g)?

## Candidate 2 — Final-syllable long vowel shortening

**Rule:** Vː → V / final syllable (after Stage 1 accent retraction)

**Provenance:** LCG-carry

**Motivation:** LCG rules 1.2 and 1.3 together show accent becoming stress-like.
Under a stress-accent system, phonemic vowel length in a universally unstressed syllable
is poorly supported. Final-syllable length reduction is the natural next step. The LCG
period saw this as free variation; daughters systematize it.

**Typological parallel:** Classical Latin shortened final long vowels before most
consonants. Proto-Germanic collapsed final-syllable quantity distinctions entirely.
The direction is strongly attested in NWIE daughters.

**Morphological consequences:** Significant. Many paradigm endings distinguish long
from short in final position:
- ō-stem nom. sg. -ōs → -os (potential merger with gen. sg. -os)
- ā-stem nom./voc. sg. -ā → -a
- Verb 1sg -ō → -o
- i-stem loc. sg. -ī → -i

The ō/os merger (nom. sg. and gen. sg. both surface as -os) is the most consequential
outcome. Whether to treat this as a problem to be repaired (analogical restructuring in
some daughters) or as an accepted merger depends on the daughter.

**Daughter differentiation notes:**

- **Western (A):** Initial stress means all non-initial syllables are already weakly
  supported. Final shortening is expected early and thoroughly in A. Combined with
  apocope (see candidate 4, syncope), A may lose final short vowels entirely at Stage 3.
- **Central (B):** Penultimate stress leaves the final syllable unstressed but not
  maximally peripheral. B likely shortens final long vowels but may retain final short
  vowels longer than A. The ō/os merger may trigger analogical leveling in B.
- **Eastern (C):** Initial stress, same structural motivation as A, but C's Stage 2–3
  system also voices and modifies sibilants extensively. Final -os, -as etc. interact
  with C's word-edge voicing rules (2C.2: s → z / V_#, R_#, N_#). If final -ōs
  shortens to -os, the -s then voices to -oz under 2C. Worth checking expected ordering.

**Assignment:** Open. Likely applies in all three daughters but at different rates and
with different morphological consequences.

## Candidate 3 — Nasal deletion before sibilant with compensatory lengthening

**Rule:** V N {s, z} → Vː {s, z}
(N = any nasal; lengthening is on the immediately preceding vowel)

**Provenance:** LCG-carry

**Motivation:** Nasal-before-sibilant deletion with compensatory lengthening is one of
the most broadly attested IE processes (Latin acc. pl. -ōns → -ōs; Greek -ans → -ās;
widespread in Balto-Slavic). Phonetically: the nasal constriction is incompatible with
the sibilant release; the nasal is often auditorily ambiguous in rapid speech before a
fricative and is the natural candidate for deletion.

**Note on rule ordering:** LCG rule 1.1b (m → n / _non-labial, including before s/z)
must have already applied. By the time daughters see the input, any -ms- or -mz- has
already become -ns- or -nz-. The feeding is clean.

**Morphological consequences:** Accusative plural endings are the most common site:
- o-stem acc. pl. *-ons → -ōs
- ā-stem acc. pl. *-āns → -ās

Combined with candidate 2 (final shortening): -ons → -ōs → -os. Two-step path;
daughters applying both rules get net nasal deletion with no length residue.
Daughters applying only candidate 3 get a new source of final long vowels.

**Scope:** Sibilants only (s, z). Extension to voiced fricatives (VNβ → Vːβ etc.) is
explicitly not part of this candidate — the nasal-fricative clusters are infrequent and
the voiced fricatives are the primary divergence axis for daughter development. Do not
extend without a specific attested form driving the decision.

**Word-initial #Ns-:** Rule does not apply (no preceding vowel to lengthen). Word-initial
nasal-sibilant clusters are daughter territory; handle per daughter when attested forms
require it.

**Daughter differentiation notes:**

- **Western (A):** A devoices z → s (gh.2.4 voicing is undone at Stage 2A). The rule
  fires on -ns- input (already unvoiced); output is Vːs. Combined with A's general
  final shortening: -ons → -ōs → -os. Acc. pl. ends up as plain -os.
- **Central (B):** B preserves z as a "systemic orphan" (ch5-03). The rule fires on
  -nz- (voiced environment) producing Vːz; this is a new source of long vowels before z
  in B. B's z-handling rules then apply to Vːz.
- **Eastern (C):** C voices s word-finally and after sonorants (2C.2); the -ns→-ōs path
  interacts: after shortening, -os → -oz (word-edge voicing). The source nasal leaves no
  trace but the z voicing makes the output look like a voiced-final form.

**Assignment:** Open. Likely relevant to all three daughters but interacts differently
with each daughter's sibilant system. Evaluate per daughter in dedicated sessions.

## Candidate 4 — Medial open-syllable syncope

**Rule:** V → ∅ / in an unstressed open medial syllable (short vowel only)

**Provenance:** Dark-Ages

**Motivation:** After LCG accent retraction (rule 1.2) moves stress off the final
syllable, the antepenultimate becomes maximally unstressed in trisyllables. Under
developing stress accent, short vowels in open medial syllables are prime candidates for
reduction and deletion in rapid or casual speech. This is the mechanism behind Latin
caldus < calidus, Germanic medial syllable loss, Celtic apocope, etc.

**Environment specification:** Short vowel; open syllable (CV_ structure, i.e., not
closed by a following coda consonant); non-initial, non-final; not in the syllable
bearing stress. This requires knowing accent position, which is tracked in the pipeline.

**Output:** Deletion of the vowel creates a new consonant cluster. The cluster type
depends on the form; daughters must then resolve each cluster type by their own active
rules. This is the main point of interest: syncope is a common trigger feeding diverse
daughter-specific cluster-reduction strategies.

**Implementation note:** Explicit syllabification logic may be required in the pipeline.
A simplified approximation (V → ∅ / CV_CV where the second CV bears stress) is workable
for the majority of cases but should be tested against actual lexical forms.

**Interaction:** Syncope may create new N-obstruent adjacencies. Check whether LCG rule
1.1 (nasal assimilation) should be treated as reapplying after syncope, or whether the
new adjacency is left for daughter-specific rules. Recommended: leave new adjacencies for
daughter treatment; do not reapply 1.1 after syncope.

**Daughter differentiation notes:**

- **Western (A):** Initial stress creates maximum motivation for medial syncope. A is the
  primary candidate. 1–2 isoglosses expected: word-internal syncope at Stage 2;
  possibly also compound-internal syncope at Stage 3.
- **Central (B):** Penultimate stress is less aggressive; medial syllables adjacent to
  stress are partly protected. B may show selective syncope (only when two or more
  syllables removed from stress) or no regular syncope at Stage 2, with optional
  later developments.
- **Eastern (C):** Initial stress, same structural motivation as A. However, C already
  has substantial Stage 2–3 changes (fricative hardening, sibilant voicing, rhotacism).
  Syncope may be Stage 3+ in C, after the Stage 2 consonant system has stabilized.

**Assignment:** Tentative — primarily Western (A) at Stage 2; possibly Eastern (C)
at Stage 3; Central (B) unlikely at Stage 2.

## Candidate 5 — Word-initial prevocalic j-strengthening

**Rule:** j → dj / #_ V

**Provenance:** Dark-Ages

**Motivation:** Word-initial palatal glides are phonetically weak in onset position and
are subject to strengthening (affrication) in many languages under stress accent, when
the onset needs more phonological weight. Attested: Latin (PIE *y- → j- → eventually
underlying affricate in daughter languages), Old French, many Slavic languages.

**Scope:** Word-initial position only, before a vowel. Word-internal j (which is far
more frequent in the Ghandwa lexicon) is unaffected by this candidate and should be
addressed separately per daughter if needed.

**Daughter differentiation notes:**

- **Western (A):** Strong candidate for affrication under initial stress. dj- would then
  be subject to A's Stage 3 cluster behavior (simplification or retention).
- **Central (B):** Less certain. B's overall tendency is toward preservation and
  environment-sensitive outcomes. B might retain j- at word-initial position.
- **Eastern (C):** Stage 2C hardens voiced fricatives to stops; a parallel hardening of
  j- to dj- or d- at Stage 2 or 3 would be consistent with C's general fortition tendency.

**Assignment:** Open. Evaluate lexical coverage of j-initial forms before assigning;
if the category is small (few word-initial j- items), this may not be worth formalizing
as a rule.

## Candidate 6 — Geminate simplification (ss → s)

**Rule:** ss → s

**Provenance:** Dark-Ages

**Context:** Stage 0 Ghandwa produces ss from TT→ss (gh.1.1) with geminate-before-C
simplification (ssC → sC, gh.1.2), but ss before a vowel survives. The LCG period
retains ss. Daughters diverge in whether they simplify or preserve.

**Motivation for simplification:** Geminates are typologically fragile under stress
accent when they arise phonologically rather than morphologically. A daughter that is
simplifying consonant clusters generally (e.g., under syncope or stressed-syllable
augmentation) will tend to simplify ss along with other geminate sequences.

**Motivation for retention:** A daughter that develops geminates as a productive or
morphologically meaningful category (e.g., from nasal assimilation producing mm, nn,
etc.) has systemic support for geminate contrasts and is less likely to simplify ss.

**Interaction with z-handling:** This candidate is most interesting when read against
each daughter's treatment of the s ~ z alternation. ss is not itself subject to
intervocalic voicing (gh.2.4 requires voiced on both sides; the first s of ss is not
voiced), so the geminate is immune to the s→z rule. What happens to VssV after the z
environment shifts depends entirely on the daughter's z-handling strategy.

**Daughter differentiation notes:**

- **Western (A):** A devoices z → s, simplifying the sibilant system to s only. A has
  no systemic need for the ss ~ s distinction; geminate simplification expected.
- **Central (B):** B develops productive geminates through nasal assimilation
  (2B.1: Vβm → Vmm, Vðn → Vnn). B has systemic support for geminates and is the
  primary candidate for retaining ss. B's z-fate (preserved as systemic orphan) makes
  the ss ~ s ~ z system more complex but manageable.
- **Eastern (C):** C's Stage 3 rhotacism chain (s→z→ʁ→r) applies to inherited s in
  specific environments. ss before a vowel (VssV) is immune to the initial s→z step
  (first s not flanked by voiced segments). C likely simplifies ss → s (Stage 2 or 3);
  surviving s subject to Stage 3 rhotacism environment conditions.

**Assignment:** Tentative:
- Western (A): simplifies ss → s (Stage 2, consistent with devoicing policy)
- Central (B): retains ss (systemic geminate support)
- Eastern (C): simplifies ss → s (Stage 2 or 3); surviving s subject to Stage 3
  rhotacism environment conditions
