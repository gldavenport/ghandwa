## 4. Morphological Directions

Phonology alone is not enough. The daughters should also remodel grammar differently.

### Western (A): conservative stems, reduced case system, more analytic syntax

Current direction:

- Western retains many older **consonant stems**
- Western does **not** aggressively regularize everything into a few productive stem classes
- instead, the **case system collapses**
- grammatical work shifts to:
  - **determiners**
  - **particles**
  - **adpositions**
- Western therefore becomes more **analytic** in syntax

Compact characterization:

> **Western preserves many old stems but loses most cases in favor of determiners/adpositions.**

### Central (B): mixed retention and mixed regularization

Current direction:

- Central preserves more inherited irregularity than either edge branch
- it should keep:
  - more case morphology than Western
  - more stem diversity than Eastern
- but it should also show analogical leveling
- Central is the true intermediate branch, both phonologically and morphologically

### Eastern (C): strong paradigm regularization into productive stem classes

Current direction:

- Eastern is likely to remodel nouns heavily into **o-stems** and **ā / a-stems**
- consonant stems may be absorbed analogically into these productive classes
- endings are shortened
- example: **walpos > walps**
- another likely direction: **-ā > -a**, then former consonant stems pulled into **a-stems**

Compact characterization:

> **Eastern regularizes stem classes hard, especially into o- and ā/a-type nouns, while shortening endings and eroding the right edge.**

### Useful summary contrast

- **Western**: old stems survive; case marking weakens; syntax becomes more analytic
- **Central**: mixed retention and mixed leveling
- **Eastern**: old stem classes collapse into productive paradigms; short clipped endings survive
