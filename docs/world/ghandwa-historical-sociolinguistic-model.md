## Core Premise

Late Common Ghandwa was maintained by a wide Bronze-Age pastoral, farming, and exchange network. As iron production and other local industries reduced dependence on long-distance prestige exchange, the older network contracted. Regional centers, towns, and city-states became the main sites of power and standardization.

Sound changes that had once diffused across the whole dialect pool now stopped at harder social and geographic boundaries, producing the early Post-Ghandwa daughter languages.

## Social and Linguistic Stages

| Period | Social Economy | Linguistic Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Stage 0 (Common Ghandwa)** | Long-distance exchange, mobile elites, pastoral-farming networks, marriage/client ties | Broad intelligibility; innovations diffuse widely; scribal norm maintained by specialist class |
| **Stage 1 (Late Common Ghandwa / Collapse)** | Network still functioning but under strain; regional centers emerging; literacy contracting | Vernacular changes already active in speech surface as the scribal infrastructure fails |
| **Stage 2 (Dark Age)** | Trade contraction, insecurity, smaller exchange zones, local power centers; literacy reduced to a small discontinuous specialist class | Isoglosses stop propagating evenly; dialect-chain weakening; written tradition interrupted |
| **Stage 3 (Early Daughters)** | Cities, fortified towns, regional cults; literacy restored under local institutional auspices | Local dialects standardize; writing re-emerges reflecting local spoken norms with archaizing elements |
| **Stage 4 (Flourishing)** | Stabilized literary standards; schools and learned traditions; canonical texts | Written prestige forms diverge from everyday speech; visible high-register change slows |

## Dialect Pool Model

Common Ghandwa — through Stage 1 — is a **network-maintained dialect pool**: a broad interaction-sphere language held together by Bronze Age trade, mobility, and prestige rather than by any single institutional standard or uniform proto-language. It is not locatable in any single zone. Dialectal differences existed within it but were socially marked rather than structurally distinct; regional speakers were intelligible to one another, though social attitudes toward regional pronunciation varied (compare Roman attitudes toward provincial Latin).

The closest typological parallel is Koch's model of pre-Celtic Europe: a broad middle zone speaking a prestige interaction-sphere medium, with edge branches (Italic cut off by the Adriatic and the Italian peninsula, Germanic coming in from the north and northeast) crystallizing first under weaker contact pressure while the center remains conservative and diffuse. In Ghandwa terms, Central (B) is the residue of that center — the last to harden, the most conservative, the phonologically messiest. Western (A) and Eastern (C) are the edge branches.

The term *koine* is deliberately avoided for Stage 0–1 Ghandwa. A koine is a post-divergence contact variety, leveled from already-differentiated dialects and spread artificially (Greek Koine being the canonical case). Common Ghandwa predates divergence; it is the ancestor, not a leveled product of contact. *Koine* is the more appropriate term for Neo-Ghandwa at Stage 3 — a learned prestige variety reconstructed from fragmentary evidence after fragmentation, spreading through contact among the daughter communities.

The geographic grid below is scaffolding. The operative axis at present is **W ↔ C ↔ E**, corresponding to daughters A, B, and C respectively. North-South rows are reserved for future development and should not be treated as linguistically active.

```text
NW   N   NE
W    C   E       ← operative axis
SW   S   SE
```

At Stage 0–1, speakers from any zone can communicate in speech and writing. They may notice regional pronunciation, different degrees of final-vowel reduction, different treatment of *s*, or different cluster developments, but these differences are not yet strong enough to break mutual intelligibility.

## Network Strength and Isogloss Propagation

Before contraction, innovations can move through the full network:

```text
W ↔ C ↔ E
```

After contraction, the network becomes patchier:

```text
W ↔ C      E
```

The same sound change can therefore behave differently depending on the historical period:

```text
same innovation + strong network = shared or leveled feature
same innovation + weak network = regional boundary
```

## Sound-Change Chronology

### Pan-Ghandwa Changes

Changes belonging to Stage 0 are shared across the whole dialect pool and inherited by all daughter languages.

Examples:

```text
centum merger
voiced aspirates > voiced fricatives
TT > ss
intervocalic s > z
```

### Stage 1 Changes

Stage 1 changes were active in vernacular speech during Stage 0, suppressed by the scribal norm. They surface in writing as that infrastructure collapses. Because they were pan-dialectal before the collapse — either spreading through the full continuum early or arising convergently — they appear as shared across all daughters.

Examples:

```text
nasal-obstruent assimilation
final-syllable accent retraction
trimoraic glide-sequence regularization
```

### Late Dialect-Pool Isoglosses

Changes beginning during Stage 1 can spread unevenly across the W/C/E axis.

Example: final-vowel erosion beginning in the East.

| Zone | Stage 1 | Stage 2 Result |
|---|---|---|
| **West (A)** | final vowels retained | retained |
| **Central (B)** | weak reduction in casual speech | final *a/e/o* lost; *i/u* retained |
| **East (C)** | reduction already common | all final short vowels lost |

### Daughter-Defining Changes

Changes beginning at Stage 2 remain local.

Example: western rhotacism.

```text
Common Ghandwa: intervocalic *s > z
Western daughter (A): z > r
Eastern daughter (C): z remains z
```

So:

```text
Stage 0–1: ménezos
Stage 3 Western (A): méneros
Stage 3 Eastern (C): ménezos
```

## Writing and Literacy

Stage 0 Ghandwa had a literate specialist class (priestly caste, temple functionaries, urban scribes) and a broader partially literate population. Ordinary writing was restricted to grave markers, dedications, legal and cultic formulae, short administrative records.

Stage 1 represents an interrupted literacy event. Continuous institutional writing broke down; the written tradition survived only in fragmentary form among a small, discontinuous specialist class. This is the Linear B gap model, not the Latin chancery model.

When writing re-emerges in Stage 3, it reflects local spoken norms with archaizing elements. There is no persistent common written standard constraining daughter divergence.

Illustration of speech divergence during Stage 1:

```text
Western speech:  wekʷeti
Central speech:  wekʷetə / wekʷet
Eastern speech:  wekʷet
```

Stage 3 daughter writing reflects local spoken forms:

```text
Western daughter writing:  wekʷeti (conservative)
Central daughter writing:  wekʷet
Eastern daughter writing:  wekʷet / wekʷt
```

## Stage 3: Neo-Ghandwa

Stage 3 is the first recovery phase after the Dark Age collapse. Three sociolinguistic layers are to be distinguished (see also `docs/grammar/ch5-daughters/ch5-03-comparative-phonology.md` §3.1 for the linguistic detail):

1. **Neo-Ghandwa** at the top, primarily priestly-scholarly in status;
2. beneath it, a practical **merchant-warrior koine** serving trade, movement, and interregional contact;
3. beneath that, the **common tongues** — the ordinary daughter vernaculars.

Neo-Ghandwa is a **koine** in the proper sense of the term: a post-divergence contact variety, leveled from already-differentiated daughter dialects and spread artificially through learned channels. This is why the term was deliberately avoided for Stage 0–1 Common Ghandwa, which predates divergence and is the ancestor, not a leveled product of contact. At Stage 3, the conditions for a koine are finally met: the daughters have already diverged, and Neo-Ghandwa is reconstructed from fragmentary evidence rather than continuously transmitted.

Because the surviving Stage 0–1 corpus is narrow and skewed — dominated by priestly, ritual, legal, and inscriptional language — Neo-Ghandwa is expected to be formulaic, ideologically conservative, only partly authentic to Stage 0 speech, and disproportionately influenced by those registers. It is a learned recovery, not a living dialect. Its spread through the daughter communities depends entirely on the institutions — priestly orders, scholarly networks, interregional cult centers — that maintain and transmit it.

This dependency is also its structural vulnerability. The same institutional class responsible for Neo-Ghandwa is the class that maintained the unified Agnis theology (see `docs/world/lore.md` §"The Religious Landscape"). The theological synthesis — the "all aspects are one Agnis" position — and the linguistic koine share a single institutional infrastructure. When that infrastructure is strong, both the theology and the prestige language propagate together. When the class disperses — as it does at Stage 1, and as it may again in localized ways at later stages — both the synthesis and the koine lose their transmission mechanism simultaneously. The religious divergence of the daughter traditions and their linguistic divergence are two faces of the same institutional collapse.

## Typological Analogies

| Analogy | Relevance |
|---|---|
| **Ancient Greek dialects** | Dialect continuum; overlapping isoglosses; shared inherited language; regional literary norms |
| **Mycenaean Greek → Dark Age** | Literate specialist class; collapse of the palatial system; interrupted writing tradition; re-emergence under new institutional conditions |
| **Koch's Celtic interaction-sphere model** | Broad prestige medium maintained by network coherence across a wide zone; edge branches crystallize first; center remains conservative and diffuse; branches harden at periphery when contact weakens |

## Practical Encoding for the Generator

Each sound change carries the following metadata. `geographic_origin` and `chronological_stage` are separate fields; pan-Ghandwa changes have `geographic_origin: null`.

The `register` field tracks three layers:
- `formal_writing` — scribal/chancery norm
- `sub_formal_writing` — informal or sub-elite written use
- `ordinary_speech` — vernacular

Example — pan-Ghandwa change:

```yaml
change: intervocalic s > z
geographic_origin: null
chronological_stage: stage-0-common-ghandwa
start_stage: stage-0-common-ghandwa
completion_stage: stage-0-common-ghandwa
reach:
  all_nodes: complete
register:
  formal_writing: active
  sub_formal_writing: active
  ordinary_speech: active
status: pan-Ghandwa
```

Example — Stage 1 change (vernacular-first):

```yaml
change: nasal-obstruent assimilation
geographic_origin: null
chronological_stage: stage-1-late-common-ghandwa
start_stage: stage-0-common-ghandwa
completion_stage: stage-1-late-common-ghandwa
reach:
  all_nodes: complete
register:
  formal_writing: absent
  sub_formal_writing: present
  ordinary_speech: active
status: pan-Ghandwa
notes: Active in vernacular speech during Stage 0; surfaces in writing only at Stage 1 as scribal infrastructure collapses.
```

Example — dialect-pool isogloss:

```yaml
change: final non-high vowel apocope
geographic_origin: East
chronological_stage: stage-1-late-common-ghandwa
start_stage: stage-1-late-common-ghandwa
completion_stage: stage-2-dark-age
reach:
  East: complete
  Central: partial
  West: absent
register:
  formal_writing: absent
  sub_formal_writing: absent
  ordinary_speech: active
status: dialect-pool isogloss
```

Example — daughter-level change:

```yaml
change: z > r
geographic_origin: West
chronological_stage: stage-2-dark-age
start_stage: stage-2-dark-age
completion_stage: stage-3-early-daughters
reach:
  West: complete
  Central: partial lexical diffusion
  East: absent
register:
  ordinary_speech: active
  sub_formal_writing: regionally variable
  formal_writing: regionally variable
status: daughter-level innovation
```

Example — parallel innovation (convergent, not inherited):

```yaml
change: word-final obstruent devoicing
geographic_origin: null
chronological_stage: stage-3-early-daughters
start_stage: stage-3-early-daughters
completion_stage: stage-3-early-daughters
reach:
  West: complete
  Central: absent
  East: complete
register:
  ordinary_speech: active
  formal_writing: regionally variable
status: parallel-innovation
notes: Independent development in A and C; absence in B confirms non-inheritance.
```

## Diagnostic Rule

```text
Pan-Ghandwa change       = stage-0 or stage-1; geographic_origin: null; reach: all_nodes
Gradient isogloss        = stage-1; geographic_origin specifies source zone; reach uneven
Daughter-defining change = stage-2 or later
Parallel innovation      = stage-3 or later; non-adjacent daughters only; not reconstructible to Common Ghandwa
```

## Summary

Common Ghandwa is a network-maintained dialect pool — a broad interaction-sphere language held together by Bronze Age trade, mobility, and prestige — not locatable in any single dialect zone. Regional innovations begin while the network is still active, diffusing as soft isoglosses rather than hard boundaries. Stage 1 changes were running in vernacular speech before the collapse and surface in writing only as the scribal infrastructure fails. When the exchange network contracts at Stage 2, the dialect chain weakens and literacy collapses to a discontinuous specialist class. When writing re-emerges at Stage 3, it reflects local spoken norms rather than a maintained common standard. Formerly soft regional variation hardens into the early Post-Ghandwa daughter languages: A (West), B (Central), C (East).
