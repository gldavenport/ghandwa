## Settlement Pattern

The Ghandwa-speaking world expands from an initial coastal/delta zone along multiple river systems. The delta and shoreline establish the original base; upriver penetration along distinct river corridors creates the divergent dialect trajectories that eventually produce the daughter branches.

This geography generates the characteristic features of the Ghandwa dialect situation: prolonged dialect chains along river corridors; overlapping innovation zones at tributary confluences and coastal junctions; substrate contact variation throughout secondary settlement zones; mixed dialects in high-contact central zones; conservative dialects at isolated inland headwaters or peripheral coastal inlets.

The geographic model is compatible with a Koch-style interaction-sphere analysis: the river-maritime network that sustains economic contact also sustains linguistic coherence at the Common Ghandwa stage. Its fragmentation during the Iron Age collapse is a principal driver of dialect crystallization. See `docs/world/ghandwa-historical-sociolinguistic-model.md` for the full sociolinguistic model.

## Economy

Combined maritime and riverine trade:

- maritime networks link coastal and delta settlements across considerable distances
- riverine penetration carries settlement and exchange into the interior
- coastal prestige networks maintain linguistic and cultural contact among otherwise distant communities
- inland tributary economies produce agricultural and extractive surplus that flows toward coastal nodes

The economic model explains the dialect geography. Coastal settlements, connected through trade, remain in contact and stay linguistically conservative longer. Inland valleys, insulated from coastal networks by terrain, diverge more rapidly. Confluence cities accumulate mixed dialects from multiple upstream tributaries and function as centers of koineization.

## Septakvās

*Septakvās* ("Seven Rivers") is the major confluence city and prestige center of the Common Ghandwa period. A Bronze Age river-maritime city at the convergence of multiple major river systems, it is the institutional center of the prestige koine — the source of the scribal, administrative, and priestly norms that function as a roof over regional spoken variation.

The sociolinguistic model treats *Septakvās* and comparable urban nodes as the political-institutional anchors of the old hegemonic order. When climate stress and internal crisis bring that order down, the loss of these centers is a principal mechanism of the Stage 1 collapse: the normative infrastructure departs with the regime, and regional vernaculars become the new standard. See ch5-02 §2.1.

Central Ghandwic may preserve the most direct continuation of the Septakvās metropolitan dialect — the leveled, mixed speech of the confluence zone, inheriting the old koine without the institutional machinery that maintained it.

The "Seven Rivers" etymology is transparent (*septam* + *akvā-*). The city's mythological and cultural profile is pending development; currently mentioned only in ch5-02 §2.1.

## Ethnonyms

*teutā treies akvās* — "the people of the three rivers" — formal phrase for a Ghandwa-speaking group identified with a particular three-river confluence.

*treiakvaskōs* — "Three-Rivers person" — derived singular ethnonym.
