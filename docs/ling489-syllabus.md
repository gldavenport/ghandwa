# LING 489: Constructed Proto-Indo-European Daughter Languages
## Special Topics Seminar — Don Ringe, University of Pennsylvania

**Enrollment:** 2–5 students by instructor permission only.  
**Prerequisites:** Historical Linguistics (LING 301), Indo-European (LING 420). Exceptions at instructor discretion for students who can demonstrate equivalent preparation.  
**Format:** Weekly seminar meeting (~90 min). No lectures. Students are expected to arrive having done the reading and ready to defend their current work.

---

### Course Description

Students construct a plausible unattested daughter of Proto-Indo-European meeting scholarly standards of regularity, parsimony, and attestation. The conlang is a vehicle, not the destination — the goal is deep fluency in the comparative method, diachronic phonology, and morphological reconstruction. The final deliverable is a justified lexicon of 200 entries with full etymological apparatus, negotiated individually with the instructor.

Computational implementation of the rule set is encouraged but not required. A student who applies ordered rules rigorously by hand and catches their own inconsistencies is doing the core work. A working transformer that automatically derives and audits all lexicon entries will satisfy the consistency audit requirement and is a legitimate final artifact in its own right — but the linguistic judgment behind it is what is being evaluated, not the code.

---

### A Note on Format

This is a seminar of at most five people. There are no anonymous peer reviews, no standardized rubrics, and no lectures. Each meeting functions as a lab session: students present current work, the group identifies problems, the instructor pushes. Background and specialization will be known to the instructor from the start; expectations will be calibrated accordingly. A Germanic specialist will be held to a higher standard on Germanic cognates. A Hellenist will be expected to justify every laryngeal decision by reference to Greek evidence. The reading list below is a shared baseline; individual reading will be assigned as gaps emerge.

---

### Unit 1 — Foundations (Weeks 1–3)

**Week 1: The Comparative Method as Constraint**
- What makes a sound change a law rather than a tendency
- Neogrammarian regularity: exceptions require explanation, not tolerance
- Reading: Ringe 2017 ch. 1–2; Osthoff & Brugmann 1878 preface

**Week 2: The PIE Inventory**
- Stops, laryngeals, syllabic resonants — what is and isn't settled
- The limits of reconstruction: what "PIE" actually names
- Reading: Ringe 2017 ch. 3; Fortson §3–4

**Week 3: Daughter Language Typology**
- What distinguishes WIE from IIr from Anatolian as bundles of innovations
- How to position a new language: choosing your isoglosses deliberately
- Reading: Ringe 2017 ch. 4–5; Clackson *Indo-European Linguistics* ch. 3

---

### Unit 2 — Design (Weeks 4–6)

**Week 4: Phonological Innovation I — Consonantism**
- Designing obstruent changes: mergers, splits, lenitions
- The three-way stop contrast and what happens to it cross-linguistically
- *Assignment:* Propose and justify two consonantal changes that "change the nature" of your language. Be prepared to defend what "change the nature" means.

**Week 5: Phonological Innovation II — Syllabic Resonants and Vowels**
- The CaRC / RaC / RuC options and their attestation across daughters
- Laryngeal coloring, compensatory lengthening, vowel mergers
- *Assignment:* State your complete syllabic resonant treatment with typological justification.

**Week 6: Rule Ordering and Feeding/Bleeding**
- Why order matters: demonstrate a feeding and a bleeding interaction in your own system
- The importance of committing rules before deriving forms — revision after the fact is the primary failure mode
- *Assignment:* First full draft of ordered sound change document. Bring to seminar for group review.

---

### Unit 3 — Justification Standards (Weeks 7–9)

**Week 7: Pre-forms and Provenance**
- Every surface form must trace to a PIE or PIE-like pre-form
- Four categories: mechanical inheritance, analogical extension, language-internal derivation, substrate borrowing
- Every entry must be classifiable; "I'm not sure" is not a category
- *Assignment:* Audit 20 lexicon entries; classify each; flag unjustified forms.

**Week 8: Analogical and Internal Formations**
- What counts as a valid analogy: you must name the exemplar, the pattern, and the extension
- What morphology is available to a daughter: only what PIE had, or what your daughter has independently innovated and can document
- Reading: Ringe 2017 ch. 6; Weiss *Outline* ch. on analogy

**Week 9: The Etymological Dictionary Standard**
- De Vaan EDL and Kroonen EDPG as models: what apparatus is required
- Cognate columns as corroboration, not justification — a real Italic cognate supports your etymology; it does not replace it
- LIV² root citation: when obligatory, when optional
- *Assignment:* Write five full etymological dictionary entries to EDL standard. These will be discussed line by line in seminar.

---

### Unit 4 — Core Vocabulary (Weeks 10–11)

**Week 10: Coverage Obligations**
- Why basic vocabulary is the hardest test: most likely inherited, most likely to show suppletive or analogical irregularity
- Pronouns, numerals, kinship: the high-obligation paradigms; no language gets to skip these
- The Swadesh-100 as a minimum floor, not a target ceiling
- *Assignment:* Coverage audit against Swadesh-100; bring gap list to seminar.

**Week 11: Lexicon Negotiation**
- Individual meetings with instructor to finalize the 200-entry target list
- Every student's list will cover Swadesh-100 fully; remaining entries will be drawn from semantic fields appropriate to the student's mythological or cultural framing, with hard cases distributed deliberately
- Students who have avoided a difficult form class (e.g. athematic verbs, heteroclitic nouns) will find one assigned

---

### Unit 5 — Verification and Deliverables (Weeks 12–14)

**Week 12: The Unintelligibility Test**
- Reading aloud: can a trained PIE reconstructionist hear through your changes?
- Writing legibility: can the same person read a written text with effort and a grammar sketch?
- In-seminar exercise: students exchange a short text; attempt decipherment without the rule set.

**Week 13: The Consistency Audit**
- Every form derivable by stated rules from a stated pre-form
- The group works through each student's rule set looking for counterexamples, unordered interactions, and silent exceptions
- Students who have implemented a transformer run it against the full lexicon and bring the output; students working by hand bring a derivation table

**Week 14: Defenses**
- Each student presents: one phonological innovation argued typologically, five lexicon entries defended to EDL standard, one irregular or analogical form fully justified
- The seminar functions as examining committee. Questions will not be gentle.

---

### Final Deliverable

A lexicon of 200 entries, list negotiated with instructor in Week 11, meeting the following standards:

1. Every entry has a stated PIE or PIE-like pre-form with LIV² or NIL citation where applicable
2. Provenance category stated for every entry: inherited / analogical / internal / substrate
3. At least one cognate column populated from a primary reference (EDL, EDPG, EDPC) wherever a real cognate exists
4. Surface form derivable by the student's stated ordered rule set — either by transformer output or by an explicit hand derivation on file
5. Swadesh-100 fully covered
6. Minimum 10 significant phonological changes documented; at least two argued as typologically fundamental with cross-linguistic support

**Grading:** 40% final lexicon, 30% ordered rule document, 20% etymological dictionary entries (wk. 9), 10% seminar participation. Participation means substantive criticism of others' work, not attendance.

### Primary References

Students are expected to own or have continuous library access to:

- Ringe 2017 — *From Proto-Indo-European to Proto-Germanic*, 2nd ed. (primary framework)
- De Vaan 2008 — *Etymological Dictionary of Latin* (EDL; cognate and apparatus model)
- Kroonen 2013 — *Etymological Dictionary of Proto-Germanic* (EDPG)
- Rix et al. 2001 — *Lexikon der indogermanischen Verben*, 2nd ed. (LIV²)
- Matasović 2009 — *Etymological Dictionary of Proto-Celtic* (EDPC)
- Weiss 2020 — *Outline of the Historical and Comparative Grammar of Latin*, 2nd ed.
- Fortson 2010 — *Indo-European Language and Culture*, 2nd ed. (background reference)

Additional reading assigned individually as gaps emerge.
