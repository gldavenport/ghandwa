## Chapter 6. Early shifts of back vowels.

It is difficult to say much about the relative chronology of the changes discussed in this chapter. I collect them here because they all involve back vowels and
all seem to have preceded the general restructuring of the vowel system (on which see
Chapter 8).

### 38. Word-final *o.

In absolute word-final position *6 normally became *t (Penney 1976:79,
Normier 1980:255 fn. 14; cf. already Pedersen 1941:229). Note the following examples:

PIE *du6x ‘two’ (see 3) > *du6 > *dw6 > *wo (see 27) > *wii > PT *wti >
TA masc. wu (Winter 1962b:29);

PIE *ku6 ‘dog’ (see 16) > *kwé > *kwii > *kii > PT *kt > TB, TA ku;

PIE *xntbh6x ‘both’ (see 3) > *xntb6 >— *antphi > PT *antpi > TA fem.
ampu-k;

PIE subj. and them. pres. lsg. *-ox (see 3) > *-0 > *-0 > PT *-u > TB subj.
I (and probably pres. I) Isg. -uv, e.g. in d@yu ‘I will give’, yamu ‘I will do’ (Pedersen
1941:141; see note 1);

PIE *oKt6(w) ‘eight’ (see 16) > *oktii > *éktii (see 39) > *okti (u-umlaut,
see 43) > *okti — PT *oktd on the analogy of *soptd ‘seven’ and *fiiwo ‘nine’
(Penney 1976:89 fn. 50; cf. Ringe 1987a:107) > TB okt, TA okdit; pre-PT *-u is preserved in TA oktuk ‘eighty’ (see note 2).

It is possible that the *-0 of the PIE sequence *-oX6 escaped raising to *-0,
though the details of that development are unclear; see 4 for further discussion.

Note the loss of *w in the development of PT *ku ‘dog’. Postconsonantal
*w was not normally lost in PT (cf. PT obl. *kwéno < PIE *Kw6énm, see 16; cf. also
47, A14, and B28), so we must posit a special development for this case. The likeliest suggestion is that *w was lost because it was preceded by a consonant and followed by *a; such a change is overwhelmingly likely because [w] is difficult to hear
in such a position. If that is what happened, this loss of *w must have followed the
raising of final *0 to *0 but probably was not long delayed after the vowel change
had occurred. Since the *w of ‘two’ survived in PT, its *d must have been dropped
(see 27) before the loss of *w between a consonant and *i occurred; and if *Cwisequences did not survive for long, the latest possible date for the loss of *d before
consonants is roughly the period when word-final *6 was becoming *i.

<!-- pdf-page: 116; printed-page: 90 -->

The development of the duals ‘two’ and ‘both’ and of the Isg. ending *-ox
show that the raising of word-final *6 followed the contraction of vowels with tautosyllabic laryngeals (see 3), which is not surprising. On the other hand, the chronology of this raising of final *6 relative to the unrounding of nonfinal *6 (see 40) is not
determinable. We could conceivably argue that the change *-0 > *-i is likely to have
preceded the unrounding of *6 to *€ on the grounds that the latter change was much
more general (so that the more specific change bled the more general change); but
though such a scenario is simple and elegant, we have no real evidence that that is
what happened. Particularly disturbing is the fact that the “general” unrounding of *6
was apparently never general enough to affect the reflex of PIE *-oX6 (or possibly
*-On; see 4). Conceivably there were always some final *-6 in Tocharian until vowel
length was lost altogether.

On the other hand, the raising of final *0 to *i must have preceded u-umlaut,
since it triggered that change (see 43); it must also have preceded the general loss of
vowel length, by which *i became PT *u (see 50).

Notes.

1. TB primary active 1sg. -u actually appears only in athematic categories;
the corresponding thematic ending is -e,, ~ -au < -e- + -u, the thematic vowel -e- reflecting PT *é < PIE *o. Nevertheless the hypothesis that -u ultimately reflects PIE
thematic *-ox seems more plausible than the suggestion of a connection with Luvian
lsg. -wi, which has no other cognates outside of Anatolian. A transfer of endings
from thematic to athematic stems is scarcely surprising in a language first attested in
the 6th c. A.D. For further discussion and references (and yet another assessment of
the evidence!) see Van Windekens 1982:261.

### 2. It is also possible that *okti was replaced by *okt4n or *oktd before the
restructuring of the vowel system (in which *ti became *u). Note that this numeral
behaves in Tocharian as though it ended in *-d, not *-dw; whether that is the result of
a specific sound change *-Ow > *-0 will remain unclear so long as the problems regarding the ending of PIE ‘eight’ remain unsolved.

3. The history of nom. sg. pret. participles and adjectives in TB, TA -u was
complex, and their PT *-u- probably does not reflect PIE *0; see bérhallsdottir 1988:
188-90, 196-8 for further discussion.

### 39. Unrounding of *o.

Inherited short *o, probably including the reflex of PIE *xWe (see 3), was
unrounded in pre-PT. The precise phonetic nature of the new unrounded vowel is
not recoverable, though it must have been some sort of nonfront unround mid vowel
(cf. Jasanoff 1978:33 with fnn. 18 and 19). I shall write it as *€. There are dozens
of examples, of which the following are typical:

### 1. It is likely that there was a period during which pre-PT had no short *o.
Such a circumstance might account for the shape of the TB word for ‘fish’. The
attested forms are nom.(/obl.) sg. [dks (w. dialect; would be c./e. *laks), nom. pl.
ldksi, obl. pl. lakscim (Krause 1961b:83-4). Since its discovery this word has been
held to reflect PIE *loKs- ‘salmon’ (Lith. /@Sis, OE leax, etc.), or possibly an ablaut
alternant of the same, but in fact the only PIE vowel that could regularly underlie the
nonpalatalizing /o/ of the root syllable is *u (pace Krause, loc. cit. pp. 85-6, 89; see
50 below). The endings are best interpreted as u-stem endings (Cowsgill, p. c. 1980,
again pace Krause, loc. cit. pp. 84-5): PIE nom. sg. *-us, acc. sg. *-um > PT *-9 >
TB nom.(/obl.) 8 (= /-o/); PIE nom. pl. *-ewes > PT *-YiiwYa > TB *-Yiya > -Yi; PIE
acc. pl. *-uns > PT obl. pl. *-9 (?; the recessive accent of the TB form apparently demands a monosyllabic ending in PT and for some time thereafter) + n-stem *-no >
TB -dm. Though analogical changes (which were extensive in Tocharian nominal
inflection) can have altered the endings, the fact that this noun belongs to an unproductive inflectional class (Krause and Thomas 1960:131-2) at least suggests that any
such changes did not occur recently. It is reasonable to suppose a pre-PT form
*luks-, possibly even *luksus; and the most likely explanation for such a form is that
it was borrowed from a language in which the word was *loks-, possibly *loksos,
during the period when pre-PT had no *o and thus had to substitute some other vowel (in this case, perhaps the only short round vowel that it possessed; but see 43).
Since the unrounding of PIE “o can be a very early change, it is not implausible to
suggest that the borrowing occurred when the pre-Tocharians were still in eastern
Europe and in contact with non-Iranian IE languages; but the source language for the
borrowing cannot be identified. Of course this is not the only scenario possible; consequently the shape of ‘fish’ in TB cannot be called probative evidence for a very
early unrounding of PIE *o.

### 2. That the -o of Baktrian loanwords appears as *-é in PT need not mean that
the Tocharian unrounding of PIE *o occurred in the first century A.D. or later (pace
Winter 1971:221 and Ringe 1987a:27 fn. 27), since the Tocharian ending might have
been assigned according to a rule of morphological equivalence by speakers that
knew both languages well. (See also Schwartz 1974:409-10.)

### 40. Unrounding of *6.

Except when word-final (see 38), inherited *6, including the reflexes of PIE
“exW and “oX (see 3), appears in PT as *a (Pedersen 1941:226, etc.). Note the following examples:

PIE *6msos ‘shoulder’ (see 3) > PT *4nsé > TB dntse, TA *ays > es (see
A10);

PIE *péxwor collective ‘fire’ (see 7) > *pxudr > PT *puwar > TB puwar,
TA *pwar > por;

PIE *trix (d)K6mt ‘three tens’ (see 9) > PT *torYyaka ‘thirty’ > TB tdryaka
(Schindler 1967b:240);

PIE *gnex- ‘recognize, know’ (see 3) > *gn0- > PT *kna- > TA knda-, e.g.
in pres. ptc. kndnmam ‘knowing’; PT *aknatSa ‘ignorant’ (see 31, 44, and 66) >
TB aknatsa, TA aknats;

PIE *g¥rox-mVr- ‘heavy load’ (see 3) > *g¥romVr- > PT *k¥ramer > TB
kramdar ‘burden’ (cf. Schindler 1972:149);

PIE *dhoXnéx ‘grain’ > *dhona (Skt. pl. dhdnds coll. ‘grain’, Lith diona

‘pread’) > PT *tana- > TB tano, pl. tana.
Since the (post-)PIE starting point of this development was clearly a long vowel, and
since vowel length seems to have been lost in a general restructuring of the vowel
system not long before the PT period (see 50), I suggest that the immediate ancestor
of this PT *a was *4. We must then explain how (post-)PIE *6 became *a. That is
not likely to have been a simple development, since (post-)PIE *4 conversely became
*6 (whence PT *o, see 42); the two vowels are most unlikely to have been affected
by an “exchange rule” (a type of historical development whose existence has never
been firmly established), and they must somehow have developed along phonetic
paths that did not cross. Various solutions to this problem are possible, including
those that posit dialect differences and/or synchronic variation; but the simplest solution posits three changes as follows.

(1) Since inherited *o was unrounded to *é, which (whatever its exact phonetic realization) remained distinct from *a, it is reasonable to suggest also that nonfinal *6 was unrounded to “*é” at the same time, and that *€ remained distinct from
*4 or its reflex. (It is not clear why final *6 escaped this unrounding, though it evidently did so (see 38); presumably it was already somewhat different phonetically
before the unrounding occurred, though it need not already have become *.)

(2) Inherited *4 was rounded and possibly raised to *6. This change need not
have followed (1); all that is necessary is that the new *6’s have remained distinct
from the inherited *0’s at all times. But from that it does follow that rounding of inherited *4 must at least have begun before (3) occurred.

(3) The new long vowel *é was lowered to *4. The general loss of vowel
length (see 50) eventually followed.

I will tentatively adopt the above chronology as a working hypothesis; but it
should be remembered that the sequence of changes I posit has by no means been
demonstrated.

41. *a in final syllables.

In at least some final syllables (post-)PIE *4 appears in PT as *a. It is extremely difficult to specify the exact conditions under which this occurred, because all
our examples belong to paradigms that can have undergone extensive analogical
changes (cf. Marggraf 1975:198-9).

It seems likely that *4 in absolute word-final position eventually gave PT *a
by regular sound changes, and that this occurred even in monosyllables; at least that
is the most straightforward way to account for the shape of the following demonstrative:

PIE *séx nom. sg. fem. ‘that’ > *sa (Skt. sd, Gk. tj, Goth. so) > PT *sd >

TB sd nom. sg. fem. ‘this’ (so Winter 1981:939); or is the PT form a reflex of the
PIE sandhi variant *[s4] with loss of the laryngeal in pausa? (so Peters 1991a:242;
see 2 (f) above).
This fits well with the fact that many TB nom. sg. fem. forms (especially of adjectives) and most nom./acc. pl. neut. forms end in -a; though some of each can reflect
PIE *-x (see 14)—and feminines in -ya certainly do reflect PIE *-ix (see 9)—the
sheer number of forms in -a < PT *-a suggests that others reflect *-4 < PIE *-ex.
This conclusion is supported especially by the correlation nom. sg. masc. -e : fem. -a
in several types of TB adjectives (e.g. -sse : -ssa, -tstse : -tstsa, -lye : -lya) and in diminutives (-ske : -Ska); it is hard to believe that these pairs do not reflect PIE masc.
*-os : fem. *-ex > *-a. (Unfortunately this is not the only possible explanation; see
note 1 below.)

The fate of post-PIE *-am (see 3, note 3) in Tocharian is unclear. TB ta, the
obl. sg. of sa fem. ‘this’, might reflect post-PIE *tam (Skt. tam, Gk. thv, Goth.
po), but its ending also might have been introduced from the nom. sg. by approximate analogy with masc. nom. sg. se, obl. sg. ce (the principle apparently being that
in each nom./obl. pair the initial consonant is different but the vowel the same; it
seems less likely that the this form simply lacked *-m, pace Winter 1981:939). On
the other hand, TB obl. Sano ‘wife’ and a few similar obl. sg. forms (which could be
analogical on Sano, cf. Marggraf 1975:200) might reflect post-PIE *-am by regular
sound change, since after all the group is isolated and the formation unproductive; but
we cannot exclude the possibility that their -o has been introduced analogically from
the plural (in this case Snona ‘wives’; Marggraf 1975:202-3), whose o can reflect
post-PIE *4 provided that the latter survived as such in medial syllables anywhere in
the paradigm. There seems to be no principled way to choose between these hypotheses.

PT *tdy fem. ‘those’ < *tas < PIE *téxes (see 24) shows that the *a of post-
PIE *-as underwent rounding (see 42) in monosyllables. Peters 1991a adduces
putative cases of PT *-o < post-PIE *-as in polysyllables; though not all his examples
are equally plausible, it seems extremely likely that PT *kantw6 (see 18) does ultimately reflect nom. sg. *dnghwas < PIE *dnghwéx-s. As counterevidence one might
adduce the fact that in Tocharian the PJE neuter gender has merged morphologically
with the feminine in the plural; very many TB fem. pl. nom./obl. forms end in -a <
PT *-a, and one might argue that this is difficult to explain except on the hypothesis
that post-PIE (fem.) a-stem nom./acc. pl. *-as had become *-a in PT, thereby becoming homophonous with the corresponding neuter ending. But in fact such an argument is not probative. Precisely the same morphological merger has occurred in Rumanian, in spite of the fact that Latin neut. pl. nom./acc. -a and fem. nom. pl. -ae
(which are the relevant surviving endings) never merged phonologically in that language. (See note 2 for further details and examples.) Evidently such a morphological development does not presuppose any phonological merger of endings; consequently we cannot adduce PT fem. pl. forms in *-a as evidence for a sound change
*-As > *-a in pre-PT.

2sg. active verb forms in *-as and 3sg. forms in *-At are another potential
source of PT *-a; but again it is impossible to show that such a development occurred
by regular sound change. Tocharian a-presents and a-subjunctives (class V) offer no
evidence at all, because they are unsuffixed athematic formations to roots in PT *-a-
(Cowgill 1967:179, 180 fn. 1; Winter apud Cowgill loc. cit.) whose *-a- ultimately
reflects PIE root-final laryngeals (cf. the discussion in 14 above); they have nothing
to do with the 4-optative of Italic and Celtic (pace Marggraf 1975:199 and others),
nor with PIE presents in *-ex- (pace Adams 1988:65-7). If the endings of the class I
preterite reflect an earlier 4-preterite (as Jasanoff 1983:62-70 argues), then it is likely
that TB 3sg. -a < PT *-a reflects earlier *-at (not necessarily inherited from PIE).
But at least some such preterites reflect PIE root-aorists to roots in *-X- (cf. the discussion in Jasanoff 1983:55-61); in at least those cases we can have PT *-a < *-at
(see 32) < PIE *-X-t (see 14), and we cannot be sure that that ending has not spread
from such forms to all class I preterite 3sgs., even if some members of this class
were originally 4-preterites.

short *a underwent no changes before the PT period. If that is what happened, the
shortening must have occurred before *4 was rounded to *6 (see 42). I will adopt
that scenario as a working hypothesis.

### 2. Latin neuter nouns developed into a class of Rumanian nouns that take
masculine concord in the singular but feminine concord in the plural, exactly like PIE
neuter nouns in Tocharian; however, the fem. pl. and neut. pl. endings did not merge
phonologically. The normal feminine development is exemplified by Lat. (nom.)

Lat. (nom./acc.) neut. Ovom, pl. Ova ‘egg’ > Rum. ou masc., pl. oud fem. ‘egg’,
with no change of endings but change of gender (a unique relic in the modern language), and further Lat. (nom./acc.) neut. filum, pl. fila ‘thread’ >— Rum. fir
masc., pl. fire fem. ‘thread’, in which the change of gender has eventually resulted in

preserves substantial traces of a similar system, with no change of endings; typical
examples are uovo masc., pl. uova fem. ‘egg’, labbro masc., pl. labbra fem. ‘lip’,
etc. (Contrast fem. casa, pl. case ‘house’.) Apparently this is so natural a way for
an IE language to reduce the complexity of its gender system that it need not be triggered or even assisted by phonological developments.

### 42. Rounding of *4.

Inherited *4 (including the reflexes of PIE *ex and *exe) in nonfinal syllables
clearly appears in PT as *o (Marggraf 1975:199; Normier 1980:254; Winter 1981:
938). Note the following examples:

PIE *bbréxtér ‘brother’ (see 3) > *bhratér > PT *précer > TB procer, TA
pracar;

PIE *bbaghu- ‘arm’ (see 16) > PT *pok- in TB obl. pokai, TA poke;

PIE *wastu ‘settlement’ (see 3) > PT *wésto ‘house’ > TB ost, TA wast;

PIE fem. suffix *-e-x- (i.e., thematic *-e- plus fem. *-x-; Skt. -d-, etc.) >
*_4- > PT *-o- in fem. adj. pl. *-o-na, e.g. in *astréna ‘pure’ > TA astram, >— TB

PIE *téxes fem. ‘those’ (see 24) > *tas > PT *téy > TB toy fem. ‘these’.

(For probable examples of PT *o < *4 in final syllables see also 41.)

For the reasons adduced in 40 it is likely that the immediate ancestor of this
PT *o was *6; it therefore appears that we must recognize a general rounding of PIE
*4 to *0. As usual, the precise phonetic outcome of this change is not determinable,
though there is some likelihood that its eventual PT reflex *o was lower than the *9

of inherited *0 and the rounding of inherited *4 see 40 ad fin.

### 43. U-umlaut.

In most circumstances inherited short *o became the unround vowel *é (see
39); however, when followed in the next syllable by *u or *t (including *i < PIE
*0) it remained or became a short o-vowel. This sound change is generally called uumlaut (Normier 1980:255; cf. already Pedersen 1941:220-1). The new o-vowel did
not merge with other o’s in PT. I will write it as *o, following Normier; that it was
actually closer than other o-vowels (as this graphy suggests) seems likely on general
grounds but cannot be proved. There are two clear examples:

PIE *doru, *dréw- ‘tree, wood’ (Skt. daru, gen. sg. drés; Gk. 86pu ‘spear
(-shaft)’) > *déru, *réw- (see 27) > *éru (Schindler, p. c. 1985) > *6ru > PT *6ra
‘wood’ > TB, TA or (etymology first in Schneider 1940:203);

PIE *okt6(w) ‘eight’ (see 16) > *ékti > *okti > *oktG — PT *okts > TB
okt, TA okdt (cf. TA oktuk ‘eighty’).

I have argued that TB soy, gen. sg. seyi ‘son’ also exhibits the effects of u-umlaut;
see 25.

In the etymologies just given I suggest that *o was unrounded to *é in all
environments, and that *é was subsequently rounded (and perhaps raised) to *o by
u-umlaut. In that scenario the individual sound changes are phonetically as simple as
possible. Unfortunately no real proof can be based on considerations of simplicity;
we cannot exclude the possibility that inherited *o was never unrounded in the u-umlaut environment. In that case “unrounding of *o” (discussed in 39) and “u-umlaut”
were two aspects of the same complex sound change. (TB ‘fish’ MIGHT provide
indirect evidence that unrounding and u-umlaut were two separate changes, but is
hardly probative; see 39, note 1, for discussion.)

However u-umlaut occurred, it must have taken place after the raising of final
*9 to *t (see 38), which created one of the umlauting environments, and before the
general loss of vowel length (see 50), in which short *u was unrounded and merged
with *o.

### 44. Lowering of word-initial *o.

Initial *o, which arose from PIE syllabic resonants (see 29), became *é (cf.
Hilmarsson 1984a:116). It is reasonable to suppose that *o was a (relatively) high
vowel (cf. Jasanoff 1978:33), and that this sound change involved lowering *9 in
word-initial position. The only indisputable example is the following:

PIE *n- ‘un-’ (Gk. &(v)-, Goth. un-, etc.) > *on- > *én- > (pre-)PT
*é(n)-, e.g. in

PT *énklYéwsotté ‘unheard of’ > TB enklyausdtte and

pre-PT *éknatSa > PT *aknatSa (by a-umlaut, see 66) ‘unknowing, ignorant’
> TB akndatsa, TA aknats (etymology first in Lane 1938:36-8; on the loss of *-n- see
31).

Also plausible is the following:

PIE *nKwés ‘perishable, mortal’ (verbal adj. to *neK- ‘perish’; see 16) >
*onkwé > PT *énkwé ‘man’ > TB erikwe, TA onk (Campanile 1969:203-4).
A further possible example is

PIE *(X)rgW6nt- ‘dark’ (see 6) > *orgWént- > *érgWént- > PT *érkWént-
‘black’ > TB erkent-, TA arkant- (Krause and Thomas 1960:57, 66).

This is an allophonic split (of *9) followed by or concomitant with a merger
(of one allophone of *o with *é); the merger could not have occurred until *o was unrounded to *é (see 39).
