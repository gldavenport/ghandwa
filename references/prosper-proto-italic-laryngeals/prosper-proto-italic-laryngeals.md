## Abstract

This paper is concerned with unexpected phonetic outcomes in the Italic languages in environments containing a combination of laryngeals, labials and laterals. It will be argued that phonotactic problems may ultimately lie behind a seemingly unmotivated absence of the laryngeal in several verbal formations based on set roots. A detailed account of the outcome of IE *-l[unclear-glyph-U+0010]- and *-lH[unclear-glyph-U+0010]- in Latin is included, as well as etymologies for fulvus and mullus.

## Conclusions

Laryngeal loss in the reviewed forms may be put down to the articulatory incompatibility of postvelar sounds and labials. When the syllable structure was #C[+lab]l˚H.C-, an extra short nucleus or schwa was generated before -l-, which developed into -ol-. Tautosyllabic sounds are more prone to coalescence than heterosyllabic and especially so in codas, so that the lateral was velarized or pharyngealized and the laryngeal was subsequently lost. The resegmentation process may have led -lC- and -łC- to be contrastive for some time, but velarized laterals had perhaps already spread to most contexts. Interestingly, this may have been a resource intended to avoid unstable initial clusters such as k[unclear-glyph-U+0010]l-, ml- and [unclear-glyph-U+0010]l(but not pl-, which is a frequent onset). This would be specially likely to happen if paradigmatic uniformity was to be preserved, and would partially account for the deviant form flatus, where the initial cluster is stable and the sequence fla- occurs in the present, perfect and infinitive. Leaving aside the possibility of rephrasing the rule as H > ø / #C[+lab]l˚_.C[+stop] (which eliminates the problem of planus, lana, and paves the way for an alternative treatment when -C2- is a labial sonorant), the counterexamples are amenable to other explanations, including intraparadigmatic analogy and secondary full grades. Our best examples are Italic *polto-, *molto- and *[unclear-glyph-U+0010]olto-/-i-, the three of which are attested in more than one dialect. The past participles *k[unclear-glyph-U+0010]olto- and *[unclear-glyph-U+0010]olto- > *[unclear-glyph-U+0010]olso- are likely cases, but they are only attested in Latin; at all events their identification as primitive regular zero grades with laryngeal loss overrides their possible intepretation as secondary /e/ grades.1 Refashioned forms with -avocalism like Umbrian comatir are compatible with this view, but ultimately not diagnostic. Even if the above mentioned reason for this change is accepted, we have to make a case for its articulatory likelihood. The usual Italic evolution of a sequence #C[unclear-glyph-U+000E]HC- is #CRaC-, with laryngeal loss and compensatory lengthening due to moraic loss. This outcome points to the vocoid being developed after the sonorant, as #CRŒH.C-. Consequently, if Proto-Italic had an alternative, slightly diﬀerent distribution #CŒRH.C- in a very specific context involving labials and velarized laterals, the expected outcome would be the same as in the simpler sequence #C[unclear-glyph-U+000E].C- and the analogous structure #C[unclear-glyph-U+000E]C.C- of Lat. mulctus. One should bear in mind that most cases are past participles in which the root was not accented, which may have contributed to this treatment decisively.
