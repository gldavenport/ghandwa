# The Italic words for ‘moon/month’ and ‘sun’

## New evidence from the Sabellian languages

Paolo Poccetti  
Università di Roma 2 “Tor Vergata”

## Source metadata

From Etymology and the European Lexicon: Proceedings of the 14th Fachtagung der Indogermanischen Gesellschaft, 17–22 September 2012, Copenhagen, edited by Bjarne Simmelkjær Sandgaard Hansen, Benedicte Nielsen Whitehead, Thomas Olander and Birgit Anette Olsen. Wiesbaden: Reichert Verlag, 2016.

## Abstract

This paper analyses two Oscan words, attested in a recently discovered Iuvilas inscription. The words at issue are minnaris, occurring in the noun phrase fisiais minnaris (dat./abl.pl.) and suleis (gen.sg.), occurring in the noun phrase prs suleis bias.

Minnaris (dat./abl.pl.), an attribute of fisia- < fēsiā- (= Lat. fēria) < *dheh₁s-i̯eh₂- ‘festival’, is an āli-adjective from the IE stem *mēns-n- < *meh₁ns-n- ‘moon/month.’ It is likely to be traced back to a preform minna- < mēnā- < *meh₁n-eh₂- or mēnnā- < *mēn-s-(n)-ā- < *meh₁n-s-(n)-eh₂-, comparable with Lat. mena and Greek μήνη. In the context, the most suitable meaning is ‘moon’. We are thus entitled to identify the inherited term for ‘moon’ in Sabellian, inflected as an ā-stem as in Latin and Greek.

As for suleis (gen.sg. of a consonant stem), it represents the Oscan (and more generally Sabellian) outcome of PIE *séh₂ul/n- ‘sun’. Just like Latin sōl, the Oscan word raises some problems concerning the rendering of the root vowel. Both lexical items point to a common Proto-Italic preform.

The meanings of these words are consistent with the sense required by the context of calendar dates connected with religious festivals that are usually mentioned in this type of text.

<!-- source-page: 353 / pdf-page: 2 -->

An Oscan inscription, recently discovered in a pre-Roman sanctuary of ancient Capua in Campania and not yet published,[^1] acquaints us with two new lexical items of the Oscan language: the words for ‘moon’ and ‘sun’, that have considerable consequences not only for the Italic branch of Indo-European, but also in a wider comparative perspective.

The new inscription belongs to the set of so-called Iuvilas texts. This small corpus of texts generally serves to describe religious ceremonies related to local aristocratic families and to preserve archaic rituals connected with religious festivals and official calendar dates of ancient Capua in pre-Roman Campania.

The new text reads:

1. [ekuk iuvilu ?] Pk.Kaluvieis inim fratrum muinik est fisiais minnaris pas prs suleis bias nessimas fusent sakrasias Tr Blussiieis medikkiai

Based on alphabetic features, the inscription is to be dated back to the early 3rd century BC. At any event, this document cannot be later than 211 BC, the date of the Roman conquest of Capua. Within the corpus of Iuvilas inscriptions the text is strikingly parallel to the following:

2. Ek(úk) iuvil(ú) Sp. Kalúvieis inim fratrúm múinik(ú) est fíisiais pumperiais pas prai mamerttiais set sakrasias L.Pettieis meddikiai fufens (ST Cp 29)

Both texts display the same initial formula: ek(úk) iuvil(ú) Sp. (or Pk.) Kalúvieis inim fratrúm múinik(ú) est, to be translated “this iuvil(ú) is common to Sp. (or Pk.) Kalúvis and his brothers.” This expression indicates that both monument and ceremonies, recorded by the respective documents, belong to members of the same family, named Kalúvis, with their ‘brotherhood’ (fratrúm: gen.pl.).

<!-- source-page: 354 / pdf-page: 3 -->

Even if, according to the different spelling rules particular to the vowels /i/ and /u/, the latter inscription seems to be slightly later than the former (but still earlier than 211 BC), the striking convergence of the syntactic structures in comparison with other texts of the same class shows that the formulas adopted in these texts were particular to each family. Nevertheless, some variants differentiate the texts. The prominent aspect distinguishing text (1) from text (2) concerns the different use of the inflectional categories of the verb ‘to be’. In the independent clauses closing both texts, text (1) presents no expressed verb in contrast with the 3.pl.pf.ind. fufens of (2); in the relative clause, text (1) displays a 3.pl.fut.ind. fusent contrasting with 3.pl.prs.ind. set in (2),[^2] thus pointing to different types of tense and modality.

Here it is important to stress the outstanding parallelism between the two noun phrases, focusing temporal and circumstantial data by reference to the religious festivals where the ceremonies recorded by each text took place:

| 1 | 2 |
|---|---|
| fisiais minnaris | fíisiais pumperiais |
| pas prs suleis bias nessimas fusent | pas prai mamerttiais set |

## 1. fisiais minnaris

The sequence fisiais minnaris is a close parallel to further noun phrases occurring in the Iuvilas texts, namely fiísiaís púmperiais; fiisiais vesu[lliais] deiv[inais; fiisias vesu[llias] deiv[inas]; fisiais eiduis luisarifs, which all have in common the word fi(i)siā- < *fēs-ia- < *dheh₁s-i̯eh₂-, corresponding to Lat. fēriā- ‘festival’. These expressions match a wider range of calendar data without the word fi(i)siā-, also mentioned in the Iuvilas texts, such as eídúís mamerttiaís, vesulliaís fertalis, vesuliais deivinais, pumperiais sullemnais, pumperias pustmas. However the comparison of phrase pairs such as

- fisiais eiduis luisarifs ~ eídúís mamerttiaís
- fiísiaís púmperiais ~ pumperiais sullemnais
- fiisiais vesu[lliais] deiv[inais ~ vesulliaís deivinais ~ vesulliaís fertalis

proves that the word fi(i)siā- is implied in the formulas on the right, its omission being simply due to shortening. Like fiisiais vesuliais, fiísiaís púmperiais and fisiais eiduis luisarifs (and the other instances where fiisiais is unexpressed), the phrase fisiais minnaris is to be recognized as noun + adjective inflected in the dat./abl.pl.

Undoubtedly minnaris, an adjective syntactically connected with fisiais, is marked by the common Italic dat./abl.pl. i-stem ending, i.e. *-ibhos, whose outcome in the Sabellian languages is -ifs > -iss- > -is (Klingenschmitt 1992: 114). Each step of this development is attested in the Oscan Iuvilas texts, in the forms luisarifs, sakriss, fertalis. Consequently, the ending of minnaris must belong to the final stage of the development, which is consistent with the chronology of the inscription suggested above.

Evidently minnaris is provided with an āli-suffix, like luisarifs and fertalis just mentioned. The productivity of this morpheme for deriving adjectives from both vowel and consonant stems is common to Latin and the Sabellian languages, that converge also in employing this morpheme in specific semantic fields, in particular names of religious festivals, such as Lat. Vinālia, Meditrinālia, Volcanālia (Radke 1988: 367), and terms pertaining to the calendar, e.g. Lat. annuālis, hiemālis, autumnālis.

<!-- source-page: 355 / pdf-page: 4 -->

As is well known, -āri- replaces -āli- after stems containing an -l- (cf. Leumann 1977: 250). This dissimilatory process is well known in Latin (e.g. militāris < *militālis; consulāris < *consulālis), and is attested in Sabellian as well (e.g. U staflarem; O luisarifs). In minnaris, however, no l-sound that could have triggered this dissimilation is present, so that theoretically *minnalis would be expected. Nevertheless, Oscan exhibits further evidence for unexpected -āri- instead of -āli-, e.g. dekkviarim; casnar (< *casnaris). Analogous exceptions to the rule l ~ -ali- > l ~ -ari- occur in Latin as well, e.g. extāris for extālis and coquināris for coquinālis (Wackernagel 1912: 256).

Moreover, Latin -āli- is occasionally found even where -āri- would be expected, e.g. glaciālis and letālis and the festival names Volcanālia and Palatuālia. Semantic connection may give way to analogical remodeling in morphology, as in Lat. mortālis > letālis; Vinālia, Carmentālia, Saturnālia, Consuālia > Volcanālia, Palatuālia. Yet another clue for a synchronic change of -āli- into -āri- is provided by Florālia which, judging from Sab. flusare, shows that -āri- changed to -āli- due to the rhotacism of *flosāris > *florāris. In conclusion, the dissimilatory process of -āli- into -āri- was not rigorously followed, neither in Latin nor in Sabellian.

Analogical remodeling or dialectal variations could have been responsible for, on the one hand, the diffusion of -āri- in the place of -āli- (von Planta 1892–1897: II, 47.) and, on the other hand, the preservation of -āli- where -āri- was required. In terms of attestations, the unexpected occurrences of -āri- appear to be more frequent in Oscan and Umbrian than in Latin. Thus minnaris is consistent with the overwhelming presence of -āri- that gives us the impression that some isolated occurrences of -āli- result from a dissimilation of -āri- in the presence of an /r/ sound: this could be the case of fertalis.

As for the root of minnaris, it may be preliminarily observed that in this text the letter `<i>` can represent /ē/, as clearly shown by the spelling of fisiā- < *fēs-iā- < *dheh₁s-i̯eh₂-. Here, it may be noted that different spellings of the first syllable of the same word are known to occur in the Iuvilas texts, as illustrated by the various spellings of the word just mentioned, *dheh₁s-i̯eh₂- > *fēs-iā-. The variants fisiā-, fiisiā-, fiísiā- can be assigned either to the evolution of the spelling rules of the Oscan writing system (Antonini 1989) or to both synchronic and diachronic variations in the language.[^3] However, what is important here is that within the same text, `<i>` may represent the outcome of an original /ē/, so that minn- can easily be traced back to *mēn-(s) < *meh₁n-(s), the word for ‘moon’ and ‘month’ widespread within the IE languages. Heteroclitic inflection of this stem, differently distributed among the IE languages, gives way to a semantic distinction between ‘moon’ and ‘month’, as in the Germanic languages: e.g. Gothic mena ‘moon’ from *mēn-an-, a nasal stem, and menoþs ‘month’, a consonant stem (Casaretto 2004: 581–582). In contrast, both ‘moon’ and ‘month’ may, in other languages, be expressed by the same word: thus Lith. ménesis (gen. menesio), Old Slav. mĕsęcъ.

Latin preserved this root in mensis ‘month’, but replaced the term for ‘moon’ by lūna < *lou̯ks-nā-. Greek likewise preserved it in μήν (Ionic μείς) ‘month’ and in the ā-stem derivative μήνη ‘moon’; ‘moon’ was, however, partly replaced by σελήνη. Derivatives of μήνη ‘moon’ occur in extended compounds, such as σκοτομήνιος ‘night without moon’ (Hom.), νεομηνίη ‘new moon’ (Herodot.), Νουμήνιος (personal name, meaning ‘born during new moon’). In this respect it deserves attention that in Latin, Mena is attested as the name of a goddess, as testified by Augustine who quotes Varro as his source:

> dea Mena quae menstruis fluoribus praeest…Iovis filia (Aug., Civ. 7,2 = Varr., Ant. Rer. Div. frg.95 Cardauns).

<!-- source-page: 356 / pdf-page: 5 -->

Significantly, the goddess Mena in this passage is connected with ‘female menstruations’ (menstruis fluoribus), which confirms the possible etymology from the root for ‘moon/month’, given that the cycle of menstruation is frequently aligned with the moon cycle as also suggested by Lat. menstruum ‘monthly turn, menstruation’.

However, this secondary function assigned to the goddess Mena contrasts with her role as ‘Iuppiter’s daughter’ (Iovis filia), a close descendant of the most prominent divinity of the pantheon. Thus she shares the same status as another prominent Roman goddess, Fortuna, defined in a votive inscription from Praeneste as the first-born child of Iuppiter (Diovo fileia primogenia).[^4]

Etymologically, Mena has mostly been considered a borrowing from Greek μήνη ‘moon’ (Walde-Hofmann 1982: s.v. Mena; Ernout-Meillet 1985: s.v. mensis). Now we may wonder whether this name can be regarded as a genuine Italic formation, independent of its Greek counterpart. Walde-Hofmann’s argument in favour of a Greek loanword: “gegen Urverwandtschaft […] spricht schon das späte Auftreten”, is clearly untenable: Varro’s source of the quotation by Augustine cannot be considered a late attestation at all. In contrast, Varro is an invaluable mine of information, especially on religious names and structures of both archaic Roman and Sabine communities (see Prosdocimi 1989: 484). The criticism by Christian authors, such as Augustine and Lactantius, who frequently quote Varro’s work on ancient religion (Antiquitates Rerum Divinarum), aims to emphasise ridiculous aspects of the pagan theonyms.

Thus Mena is featured in a derogatory sense by Augustine as a goddess of female menstruations, alongside her status of ‘Iuppiter’s daughter’. Summing up, Latin appears to have preserved the inherited root for ‘moon’ in the name of the goddess Mena, morphologically distinct from the word for ‘month’ (mēnsis). Thus Mena, paralleled by Greek μήνη, is likely to represent the earliest Latin word for ‘moon’ embodied as a goddess. Lunar cults and the deified moon are quite common in ancient religions including the Roman one: a temple dedicated to Luna noctiluca, located on the Palatine Hill according to Varro, and prior to another one located on the Aventine hill according to Tacitus (Lunais 1979: 102–105), was kept illuminated during the night (Wissowa 1912: 316). According to Ovid, festivals in honour of Luna Noctiluca were celebrated in the late month of March in the Aventine temple.[^5] Among the Greeks, moon festivals are reflected in lexical compounds, such as Νουμηνία ‘festival for the new moon’, Νουμηνιασταί ‘an association for new moon festivals’ and Νουμήνιος, used as both a personal name and an epithet of Apollo whose worship was connected with the new moon (Nilsson 1955: I, 644).

In contrast to what happened in Greek, the use of mena as a theonym could have favoured its complete ousting as the common word for ‘moon’ in favour of lūna < *lou̯ks-nā- ‘bright, shining’, from the earliest times. OLat. losna, engraved in a mirror from Praeneste from the 4th–3rd century BC (CIL I² 549. Leumann 1977: 33; 66), represents an intermediate stage of the development of *lou̯ks-nā- to lūna. Moreover, the theonym Mena is also significant as an indication that the feminine gender of the common noun lūna originated as a feminine adjectival attribute.

We do not know whether a similar replacement of the inherited root for ‘moon’ took place in the Sabellian languages as well. However, the Oscan attestation of minnaris now brings an important contribution to solving this problem. So far, the IE root is attested in Sabellian, as in Latin, in the word for ‘month’ in U. menzne and Sab. mesene. A compound antemenzaru (< *anter-menso-/ā- instead of the expected *anter-mens-i̯o-/ā-) occurs in Umbrian with the meaning ‘Monatsabschnitt vor dem letzten/ersten Tag also die Kalenden’ (Untermann 2000: 110). This compound parallels Latin compounds like interlunium, intermenstruum. Nevertheless, the designation of the ‘moon’ properly speaking is still unknown in the Sabellian languages.

Unlike Lat mensis, both U. menzne and Sab. mesene ‘month’ present a nasal enlargement, that is *meh₁ns-n-. Basically, to explain O. minnaris, two morphological paths starting from *meh₁n-(s)- can be taken into consideration:

1. a derivative from a consonant or i-stem based on *meh₁ns-n- with n-enlargement, implied by U. menzne and Sab. mesene, rather than on *meh₁ns- that gave way to Lat. mensis
2. a derivative from *minnā-, an ā-stem arising from either *meh₁n- or *meh₁ns-(n)-

In the case of (a), the terms for ‘moon’ and ‘month’ would not be fundamentally different. Their morphological overlap would result from merging the consonant inflection with that of the i-stems in Sabellian. In the case of (b), both variants of the stem, i.e. *meh₁n- and *meh₁ns-(n)-, could account for a secondary ā-stem *minnā- from mēnnā- that, in turn, can be traced back to either *meh₁n-eh₂- > mēnā- or *meh₁n-s-(n)-eh₂- > *mēn-s-(n)-ā- > mēnnā-. In the latter case, the geminate consonant results from an assimilation of the consonant cluster -n-s(-n-). However, there are no parallels to such a development in Oscan, where the clusters -n-s- and -s-n- are generally preserved, as in (for -n-s-) censaum, Fensernu, patensíns, and (for -s-n-) fiísnam, kersnasias, casnar (von Planta 1892–1897: I, 480, 498). Similarly, the cluster -n-s-n- is preserved in U. menzne and in Sab. mesene. These facts urge us to exclude *meh₁n-s-eh₂- as the origin of mēnnā- > *minnā-, since the cluster -ns- would probably be preserved. Consequently, the best solution for Oscan *minnā-, implied by minnaris, remains a proto-form *meh₁n-eh₂- > mēnā-, perfectly matching Greek μήνη ‘moon’ and Lat. Mena ‘moon goddess’.

The geminate consonant in Oscan can easily be explained by the so-called littera rule (Meiser 1998: 76) that produces both diachronic and synchronic alternatives between long vowel + consonant and short vowel + geminate consonant, well known from Latin words such as lītera ~ lĭttera, bāca ~ băcca, cūpa ~ cŭppa (Giannini & Marotta 1989). If so, *minnā- would represent /mĭnna-/ replacing an original *mīnā- as the regular outcome of mēnā- < *meh₁n-eh₂-. This explanation is consistent with the spelling `<i>` that points to a short vowel, differently from the spelling `<ii>` and `<ií>` used for the long vowel. In conclusion, Oscan minnaris is likely to be derived from *mĭnna-, a synchronic variant of *mīnā- arisen from mēnā- < *meh₁n-eh₂-. Admittedly, no evidence for the littera rule in either the diachronic or synchronic sense is provided by Oscan epigraphy, but it is worth noticing that several cases of unexplained geminate consonants are found in Oscan, e.g. Appelluneis < Ἀπέλλων; dekkviarim < *dekviā- (U. tekviā-).

If Oscan minna- indeed derives from mēnā- < *meh₁n-eh₂-, it follows that Oscan, Latin and Greek share the same word formation based on the IE root for ‘moon, month’, inflected as an ā-stem. Now the question of the meaning of the Oscan word remains. Considering the semantically possible alternatives, ‘moon’ and ‘month’, the derivative minnaris in this case might be interpreted as either ‘related to the moon, lunar’ or ‘monthly’. However, both specific and general facts favour the meaning ‘moon’. A specific reason is provided by the textual context of the noun phrase fisiais minnaris. As said above, fisiais minnaris in the Iuvilas texts is paralleled by similar expressions indicating precise calendar dates, such as fiísiaís púmperiais, fiisiais vesu[lliais] deiv[inais; fiisias vesu[llias] deiv[inas]; fisiais eiduis luisarifs.[^6] The attributes of fiisiais normally function as proper names for each festival. Consequently, for minnaris, a quite generic meaning, such as ‘monthly’, is unlikely, in so far as an expression ‘monthly festivals’ provides no precise calendar indication as opposed to similar noun phrases in parallel texts.

<!-- source-page: 358 / pdf-page: 7 -->

A more general fact favouring the meaning ‘lunar festival’ rather than ‘monthly festival’ is the word order noun + adjective in the phrase fisiais minnaris, where the adjective placed after the noun points to its use as a proper name resulting from the distinctive function of this syntactic position (Adams 1976: 78; Berrettoni 1967: 80). Finally, the meaning ‘moon’ for O. *minnā- < mēnā- < *meh₁n-eh₂- is supported by the fact that, in the closely related Umbrian and Sabine, the word for ‘month’ is morphologically differentiated. As already noted, a morphological distinction of the terms for ‘month’ and ‘moon’ from the same root is quite common in the IE languages. No wonder if it is found in the Italic branch as well.

## 2. prs suleis bias

This noun phrase is embedded in the relative clause pas prs suleis bias nessimas fusent relating to the preceding fisiais minnaris. The relative clause serves to narrow down the potential reference of the related phrase fisiais minnaris, focusing on a more temporal circumstance parallel to fíisiais pumperiais pas prai mamerttiais set in the Iuvila text (2) that, except for the verbal mood, matches fisiais minnaris pas prs suleis bias nessimas fusent (1). Both relative clauses specify calendar details designating when the respective ceremonies took (or should have taken) place. The informational core of the more restricted situation is the noun phrase prs suleis bias, to be analyzed as a sequence prep. + noun_gen + noun_acc(?).

The preposition prs is, so far, unknown in Oscan, but it is easily understood as a variant of pr < prŏ/prō provided with an s-enlargement, like O. az < ad-s beside U. ař < ad and Lat. ad (Untermann 2000: 46; 141). In the same way, per-s < pro-s could account for U. per because /r/ is never lost in this form.[^7] If so, prs could be the Oscan counterpart of U. pro-s > per, except with regard to the case construction. The s-enlargement of particles is, especially when they are used as prepositions, quite common in many IE languages, even in synchronic use like Latin ab ~ abs, sub ~ su(b)s, ē ~ ec ~ ex, Greek ἐκ ~ ἐξ, ἀμφί ~ ἀμφίς, OI áva ~ aváḥ.

Like U. per, O. pr(u) usually occurs in constructions with the ablative (Untermann 2000: 582), which is excluded in the phrase prs suleis bias. However, prs suleis bias is closely connected to the predicate nessimas fusent, so that nessimas prs forms a prepositional unit like E. next to. The Latin adjective proximus, semantically corresponding to O. and U. nessimo-, is frequently accompanied by prepositions in temporal or local sense, as well, e.g. proximus ab, proximus ante (Kühner & Stegmann 1997: II,1, 316; 528).

The syntactic connection with nessimas might have modified the normal construction of the preposition prs. Actually, nessimo- occurs in the Sabellian languages in either a local or temporal meaning with different case constructions: with a dative in Oscan and with an ablative in Umbrian. More precisely, in Umbrian the isolated locative nesimei has the function of a preposition accompanied by an ablative (Untermann 2000: 493).

In the new text nessimo- is combined with a prepositional noun phrase (prs suleis bias nessimas) that, in the temporal sense, is paralleled by the use of proximus ante in Latin, cf. e.g.:

> Sed nocte ea, quae proxuma fuit ante diem conloquio decretum (Sall., Iug. 113, 3)  
> “But on the night preceding the day appointed for the conference”

<!-- source-page: 359 / pdf-page: 8 -->

> Tabellarius ille quem tibi dixeram me ad Brutum esse missurum in Anagninum ad me venit ea nocte quae proxima ante Kalendas fuit litterasque ad me attulit (Cic., ad Att. XV 26, 1)  
> “the courier, whom I told you I should send to Brutus, joined me at Anagnia on the night of the 30th and brought me a letter”

In these passages, proximus ante occurs in the meaning ‘immediately prior to or just preceding’ a precise date established by an agreement (diem colloquio decretum) or a day fixed by the calendar (Kalendas). A similar meaning may reasonably be assumed for the Oscan phrase prs suleis bias nessimas ‘(these) immediately prior to suleis bias’.

As for suleis bias, both terms may be formally compared with other words already known in Oscan. The head of the noun phrase, bias, corresponds perfectly to biass, an accusative plural of an ā-stem occurring in an Oscan curse text where the meaning ‘force, power’ is required by the context.[^8] Basically, bias could be either a genitive singular or an accusative plural that may occur indiscriminately with or without geminate spelling of the final -s as the result of an assimilation process -āns > -ass > -as; the intermediate stage is attested in the form víass from Pompei.[^9] In our case, the spelling of simple -s in the acc.pl. bias(s) is consistent with that of the dat./abl.pl. minnaris (see above). The Oscan word raises the etymological problem of whether we are dealing with an inherited lexical item to be directly compared with Gr. βία and OI. jiyá- or with a borrowing from Greek βία (Untermann 2000: 146), but that question does not matter here.

As for the determiner of the noun phrase, that is the genitive suleis, a relationship with the Sabellian indefinite sollo- < *sol(h₂)no- or *solh₂-u̯o-[^10] ‘whole, all’ (cf. Lat. sollemnis, etc.) is to be excluded for the following reasons: a) the spelling without geminate consonant within a text where geminate consonants are noted (e.g. minnaris); and b) no acceptable sense would result from sollo- (< *sol(h₂)no- or *solh₂-u̯o-) ‘whole’ defining bias without any reference within the text.

Consequently, another pathway must be followed for explaining suleis in this context. Since the Oscan spelling `<u>` may correspond either to /ō/ or to /ū/ (cf. e.g. O. dunúm < dōnŏm; fruktatiuf < *frūktā- < *bhrūg-tā-; von Planta I: 129), suleis is to be traced back either to sōl- or to sūl-. Thus the Oscan form is very close to Latin sōl ‘sun’, with respect to the morphological structure and heteroclitic inflection of the IE word. Thus suleis is of relevance to both the Sabellic term for ‘sun’, so far unknown, and the relationship with the Latin word. It is well known that the /ō/ of Lat. sōl is unexpected from PIE *seh₂ul-/*sh₂ul-, as summarized by de Vaan 2008: 570:[^11]

> A preform *seh₂-ul > *saul would normally be preserved as *saul and *sh₂-ul would yield **sūl. Kortlandt apud Beekes 1984 suggests that *saul would have given sōl by sporadic development, conditioned by -l-.

We may therefore wonder to what extent the Oscan evidence may contribute to solving this problem. As for the Sabellian languages, the term for ‘sun’ has been a bone of contention for quite a long time, mostly based on the data provided by ancient glosses. Two main views have been put forward.

On the one hand, Varro’s quote sol[a] uel quod ita Sabini conveyed the idea that languages closely related with the Sabine ones, i.e. Oscan and Umbrian, would share the inherited noun, whose outcome would be very close to the Latin word. Moreover Varro’s passage, regarded as corrupted owing to haplology, was restored sol <uel a s>auel quod ita Sabini (Varr., L.L. V 68), with the aim to recognize a reconstructed preform sauel common to Latin and Sabellian (Von Blumenthal 1935: 121).

<!-- source-page: 360 / pdf-page: 9 -->

On the other hand, the following Festus gloss has been invoked in favour of the idea that the Sabine (or more generally Sabellian) word for ‘sun’ was something like *auselo-:

> Aureliam familiam ex Sabinis oriundam a Sole dictam putant, quod ei publice a populo Romano datus sit locus, in quo sacra faceret Soli, qui ex hoc Auseli dicebantur
>
> “the gens Aurelia coming from the Sabine region is said to have derived his name from the word for ‘sun’. Accordingly Romans assigned to this gens a public place appointed for worshipping the ‘sun’. That is why their members were named Auseli”

This view was more recently adopted by Rix (1998: 221) who assumed that Usil, an Etruscan designation of the ‘sun’ as confirmed by images on Etruscan mirrors, was a borrowing from the Sabine language. In other words, according to Rix’ view, the Festus gloss and the Etruscan evidence for Usil (reflected by its use as a theonym) would be concordant evidence for a Sabine word *auselo- meaning ‘sun’. However, the weakness of Rix’ arguments is proven by two facts:

1. the Festus gloss uniquely merely claims that the Roman family Aurelii originated from Sabine land and was linked with a worship of the ‘sun’. Therefore the name of the Aurelii, deriving from *auselo-, was assumed as a designation of the ‘sun’ in their homeland. In fact, this statement does not necessarily imply that the name of this family derives from the actual Sabine word for ‘sun’; and
2. as recently shown by de Simone 2010,[^12] Etruscan Usil can be explained consistently with Etruscan morphology, in that it parallels other typically Etruscan lexical items, such as avil ‘year’ and acil ‘work’. Moreover, an Etruscan personal name Usel(e) apparently matches the Sabine name Auselo- as the origin of the Aurelii family name.

The facts that (1) the personal name Usel(e) occurs in the earliest Etruscan inscriptions and that (2) its documentation is concentrated in an area close to the Sabine territory concurrently point to a possible connection between the Etruscan personal name Usel(e) and its Sabine counterpart Auselo- from which the Aurelii name arises. In other words, Etruscan Usel(e) and Sab. Auselo- > Lat. Aurelius are likely to originate from the same name: it is more reasonable to recognize Usel(e) as a rendering of Sab. Auselo- than the other way around. The formal similarity between the personal name Usel(e) and the theonym Usil (possibly used as common term for ‘sun’, like the deified Sōl in Latin and Ἥλιος in Greek) is no more striking than the one between the Etruscan personal name Av(e)le > Lat. Aulus and the lexical item avil ‘year’. In this perspective, the connection of the Aurelii family, featured by sun worship, and the Etruscan theonym Usil, characterised as the ‘sun’, is to be inscribed in the framework of Etruscan-Sabine contacts. Such a context would also be responsible for the antique tradition of sun worship by the Roman family of the originally Sabine Aurelii, as recorded by the Festus gloss.

Summing up, there is no justification for assuming in the Sabine (and more generally Sabellian) languages a term for the ‘sun’ different from the inherited one. The Festus gloss is no evidence for *auselo- as a Sabine designation of the ‘sun’. At any event, Festus’ text is not provided with an argumentational strength more compelling than that of Varro’s gloss that explicitly points out that Romans and Sabines used the same word for ‘sun’.

The reliability of Varro’s gloss is now supported by O. suleis, showing that the Sabellian outcome of the IE word is very close to the Latin one. Particularly with respect to morphology, the Sabellian languages agree with Latin in not displaying reflexes of the heteroclitic paradigm that has been reconstructed through comparison with other languages, i.e. nom.sg. *seh₂u̯ōl ~ gen.sg. *sh₂u̯ens-.

As for the root vowel, O. sul(eis) may represent either sōl- or sūl-. If sūl- should be the case, the Oscan word, then to be separated from Latin sōl-, would be a perfect outcome of a preform *sh₂ul-. Consequently, /ō/ instead of /ū/ would appear to be a morphonological change peculiar to Latin.[^13] Another path that might account for both Latin and Oscan words would be an evolution of a preform *sh₂u̯ōl > *s(h₂)u̯ōl > *su̯ōl owing to the ‘Saussure effect’.[^14]

On the other hand, if the Oscan spelling `<u>` would stand for /ō/, the result would be a complete identity between Oscan and Latin forms, likely to hark back to a Proto-Italic stage rather than to later reciprocal influence.

A final question concerns the meaning of the noun phrase suleis bias in the context. Basically, the literal meaning of this expression, that is ‘sun strength’, admits three different senses: (a) with respect to the daylight, ‘the strongest force of the sun’ is to be situated around midday; (b) with reference to the year’s seasons, ‘the strongest force of the sun’ could be a designation of summertime; and (c) as a more precise calendar point, this expression is to be understood as a designation of ‘summer solstice’.

The fact that, in other Iuvilas texts, each ceremony is defined with reference to a specific calendar date decisively argues for solution (c), that is ‘summer solstice’. In particular, the close similarity to the relative clause fíisiais pumperiais pas prai mamerttiais set ‘during the pumperias festivals, which take place before these of March’ suggests the sense ‘during the moon festivals, which should/would take place before the summer solstice’ for fisiais minnaris pas prs suleis bias nessimas fusent.

## 3. Conclusion

The Oscan noun phrases fisiais minnaris and suleis biass in a new Iuvilas inscription point out new lexical items referring to the terms of ‘moon’ and ‘sun’, respectively.

Minnaris is a derivative of minna-, a variant of mēnā- < *meh₁n-eh₂-, proving that Latin and the Sabellian languages had in common an eh₂-stem *meh₁n-eh₂- as a probable designation of the ‘moon’ parallel to Greek μήνη.

The form suleis, the inherited word for ‘sun’, shows a morphological convergence between an Oscan and a Latin word, to be traced back to a Proto-Italic preform. It follows that the unexpected root vowel of the Latin word is to be explained as one of the variations of this language.

The meaning of these words is consistent with the general sense of the relative clause fisiais minnaris pas prs suleis bias nessimas fusent, i.e. ‘during the lunar festivals, which should/would take place before the summer solstice’, required by comparison with parallel texts, namely the indication of calendar dates connected to religious festivals.
