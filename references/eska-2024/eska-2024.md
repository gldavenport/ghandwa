# Celtic in Greek characters and implications for Greek and Celtic phonology

Joseph F. Eska  
Virginia Polytechnic Institute & State University; eska@vt.edu

https://doi.org/10.1515/if-2024-0009

## Abstract

This paper examines the use of the characters tʰeta ⟨θ⟩ and kʰi ⟨χ⟩ in Transalpine Celtic inscriptions engraved in Greek characters. Since /tʰ/ and /kʰ/, respectively, were not needed for Celtic, it is conventionally presumed that these characters were repurposed in these inscriptions, tʰeta ⟨θ⟩ as the tau Gallicum phoneme and kʰi ⟨χ⟩ as the allophone [x] of the phoneme /k/ before /t/. It is argued that, in fact, the aspirated voiceless plosives of ancient Greek had become fricatives in southern Transalpine Gaul before the Celts of the region started employing Greek characters to engrave inscriptions in their language, and thus that tʰeta ⟨θ⟩ and kʰi ⟨χ⟩ in Transalpine Celtic inscriptions were employed with their proximate phonemic values in Greek. We thus can determine a proximate terminus ante quem for the frication of the ancient Greek aspirated voiceless plosives in southern Transalpine Gaul, which leads to the prospect for Transalpine Celtic having contrasted its plosive series not via the Laryngeal feature [voice], but via [spread glottis].

**Keywords:** Ancient Greek aspirated voiceless plosives, frication, allophony, tau Gallicum, quasi-phonetic orthography, slit-t fricative, laryngeal features

## 1 Prelude

### 1.1 Proto-Celtic

For proto-Celtic, we can confidently reconstruct a series of voiced plosives */b d ɡ ɡʷ/ < proto-IE */b d ɡʲ ɡ ɡʷ/ and */bʱ dʱ ɡʲʱ ɡʱ ɡʷʱ/. Thus, for example, nasal + voiced plosive groups assimilated to long nasals in intramorphemic position in the prehistory of Cisalpine Celtic, but not elsewhere in the prehistoric Celtic speech continuum, cf. (1).

(1)

a. */nd/ > /nː/

   i. alKouinos (LexLep TI‧41) < *u̯indo- ‘white’

b. *[ŋɡ] > [ŋː]

   i. loKan (LexLep PG‧1.2) < *longā- ‘vessel’

There was also a series of voiceless plosives in proto-Celtic, but we cannot be certain whether they were unaspirated */p t k kʷ/ or aspirated */pʰ tʰ kʰ kʷʰ/. In my view, they may well have been aspirated because the ultimate loss of proto-IE */p/ in initial and intervocalic position in most of attested Celtic implies that proto-IE */p/ > */ɸ/ > */h/ > ∅. However, plosives normally—though perhaps not always—become aspirated before they are fricated. This is because the duration of the occlusion + aspiration interval of aspirated plosives cross-linguistically is approximately equal to the occlusion of unaspirated plosives and the shorter occlusion may become incomplete, leading to frication (Kingston 1990: 429).

### 1.2 Cisalpine Celtic

The earliest attested Celtic that we have is Cisalpine Celtic in northern Italy and adjacent Switzerland, which is attested starting in the second half of the seventh century bce.[^1] The earliest epigraphic layer of these inscriptions, extending approximately to 550 bce, though small, is unequivocal in revealing that the plosive opposition had become /pʰ tʰ kʰ/ vs. /p t k/ in this variety of Celtic,[^2] i.e., the proto-Celtic voiced plosives had become devoiced (Eska forthcoming).

### 1.3 Celtiberian

Such is also the case in Celtiberian, which is attested later, starting in the late third century bce, but mostly after 125 bce. The indigenous script employed by the Celtiberians, which was adapted from the northeastern Iberian script, likewise indicates that the plosive opposition was /pʰ tʰ kʰ kʷʰ/ vs. /p t k kʷ/ (Eska 2023).

### 1.4 Insular Celtic

All of the Insular Celtic languages save for Breton also contrast their plosive series via the Laryngeal feature [spread glottis] rather than [voice]; cf. Jones 1984: 41 and Bell et al. 2023: 491 for Welsh; Ní Chasaide 1999: 113 and Welby, Ní Chiosáin & Ó Raghallaigh 2017: 139 for Irish; Ladefoged et al. 1998: 4–12 and Nance & Ó Maolalaigh 2021: 262–265 for Scottish Gaelic; Sommerfelt 1978: 48–56 for Breton. Breton probably originally did so as well, but adopted a voicing contrast after prolonged contact with Late Latin and French, which distinguish their plosive series via [voice]. Cf. the contrast evinced in Scottish Gaelic (Table 1) and Welsh (Table 2).

## 2 Celtic in Greek characters: Preliminaries

### 2.1 Goal of this paper

The goal of this paper is twofold:

1. to determine the sound values of the characters tʰeta ⟨θ⟩ and kʰi ⟨χ⟩, which have conventionally been presumed to be repurposed in Celtic inscriptions because the language did not possess /tʰ/ and /kʰ/ in its phonemic inventory; so, e.gg., Lejeune 1983: 747; RIGG 1–281: 444f.; Lambert 1992: 291; 1997: 39; Adiego 2020: 1058.
2. to investigate whether the Transalpine Celtic engraved in Greek characters likewise contrasted its plosive series via the feature [spread glottis], or did so via [voice].

### 2.2 The Greek aspirated voiceless plosives

The ancient Greek language possessed a three-way contrast in its plosive series, distinguishing unaspirated voiceless vs. aspirated voiceless vs. voiced plosives, represented in the Greek alphabet as seen in Table 3.

**Table 3: The plosives of Ancient Greek**

| lab | cor | dors |
|---|---|---|
| π = /p/ | τ = /t/ | κ = /k/ |
| φ = /pʰ/ | θ = /tʰ/ | χ = /kʰ/ |
| β = /b/ | δ = /d/ | γ = /ɡ/ |

One does not find the use of the characters for the aspirated plosives, viz., pʰi ⟨φ⟩,[^4] tʰeta ⟨θ⟩, and kʰi ⟨χ⟩, in the Celtic inscriptions engraved in Greek characters—save in very specialised uses, on which more below—and characters for the voiced plosives, viz., beta ⟨β⟩, delta ⟨δ⟩, and gamma ⟨γ⟩, are common, so one can understand why the standard presumption is that the binary contrast in the plosive series of Transalpine Celtic was distinguished via [voice], e.g., Lambert 2003: 45.

In this paper, I propose that the situation is not so straightforward. This hinges upon the fact that the voiceless aspirated plosives /pʰ tʰ kʰ/ became fricatives /ɸ θ x/ in the history of all Greek dialects, though at various times, as early as the fifth century bce in the Laconian dialect, in others only in the first or second century ce or, according to some scholars, considerably later; see, e.gg., Sturtevant 1940: 83f.; Schwyzer 1977: 204–207; Threatte 1980: 469f.; Browning 1983: 26f.; Allen 1987: 23–26; Horrocks 2010: 170f. for commentary.

The corpus of Greek inscriptions in France collected by Decourt (IGF) provides some tentative evidence that the aspirated plosives were fricated in the Greek spoken in southern Transalpine Gaul by the third century ce in the form of Roman names in which Lat. /f/ is transcribed by ⟨φ⟩, cf. (2).[^5]

(2)

a. ΡΟΥΦΙΝΑ ‘Rufina’ IGF 144  
b. ΡΟΥΦΟC ‘Rufus’ IGF 164  
c. ΦΟ|ΥΛΒΙΑ ‘Fulvia’ IGF 31  
d. ΦΟΥΛΒΙΟC ‘Fulvius’ IGF 31

But I will argue that there is evidence in the use of the characters tʰeta ⟨θ⟩ and kʰi ⟨χ⟩ to engrave inscriptions in Transalpine Celtic that bears not only upon the phonology of that language, but also upon the phonology of the local varieties of Greek spoken in southern Transalpine Gaul and provides for an earlier dating of the frication of the aspirated voiceless plosives.

### 2.3 The date of the start of the Transalpine Celtic epigraphic corpus engraved in Greek characters

Lejeune (1983: 737; 1985: 1), followed by Schmidt (1988: 218) and Lambert (2003: 83; 2022: 34), states that the epigraphic tradition of engraving Transalpine Celtic in Greek characters commenced at the end of the third century bce. Bats (1988: 129–133, 139–146; 2004; 2011: 212–226), followed by Mullen (2013: 98–110) and Dupraz (2022: 25), proposes that it did not begin until the last quarter of the second century bce, earlier-dated inscriptions containing Celtic names having been written in Greek.

Particularly should Bats be correct, it may be reasonable to posit that /pʰ tʰ kʰ/ became /ɸ θ x/ in the Greek of southern Gaul before the Celts of the region adapted the Greek alphabet to engrave inscriptions in their own language. This would allow us to posit that the plosive series in Transalpine Celtic may have been contrasted via [spread glottis] such that ⟨π τ κ⟩ = /pʰ tʰ kʰ/ vs. ⟨β δ γ⟩ = /p t k/.

## 3 Celtic in Greek characters: The importance of ⟨θ⟩ and ⟨χ⟩

### 3.1 Kʰi ⟨χ⟩ in Transalpine Celtic epigraphy

The character kʰi ⟨χ⟩ only appears before tau ⟨τ⟩ in the attested corpus of Celtic inscriptions engraved in Greek characters. We know, on the basis of comparative evidence across the Celtic languages, that early Celtic /k/ possessed an allophone [x] before /s t/. The sequence /ks/ is represented with the character ksi ⟨ξ⟩, cf. (3).

(3)

a. ΚΡΙΞΙΑ RIGG 1–281 G–5 = RIIG BDR-04-01  
b. ΜΕΔΟΥΡΕΙΞ RIGG 1–281 G–71 = RIIG BDR-12-09

This orthographic usage does not reveal the frication of /k/ > [x] before /s/, but a coin legend in Roman characters may do so, cf. (4).

(4) VERCINGETORIXS RIG M–302

This is not certain, however, as the group /ks/ is not uncommonly spelt ⟨xs⟩ in Roman epigraphy,[^6] cf. (5).

(5) MAXSIMVS CIL xii 4128

Certain tokens of the character kʰi ⟨χ⟩ occurring before tau ⟨τ⟩, in fact, presently number only two, cf. (6).[^7]

(6)

b. ΟΥϵ|ΧΤΙΝΙ|ΟC RIGG 501–523 G–501 = RIIG BDR-16-01

But certain tokens of kappa ⟨κ⟩ before tau ⟨τ⟩ presently number five, cf. (7).

(7)

a. ΑΝϵΚΤΑ RIGG 1–281 G–124 = RIIG VAU-05-08[^8]  
b. ΑΝϵ|ΚΤΙΑ RIIG VAU-16-01  
c. ΑΚΤΟΣ RIGG 1–281 G–108 = RIIG BDR-14-01  
d. [R]ΕΚΤΟΥ[ RIGG 556 G–556 = RIIG VAU-05-32[^9]  
e. [Τ̣]Ε̣ΚΤΙ RIIG VAU-05-30; gen.sg[^10]

One wonders, then, since the orthographic sequence ⟨κτ⟩ was considered sufficient to render [xt], why the orthographic sequence ⟨χτ⟩ occurs on occasion. The answer that suggests itself is that Greek kʰi /kʰ/ had already fricated to /x/ and was identical to the allophone [x] that occurred before /s t/ in Transalpine Celtic. The tokens of kʰi ⟨χ⟩ before tau ⟨τ⟩ thus represent sporadic instances of quasi-phonetic orthography.

### 4.1 The articulation of the tau Gallicum

The majority of scholars who have commented on the tau Gallicum assume by a large margin that it is a voiceless coronal affricate /ʦ/, e.gg., Watkins 1955: 15; Whatmough 1963: 43f. et passim; RIGG 1–281: 444f.; GLG: 71; Koch 1992: 488; Schrijver 1995: 402; McCone 1996: 48; Lambert 2003: 45f.; see Eska 1998: 116–120 for a more extensive description of the previous literature.

I find such a conclusion difficult to accept because, bearing in mind the present focus upon the Celtic inscriptions engraved in Greek characters, it should have been straightforward to write /ʦ/ as ⟨ΤΣ/ΤC⟩. While the Greek of southern Transalpine Gaul probably did not possess an affricate /ʦ/ in its phonemic inventory, Modern Greek does, and it is no surprise to find that it is spelt with the digraph ⟨τσ⟩, cf. (14).

(14)

a. τσάμι ‘pine tree’  
b. τσίνουρο ‘eyelash’  
c. τσουράπι ‘woolen sock’

In Eska 1998: 122–124, I propose that the tau Gallicum was articulated much like the southern Hiberno-English alveolar slit-t fricative, which Pandeli et al. (1997) argue should be represented as /θ͇/ in the International Phonetic Alphabet. θ as the base symbol indicates that the slit-t fricative is a voiceless coronal fricative with a flat or slit tongue shape, as opposed to /s/, which requires a grooved tongue shape that instantiates the feature [strident]. The diacritic ͇, borrowed from the Extended International Phonetic Alphabet for disordered speech, then specifies that the sound is alveolar in place.

Earlier work on the slit-t fricative had proposed numerous ways to symbolise the sound. Those known to me are listed in (15).

(15)

a. ɾ̥ e.gg., Harris 1984: 130; Lass 1987: 267  
b. s. Harris 1990: 285  
c. s/ Bertz 1987: 45  
d. s/̥ Bertz 1987: 45  
e. s Bertz 1987: 45  
f. sˊ Pilch 1959: 42  
g. t˗ — e.gg., Hogan 1934: 18; Henry 1958: 123  
h. t/ — Conrick 1981: 73  
i. t̝ — Ó Baoill 1990: 161  
j. t̜ — e.gg., Wells 1982: 429; Kallen 1994: 178[^19]  
k. ṱ — Hickey 1984b: 675f. 1984: 234f.; 1993: 221f.; 1995: 123f.  
l. ts — e.g., Henry 1958: 123; Barry 1981: 68  
m. t˗s — Henry 1958: 123  
n. θ̱ — Laver 1994: 260

While some of these, viz., [t̜] = IPA [t̞] and particularly [θ̱], approximately represent the articulation of the slit-t fricative, and others are, by and large, ad hoc at best, the range of representations calls attention to the great difficulty in representing the sound in written form.

I submit that their range of representation is not dissimilar to that which we find for the tau Gallicum in Transalpine Celtic inscriptions engraved in Greek characters, cf. (16).[^20]

(16) CC ΣΣ Τ ΤΤ Θ ΘΘ CΘ

In view of the likelihood that kʰi ⟨χ⟩ was sometimes, but not always, employed to represent the allophone [x] of Transalpine Celtic /k/ before /t/ because it represented the same sound in the Greek of southern Transalpine Gaul, it makes sense to think that tʰeta ⟨θ⟩ was not simply repurposed to represent the tau Gallicum, but that it bore phonological features that were close to it. Under the presumption that the tau Gallicum was similar or identical to the southern Hiberno-Irish slit-t fricative, which is most accurately transcribed as /θ͇/, the use of tʰeta ⟨θ⟩ to write the tau Gallicum is as good a fit as possible if it represented /θ/ in the Greek of southern Transalpine Gaul.

## 5 Conclusions

We may draw the following conclusions from the foregoing discussion:

1. The characters tʰeta ⟨θ⟩ and kʰi ⟨χ⟩ were not repurposed with new values when employed to engrave inscriptions in Transalpine Celtic; kʰi ⟨χ⟩ was employed to write the allophone [x] of /k/ because it was articulated the same in both languages; tʰeta ⟨θ⟩ = /θ/ was employed because it coded all of the presumed features of the tau Gallicum phoneme save for alveolar place.
2. This does not tell us when the Transalpine Celts first adapted the Greek alphabet to engrave inscriptions in their language, but it does tell us that the aspirated voiceless plosives /pʰ tʰ kʰ/ of the Greek of southern Gaul had become the fricatives /ɸ θ x/ before they did so. Thus, the terminus ante quem for this change may be said to be ca. mid-second century bce under Bats’ dating for the start of the Transalpine Celtic epigraphic tradition in Greek characters, ca. mid-third century bce under Lejeune’s dating.
3. The prospect that the plosive series in Transalpine Celtic were contrasted via [spread glottis] such that ⟨π τ κ⟩ = /pʰ tʰ kʰ/ vs. ⟨β δ γ⟩ = /p t k/ is left open as a possibility, but not proved.
