This work aims to clarify a number of hitherto insufficiently explained issues related to two Cisalpine Celtic funerary inscriptions on stone, respectively written in Lepontic (Vergiate, Transpadana) and Cisalpine Gaulish (Todi, Umbria). They have one interesting trait in common: the funerary rituals are seemingly divided into two different parts, possibly alluding to two different stages of the obsequies. The etymologies of the four direct objects mentioned will prove instrumental to the understanding of funerary practice in Celtic Italy. Some phonetic and morphological aspects of the studied forms will also illuminate some aspects of the dialectal relatedness of Lepontic and Gaulish.

##

## 1 Introduction: the Cisalpine Gaulish inscription of Todi

The funerary inscription of Todi (the ancient Tuder, Perugia, Umbria), first edited in CIL XI 4687, is carved on a paralleliped of Travertine marble and dates back to the 2nd–1st c. BCE. It is opisthograph, that is to say, covered by text on both sides. We are apparently dealing with two closely similar bilinguals. In both of them, the Latin text is written in the Latin alphabet and precedes the Celtic text, which is written in the “Lugano script.” The reasons for the presence of Gauls in Umbrian territory and at such a late date is unknown, but, as Lejeune pointed out as early as 1971 (389–90), they were newcomers to the area, and did not belong to long-established families eager to show off their Celtic ancestry. The fact that the Latin version precedes the Gaulish one is indicative of Latin being the language of prestige and power (see Adams 2003:188), though, given the probably shallow presence of Celtic in this region, it is not possible to ascertain whether the commissioner of the slab had become conscious of this in Umbria or in his original northern homeland. As noted by

Langslow (2002:30) and, in his wake, by Poccetti (2015:389), the Latin text has been translated from Gaulish, not the other way round, and Gaulish is likely to have been the commissioner’s native language (in fact, none of the persons involved was a Roman citizen). As observed by Poccetti (2015:398–9), this explains the use of information never found in Latin funerary epigraphy, such as the specification of the age of the dedicant (FRATER EIVS MINIMVS) with respect to his deceased brother, rendered as ateknati/ATEGNATI in the Gaulish version, possibly meaning ‘firstborn’, and then coterminous with CINTVGNATVS elsewhere in Gaul. In addition, the Latin text is longer, and both Gaulish and Latin are written by the same hand, but two different scribes are respectively responsible for Face A (1, 2) and Face B (3, 4). Though the text is partly mangled, the erased parts only affect forms that are easily recoverable from the context. This is the reading, according to RIG-II/1 E-5:

## 2 The form artuaś: etymology and meaning

According to RIG-II/1, 49, “le second (plurale tantum) adjectif feminin ou substantif est rapproché du lat. arduus, virl. ard ‘haut’ et designerait les parties hautes, les superstructures de la construction funéraire.” Lejeune is followed in this by Solinas (1995:383) without further ado. The formal side of this idea is unimpeachable, since, as is well known, the Lugano alphabet does not distinguish voiced and voiceless obstruents (except perhaps, and rather inconsistently, at the first stages of experimentation). It simply goes back to the original -tu- stem without changes in the derivational morphology. artuaś is consequently likely not to mean ‘grave’ but ‘obsequies, funeral rites/arrangements’.

## 3 The text architecture of Todi

Let us now take a look at the Latin text. While the formula LOCAVIT ET STATVIT seems to mean at first glance “put on this site and erected,” this looks strangely redundant. If the Latin part actually translates the Gaulish part, there must have been some crucial difference between karnitu artuaś and karnitu lokan that justifies the doubling of the verb in Latin. RIG-II/1, 49 only speculates: “Pensera-t-on que locauit convient mieux à KARNITU LOKAN, statuit à KARNITU ARTUAŠ et que le couplage latin traduit, en quelque sorte l’ambivalence sémantique de KARNITU?” In other words, in Lejeune’s view each verb somehow corresponds to one of the Gaulish nouns governed by karnitu, or, more precisely, to the periphrastic expressions karnitu lokan and karnitu artuaś (cf. also Lejeune 1971:35; Lambert 1995: 74–6). This is in my view a step in the right direction and, taken literally, would be an ingenious solution if the accusative forms were in fact referring to the lower and higher parts of the monument, which is unfounded.

All things considered, one may be allowed to assume that LOCAVIT ET STATVIT and its variant LOCAVIT STATVITQVE allude to consecutive stages of the global action of erecting the funerary monument, and that Gaulish expresses them separately. The key to the understanding of the Gaulish section is the meaning of the Latin expression locare et statuere. LOCAVIT (SEPVLCRVM) is not likely to be rendering the act of deposing the body or the ashes in the burial place, if only because by this account the second verb would require a different DO alluding to the setting up of a visible stone monument. He is non-committal as to the origin of lokan and artuaś. For him, the fact that Face B is coarser and two carvers are involved explains the orthographic and epigraphic discrepancies; the two hands, however, were simultaneously involved in a unitary project. The coarser appearance of Face B can be taken to account for the more superficial and less careful strokes. In my view, by contrast, this may be taken to mean that Face B is, in fact, Face A, and that karnitu artuaś precedes karnitu lokan, both logically (if the arrangements took place prior to the actual setting and ceremony) and chronologically (if this face was written before the other one). If, as observed by Estarán Tolosa (2016: 16, upon autopsy), the engraver who wrote Face B (whose text is more deeply incised) also started writing Face A, this strongly suggests that Face B is, in fact, Face A. In her view, since Face B is coarser, it could have served as a first draft for the definitive text of A. While this remains possible, it would definitely diminish the final value of the slab. In addition, carvers are held to have had a written model available, especially if the text was long and complex (cf. Franchi de Bellis 2012). Thirdly, if the author of Face A is held to have started writing face B, it follows that Face B was started immediately after Face A was finished, which is slightly inconsistent with the idea that Face A served as a previous model. This also fails to explain the decision to substitute lokan for artuaś in the “definitive” text, especially taking into account that it constitutes the model for the Latin text: we would expect the Latin formula to be modified in accordance with the changes applied on second thought to the indigenous text. As suggested above, however, both sides are equally important, but artuaś takes precedence because it has an abstract meaning that refers to preliminary steps that must be taken when organizing a burial, like the acquisition of the burial place. ## 4 A note on Vergiate

The form pruiam in Vergiate has never been persuasively clarified. The unanimously admitted etymology *h3bʰruH- ‘eyebrow’, whose -(i)i̯ o- derivative somehow metaphorically managed to designate a curved, vaulted, or “bridge-like” construction, goes back to Pisani (1953:373) and is eminently dependent on the Germanic evidence, specifically ON, Norw. brú f. ‘bridge’, and its *-ih2- derivative (see EDPG:79). By contrast, MIr. broí, etc., means ‘eyebrows’, and the Celtic form for ‘bridge’ is *brīu̯ ā. pruiam may be alternatively traced back to *ku̯ roi̯ h2-eh2 ‘purchase, transaction’, a stem of the “τομή” type, built from the root *ku̯ rei̯ h2- ‘to acquire, exchange’ (cf. LIV:395), attested in Gaul. PRINAS ‘bought’ (La Graufesenque), OIr. -cren ‘buys’, etc., which would then be formationally comparable to lokan and possibly to palam (see below). For this to be true, we would have to assume that the context-bound pronunciation of /o/ was [ʊ], which, in a language that contrasts short/lax and long/tense vowels, would be automatically parsed as /u/. In fact, there is a close cognate of this form in Skt. krayá- (m.) ‘price, purchase’, from *ku̯ roi̯ h2-ó-; cf. also Lith. krienas ‘bride-price’ (< *ku̯ re/oi̯ h2-no-). Crucially, MW gobrwy (m.) ‘reward, payment’ and MIr. tinnscra, tochra (n. -i̯ o- stem) ‘dowry, brideprice’ can be traced back to *ku̯ r(e/o)i̯ h2-o-. Schrijver (1995:290) favored a preform *ku̯ roi̯ h2-óm for British Celtic *proi̯ om. Its plural form *proi̯ -ā would directly account for the MW plural gobreu. This is now confirmed by the Cisalpine form, which might even presuppose a secondary singularization of the plural *proi̯ -ā. The sentence may mean something like “Deu placed the purchase order.” Therefore, pruiam karite may be roughly equivalent to karnitu artuaś in Todi. ### 5.1 Indo-European *-ns# in Cisalpine Celtic

artuaś and siteś (LexLep CO, 48, Prestino, Transpadana, 6th c. BCE) are the only accusative plural forms found thus far in Cisalpine Celtic texts written in the Lugano alphabet. Both forms end up in a sibilant reflected by the “butterfly-sign,” the continuant of Greek san in this script. ### 5.5 A related issue: the Celtic genitive plural

The traditional reconstruction of the genitive plural ending is *-om (for consonant stems) and *-ōm (for thematic stems). Eska (2006a) has contended that the gen. pl. ending was Proto-Celtic *-ōm for both the thematic and the athematic inflection, and that it was shortened to *-om everywhere except in the ancestor of HispanoCeltic. Later on, Proto-Celtic /oː/ became /uː/, which accounts for Celtib. -um. He bypasses the predictable objection that the first change cannot be placed before the second without contravening the principles of relative chronology by asserting that “the shortening of long vowels before final nasals was a sound change that did not

reach that part of the Proto-Celtic speech continuum which eventually broke away to become Hispano-Celtic.” This, however, typically begs the question, since, to begin with, there are few changes separating Hispano-Celtic from the rest that cement the idea that its immediate ancestor had a personality of its own inside the Proto-Celtic continuum.12 Furthermore, /oː/ became /uː/ everywhere and in every context in word-final position, including *-ō#, *-ōi̯ , *-ōd, and *-ōs, which is a surprisingly uniform outcome if the change was of Common Celtic age.13 Finally, if we started from a Proto-Celtic stage *-ons and not *-ōns and assumed that /uː/ in OIr. -uH, etc., is the product of compensatory lengthening, this change would also have to be wedged before raising of /oː/. In point of fact, there are several, if generally overlooked, examples of a Hispano-Celtic gen. pl. ending *-on/-om, both in the Iberian script and in the Latin alphabet.14 They all come either from peripheral areas or from Burgos, specifically the area of Lara de los Infantes (ALTICON, BELVICON, CABVECOṆ; cf. ## 6 Cisalpine Gaulish artuaś: the dialectal implications

The above conclusions pose a number of questions regarding the dialectal filiation of Lepontic and Cisalpine Gaulish, but are also paradoxical from the point of view of chronology, since Gaulish -anᵗs in artuaś reflects an older language stage than -enᵗs in siteś, in spite of the fact that Prestino is nearly five centuries older than Todi. This did not raise questions when artuaś was held to be a feminine *-āns, but -u-ănᵗs is indirectly diagnostic of the contemporaneous existence of other accusative plural forms in -C-ănᵗs. Therefore, preservation of /an/ at a late stage is problematic. Lepontic and Cisalpine Gaulish are often treated as two separate, albeit close, Celtic dialects. But a fraction of modern scholarship would certainly object that the distinction between these two entities is scholastic, and that these are only suitable names for successive stages of the same Continental Celtic dialect. ## 7 The morphology of the Lepontic preterite: karite, kalite

As for the relevance of the preterite forms karite, kalite, and karnitu for Celtic dialectology, we may have been looking at the problem from the wrong side. A further objection has been raised against the ultimate attribution of Lepontic and Cisalpine Gaulish to one single variety: these forms contain different endings, and different inflectional morphology is diagnostic of the existence of different dialects. The first interpretation of the Lepontic forms known to me was offered by Pisani (1953:272), who viewed them as 3rd person singular present forms. They contain the root of Skt. kr̥ ṇóti (that is to say *ku̯ er-) and an ending -te from -ti (with unexplained lowering) or from *-tai̯ (a middle ending whose antiquity had been discredited by the decipherment of Mycenaean by that time, however). Lejeune (1971:445–8) specifically started from a present formation in *-i̯ o/e-, -i/ī- of the roots *gʰer- ‘to enclose’ and *kel- ‘to raise, culminate’. The double construction must have meant “to enclose the monument” and “to erect the stone,” “approximately” corresponding to the formula LOCAVIT ET STATVIT in Todi. The forms in -t-e and -t-u are weak preterites in -t-, whose suffix may be compared to Germanic -ð- (if from -t-) and possibly to Oscan -tt-. For Schmidt (1980:177), karite continues an inherited imperfect form, to which 3rd sg. perf. *-e was added. By this expedient, an innovative -t- preterite arose. Since, however, final -t must have been lenited early on (see Lipp 2016 and Prósper 2022:18–9), one wonders at this mechanical reconstruction, and especially at the addition of a perfect ending (missing in all Gaulish and Celtiberian preterite forms originally ending in -t) that could only serve to block its imperfective value. Why such an accumulation of perfectly recognizable endings was felt to be necessary is unclear to me, since hypostasis is out of the question (as opposed, for instance, to the *CeC-e/o stative, recharacterized as *CeC-e-t(i) ← *CeC-e + *-t(i) in non-Anatolian Indo-European).
