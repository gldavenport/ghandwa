# Grazing grass in the spring

## On the etymology of the PIE word *u̯es-r/n ‘spring’

**Chams Benoît Bernard**

> Ἀὼς ἀντέλλοισα καλὸν διέφανε πρόσωπον,
> πότνια Νύξ, τό τε λευκὸν ἔαρ χειμῶνος ἀνέντος·
> ὧδε καὶ ἁ χρυσέα Ἑλένα διεφαίνετ’ ἐν ἁμῖν.
>
> — Theocritus, *Idylls* 18:26–28.

## Abstract

In this article it is argued that the Proto-Indo-European word for ‘spring’, possibly postdating the separation of Tocharian in its meaning ‘spring’, derives from the root *u̯es- ‘grass, pasture’, and not *h₂u̯es- ‘to shine’ as is commonly held.

## Keywords

Indo-European etymology, reconstruction, Indo-Iranian, Tocharian

The Proto-Indo-European word *u̯es-r/n ‘spring’ is a well-attested, easily reconstructed proto-form, with a wide distribution among daughter languages.[^1] Proto-Greek *ϝῆhαρ (dial. *ϝέαρ, routinely written ‹γέαρ› in the mss.), ἔαρ ‘spring’ < *ϝέhαρ; Vedic vasantá- ‘spring’; Av. vaŋhar ‘spring’, vaŋri < *u̯asr-i ‘in the spring’; Old Persian wāhar- in ϑurawāhar(a) ‘second month of spring’, Middle Persian wahār and New Persian bahār (on the vocalism of which see below); Old Norse vár, Old Frisian wars, wērs ‘spring’ < PG *wasra- ~ wazra- n. ‘spring’ (cf. Kroonen 2013: 575); Arm. garun ‘spring’ (*u̯es-r > *gehar > *gar-); PSl. *vesnà f. ‘spring’; Old Welsh guiannuin ‘spring’ (for Celtic words for ‘spring’ see most recently Joseph 2023: 6 f.); Lat. vēr, vēris n. ‘spring’ – on which see §3.1 –, etc. (I spare the reader references to the scientific literature on each of these etymologies, as they are well-known). As one can observe, the meaning ‘spring’ is very stable in these languages. Two exceptions to this are to be found: first in Gambiri, a Nuristani language closely related to Waigali (Morgenstierne 1952: 122), the word for ‘summer’ is wosˈɔ̃ːt, (cf. Morgenstierne 1952: 129), from Proto-Nuristani *wasanta ‘spring’ (see below). The meaning ‘spring’ can be

reconstructed for Proto-Nuristani (cf. Kümmel 2020: 241) so this is a secondary shift, with very little incidence on the ultimate etymology of the word. Second, in Baltic languages (Mallory & Adams 1997: 504), Lithuanian vãsara ‘summer’, Latvian vasara ‘id.’ (cf. Derksen 2015: 492). The word pavãsaris, dial. pavãseris ‘spring’ (Eastern Lithuanian, Žemaitian) should originally mean ‘just before summer’ (cf. Morgenstierne 1927: 60). As the meaning is ‘spring’ in all of Slavic as well, the Baltic meaning should be taken as a later development, with no incidence on the reconstruction of the Proto-Indo-European meaning. That a semantic shift for this word can only be observed in three out of hundreds of Indo-European languages shows how stable the meaning ‘spring’ was for this word. The “traditional” etymology, I contend, presents formal and semantic issues. In the present article, I will first discuss it, including its shortcomings, after which I will introduce a new etymology of PIE *u̯es-r/n.

## 1. Discussion of previous etymological proposals

The etymology of PIE *u̯es-r/n is not very often mentioned, even less discussed. The major, if not the only idea found in scientific literature is the connection of *u̯es-r/n to *h₂u̯es ‘to be bright, to shine’ (from which comes the IE word for ‘gold’), going back at least to Pokorny (IEW I 86, 1174)[^2] and repeated throughout the ages, see for instance EWAIa II: 532– 33, Lipp (2019: 103) who even posits a noun with the meaning ‘bright light; morning light, spring’ (whether in PIIr. or PIE, that is not clear). One finds the same connection in the NIL p. 357 f. Nikolaev (2009: 470²⁴) finds this etymology doubtful, and writes that “[…] there is no proof that the word for ‘spring’ is derivationally related to the word for ‘dawn’ and there is no independent evidence in favor of an initial *h₂ in ‘spring’.” This etymology is also rejected by de Vaan (2008: 664), notably on the basis that the Proto-Greek form *ϝέhαρ precludes a reconstruction with an initial laryngeal (see e.g. de Vaan 2008: 664), which is why the authors of the NIL have put the laryngeal between parentheses when mentioning the ‘spring’ words (as a way of acknowledging the issue). Peters (1980) proposed a sound law, namely that the laryngeal was dropped in Greek when followed by a consonant followed by a vocalic liquid (“dann kann auch ἔαρ von *h₂u̯esr hergeleitet und zu Pokornys au̯es- “leuchten” […]

gestellt werden”). This sound law has not been widely accepted, as far as I could see, but even if it were correct, there is another problem with this etymology: its semantics. As often with IE etymologies, there is a tendency to accept Pokornian (vel sim.) ideas based on apparently obvious connections, without looking at things much further. In the present instance, the idea, of course, is that spring is the season with more light, the time of the year which becomes brighter. This is not false per se, as the equinox and the beginning of spring coincide, after which daytime becomes longer and nighttime shorter. No matter how good ancient peoples were at noticing all types of natural phenomena with precision, and they were, the ones that they related the more to were those concretely useful to them. In a way, two semantic objections can be held up against the current etymology: 1. although the fact that daytime starts becoming longer in the spring, it is not a feature of that season only, as it is also the case, and more so, in the summer, and 2. the longer days as being the defining feature of spring is more specific of a city-life, as there are many more important natural phenomena relative to spring for rural-dwelling peoples. Further, if what is implied by that etymology is that spring is “heated” or “hot”, suffice it to mention the French proverb “en avril, ne te découvre pas d’un fil ; en mai, fais ce qu’il te plaît”.[^3] It thus appears that the current etymology of *u̯es-r/n is neither formally nor semantically satisfactory.

## 2. Spring, the grazing season

For nomadic or semi-nomadic peoples, as the Proto-Indo-European speakers probably were (cf. Mallory & Adams 1997: 651; Anthony 2023), spring and early summer is the season of migrations. The frozen waters of winter melt, birds sing their love songs, the sun breaks its bleak prison of clouds, it is the lambing season – lambs are born –, and grass grows. From under the molten ice – first in the lowlands, then higher up – springs of water gush forth, like unto natural fountains. Pastors take their herds to their spring and summer camps, through greener and greener landscapes, which landscapes provide food and where they can remain at least throughout summer. All these characteristics are much more important than the fact that the days lengthen, and little is more crucial than the fact that animals can eat more, reproduce, and give birth. Pastoralist peoples drive their animals to either highlands or lowlands, depending on which are the greenest, and the least susceptible to succumb to spring floods. Among desert nomads of Central Asia, herds are brought to the desert in early spring when it is covered with vegetation (cf. Kuzmina 2008: 64). The main concern, no matter the environment, of these peoples, is to feed their herds. It should thus not be a surprise if this very phenomenon gave its name to the season in which it occurs. Indeed, I contend that *u̯es-(e)r/n (see below on the suffix) derives from the root *u̯es-, a root meaning ‘grass, pasture’, e.g. Old Irish fess f. ‘food’ < *u̯es-teh₂-, Tocharian A wäsri ‘grassy spot’ (see below), Vedic svásara nt. ‘Weide, Weideplatz’, Avestan vāstra n. ‘pasture’, vāstriia- ‘herdsman’.[^4] This root is also found as a verbal root with the meaning ‘to graze’ (LIV²: 693). The verbal root ‘to graze’ has a very limited attestation (LIV²: 694): Lat. vēscor ‘to feed oneself’ (cf. Keller 1992: 99–100) and Hittite wešiye/a-, wešiyattari ‘he grazes’ which is a denominal formation from weši ‘pasture’ (Kloekhorst 2008: 1007; Inglese 2020: 355 – differently Grestenberger 2016: 134 f.). It thus appears to primarily be a nominal root. Turkic nomadic peoples also saw the first characteristic of spring as being the regrowing of grass, the greening of the land (cf. Bazin 1991: 77, 79).[^5] The ancient Turkic year “commençait avec la première Nouvelle Lune où l’on constatait un reverdissement des herbes, ce qui coïncidait à peu près avec l’Équinoxe de printemps.” (Bazin 1991: 81). In Ossetic Iron kærdæʒy mæj ‘month of grass’ designates early spring, namely March-April (cf. Abaev 1970: 2). Avestan zarǝmaiia- ‘spring’ is also a parallel, as it derives from the root zar- ‘green, yellow’: it is the “green season” (cf. already Schneider 1940: 202). Even for sedentary peoples, the fact that plants and grass grows in spring is a major feature thereof: hence English spring, the season when buds, plants, grass, spring.[^6] It thus appears that the most prominent feature of the arrival of spring is not the heat or the longer days, which are features of summer, but the growing of grass and the green grazing lands. This is the basis of my etymology.

## 3. On the etymology of ‘spring’

### 3.1 ‘Spring’ in Indo-European languages

Indo-European nouns going back to *u̯es- for the name of ‘spring’ do not all come from the exact same prototype, which indicates, in my opinion, that the word formation is rather late in the history of Proto-Indo-European. However, as the meaning is stable, as we have seen above, it seems that the association of the concept of ‘growing grass, grazing pastures’ and ‘spring’ is a very strong one among Indo-European peoples. To note, if one takes the Indo-Anatolian theory into account, this word *u̯es- with the meaning ‘spring’ could perhaps be post-Anatolian. The form *u̯es-r/n probably derives from a delocative of *u̯es-er ‘in the spring’ < ‘at the time of pasture, of grazing’ < ‘in the pasture’ (according to the model of delocative derivation from an adverbial form in -er, parallel to -en, cf. Nussbaum 1986: 189–190, 236–243, 289–292; see also Nikolaev 2009: 465 f.).[^7] As it happens, some of these delocative derivatives eventually adopted the same meaning as the base noun, through hypostasis: *h₂us-ér ‘at dawn’ found in Ved. uṣarbúdh- ‘awake at dawn’, built on the s-stem *h₂us-s-ér, then takes on the meaning ‘dawn’: uṣár, gen. usrás ‘dawn’. Another example, even more evocative here, is Greek χεῖμα ‘winter, cold’ < *g̑ʰei̯-m-n̥, derived from a locatival -en formation *g̑ʰei̯-m-en > Ved. héman ‘in winter’. That locatival *g̑ʰei̯-men would have been “re-interpreted as the endingless locative to a men-stem”, hence the back-formation *g̑ʰei̯mn̥ in Greek (Nussbaum 1986: 52¹¹). The locative adverbial suffixes -r and -r/-n are found among several nouns, in particular for time units and seasons, e.g. *g̑ʰei̯m-er(-i) ‘in the winter’, basis of Greek χείμερος.[^8] This locative form presupposes the existence of an acrostatic root-noun *u̯os-/*u̯es- or *u̯ēs-/u̯es-.[^9] In this perspective, *u̯es-er/n is technically not a heteroclitic noun, but rather there existed, in PIE, a locative in *-er for *u̯es-, namely *u̯es-er and a locative in *-en, namely *u̯es-en-, the *-r locative being more productive generally for this specific word. Already in Proto-Indo-European, it is likely that *u̯es-er/n was reinterpreted as a locative of an r-stem with -r throughout the paradigm. As a consequence, this stem -r- became the basis of further derivatives: *u̯es-r-. While this word may appear to be a heteroclitic noun from a reconstruction perspective, it is more likely that two variants of the same word, a substantivized locative, existed in (P)IE, and thus the spelling *u̯es-r/n, traditionally indicating a heteroclitic noun, I adopt mostly out of convenience. Lipp (2019: 104) explains Vedic vasantá- as a derivative from “the original locative *h₂u̯os-én → PIIr. *(H)u̯ās-án (as potential base of Ved. vasantá- ‘spring’ = *‘time in the bright light, time in spring’) […]” Naturally, there is no reason to assume an o-grade when Brugmann’s law does not apply here. Indeed Vedic vasantá- and Nuristani *wasanta- (cf. Kümmel 2020: 241) are built on the delocative *u̯as-an (no laryngeal) (< *u̯es-én), with a suffix -ta-.[^10] In parallel, the word for ‘winter’, which perhaps influenced *wasanta-, Ved. hemantá- m. comes from PIE *g̑ʰei̯m-en- (delocative) + -to-.[^11] The same holds also for Brittonic forms going back to *wesantēno- < *wes-n̥t-ēnV- (cf. Matasović 2009: 416–17; Joseph 2023: 6). The Latin form vēr nt. ‘spring’ does not derive from Proto-Italic *wēsr- (pace Schrijver 1991: 128), as internal *-sr- > Lat. -br- (cf. Weiss 2020: 177; Proto-Italic *wes-r-i loc. sg., cf. Av. vaŋri, on which see below, would become **vebre in Latin). The form *wes-r- was replaced by vērnum (tempus), cf. vērnō ‘in the spring’ (Cato). In the very same way, hībernum (tempus) ‘winter’ replaced hiems f. ‘winter’. The form hībernum derives from the adjective hībernus ‘occurring in winter’ from Pre-Latin *heimrino- < Proto-Italic *χeimer-ino-,[^12] cf. Lat. vērnus adj. ‘occurring in spring’ < *vēr-ino-. In his dictionary, de Vaan (2008: 663–664) claims that Proto-Italic *wes-or, *wes-n-os nt. ‘spring’ would surface as Pre-Lat. *veror, *vēnos restored as first *veror, vēris and then vēr, vēris. Rather, Lat. vēr is back-formed after spoken Latin *vērānum (tempus) ‘spring’ < *vērrānum (geminates are simplified after a long vowel, cf. causa < OLat. caussa or mīsī ‘I sent’ < *mīssī). The form *vērrānum itself derives from *vēror-ānum, with syncope à la Garnier (Garnier 2012). The non-standard Latin vērānum is attested in glosses, and reflected by Romance languages (Spanish verano ‘summer’ – arch. ‘late spring, early summer’ etc.) and gentilicia such as Vērānius and Vērānus. As a result, one may assume a Proto-Italic form *wēsor (< PIE *u̯ḗs-ṛ), strict cognate of Proto-Greek *ϝῆhαρ. Such a form would yield Lat. *vērur, -oris nt. with regular rhotacism.

### 3.2 Tocharian words related to *u̯es-

It has been suggested that the Tocharian A form wäsri* f. ‘place abounding in grass, grassy spot’ only found in the plural and corresponding to Sanskrit śādvala- (cf. DToA : 442), comes from the word for ‘spring’ (Schneider 1940: 202). It would thus represent a semantic evolution from ‘spring’ to ‘grass’. However, Van Windekens made a case for a connection with Hittite weši, Av. vāstra- (cf. Van Windekens 1976: 565, with reference to previous literature). On the other hand, Van Windekens does not pursue this track towards the reconstruction of the word for ‘spring’ (he considers the connection to Lith. vãsara ‘été’ too doubtful from a semantical point of view). Further, the derivation proposed by Van Windekens (< *u̯es-r-i̯eh₂) cannot be held from the formal point of view, and is anachronistic. On the basis of my etymology of *u̯es-r/n as deriving from *u̯es- ‘grass, pasture’, it seems more logical to claim that the Tocharian A word represents a more archaic meaning of the Indo-European word. Etymologically, TA wäsri*, which is a feminine word, derives from PT *wǝsriya, the feminine form of the adjective *wǝsriye < PIE *u̯es-r-i̯o- ‘belonging to pastures’, on the same model as TA omlyi, TB emalya ‘heat’ which is the feminine form of TB emalle, TA omäl adj. ‘hot’. A cognate of Tocharian A wäsri is found in Vedic svásara- ‘good pasture’ < *su-vásara- (EWAia II: 796). This should originally be a possessive compound *su-vásar-a- ‘offering good grazing’. It corresponds to a marginal preservation in Indo-Iranian, – only found in this compound – of *u̯es-r- ‘pasture’. Along with other non-Tocharian Indo-European isoglosses, this Tocharian etymology could be added to the possible evidence for Tocharian separating first from “Core PIE”, with “non-Tocharian Core PIE” then sharing common innovations (Schmidt 1992; Winter 1998; Carling 2005: 98–99). Some examples have been discussed at length in the literature, such as Proto-Tocharian *yǝp- > TA yäw- and TB yǝp- ‘to enter’ vs. Sanskrit yabh- ‘to fuck’ (e.g. Winter 1998: 185, who adds “[i]t remains to be seen whether other cases of lexical retention in Tocharian can be adduced that would serve to support Schmidt’s contention” [that Tocharian “left” PIE before the other languages]). In fact, the archaism represented by wäsri is even more remarkable than that of *yǝp- in the sense that the sexual meaning of PIE *i̯ebʰ- is “only” to be found in Indo-Iranian, Greek and Slavic, whereas the meaning ‘spring’ is pervasive in Indo-European, apart from Anatolian. It can be compared as to its importance to the equation between TB kärweñe ‘stone, rock’ and its other Indo-European cognates – if one agrees with it – (cf. Winter 1998: 187; Kroonen, Jakob & al. 2022: 8–9). One can add another Tocharian etymology to the lot: TB ysāre ‘wheat’ (DTB: 567 f.; Peyrot 2018: 251) and TA wsār ‘wheat’ (DToA: 456) < Proto-Tocharian *w’ǝsare. Huld (1990: 404, 420¹⁵) suggested its prototype, *u̯esōr-[^13] derived from *u̯es-r/n, as a “reference to a seasonal variety of wheat”. Pinault (2017a: 130) explains it as originally meaning “‘[grain] of spring’, because it was cultivated in spring and harvested in summer […] Therefore, this noun may be related to the PIE word *u̯és- ‘spring’ [...]”. Further, he develops the specific etymology of *w’ǝsare, “that would be based on an adjectival derivative meaning ‘belonging to spring’, or the like, substantivized as ‘product of the spring’.” A similar etymology can be found in Peyrot (2018: 252). Pinault’s derivation ‘belonging to spring’, originally as an epithet of ‘grain’, becoming the noun through a common pars pro toto process is absolutely possible, with the caveat that the harvest time is generally more important than the sowing time, when considering cereals at least. However, in light of the rest of the discussion, I suggest viewing it as a derivative of the word for ‘pasture, grass’, here designating a specific type of grain, wild wheat, and more generally the ancestor of domestic cereals, that is gramineae, commonly called in English “grasses”. Morphologically speaking, it would be a collective noun *u̯es-ōr- ‘grasses’, like the English word grasses, but specifically designating wild grain. These grasses were to be found among the grass, notably the high grass, yet to be grazed. Interestingly, the word for ‘wheat’ is lacking from our reconstruction of Indo-European, despite it being a very important cereal (cf. Mallory & Adams 1997: 7). Naturally, absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, however, this etymology would fit well with a pastoralist people, harvesting specific grains among the pastures (cf. Shishlina & al. 2018). From an archaeological point of view, there is no evidence for agricultural activities in the Afanasievo culture (cf. Kroonen, Jakob & al. 2022: 3, 33), which is most probably the ancestral culture of Proto-Tocharians.[^14] In his article on the PIE word for ‘barley’, Weiss (2021) suggests that “Nuclear PIE speakers on the Dnieper” (so, in the West) knew of barley and planted it, whereas Nuclear PIE speakers on the Don did maybe not know of it.” He further adds “[b]arley did not arrive with ancestors of Tocharian speakers since the assumed intermediate archaeological cultures were non-agricultural” (Weiss 2021: 515). The scenario drawn by the etymologies I have proposed here seems to suggest that neither Tocharians nor their far-away ancestors cultivated grain, which would be why they called cereals “grasses”. This is coherent with the observation mentioned above, that the Afanasievo culture did not practice cultivation. In fact, no traces of crop cultivation are found in the region of the Indo-European homeland as well, until the late Bronze Age (cf. Shishlina & al. 2018: 839–840). More generally, on early Eastern Indo-Europeans, see Anthony (2023: 23 f.). Further, as is better and better known, people from the Yamnaya culture did not have cavities from grain alimentation, and we find no carbonized grain in the Yamnaya culture excavations (cf. Anthony 2023: 24). K. T. Schmidt, first in a conference (cited and accepted by Hilmarsson 1991: 190), then in Schmidt (1994: 280) suggested to see TA yusār as meaning ‘spring’ or ‘in the spring’ (< *yän + *w’əsār ‘in spring’).[^15] This is however erroneous, as demonstrated by Pinault (1993: 144 f.), and TA yusār means ‘season’. Tocharian A yusāri ‘seasons’ in the dual, designates the two Tocharian seasons, la belle saison (spring-summer) and the cold season (fall – winter) – ṣme śärme, see Pinault 1993: 146; 1998: 362; DToA: 371–372.[^16] All in all, it is my suggestion, but it does not have to be taken as definitive, that the Proto-Indo-European word *u̯es-er/n took on the meaning ‘spring’ only after the separation of Proto-Tocharian from core IE. This would account for the different and, I argue, more archaic meaning of the derivatives of this root in Tocharian. In a way, it should not surprise us that the two branches that do not share the ‘spring’ word and concept are Anatolian and Tocharian. As Peyrot (2018: 243) states, Tocharian agricultural vocabulary is important in order to answer the question of whether PIE speakers practiced or knew agriculture. He was right in considering TB ysāre and its TA cognate as having a “secondary technical meaning”, although he believed they derive from the meaning ‘spring’. Besides TA wäsri ‘grassy spot’, PIE *u̯es-ōr-, reflected in both Tocharian languages, would have – I argue – designated wild wheat, wild grains, and not cultivated wheat, which was most probably not present in PIE culture. Wild plants, including gramineous plants, were a major component of the Don Steppe pastoralists’ diet well into the 3rd millennium BCE (e.g. Shishlina & al. 2018). Peyrot (2022: 89–90) considers the hypothesis of Tocharian splitting first (after Anatolian) still attractive, but he views the evidence in its favor as slim. The added evidence concerning the ‘grass’ words in Tocharian responds to all criteria, and brings more weight to this side of the balance. Peyrot (2022: 90) suggests that, as (in his view), the most likely candidate to have split off third is Italo-Celtic, any innovation shared by the rest of Indo-European languages should be attested in this branch. The change should be unidirectional and salient. I contend that it is the case here. Peyrot (2022: 89 f.) warns that lexical replacements and semantic change should be distinguished. He rightly reminds us that in many cases, such as Tocharian and Anatolian *u̯el(H)- ‘die’ vs. *mer- ‘die’ in the other languages, it cannot be proven that Tocharian has the more archaic meaning of the root. The words mentioned in this section do not necessarily have the same meaning as in Proto-Indo-European, but they reflect a more original meaning. It is definitely a strong argument for the “Tocharian second” hypothesis.

Although it is more speculative, I wish to suggest that the two-seasons system of Tocharian might reflect the original Indo-European year division, predating the division of the ‘hot season’ into spring and summer, which would postdate the Tocharian split. Hittite would have also innovated differently, by dividing the ‘cold season’ into ‘autumn’ and ‘winter’, but leaving the ‘hot season’ intact.

## 4. The Iranian spring

It befits here to briefly discuss a few Iranian forms relative to ‘spring’, and to correct a number of misconceptions found in the scientific literature.

### 4.1 Iranian words deriving from *u̯es-er

As is known, the Avestan form vaŋri ‘in the spring’, is derived from *u̯as-r-i, the regular locative rebuilt on the restructured r-stem: in Indo-Iranian, locatives were often rebuilt on zero-grades, analogically from the other oblique cases. However, a number of Iranian forms derive from other prototypes. The basis of my discussion will be Lipp (2019). Unlike what is suggested by Lipp (2019) and apparently by others, Middle Persian wahār, New Persian bahār cannot derive from Old Persian wāhar-,[^17] as such a metathesis of vowel length would not be explainable.[^18] The Middle and New Persian forms derive from PIr. *u̯ahā(r)-[^19] < PIE *u̯esōr- ‘times of grazing’, similar to Greek νύκτωρ ‘by night’, adverb based on the accusative of a decasuative neuter with collective value (cf. Pinault 2017: 648) but also in the sg. ‘night after night’, hence ‘every night’ (cf. Pinault op. cit.). Old Persian wāhar(a)- derives from PIr. *u̯āhar- possibly from *u̯os-er- with Brugmann’s law or rather, as Prof. Michael Weiss suggests (p.c.), from *u̯ēser or a vṛddhi *u̯ēsero- ‘springlike’. The collective stem *u̯esōr- is supported by Middle Iranian and by Tocharian (see §3.2), whereas there existed a singular *u̯ēser reconstructed on the basis of Old Persian, and *u̯eser, reconstructed on the basis of Tocharian, Latin and the other languages. Probably both forms coexisted in Proto-Persic (cf. Bakhtiari bahår, bohār < u̯ahār-).[^20]

### 4.2 Iranian words for ‘spring’ not deriving from *u̯es-er/n

In my view, none of the words discussed in the following derive from *u̯es-er/n, they are all inner-Iranian innovations designating the ‘spring’. However, some of these have been thought to be cognates of the words discussed above. These are: Pashto wóṛay ‘late spring’, Sogdian wrty, Yidgha wáṛo ‘spring’. The etymology proposed by Lipp (2019: 103) – although not his – for these three words is difficult to accept (*u̯āhṛt-aka- etc.). It goes back to Szemerényi (1959: 69–70) whom Lipp does not quote, and who suggested that there was an element *u̯āhart- which he thought to recognize in Ossetic walʒæg ‘spring’ (based on an etymology by Abaev, and another attempt by Benveniste 1959: 32), as well as in Sogdian wrty. Szemerényi suggested, very cautiously (1959: 70²) that *u̯āhart- might perhaps come from *u̯āhara-ratu- ‘springtime’, with haplology, then “syncope of *wārat to *wārt […] and then shortening?” These phonetic steps are ad hoc and some simply do not work (OIr. *-aha- > Sogd. -ā-, the vowel shortening suggested here does not occur in all of these languages). The idea that sprang to my mind was that all these words simply derive from *u̯art- ‘to turn’ (EDIV: 243–245), as in “the turn of the year” (spring is the first season of the year among all Iranian peoples – as well as for many peoples, among which the French, the English, the Scots),[^21] but I soon realized – it turned out – that this idea had sprung in the mind of the late and revered professor Gharib many years ago (SD: 413). All three forms are regularly explained therewith, with no need for varying etymologies, and long/short vowel alternations, and it is much more efficient than Szemerényi’s proposal. As is known, the Indo-European speakers conceived of the year as a wheel (e.g. Joseph 2023: 1), and the starting point of that metaphoric wheel is probably springtime. At least certainly for Iranian peoples it was (and is). Semantically a bit farther away, one can think of Lat. Vertumnus, the name of the God of gardens, seasons and fruits, so beautifully represented by Arcimboldo. It is probably derived from *vertumen, a neuter in -men based on the root *vert- ‘to turn’ (on the association to the root *u̯ert-, already by Latin authors, see e.g. Welch 2005: 37, 177⁴; Weiss 2020: 311). One cannot exclude that Vertumnus, who was also the god of change, was originally the divinity of spring, of that very turn of the year when what is dead becomes alive again, when nature changes, hence his association with gardens and fruits, which do blossom and bloom in the spring. Spring being the first season of the year, it is called vasal in Juhuri (a Persic language)[^22] and avasāl, avasōr in Taleshi < *upa-sarda- literally ‘next to the year’, meaning ‘at the beginning, next to the (rest of) the year’. Such an etymology can also be attributed to Pashto psarláy ‘spring’ < *upa-sarda-ká- and a similar one to Khotanese pasāla- ‘springtime’ < *pati-sarda- ‘onto the year’ (cf. NEVP: 66), structurally similar to Old Cornish kyniaf, Middle Welsh kynhayaf ‘autumn’ < *‘before winter’ (Joseph 2023: 10). With a different etymon, also meaning ‘first time of the year’ is Paračī rhaɣʷām ‘spring’ < *fra-gāma- (cf. Charpentier 1929: 195). I interpret this as meaning ‘first step, first time’, cf. Persian gām ‘footstep, step’, so something similar to French printemps < Lat. prīmum tempus or OFr. prin tans ‘first time (= season)’. Charpentier (1929: 195) proposed that Pashto warɣúmay m. ‘kid, up to one year old’, which also comes from *fra-gāmaka originally meant ‘one spring old’ (so CDIAL p. 481), cf. Munǰī fraɣomïy ‘male kid’, Nuristani Waigali prāmä, Prasun pāmə ‘one year old kid’ (on the same model, Ved. vatsá- ‘calf’ < *u̯ets-ó- ‘having a year’ or alternatively ‘of the year’). This etymology has been rejected by Morgenstierne (NEVP p. 90), who connects both the Paračī word for ‘spring’ and the ‘kid’ words to frā-gam- ‘to approach, be completed’, Khwarezmian frɣʾmk ‘adult’; Ossetic rægæd ‘ripe, mature (fruit, persons)’ < *fra-gata-. Sims-Williams (apud Sundermann 1981: 196) proposes that Sogdian βrɣ-ʾmʾk could mean ‘kid’ and be connected to the other words mentioned above. He cites the Shughni word anjům ‘male yearling lamb’ < *ham-ǰāma- and adds: “[s]ince *fra- and *han-gam- are virtually synonymous in the sense “finish” […], it is possible that the etymological meaning is ‘(an animal) which has reached maturity’.” Following this proposal, he connects these words to the Khwarezmian word frɣʾmk ‘adult’. One can nonetheless object that a kid is not an adult, and that ‘to finish’ yields nothing close to the meaning of ‘spring’. Thus, we can either explain ‘spring’ and ‘kid’ on one side or ‘kid’ and ‘adult’ on the other. In any case, the Shughni word (as well as Sariqoli naǰom ‘male, 1–2 years old’) remains unexplained from a semantic point of view, and the entire question is still thorny. The Pashto word caṛmə́nay ~ caṛmúnay ‘spring’, Ormuri ciṛmū́näi ‘early spring’ (Morgenstierne 1932) might come from *čarta-mā(ŋ)haka- ‘the grazing month’ (NEVP p. 18). However, Morgenstierne (NEVP p. 18) is opposed to this etymology, apparently on semantic grounds, although it is not entirely clear. In this case, it would have designated the first month of spring, when grazing has become possible in the valleys again, and then extended to ‘early spring’ as a whole (which, after all, does not last more than a month and a half to the most). In this case, the Ormuri meaning is more archaic than the Pashto one. There are many other Iranian words unrelated to *u̯es-r/n, the covering of which would need a separate study on its own (for instance Ossetic walʒæg – уалдзӕг – ‘spring’). Let us finish the present discussion with the Kumzari (a further Persic language) word for spring, ǰowa (Anonby 2015: 345). I believe it could perhaps be connected to the Iranian word for ‘barley’ *i̯au̯a- (PIE *i̯eu̯o- ‘grain’ cf. Weiss 2021), as in ‘the season when barley is harvested’. As a matter of fact, barley is harvested in spring. While in Kumzari the reflex of *-au̯a is -ā, as in ǰā ‘barley’,[^23] it appears that the Kumzari reflex of *-au̯- and *-au̯a- is respectively -ō- and -ōw- as in kō ‘mountain’ < *kau̯fa-; nōwad and nōẓḍa respectively ‘ninety’ and ‘nineteen’; rōẓ ‘day’; ǰōɁar ‘pearl’ ← Pers gawhar or Arabic ǰawhar (if not inherited). Further, *-ā- becomes -ō- even before -u- as in, for instance, ōǰuẓō ‘old person’ ← Ar. ʕāǰiz ‘disabled, powerless’ (cognate of Tunisian əʕzūz ‘old man’, ʕzeyyez ‘the elderly’ etc.), so that an assimilation of the -ā- to the -w- of ǰowa cannot be entirely excluded.

## 5. Conclusion

In conclusion, *u̯es-er/n ‘spring’ derives from *u̯es- ‘grass, pasture’, and not *h₂u̯es- ‘to be bright’. In order to demonstrate this etymology, which is formally and semantically more efficient than the traditional one, I used typological parallels and formal data. I have also proposed that the Tocharian words related to the root *u̯es- refer directly to grass and pastures, and probably predate the “core IE” shift of this derivative form (*u̯es-r-) to the meaning ‘spring’. It is also suggested that PIE *u̯esōr-, reflected in Tocharian B ysāre and Tocharian A wsār ‘wheat’, originally designated wild cereals/wheat and not cultivated grain, and that this can be used as an argument in favor of the hypothesis that Tocharian was the second branch to split (after Anatolian), as well as for the absence of agriculture in Proto-Indo-European society. This article ends with a short discussion of various Iranian words for ‘spring’, both those cognate of *u̯es-r and those from unrelated roots. Both of those derives from the world for ‘barley’, as spring is the season during which barley is usually harvested.
