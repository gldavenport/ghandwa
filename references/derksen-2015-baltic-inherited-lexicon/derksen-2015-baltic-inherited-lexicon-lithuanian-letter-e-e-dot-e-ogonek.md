**LITH** džiaūgtis 'be glad, rejoice', 3 pres. džiaugiasi, 3 pret. džiaūgėsi

### engti
**LITH** engti 'peel, skin, scrape, tear, beat, oppress, torment', 3 pres. engia, 3 pret.
           engė (the LKŽe mentions a variant engti, which in the many illustrative
           sentences occurs only o nee)
In my Slavic dictionary ( 2008a: 157) , I connected engti 'oppress, torment' with PSL
* ędza, *ęga, e.g. OCS jędza 'disease', Ru. jaga 'Baba-Yaga', Pl. jędza 'witch', which
etymon may be cognate with Ok. ekki m. 'pain, anguish', OE inca f. 'pain, suspicion,
fight'. The question is if engti 'peel, skin, scrape, tear, beat' is etymologically the same

<!-- source-pdf-page: 168; source-page: 154 -->
verb. If so, one might consider abandoning the connection with PSI. *ędza, *ęga, as
lupti 'peel, skin, beat' is also attested with the meaning 'exploit, oppress'. This does not
necessarily hold for engti 'etwas miihsam und schwerfallig thun' (Kurschat 1883: 107),
which is clearly cognate with →angils 'lazy'.
See also: anglis; ankštis; ingti

### eršketas
**LITH** eršketas 2 'sturgeon'; VAR eršketras 1 (ešketras (Bretk.) 'whale' is probably a
           Prussianism)
**OPR** esketres (EV) 'sturgeon'
**BSL** * esetros
**PSL** * esetro m. o; esetra f. a (b) 'sturgeon'
**SL** Ru. osetr m„ Gsg. osetra; oseter (dial.) m.; ORu. jesetro m.; osetro m.; Cz.

           jesetr (arch.) m.; jasotr (arch.) m.; LSrb. jesotr m.; SCr. jesetra f.; Sln. jeset"7'

It seems highly plausible that eršketas is cognate with →ešerys 'perch' and that in spite
of the initial *e- both etyma belong to PIE *h₂ ek- 'sharp', cf. Lat. acipenser 'sturgeon' <
*hzeku-. The Baltic forms are not without problems, however. Forms like eršketas and
eršketras were probably influenced by →eršketis 'thorn' (though it must be admitted
that a development eršketas < ešketras is plausible in itself, cf. Toporov PJ II: 89 ), but
it is clear that the original form contained a k, cf. OPr. esketres, which is absent in
Lith. ešerys. This may be the familiar intrusive velar which in Baltic frequently arose
before s or z. In that case we would have to start from a Baltic proto-form *eksetras.

### ežgys
**LITH** ežgys (dial.) 4 'ruff'; VAR egžlys (arch.) 4; ežegys (Pruss.) 3b; ežgl 4
**OPR** assegis (EV) 'perch'
**BSL** *egž-io-; *fžg-io-
**PSL** *ėždži, m. jo; *ėždžiko m. o; *ezgarb m. o; *ėzgarb m. jo; *ėskarb m. jo 'ruff'
**SL** Bel. jadžgar; Ukr. jazgar (dial.); Cz. ježdik; Pl. jaždž (arch.) m.Qo); jaszcz
         (arch.); jazgar (dial.); jazgier (dial.); jazgarz; jazgierz (arch.); OPI. ježdžyk;
         Kash. jwžj; jwšč
It seems perfectly plausible to me that this etymon is cognate with →ežys 'hedgehog'.
(thus Būga RR II: 217). Like its close relative the perch, the ruff has prickly fins ( cf.
MoHG Barsch 'perch', Borste 'bristle', as well as Lith. ešerys 'perch', which presumably
derives from *h₂ ek- 'sharp' ). The g of this word may be the well-known Baltic
intrusive velar. In that case, archaic Lith. egžlys would have preserved the original
constellation. The Slavic forms would have to be borrowings from Baltic, which in
view of their distribution is not unlikely.
