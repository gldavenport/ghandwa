### šeima
**LITH** šeima 4 'family'; VAR šeiml 4 'family, household'; šeimas 'migration (of
          birds), nest, offspring'
**LATV** saime [ai, ai 2, ai 2] 'members of a household, (extended) family'
**BSL** *šoi-mPSL      *sėmbja f. ia
**SL** RuCS sėmija 'servants'; Ru. sem'ja 'family, (folkl.) wife', Asg. sem'ju (AP (e) in
         Old Russian); ORu. sėmija 'servants, members of a household, family,
         husband, wife'; Ukr. sim'ja
The root of this noun is probably PIE *kei- 'lie'. In view of the Slavic evidence, l
assume that the Lithuanian e-vocalism is secondary. We may compare Go. haims f.
'village', Olc. heimr 'home, world', OHG heim < *koi-mo-. Forms such as →kiemas
'courtyard, farmstead, village', OPr. caymis, are probably borrowings from Germanic.
Sabaliauskas (1990: 24) notes that in spite of the fact that in old texts we exclusively
find →šeimyna, Fi. heimo and Est. hoim, which are borrowings from Baltic, testify to
the antiquity of šeima.

**PIE** *suekr-uH-
**IE** Skt. švasrd- f. 'mother-in-law'; Lat. socrus f. 'mother-in-law'; OHG swigar f.

According to Karaliūnas (1999: 86-88, 2006), švašva 'daughter (Plungė district), sister
(Dotnuva, Kėdainiai district)' continues the Lithuanian counterpart of PSL *svekry,
which is generally believed to have been ousted by →anyta. Karaliūnas's etymology
deserves serious consideration, but involves a number of difficulties. First of all , there
is the semantic shift. Karaliūnas suggests an intermediate stage 'mother-in-law's
daughter', which does not seem unreasonable, cf. MDu. swager 'father-in-law,
brother-in-law, son-in-law'. Formally there are multiple issues. As in the case of

→šešuras, the initial š may be due to assimilation. The medial š would have to be
analogical after šešuras, as before r we would expect depalatalization, cf. OCS svekry.
Unexplained remains the fact that in švašva the *u of the initial constellation was
retained, wheras in šešuras it was not. The a of the root, Karaliūnas argues, may have
developed from unstressed e. A formation *švešr(u)va (cf. sesuva, sesva 'sister'),
which amounts to *-uH- being replaced by *-uH-ehr, is natural enough and directly
comparable to the South Slavic forms. In the case of švašva, however, there is no
trace of an r, which weakens the hypothesis.

The Latvian word is a borrowing from East Slavic. OPr. swints is often assumed to

Interestingly, the zero grade of the root may be present in Latv. svinet 'celebrate'.
