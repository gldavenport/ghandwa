# Grounding Celtic diachronic phonology II*

JOSEPH F. ESKA

**Abstract:** This paper continues a series of treatments of sound changes in the Celtic languages that have not been satisfactorily or fully explained to date. Sound changes that occurred in proto-Brittonic and early Welsh are treated: (3) the shift of */j/ > /ð/ / ˈVr_V in proto-Brittonic; (4) the shift of the group *-/ntʰL/- > -/θL/- in Old Welsh; (5) the evolution of the group */ltʰr/ in Welsh.

§1. This paper continues a series of treatments of non-straightforward sound changes attested in the Celtic languages with the goal of motivating them in a non-stipulative way.¹

§4. The difference between the positions of Morris-Jones and Russell vs. Jackson and Schrijver is whether this sound change occurred prior to or after apocope. In view of the extreme rarity of the sound change */j/ > /ð/ in the languages of the world,⁹ it is very likely that Jackson is correct that this change after */r/ is “co-eval with that of intervocalic <i>i̯</i> > <i>ð</i>”,¹⁰ which clearly occurred prior to apocope upon the basis of MONEDORIGI (CIIC I 413 = CL Gn–12 = CEMIS III MR1) < proto-Brit. */mo.ˈni.jo/- and VEN͡EDOTIS (CIIC I 394 = CL Gn–32) < proto-Brit. */weː.ˈni.jaː/ (cf. §5. Many languages avoid sequences of rhotic¹² – whether denti-alveolar trill [r], flap [ɾ], or approximant [ɹ]; retroflex flap [ɽ] or approximant [ɻ]; or uvular trill [ʀ] or fricative [ʁ]¹³ – plus high front vocoid, in either order, as discussed in detail in Hall & Hamann 2010. In the Brittonic languages, the proto-Brittonic consonant group */rj/ is avoided in post-tonic position, while it is continued unchanged in pre-tonic position.

§6. The gestural positioning of apical rhotics such as Brittonic /r/ = [r̺] is highly constrained, requiring a lowered tongue blade and a lowered and retracted tongue dorsum, thus resulting in a concave shape for the entirety of the tongue (Recasens 1991, 268; Recasens & Pallarès 1999; Solé 2002, 658).

§7. High front vocoids, similarly, require a high degree of articulatory precision. They, unlike /r/, are produced with both the tongue blade and the tongue predorsum raised and fronted, thus resulting in a convex shape for the entirety of the tongue (Recasens 1990; Recasens et al. 1993, 225).

§8. Ladefoged & Maddieson 1996, 221, thus, note that the articulatory gestures required to produce a palatal consonant or secondary palatalisation make it difficult to maintain the conditions necessary for trilling, to which we may add that the situation also applies in the reverse. Recasens & Pallarès 1999, 144, indeed, discussing /r/ to /i/ coärticulation, describe their gestures as “antagonistic” to each other.

§9. Hall & Hamann 2010, 1822 demonstrate that there are three avoidance strategies for the group /Rj/:

(3) a. /Rj/ does not occur, as in most varieties of English.

b. /Rj/ is blocked from being derived,¹⁴ as in German, e.g., the sequence [VCiV] may optionally be realised as [VCjV] as in <i>Familie</i> [fa.ˈmiː.ljə] and <i>Flexion</i> [flɛ.ˈksjoːn] ‘inflexion’, but [j] cannot be derived after /ʁ/ as in <i>Bakterie</i> [pak.ˈteːʁɪ.ə] ‘bacterium’¹⁵ and <i>Ferien</i> [ˈfeː.ʁɪ.ən] ‘holidays’ (Hall & Hamann 2010, 1826 f.).¹⁶

c. /Rj/ is repaired, as in Cypriot Greek, in which /j/ undergoes fortition to [k], e.g., underlying /psarju/ surfaces as [psarku] ‘fish (gen.sg)’ (Hume & Odden 1996, 366 f.).¹⁷

The logical repair strategies that they outline are:

(4) a. The rhotic may be deleted or changed.

c. A vowel may be inserted between the rhotic and high front vocoid.

§10. In proto-Goidelic, *-/C.jV/- usually became -/Ci.jV/- (Pedersen 1909, 68; Thurneysen 1946, 121; but cf. Uhlich 1993), so the resulting -/ri.jV/- did not require repair. Thus proto-Celt. */ˈkʷʰar.jo/- > proto-Goid. */ˈkʷʰa.ri.jo/- > MIr. <i>coire</i> ‘cauldron’. However, with the rise of palatalisation, */rʲ/ developed. According to Hickey 2011, 220 f., palatalisation is retained in the environments in (5) in Modern Irish:¹⁸

(5) a. In syllabic onset preceded by obstruent: <i>príomh-</i> /pʰʲrʲiːv/- [pʰʲɾʲiːv]- ‘chief, major’

But in the environments in (6), /rʲ/ is depalatalised, thus following strategy (4b):

The articulation of /rʲ/ varies across dialects, of course. For example, Ó Cuív 1944, 49 describes the articulation of /rʲ/ as a palatalised voiced post-alveolar fricative – presumably [ʒʲ] – intervocalically, in post-vocalic word-final position, and preceding palatalised bilabial and palato-velar consonants and /h/ in the Irish of West Muskerry (Co. §11. It is strategy (4b) that occurred in proto-Brittonic. The first step is the fortition of */j/ to */ʝ/. Recasens 2013, 14–17 demonstrates that it is more anterior during gliding movement and that it is frequently articulated at the alveolopalatal place, the tongue blade being alveolar and the tongue dorsum palatal. “Thus,” as per Eska 2018/2019 [2020], 23, “it is not particularly surprising to find that /j/ became articulated as the voiced post-alveolar fricative [ʒ] in Argentine Spanish (Colantoni 2006; Kochetov & Colantoni 2011, esp. 329–331). Hence, <i>calle</i> ‘street’ is pronounced as [kaʎe] in Castilian Spanish and [kaje] in Andalusian Spanish, but as [kaʝe]/[kaʤe]/[kaʒe]/[kaʃe] in Argentine Spanish (Ohala & Solé 2010, 59 f.)” (cf. also Ohala 1983, 204).

§12. In Eska 2018/2019 [2020], 21–24, I propose that the very rare sound change */j/ > /ð/ (in the environment ˈ{e i}._V) in proto-Brittonic is the result

 of post-alveolar fronting, a developmental childhood speech sound disorder whereby a post-alveolar phone(me) in syllable-initial or syllable-final position preserves its manner and voicing, but is articulated at a more anterior place. Thus, after */j/ > *[ʝ] > *[ʒ] in proto-Brittonic, *[ʒ] > /ð/ via post-alveolar fronting, but, instead of being naturally corrected in childhood, persisted and became intractable in the speech of one or more elite members of society and spread through his/her social group as a prestige variant which was subsequently adopted throughout the speech community.

§13. I posit, then, that after */ʝ/ > */ʒ/ following /r/ in post-tonic position, it became /ð/ in proto-Brittonic via post-alveolar fronting, and, as Jackson surmised, was “connected and co-eval” with the change of */j/ > /ð/ / ˈ{e i}._V, likewise in post-tonic position.

§14. We may turn now to the double treatment of */j/ / ˈVr_V in proto-Brittonic, whereby proto-Brit. */ˈkʰor.jo/- > OW/MW <i>kord</i>, but proto-Brit. */ˈpʰar.jo/- > MW <i>peir</i>. I propose that, in fact, they may both be considered as regular developments. It is crucial that we recognise that “sound change is drawn from a pool of synchronic variation” (Ohala 1989), i.e., that there would have been considerable phonetic variation across the proto-Brittonic speech area from which sound changes would arise at various focal points and then disperse. A sound change could propagate through the entire speech area, e.g., proto-IE */oː/ > proto-Celt. */aː/ in non-final syllables, but could fail to do so, e.g., Vː > V / _[nasal]# spread through the most of the proto-Celtic speech area, thus yielding gen.pl. <i>-on</i> -/on/ in, e.g., Transalp. Celt. <i>neđđamon</i> ‘of neighbours’ (RIG L–50), but the change ceased to propagate before it reached the portion of the proto-Celtic speech area that broke off to become Hispano-Celtic. Thus, one finds gen.pl. -um -/uːm/ in Celtib. aPuloKum ‘of the *Apulokʰī’ (e.g., MLH K.16.1 = Hesp. IB.01.01), which evinces the change of proto-IE */oː/ > proto-Celt. */uː/ / _C₀# (Eska 2006).

§20. I propose that *-/ntʰL/- > -/θL/- occurred in two stages. The first is that the feature [continuant] spread from the liquid to the preceding aspirated plosive, thus:

Russell 1984 posits a similar leftward spreading of the feature [continuant] in his discussion of the variation in Welsh forms such as <i>anadl</i> ‘breath’ beside <i>anaddl</i> and <i>gwadn</i> ‘sole of the foot; boot; sock’ beside <i>gwaddn</i>. He proposes that */tl tn/ *[t̬l t̬n] remained unchanged in intervocalic position – hence they are heterosyllabic –,²⁷ but that the spreading obtained in word-final position – hence they are tautosyllabic –, after which levelling in either direction took place. Schrijver 1995, 360–365 argues, however, that the spreading occurred in both tautosyllabic and heterosyllabic groups, after which /ð/ > [t̬] / _{l n} via [continuant]-dissimilation in heterosyllabic groups. I take proto-Brit. *-/ntʰl/- and *-/ntʰr/- to have been syllabified as *-/n.tʰl/- and *-/n.tʰr/- upon the basis of syllabic onsets as attested in MW <i>tlws</i> ‘jewel’ (e.g., HPE 9.30) and OW <i>trum</i> ‘heavy’ gl. ‘abrupta’ (Juv. 44r20), <i>trumm</i> gl. ‘aegrum’ (Juv. 47v13). Both *-/tʰl/- and *-/tʰr/-, thus, are tautosyllabic and after being subject to [continuant]-spreading retained it. The resulting -/nθL/- may be attested by OW <i>cénthlíát</i> gl. ‘Dauida canorum’ (Juv. 26r26)²⁸ (> later <i>ceithliad</i> ‘singer’).²⁹

§21. The loss of /n/ is a matter of interarticulatory timing. Solé 2012, 124 explains that “different outcomes of change may arise from small variations in the magnitude or coordination of consecutive segments”. In the case of nasal + fricative groups, the required respective settings of the velum are antagonistic

<!-- source page 11 -->

 to each other: it must be open to allow airflow through the nasal cavity to enable nasal phonation, while it must be closed to allow the generation of sufficient turbulence to enable frication. As Solé 2007, 47 f. notes, it is common for anticipatory closure of the velum prior to the articulation of the fricative to result in the effacement of the nasal, e.gg., Lat. <i>institutum</i> > Ital. <i>istituto</i> ‘institution’ and proto-Gmc. *<i>fimfe</i> > OE <i>fīf</i> ‘five’.³⁰ Thus, we may conclude that after proto-Brit. *-/ntʰL/- > -/nθL/- in Old Welsh via [continuant]-spreading, -/nθL/- > -/θL/- via nasal effacement owing to articulatory constraints.

## 5. The evolution of */ltʰr/ in Welsh

§22. The consonantal group */ltʰr/ is continued as -/θr/- in intervocalic position in MW <i>athro</i> ‘teacher’ beside OSWBrit. <i>altro</i> (DGVB 59) ‘lord’, OCorn. <i>altrou</i> gl. ‘uictricus’ (sic; VC 81 §140), and MIr. <i>altra</i> ‘foster-father’ < proto-Celt. */altʰro/- ‘nourisher; fosterer’ (cf. OIr. <i>altram</i>(<i>m</i>) ‘act of nourishing, fostering’) + */awon/- ‘maternal uncle’ (cf. MW <i>ewythyr</i> ‘uncle’ and Bret. <i>eontr</i> < */awon-tʰiːr/) (Schumacher apud Stüber 1998, 109 f.). Pedersen 1909, 137 and Jackson 1953, 401 note, however, that one also finds -/ɬt/- in MW <i>alldraỽon</i> (pl.) ‘godfather; sponsor’,³¹ which is sparsely attested in Middle Welsh (but a single medieval token is listed in GPC: LlA 158.15 [1346]). The feminine form <i>elltrewyn</i> ‘stepmother; mother-in-law’ is, likewise, sparsely attested in Middle Welsh (GPC, again, lists but a single medieval token: DMh17 3.48), to which compare OSWBrit. <i>eltroguen</i> gl. ‘nouerca’ (DGVB 157) and OCorn. <i>altruan</i> gl. ‘nouerca’ (VC 81 §141). P s.v. <i>elltrewen</i> in 1793 glosses the form as ‘tutoress; a sponsor; a gossip, a stepmother’. It appears, then, that we are dealing with a lexical split. In word-final position, */ltʰr/ is continued as -/ɬtr/ in OW <i>cultir</i> gl. ‘cultrum’ (Ox² 4.31), MW <i>cỽlltẏr</i> (e.g., WML 108.2),³² and MBret. <i>coultr</i> ‘coulter’ ⇐ Lat. <i>cultrum</i>.

d. Isaac 2004, 67 states that *-/ltʰr/- > *-/lttʰr/-.³³

Aside from Lewis’ 1943, 22 stipulation that Brit. Lat. /ltʰr/ > W -/ɬtr/ in final position, I am unaware of any previous commentary on this sound change.

§24. As noted in §19, Geminate Inalterability does not allow for geminate plosives to change directly to fricatives, so Morris-Jones’ and Isaac’s views cannot be correct, while Jackson and McCone assume deletion of the *-/l/-, but without providing motivation.

§25. It is pertinent to examine the treatment of the group */ltʰ/ in Welsh. In intervocalic position, it evolves to -/ɬ/-, while in final position, it first became -/lθ/, as demonstrated by Schumacher 2019, by spreading of the feature [continuant] from */l/ to */tʰ/, leading to the frication of /tʰ/ to /θ/, as in *-/lkʰ/ > -/lx/ in <i>balch</i> ‘proud’ < */palkʰ/.³⁴ I have argued that -/lθ/ > -/ɬt/ by metathesis of the feature [spread glottis], whereby it spreads from /θ/ to /l/, after which it is delinked from /θ/ (Eska 2019), thus:

(12)

```text
l     θ
└──→ [sg]
```

This results in */θ/ being occluded to /t/, and devoices the */l/. The increased airflow through the open glottis during the articulation of */l/ leads to turbulence, which results in /ɬ/ (Ohala 2005, 29; Ohala & Solé 2010, 43). This explanation also applies to the treatment of *-/ltʰr/ in final position in OW <i>cultir</i> / MW <i>cỽlltẏr</i>.

§26. Upon the basis of MW <i>porffor</i> ‘purple’ ⇐ Lat. <i>purpura</i> and <i>kylchet</i> ‘blanket’ ⇐ Lat. <i>culcita</i>, it is clear that intervocalic heterosyllabic *-/l.t/- would have become *-/l.θ/- in Welsh. In view of the treatment of /lθ/ in syllable-final position as described in §25, I propose that the feature [spread glottis] then spread from /θ/ to /l/, but did not delink from /θ/ across the syllable boundary, thus yielding *-/ɬ.θ/-:


<!-- source page 13 -->

(13)

```text
l     θ
└──→ [sg]
```

But *-/ɬ.θ/-, as a sequence of two voiceless fricatives – a disfavoured sequence not otherwise known in Welsh –, then dissimilated by deletion of */θ/³⁵ so as to result in attested intervocalic -/ɬ/-.³⁶

§27. In the case of the form <i>athro</i>, I propose that *-/l.tʰr/- first changed to *-/l.θr/- by spreading of the feature [continuant] from */r/ to tautosyllabic */tʰ/ as described in §20.³⁷ The feature [spread glottis] then spread from */θ/ to heterosyllabic */l/ as described in §26, after which the resulting disfavoured intervocalic consonant group *-/ɬ.θr/- dissimilated to attested -/θr/- via deletion of /ɬ/.³⁸

§28. We now turn to the forms <i>alldraỽon</i> and <i>elltrewyn</i>, which appear to have the treatment of proto-Brittonic */ltʰr/ as attested in tautosyllabic final position in OW <i>cultir</i> / MW <i>cỽlltẏr</i>.³⁹ We may note that proto-Brittonic */ltʰr/ is continued unchanged in Southwestern Brittonic, so it may be that it was initially likewise continued unaltered in southeastern Western Brittonic, which likely was a transition zone with Southwestern Brittonic.⁴⁰ Under this view, */ltʰr/ subsequently underwent metathesis of the feature [spread glottis] as described in §25 so as to yield attested -/ɬtr/-. Alternatively, these forms could possibly have been borrowed from the plural of OCorn. <i>altrou</i> and <i>altruan</i>,


<!-- source page 14 -->

respectively – in which case the substitution of W -/ɬ/- for Corn. -/l/- / _t is required. That these etyma underwent a lexical split between proto-Western Brittonic and proto-Southwestern Brittonic perhaps suggests that the latter possibility may be more likely, though the paucity of evidence hardly allows a definitive analysis.
