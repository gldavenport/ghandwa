## Abstract

The inscribed gold fibula of Praeneste, uncovered 35 kms. to the South-East of Rome, is held to contain the oldest extant inscription conducted in the Latin language and in an archaic version of the Latin alphabet. After a long debate regarding its authenticity, partly due to the fact that it was devoid of an archaeological context, it has been declared authentic «beyond any reasonable doubt» in 2011. Still, in spite of its brevity, a substantial number of questions remains unanswered concerning its internal structure, meaning and purpose, as well as the morphology of the verb form vhevhaked. They have been repeatedly wielded as arguments against its authenticity, but have been unduly forgotten ever since the fibula was cleared of any suspicion of being a forgery. This work addresses these questions and tries to solve the problems by means of an in-depth study of the morphology of the four words it contains, their syntax and the circumstances of its making, and additionally reconsiders its dialectal filiation. The fibula was presented to the public by Wolfgang Helbig on January 7th 1887, immediately raising doubts as to its authenticity, since its origin was shrouded in mystery and it lacked the preceptive archaeological context. It would be idle to review all the literature devoted to the increasingly acrimonious discussion on its authenticity, exacerbated beyond the limits of academic procedure by the relentless and basically iniquitous attacks of Margherita Guarducci. Suffice it to say that all suspicions have been cleared in the last decade on independent scientific grounds, regarding both the object and the estimated age of the engraving,1 to the dismay of a number of epigraphists and to the joy of many, but crucially not all linguists. In point of fact, there are too many things that Helbig could not even have guessed at the time in which he was allegedly busy forging what was going to become our oldest Latin text, including such writing particularities as the syllabic interpunctions and the Etruscan use of the digraph <vh> for /f/ (Duenos reads <feced>; see Wachter 987, 57; the use of <vh> in Etruscan was confirmed in 1888). The interested reader can find an exhaustive historical and linguistic account in P. Poccetti (2011-14). Notwithstanding the final solution of the prolonged «affair of the fibula», some unsettled issues, mostly involving text pragmatics and verb morphology, still deserve attention. What follows shall consequently not focus on those linguistic or epigraphic aspects that contribute to the corroboration of the authenticity of the text of the fibula, but on those that have been quietly swept under the rug in the last decades.

2. The text architecture of the fibula: only four letters and yet such an intriguing message Apparently, this short text conveys no mystery. The fibula is a talking object, and med is the accusative singular of the Latin personal pronoun egō, attested in other archaic inscriptions and considered as the immediate predecessor of classical Latin mē. manios is a personal name, the subject of the sentence in the nominative singular, and nvmasioi is another personal name in the dative singular, the beneficiary of the action expressed by the perfect form vhevhaked ‘made’ corresponding to the present *făk-i̯e/o-. The nature of the action involved remains slightly inconcrete, anyhow: it could be the case that vhevhaked designates the very process of manufacturing the fibula (in which case Numasios is the commissioner/purchaser and/or final destinatary), or the more complex and several-staged decision and act of having it made and paying for it in order to offer it as a present (and then Manios is the commissioner/purchaser, and Numasios the final destinatary of the luxurious present). Most of what follows revolves around this problem, and, in contrast to former scholarship, I think it leads us to a dead end. 3. A different history As implied above, several accounts seem to take it for granted that the reduplicated perfect is inherited from Indo-European. This is both unwarranted and problematic, as we are going to see in what follows. Under the alternative assumption that this perfect is innovatory from the point of view of Proto-IndoEuropean, but inherited in the separate Italic languages, it must have been created ex novo in Proto-Italic in analogy to the aorist exactly when the ex hypothesi more complex inherited system was undergoing extreme reduction. The emergent, tense oriented system did not need two coexistent past forms anymore, and a fortiori could not bring back the moribund distinction anywhere without literally swimming against the tide. The perfect must additionally have existed long enough for the zero grade of the root to spread to the singular forms. And yet, it incomprehensibly failed to replace the original aorist, which somehow remained available as a byform in exactly the same constructions, at least in Latin. In addition, the perfect must have been eventually ousted by the remnants of the alternating aorist *fēk-/*făk- in Latino-Faliscan and Umbrian (and Venetic, whose remodeled preterite vha.g.s.to can only go back to an aorist form *făk-). This reasoning is extremely convoluted and unrealistic. But a third scenario would have to assume an even later innovation, by which reduplicated forms could have been independently created in both dialects only to be immediately discarded in Latin. This is not borne out, either, as pointed out by E. P. Hamp (1981), who claimed the fibula to be a hoax for this and other (less compelling) reasons. It strains imagination that Latin created *fe-făk-e- with no recognisable function, at a time in which most verbs had already selected a single preterite, and then toyed with it for a short while before it receded from use and the inherited aorist gained ground again. Indo-European never had a 3rd p. sg. perfect **dhe-dheh1-k-e, which would present a number of problems besides its deviant root vocalism. Besides the existence of Gk. τέθηκε, which is a monoglottic innovation, the main support for the reconstruction of such a form seems to be J. Untermann’s (1993) revered statement that -k- has to be analysed as a root enlargement and not as an aorist formant. Unfortunately, given that it is only found in root aorists in the rest of indo-European, this can hardly be called an argument. To this, Maras (fn. 18) responds that the remaining space could have been left vacant until the moment in which the fibula definitively went out of circulation, the text was rounded out with the destinatary’s name and the object came to form part of the grave goods. But the idea that the needs associated with the final destination of the object had been foreseen in this way is highly artificial, and it remains unclear why the other side of the fibula could not have been put into service, if needed, to host the destinatary’s name nvmasioi. In addition, even if there was little room available on the reverse side of the fibula, this alternative disposition of the text would have represented an advantage over the actually attested one, since the text would have been interpreted as consisting of two separate parts: the artisan’s signature and the destinatary. The postulation of this complex, two-staged process for the incision of a short text on a small sumptuary item and its alleged circulation in the meantime constitute an ingenious escape route, designed to come to terms with the fact that the text as a whole lacks strictly identical parallels, as we have seen above. In sum, the idea that the imaginative scribe at first came out of the mess by trying to recover vheked in a rather unaesthetic way, but some time later, when confronted with the problem again, changed his mind and resigned himself to glossing it over as vhevhaked, clashes with the fact that this presupposes two synonymous and strictly synchronous preterites (or three, if we reckon with vhaked as Maras does), which are mutually irreducible in historical terms and incompatible in synchronic terms (as opposed to, e. g., tulī and its immediate ancestor tetulī). In sum, it is hardly worth the trouble to go to such argumentative lengths only to end up with a patron who was himself a donor, a reduplicated form vhevhaked that came out of the blue after two consecutive corrections, and a recipient of the gift that cannot have been included in the text from the beginning if the four words have to make sense. 6. «Praenestine» and the fibula The uncertain feeling that something is still unresolved about this text has occasionally led scholars to compromise solutions regarding the language spoken by the author: for instance, G. Devoto notably affirmed in a number of works (the last one being 1974, 77) that only manios med was Latin. The rest was to his mind closer to Oscan, since vhevhaked was an old Indo-European perfect attested in Oscan but not in Latin, and the personal name nvmasioi had a dative ending -oi̯ different from L. -ō. Also G. Meiser (2003, 179, fn.) has invoked this possibility. However, this dative ending, synchronically -oi̯ or -ōi̯, is not necessarily Oscan, since it is also attested in the Duenos inscription. Furthermore, it is difficult to see how such a mischsprachlicher text could have come into being at such an early date. LG, 6 clinched the (undiscussed) matter with a forthright «nicht Oskisch», and in recent years the «Oscan connection» has been systematically sidestepped. P. Poccetti (2006a, 105) has put forward the more nuanced hypothesis that Manios and Numasios, respectively donor and recipient, may have spoken different languages: the scribe, additionally, spoke the «urban» variety and naturally tended to write feced, whereupon he immediately corrected himself. Poccetti alternatively allows for the possibility that the «erroneous» letter underlying <a> was not <e>, but <i>: the scribe was of Sabellic or Faliscan ancestry and inadvertently wrote a form similar to Pre-Samnite fεfικεδ or Faliscan fifiked. As A. Franchi de Bellis (2012, 142) has contended, the scribe, who may even have been illiterate himself, was not extemporising or just listening to instructions when he had such an expensive object in his hands. There had to be a previous available model, which was correctly spelt. In her words, the text «non registrava ‘interferenze’ o competenze linguistiche multipli, bensì era scritto correttamente nella lingua parlata dal committente o dall’autore dell’opera». Consequently, the dittography can only be explained as a perseverative error: the scribe unintentionally repeated <e> in the second syllable, which he, on second thoughts, corrected as <a>.

It could consequently be the case that the fibula was inscribed in a Latin-speaking area, and that the scribe found vhevhaked unfamiliar enough to trigger the misspelling. When he was copying the text, which probably included the syllabic interpunction, he unconsciously detached the reduplication <vhe> from the following sequence as if the latter were a different form, which he «normalised» as <fheked>. At all events, the question of the dialectal filiation of vhevhaked remains unanswered. Finally, vhevhaked has been held to be a local form, in turn possibly borrowed from Oscan (C. Trümpy 1983, 70), or simply an archaic «Praenestine» dialectal form, ousted in «Urban» Latin by the equally inherited *fēked (cf. E. Campanile 2008; C. De Simone 2006, 173; G. Bakkum 2009, 164-166; F. Klug 2017, 62, etc.). In the same vein, W. P. Nevertheless, given our limited acquaintance with «Praenestine» as a nonRoman variety of Latin, one can hardly resort to the expedient of ascribing each preterite to a different Latin dialect without incurring a subtle terminological fallacy that saves the label «Latin» at the cost of divesting it of any content. The abusive use of the label «Praenestine Latin» is especially conspicuous if we take into account that the archaic form fecid (CIL i, 561, 4th c. BC) is attested in the same area. While some scholars will predictably clutch to the idea that fecid must be Roman Latin, as opposed to the pristine, peripherally attested vhevhaked, this adds up to circular reasoning. Still another analysis The stubborn attribution of the fibula to a primitive stage of the Latin language was inaugurated by Helbig in his letter to Theodor Mommsen on January 1st 1887, in which he sought advice one week before he presented the fibula to the public. It was immediately sanctified by Mommsen in person, who, on January 23th, wrote to his son-in-law, Ulrich von Wilamowitz-Möllendorff: «Hast Du die neue pränest(inische) Inschrift gesehen, die ich beschreibe? Das ist uraltes Latein, und glücklicherweise verständlich».1 While 144 years ago this was quite understandable, nowadays it constitutes an exaggeration of the gap-filling methodology that treats every unexpected trait of word-formation as a welcome missing piece in a lost system. In the case of *dheh1-, the resulting «diasystem» demands the reconstruction of a richer morphology than that of any other verb. This has been justified with the impressionistic argument that the disiecta membra of the Proto-Italic paradigm have been reshuffled and variously integrated in the verbal system of the Italic dialects, irrespective of the conceivable evolution that led to the elimination of some forms and the survival of others, and of the internal logic of the resulting paradigms.
