# Linnaean linguistics

## 'Bear', 'horse', 'wolf' and the Indo-European phylogeny from a zoographical perspective

**Stefan Höfler**  
Austrian Academy of Sciences & University of Vienna

## Abstract

Taking inspiration from the biological taxonomy of mammals, this paper explores the diversification of the Indo-European language family through a zoographical lens. It investigates shared innovations of phonology, morphology, and semantics in zoonyms across language branches. The aim is to uncover evidence for early splits within the family tree. The study primarily centers on Anatolian versus Core Indo-European and features an extensive discussion of *h₂ŕ̥tk̑o- 'bear' (Hittite ḫartakka-) vs. *h₂ŕ̥k̑þo- (Vedic ŕ̥kṣa-, Greek ἄρκτος, Latin ursus, etc.), *h₁ék̑u- 'horse' (Hittite */ekkus/, Luwian /azzu-/) vs. *h₁ék̑u̯o- (Vedic áśva-, Latin equus, Tocharian B yakwe, etc.), and *u̯ĺ̥kwo- 'lion' (Luwian walwa/i-) vs. *u̯ĺ̥kwo- 'wolf' (Vedic vŕ̥ka-, Lithuanian vil̃kas, Tocharian B walkwe, etc.). Thorough analysis of these examples will determine their relevance within the proposed scenarios.

## 1. Introduction

The way individual members of a language family are distributed on a phylogenetic tree is reminiscent of models that have been in use in biology for over 200 years.[^1] The hierarchy of parent languages, branches, sub-branches, and individual idioms can be compared to the system of taxonomy established by the Swedish botanist, zoologist, and physician Carl Linnaeus (1707–1778).[^2] The greatest change to this system was the widespread acceptance of evolution as the driving force behind biological diversity subsequent to Charles Darwin's publication of *On the origin of species* in 1859.

Without taking the analogy too far, a simple comparison of a traditional phylogenetic tree of the class Mammalia with a tree of the Indo-European language family shows superficial similarities in how orders and subfamilies branch off and bifurcate. The ramification of the mammal tree depends on the evaluation and integration of shared innovations and retained archaisms. In linguistics, too, the combination of shared innovations and retained archaisms plays a vital role in establishing a family tree.

For linguistic subgrouping, shared innovations are the most significant factor. Only a non-trivial change of a feature x to y, shared by a group of branches and not explicable as a parallel development, can be used for ascertaining a closer genealogical relationship. The evidence can come from phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, and the lexicon.

Evidence from phonology is much more useful than from semantics or lexicon, especially when it pertains to typologically rare sound changes. Morphological innovations are generally considered the best indicator for genetic subgrouping, as changes in this component are typically least predictable.[^11]

The goal of this contribution is to establish whether animal names furnish evidence to corroborate an early split-off hypothesis of the Anatolian branch. Terminology follows Olander (2019): "Proto-Indo-European" (PIE), "Proto-Indo-Tocharian" (PIT, after split-off of Anatolian), and "Proto-Indo-Celtic" (PIC, after split-off of Tocharian).

## 2. Phonology – the word for 'bear'

The word for 'bear' is attested (directly or indirectly) in almost every branch except Germanic, Slavic, and (presumably) Tocharian: Hitt. ḫartakka-, Ved. ṛ́kṣa-, YAv. arša-, Pers. xirs, Gk. ἄρκτος (f.), Lat. ursus, OIr. art, Welsh arth, Arm. arǰ, OAlb. ar; Lith. irštvà f. 'bear's den' (< *(*)iršas).[^17]

Prior to Hittite, 'thorn clusters' (a cluster of a velar plus dental fricative *þ) were reconstructed: *ŕ̥k̑þo- (IEW: 875; laryngealistic *h₂ŕ̥k̑þo-). The corresponding 'carpenter' root was *tek̑þ-.

The discovery of Anatolian challenged this view. The Hittite word for 'earth', nom. sg. tēkan, gen. taknaš, implies a revised reconstruction of the 'earth' word as *dhég̑hōm, *dhg̑hm-é/ós — dental before velar, not after. Tocharian A tkaṃ, B keṃ 'earth' likewise shows TK order. This suggests that thorn clusters began as *-TK- sequences, preserved in Anatolian and Tocharian, but metathesized to *-KÞ- in the remaining branches.[^21]

The more economical hypothesis: thorn metathesis is a shared innovation of Proto-Indo-Celtic, after Anatolian and Tocharian split off.[^22] The PIE word for 'bear' is therefore *h₂ŕ̥tk̑o- (Hitt. ḫartakka- /ḫartka-/), which metathesized to *h₂ŕ̥k̑þo- in PIC (Ved. ṛ́kṣa-, Gk. ἄρκτος, Lat. ursus, etc.).[^23]

The exact phonetic reality behind *-KÞ- is debated. Recent suggestions identify the second element as a dental affricate (Melchert 2003; Byrd 2015: 24) or a palatalized dental (Jasanoff 2018). Whatever it was, the metathesis affected all tautosyllabic *-TK- clusters throughout the language system and is not trivial — it can be used as evidence for phylogenetic subgrouping, drawing a line between Anatolian/Tocharian and the remaining ten branches.

## 3. Morphology – the word for 'horse'

The word for 'horse' is attested in every branch except Slavic. All forms trace to *h₁ék̑u- or *h₁ék̑u̯o-. The u-stem *h₁ék̑u- appears limited to Anatolian (Hitt. *ekku- [from phonetic complements on ANŠE.KUR.RA], CLuw. *azzu-, HLuw. á-zú-); all other languages require or are compatible with thematic masculine *h₁ék̑u̯o-.

Kloekhorst (2008: 10) argued that no phonological development converts *h₁eḱu̯o- > PAnat. *h₁eḱu-, and therefore the u-stem is original, with thematization as a PIT shared innovation.

The paper argues against this. Thematization of u-stems to *-u̯-o- is not attested as a large-scale process anywhere in PIE or PIT. The PIT thematization would require that speakers still analysed the fully lexicalized word for 'horse' as transparently derived from an abstract 'swiftness', then re-derived a new word by a different morphological process — a scenario with multiple costly assumptions.

The more convincing alternative: PIE had thematic *h₁ék̑u̯o-; Anatolian inherited it and reanalysed it as a u-stem in Hittite and Luwian, through phonological overlap of case endings (*-u- and *-wa- stems share all endings except nom. and acc. sg.) and the attested sporadic syncope of *-(u)wa- → *-u- in both languages. Lycian esb(e/i)-, potentially a further Anatolian cognate, may also continue a thematic stem (Sasseville 2017; Schürr 2019).

Conclusion: the 'horse' word does not demonstrate an early split-off of Anatolian.

## 4. Semantics and Lexicon – the word for 'wolf'

*u̯ĺ̥kwo- 'wolf' is represented across most branches (Ved. vṛ́ka-, Gk. λύκος, Lat. lupus, PGmc. *wulfaz, Toch. B walkwe, etc.) but is absent from Anatolian in the meaning 'wolf'. CLuw. walwa(/i)- 'lion' has been equated with it (Lehrman 1978; Wallace 1986), with the claim that PIE *u̯ĺ̥kwo- originally meant 'lion' and that PIT shifted the meaning to 'wolf'.

The paper argues that the formal identification of CLuw. walwa(/i)- with *u̯ĺ̥kwo- is phonologically problematic: *-kw- > Luw. -w- is unexpected in this position. However, Hitt. tarku-, CLuw. taru- 'dance' (< *terkw-) and Hitt. šākuwa-, CLuw. tāwa/i- 'eye' (< *sókwo-) provide examples of *-kw- > PAnat. *-gw- > Luw. -w- in medial position. PAnat. *walkwa-, *walgwa- 'lion' therefore appears unavoidable.

Nevertheless, the semantic change 'wolf' > 'lion' or 'lion' > 'wolf' is unmotivated in either direction. The preferred analysis: *u̯ĺ̥kwo- is a substantivized adjective 'the dangerous one' (< *u̯l̥kwó- 'dangerous', cf. OIr. olc 'evil', Ved. a-vṛká- 'safe'); PAnat. *walkwa-, *walgwa- 'lion' is independently a substantivization of *u̯olkw-o- 'dangerous/monstrous' (o-grade of the same root). The two formations are parallel, not identical in origin.

Anatolian's word for 'wolf' is ulip(a)n(a)- (= UR.BAR.RA-n(a)-; cf. CLuw. walipna/i-), which is reminiscent of words for 'fox' and 'wildcat' in other branches (Lat. uolpēs, Av. urupi-, Lith. vilpišỹs), derived from PIE *u̯l̥p-. It is possible, though not conclusively demonstrated, that Anatolian preserves an older word for 'wolf' which was replaced by the taboo formation *u̯ĺ̥kwo- in PIT after the Anatolian split.

## 5. Conclusion

- **Bear:** The thorn metathesis (*h₂ŕ̥tk̑o- > *h₂ŕ̥k̑þo-) is a valid shared innovation of PIC, consistent with early split-off of both Anatolian and Tocharian.
- **Horse:** The Anatolian u-stem is secondary; the 'horse' word does not support Anatolian early split-off via morphological innovation.
- **Wolf:** The semantic and lexical evidence is inconclusive for subgrouping purposes. Anatolian and PIT each have independent formations from the same root for 'dangerous large predator', applied to different animals.
