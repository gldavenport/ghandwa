# Chapter 2. The Syllabification of Medial Consonant Clusters in Vedic

## 2.0 Introduction
In this chapter we begin our exploration of medial consonant syllabification in the ancient Indo-European languages, with an extended consideration of the phenomenon in Vedic Sanskrit. Various scholars have explored syllabification in Vedic—including the Sanskrit grammarians themselves—and as is to be expected, the resulting accounts agree on some points but differ on others. In the word-internal domain, a single intervocalic consonant, for instance, is uncontroversially associated with the syllable headed by the following vowel, functioning as its onset.1 But regarding sequences of consonants, for which the possibilities for syllabification understandably increase, there has been less of a consensus as to how they are treated in the language. The main goal of this chapter, then, is to arrive at a comprehensive understanding of medial consonant syllabification in Vedic. We begin in 2.1 with a focus on VCCV sequences, surveying the evidence for the two positions on their treatment, V.CCV versus VC.CV, and arguing in favor of the latter. We then move on to larger VCCCV sequences in 2.2, and show how their attested distribution can be used as an indication of how they are syllabified. Our findings in these first two sections will inform our approach to the phenomenon we explore in depth in 2.3, the Vedic perfect union vowel, which, while constituting an exception within the larger system, nevertheless offers support for the view of syllabification we maintain. Finally, we conclude in 2.4 with a review of our claims.
