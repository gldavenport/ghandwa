## 8.3 Generating Medial Consonant Heterosyllabification
Having successfully incorporated into the Optimality-Theoretic analysis a means of selecting both*k̑u̯n̥bhis over †k̑unbhis and *peru̯r̥ over †perur, we next turn to the issue of obtaining in the same account the heterosyllabification of medial consonants. As argued in the first part of this book, there is convincing evidence in ancient Indo-European languages for the heterosyllabic treatment of medial consonants, and in particular, for the patterns we observe in (72): (72) Patterns of Medial Consonant Syllabification in Ancient Indo-European Languages a. VC.CV b. VRO.OV c. VO.ORV d. ### 8.3.1 A Positional Markedness Approach to Medial Consonant Heterosyllabification
Positional Markedness (Zoll 1998) characterizes a situation in which marked
structures are permitted only in prominent positions, and disfavored elsewhere. In other words, it allows for prominent positions in the word to abide by
markedness restrictions less strictly than non-prominent positions. Positional
Markedness is crucially to be differentiated from the earlier proposed and
related notion of Positional Faithfulness (Beckman 1999), which holds that
underlying structure is most faithfully realized in prominent positions.43 As
an example to differentiate these two notions, Zoll presents the case of vowel
length in the Australian language Guugu Yimidhirr. In this language long
vowels are licit only in the first two syllables of a word, whether this length
is underlying, as in (78), or derived through morphological concatenation, a
process which may trigger lengthening in the final syllable of a base, as in (79).

The relationship between Sonority-Sequencing and *ComplexCoda is crucial: if the constraints were reversed, we would instead predict the syllabification †V.ROOV, since, with the candidates in (93c.–d.) eliminated due to their complex codas, the evaluation would come down to the Alignment constraint. We see, then, that the proposed syllabifications of triconsonantal sequences in Proto-Indo-European, extended from the situations in Vedic and Greek, find relatively straightforward treatment in the Optimality-Theoretic account we have been constructing. Indeed, of the adjustments we were compelled to make so as to satisfactorily produce VO.ORV and VR.ORV, the introduction of the constraint Sonority-Sequencing into the ranking will actually find independent motivation, as we proceed in the next section to deploy the analysis to account for a wide-ranging array of sonorant-featuring forms. As for the introduction of *ComplexCoda, at this point it is really justified only in so far as we find the syllable-based analysis of Greek palatalization convincing as an indication of the treatment of VRORV in Proto-Indo-European; but we note that if it does not, the Optimality-Theoretic analysis can be easily adjusted back to generate VRO.RV, by simply removing this constraint from the ranking.
