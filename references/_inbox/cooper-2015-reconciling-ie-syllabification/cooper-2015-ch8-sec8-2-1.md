### 8.2.1 Problematic Identifications of C
#### 8.2.1.1 The (Persistent) Problem with NoCoda
Simply put, C prefers a right-oriented syllable nucleus over a left-oriented one: the first sonorant is more preferred as (part of) an onset, than the second sonorant is as (part of) a coda. The most obvious candidate for the constraint C, then, is NoCoda, but a restriction on coda consonants is no less problematic here than it was in the case of the previous Optimality-Theoretic accounts of Kobayashi (2004) and Keydana (2008 [2010]). Returning to the form /per-u̯r/, which we highlighted the undesirable treatment of in each of these two earlier frameworks, we find that we obtain similar results if we evaluate this input according to the ranking given in (15) above, replacing C with NoCoda: (16) *per-u̯r̥ > Gk. πεῖραρ nom. acc. sg. ‘end, boundary’ /per-u̯r/ *Pk/Obs *Mar/V Dep-IO NoCoda *Pk/N *Pk/L *Pk/G L a. per.u̯r̥ * *! #### 8.2.1.2 The Problem with Minimized Consonant Moraicity
We next consider a second possibility for the identification of the proposed constraint C, one involving restrictions on the assignment of syllable weight. While this alternative will ultimately prove untenable given the workings of

Optimality Theory, nonetheless it provides a useful point of comparison before we move on to our favored solution in 8.2.2. Keeping in mind the problematic tableau for *peru̯r̥ given above in (16), which, again, shows the inability of the constraint ranking to converge on a single output candidate before reaching the Peak constraints intended to be inactive in Proto-Indo-European, a revision to the analysis incorporating observations about syllable weight, such that the desired winner in (16a.), *per. u̯r̥, can actually be selected, proceeds as follows. We first recognize that a locus of distinction between the candidates *per.u̯r̥ and †pe.rur in (16a.) and (16b.) lies in the shape of their respective second syllables, either .RR̥. or .CR̥R. These syllable shapes should be crucially different with respect to weight; according to the understanding of Proto-Indo-European syllable structure one can reconstruct on the basis of e.g. metrical practice in Vedic (see chapter 2), the former is light, while the latter is heavy, as the coda R should be moraic. We might posit that the language disfavors candidate (16b.) precisely because of the bimoraicity of its second syllable. Crucially, though, we cannot straightforwardly enforce this preference by introducing a constraint such as *2μ, which would ban bimoraic syllables, in view of the fact that bimoraic syllables do not seem to be restricted generally in the language (it is after all a quantitysensitive system), and more specifically, that this constraint would also presumably be violated by candidate (16a.), the initial syllable of which has a coda consonant, and is thus heavy too. Rather, we propose to capture the disfavored status of the syllable .CR̥R. as a consequence of its having two consonantal moras, as opposed to the single consonantal mora of syllable .RR̥. in (16a.). To properly formalize this distinction, we must first introduce a constraint ranking generating moraic coda segments, so that we can actually differentiate light .RR̥μ. from heavy .CR̥μRμ. Specifically, we rank the constraint *Appendix (1d.), which disfavors postvocalic segments from being affixed directly to the syllable node without an intervening mora (the presence or absence of onsets notwithstanding)—i.e., from functioning as syllable appendices, as shown in (17), over *μ/Consonant (1e.), which disfavors consonants from contributing to weight, as in the structure given in (18).12 12 Cf. the account of Vedic in Chapter 3, where we assumed this ranking to hold for the language, but given the focus of the analysis, did not explicitly integrate it into the overall constraint hierarchy. Yet as *μ/Consonant cannot be easily reimagined as an Alignment constraint (unlike NoCoda, which as we have seen Kobayashi in his analysis of sonorant vocalization deployed in the form of Align-R(Nuc, σ)),14 the very definition of which concerns assessment of each and every member of a stated category, it is difficult to conceive of a viable way of accomplishing this. To be sure, constraints may be relativized to certain types of syllables (initial, stressed, etc.), or, for that matter, other types of phonological units (place features, manner features, etc.), but so far as we can tell, in such cases no formal distinction is made between the violations incurred by one relevant unit as opposed to another—violations are considered cumulatively. Indeed the cumulativity of constraint violation evaluation in general means that, even if we could reformulate *μ/Consonant as an Alignment constraint, it would not be possible under the current conception of Optimality Theory to truly discern and appreciate violations in any non-global way. Assuming for the moment, however, that a syllable-by-syllable identification of violations, as in the tableau in (21) above, were not merely a convenient representation, but rather possessed some formal substance that the system could be sensitive to, we come to the next issue with instantiating the approach in Optimality Theory. The grammar, specifically the EVAL component, requires the capability to appreciate and compare different violation profiles for a given constraint. Here, it would need to distinguish between an output candidate with a violation profile of <**> for the constraint *μ/Consonant (*pe.rur), versus one with a profile of <*,*> (*per.u̯r̥), and judge in favor of the latter. In other words, the system needs to be sensitive not just to total violations of a given constraint by each output candidate—and it needs to be sensitive to this, because if there were a candidate with only one violation of *μ/Consonant, all else being equal we expect it should be selected as most optimal in comparison to both *pe.rur and *per.u̯r̥—but also, for any given subset of candidates sharing the same number of violations, to how those violations are arrayed across the units assessed (here, syllables).
