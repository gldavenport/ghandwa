## 8.4 Deploying the Analysis
At this point we have built up an Optimality-Theoretic analysis of Proto-Indo-European nucleus selection and sonorant vocalization that 1) appropriately restricts the property of syllabicity (through introduction into the constraint ranking of members of the fixed subhierarchies of Peak and Margin constraints); 2) is capable of generating right-hand vocalization of sonorants (through introduction of mora-based Alignment, a variation on Mester and Padgett’s [1994] constraint-based encoding of directionality in prosodic structure assignment); and 3) is compatible with medial consonant heterosyllabification (through introduction of the constraint Coincide, which disfavors non-initial complex onsets). The active constraint ranking we have arrived at over the course of the discussion in 8.1–3 is given below in (95). (95) Sonority-Sequencing, *Pk/Obstruent, *Mar/Vowel » *ComplexCoda, Dep-IO » Coincide » Align-L(μ, PrWd), Onset » *Pk/Nasal » *Pk/Liquid » *Pk/Glide Additionally, though the ranking *Appendix » *μ/Consonant has not been explicitly integrated into the overall hierarchy, nevertheless we do continue to

<!-- pdf-page: 253; source-page: 238 -->
assume an active role for it in the Proto-Indo-European system, in that it compels the moraicity of coda consonants. Relying on the constraint ranking in (95), we will now seek to exhaustively account for the behavior of sonorants in all of the environments in which they may occur. We will begin in 8.4.1 with environments containing a single sonorant, then proceed to those containing two sonorants in 8.4.2 and three sonorants in 8.4.3. In general we will abstract away from consideration of output candidates violating high-ranking *Pk/Obstruent and *Mar/Vowel, since obstruents are never syllabic and vowels are never marginal; in some cases, though—specifically the tableaux in (99a.) and (108)—inclusion of such forms proves useful for purposes of comparison. Further, while we will not explicitly show moraic structure for the input candidates to be evaluated (in the interests of space), remember that we assume the following segments to be moraic: syllable peaks (whether vowel or syllabic consonant) and syllable codas. In the interests of ease of interpretation, violations of Align-L(μ, PrWd), which can be sizeable in number, are given numerically, with a breakdown by moraic segment.
### 8.4.1 Single Sonorant Environments
We first consider cases in which the single sonorant is consonantal (8.4.1.1), followed by cases in which it is syllabic (8.4.1.2). Environments are further differentiated by the sonorant’s ultimate position in the word: word-initial syllable (onset or nucleus), word-medial position (i.e., following at least one vocalic environment), and word-final syllable.

#### 8.4.1.2 Sonorants are Syllabic
Sonorants become syllabic in the absence of an established syllable nucleus. We can thus conceive of their permissibility in such cases as a means of resolving violations of the sonority sequencing principle; in an Optimality-Theoretic analysis, this is reflected in a high-ranking (if not undominated) position for the constraint Sonority-Sequencing (1f.). As we have seen, this constraint militates against onsets that do not rise in sonority in the approach to the nucleus and codas that do not fall in sonority. Importantly, while we assumed

<!-- pdf-page: 256; source-page: 241 -->
in 8.3 that word-medially, the sonority sequencing principle for Proto-Indo-European is as strong as it is in Vedic (see 2.2.2 and 3.1.3 in Chapters 2 and
3), word-initially a relatively weakened notion of sonority sequencing would
appear to be warranted, which, if differentiating only between sonorants and
obstruents, tolerates sonority plateaus (segments of like sonority, i.e. #OOV,
#RRV) in word-initial complex onsets, but does not abide sonority reversals
(segments falling in sonority, i.e. #ROV).50

### 8.4.2 Two Sonorant Sequences

Both of these scenarios fall out of the ranking established thus far (including high-ranking Sonority-Sequencing and *ComplexCoda). The first is illustrated by the tableau in (101a.). ### 8.4.3 Three Sonorant Sequences
In this final subsection we consider sequences of three sonorants, as they occur word-initially and word-finally. As was observed in the case of two sonorant sequences, sequences of three sonorants also differ in their treatment in absolute word-initial position, depending on whether they precede a vowel or a consonant. Before a vowel, the middle sonorant vocalizes, while the two segments flanking it do not. Before a consonant, the third sonorant vocalizes, while the first two constitute a complex onset. These outcomes are exactly those predicted by the analysis; the three tableaux in (107) demonstrate as much. The conception of Sonority-Sequencing noted above is formed in view
of the fact that none of the i. candidates, in which no vocalization occurs,
match the reconstructed syllabifications we aim for. In (107a.–b.), the majority of candidates are eliminated due to violations of the Alignment constraint
and/or Onset; the latter is a constraint which the winners, (107a.iii.) and
(107b.ii.), satisfy. We see in these cases evidence for the need to include both
of these constraints in the ranking; whereas elsewhere we saw their influence
overlap, here we see that only functioning together are we able to generate
the desired results. As for the form in (107c.), the evaluation proceeds along
similar lines; but because the sonorants are followed by a sequence of obstruent + sonorant, we also have an additional opportunity to see the influence of
Coincide, violation of which eliminates the candidates in (107c.iii., v.), which
treat the sequence as a (non-initial) complex onset of rising sonority.
