## 8.5 Conclusion
Over the course of this chapter, we have built up an Optimality-Theoretic analysis of nucleus selection and sonorant vocalization in Proto-Indo-European
that, we argue, more satisfactorily addresses the relevant generalizations,
repeated below, than previous constraint-based approaches have.
(109)
Generalizations about Syllabicity in Proto-Indo-European
a. Obstruents are never syllabic.
b. Non-high vowels are never glides, i.e., non-syllabic.

<!-- pdf-page: 268; source-page: 253 -->
c. Sonorants are syllabic when not adjacent to a syllabic segment. d. When a sequence of more than one sonorant is not adjacent to a syllabic segment, it is the right-hand one which is syllabic, regardless of its relative sonority vis-à-vis the preceding one. In 8.1 we tackled the first two of these, establishing the foundations of the account with the introduction of members of the Peak and Margin families of constraints (Prince and Smolensky 1993 [2004]), whose fixed ranking enabled us to formally encode dispreferences for obstruents functioning as syllable peaks and (non-high) vowels functioning as syllable margins. The third generalization also follows from this hierarchy: in the absence of true vowels, sonorants can function as syllable peaks as well. But in the particular configuration noted in the fourth generalization, the Peak and Margin constraints, whose subrankings strictly adhere to the sonority hierarchy, came up short: absent any interference, as we saw, higher-sonority segments were always preferred over lower-sonority segments for vocalization. This undesirable result was circumvented not by introduction of a constraint militating against codas (as previous analyses had done, with unintended effects), but by a more direct instantiation of Schindler’s original directional insight, the mora-based Alignment constraint introduced in 8.2 on the model of Mester and Padgett’s (1994) syllable-based Alignment constraint translating the directional syllabification examined by Itô (1989). With one piece of the apparent ranking paradox seeming to plague right-hand sonorant vocalization versus medial consonant heterosyllabification, that is, the constraint NoCoda, thus falling out of the analysis, we proceeded in 8.3 to tackle the other piece: the constraint *ComplexOnset. We recognized the tolerance that Proto-Indo-European bears for complex onsets in word-initial position over word-medial position, and formalized this preference by introducing the Positional Markedness (Zoll 1998) constraint Coincide, introducing it in place of *ComplexOnset in the ranking to obtain a more satisfying set of results, for both bi- and proposed triconsonantal syllabifications. With the core of the analysis thus in hand, in 8.4 we set out to assess its empirical adequacy for a whole host of sonorant-related configurations, with satisfying results. The final constraint ranking is shown in (110), repeated from (95) above. We also include justification here for individual rankings holding within the overall system, that is, the desired (and undesired) treatments of relevant Proto-Indo-European forms, which show crucial support for them.

appear to be undominated in Proto-Indo-European. For the ranking of *Pk/

‘extend the hand’ (LIV 618–619; IEW 1057–1058).55 Lastly, we list the ranking relationships in (110j.–l.) last since as of now they ony have been made in view of proposed syllabifications of triconsonantal sequences, based on evidence from Vedic and Greek. Under this conception of the phenomenon of sonorant vocalization in Proto-Indo-European, syllabic sonorants are countenanced only when the alternative would violate the sonority sequencing principle. That being said, when faced with this situation, a string of segments containing multiple sonorants will be syllabified in such a way as to incur the fewest violations of Coincide and Onset. Furthermore, in such a string, all else being equal, it is the right-hand sonorant which is preferentially syllabic, an outcome of the influence of the constraint Align-L(μ, PrWd). 55 This same ranking will be formally proposed in the morphophonological analysis developed in the following chapter.
