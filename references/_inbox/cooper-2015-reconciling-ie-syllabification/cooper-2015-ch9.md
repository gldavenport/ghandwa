# Chapter 9. Nucleus Selection as a Morphophonological Operation?
## 9.0 Introduction
To reiterate, the current reconstructed state-of-affairs for Proto-Indo-European involves two generalizations about the behavior of sonorant segments with respect to syllabicity. The first is, simply, that sonorants have conditioned syllabic allophones in this language; generally speaking, these allophones arise in environments in which no vowel—hence no segment capable of being a syllable nucleus—is found. The second is that, in an environment amenable to the vocalization of either of two sonorants in sequence (e.g., between consonants), it is the second that will be syllabic, regardless of its relative sonority vis-à-vis the first one—so the example of *k̑u̯n̥bhís inst. pl. ‘dog’ is thought to demonstrate. In the previous chapter we developed an analysis in Optimality Theory to account for both of these generalizations. The backbone of the constraint ranking we saw fit to establish consisted of the fixed members of the Peak family of constraints interrupted at a crucial point—above the Peak constraints targeting sonorants—by the Alignment constraint Align-L(μ, PrWd). This constraint, an extension of Mester and Padgett’s (1994) syllable-based Alignment, prefers that moras be closer to the left edge of the prosodic word. As such it allows for right-hand sonorants to preferably vocalize over left-hand ones, in positions where either could conceivably do so. Introducing this constraint also allows us to maintain a fully phonological account of sonorant vocalization, and so to adhere more closely to Schindler’s original rule-based approach to the phenomenon. Yet at the same time, this reconstruction of the Proto-Indo-European system might appear quite odd. The peculiarity of the system, we would argue, lies in the differential influence of sonority on syllabicity. On the one hand, we can characterize the Proto-Indo-European system of nucleus selection as sonority-driven, given that only relatively highly sonorous segments can be syllable nuclei (all manners save for obstruents). Further, the implication proposed by Blevins (1995) and Zec (1995), that lower-sonority syllabic segments imply higher-sonority syllabic segments, appears to hold for this language, if we maintain a typical sonority hierarchy differentiating vowels from glides

On the other hand, when it comes to multiple sonorants in sequence, each of which is ostensibly capable of serving as the peak of a syllable, we see the consistent selection of the right-hand sonorant to fill this role. In other words, in such cases sonority-driven syllabicity would appear to be suppressed. That sonority does not uniformly drive syllabicity in Proto-Indo-European ostensibly poses a typological issue, given the proposal of Blevins (1995) and Zec (1995). Although as we have argued elsewhere (Cooper 2013b), any major concern about the typological plausibility of the Proto-Indo-European would appear to be unwarranted—see also the brief discussion in the next chapter— nevertheless, the insensitivity to the general scheme of sonority does give pause, and could be a source of concern as to the Proto-Indo-European reconstruction. At the least, it invites consideration of alternatives. In this context we might consider the example of the Proto-Indo-European stop series, the perceived typological rarity of which is often cited in connection with the development of the Glottalic Theory (Gamkrelidze and Ivanov 1973, Hopper 1973). While the approach is highly problematic (see Barrack 2002 for arguments against it), the particular concern of improving the series’ typological profile is still nevertheless evident in work as recent as Weiss (2009a).1 The aim of this chapter, then, is to develop and evaluate an alternative to the traditional account of sonorant vocalization. Where the traditional story was purely phonological in nature—Schindler’s rule makes no reference to morphological structure, save for word boundaries—the analysis we construct here assumes the relevance of morphological structure. As we will see, however, this alternative approach, incorporating information about such structure with the aim of privileging right-hand sonorants for vocalization, proves inelegantly cumbersome once we have instantiated it within the Optimality-Theoretic framework. Ultimately, we maintain that this inelegance serves to reinforce the validity of the analysis we developed over the course of the previous chapter. The rest of the chapter is organized as follows. In 9.1 we sketch out a descriptive account of the Proto-Indo-European sonorant vocalization data from a morphophonological perspective. We then present in 9.2 two analyses developed in a similar vein for Sanskrit, and assess their applicability to Proto-Indo-European. In 9.3 and 9.4 we formalize the analysis, in the former clarifying the nature of zero-grade ablaut from the perspective of moraic theory, and in the latter constructing an Optimality-Theoretic account. Moving on, we note that given a morphophonological perspective on sonorant vocalization, which pays attention to the relationship between full- and zero-grade forms, we are compelled to explain the behavior of zero-grades featuring sequences of obstruents. With respect to such environments, we predict two possible outcomes, characterized according to position in the word. The first is retention in some capacity of the full grade vowel, a result which occurs in word-initial syllables. Consider scenarios in which two obstruents would be adjacent as the result of zero-grade ablaut: for example, in -i̯é/ó-presents built to roots of shape OeO. Stem forms of this sort are securely reconstructed for the roots *ped- ‘step’ (LIV 458; IEW 790–2), 1.*pekw- ‘make ready’ (LIV 468; IEW 798), and 1.*tek- ‘extend the hand’ (LIV 618–619; IEW 1057– 1058), and all show the presence of an ostensibly epenthetic vowel, to break up the otherwise disfavored obstruent cluster: *ped-i̯é- (Ved. pádyate), *pekw-i̯é(Ved. pácyate), *tek-i̯é- (OEng. ðicgan). The position of this vowel is clearly consistent with the position of the vowel of the associated full-grade. On the other hand, when the zero-grade of a root of shape OeO occurs word-internally, no intervening vowel need surface at all. Take for instance the i-reduplicated thematic present stem of *tek̑- ‘give birth’ (LIV 618; IEW 1057), *ti-tk̑-é- (Gk. τίκτω, with metathesis), which shows no trace of a vowel between the two obstruents of the root. In terms of syllable structure, we might reanalyze the dual outcome of the vocalic remnant between obstruents as conditioned by whether or not the obstruents in question would otherwise necessarily be tautosyllabic. If so (as in word-initial position), then the full grade vowel persists (in some capacity); but if not (as in word-medial position, where the obstruents could be heterosyllabified), then it is deleted. Furthermore, it is likely not insignificant that of the examples considered here, the word-initial obstruent sequences occur before a consonant (glide *i̯), while the word-medial sequence occurs before a vowel (*e). ## 9.2 Zero-Grade in Sanskrit
While in the introduction to this chapter, we expressed some mild concern as to the varying influence of sonority on the phenomenon of sonorant vocalization as traditionally analyzed for Proto-Indo-European, this point requires some clarification. In fact the situation in Sanskrit shows—not unsurprisingly— a marked similarity. Importantly, though, in the case of this language some effort has been made in the literature to analyze the data not in terms of the traditional view of straightforward (i.e., purely phonological) right-hand vocalization, but rather as a phenomenon crucially attuned to the morphological structure of the Sanskrit root. ### 9.2.1 Steriade (1988)
As part of the background to her broader examination of reduplication in Sanskrit, Steriade (1988: 94–102) reviews the facts of sonorant syllabicity in this language. Save for some details, Sanskrit essentially (and not surprisingly) reflects the system reconstructed for Proto-Indo-European, including the fact that of two sonorants in sequence, it is the second which vocalizes, provided neither is vowel-adjacent. Importantly, though, Steriade departs from tradition—or what the traditional approach might have to say about Sanskrit—in accounting for the vocalization of the sonorants by acknowledging the morphological relationship between an inflected form and its root, rather than treating forms for these purposes as purely phonological objects. She makes the observation that the sonorant always preferentially vocalized is the one which follows the vowel in the associated full-grade. The transition to zero-grade is a crucial component of her analysis, because it is information contained in the full-grade which bears on the ultimate outcome. All else being equal, the full vowel can be the result of epenthesis (to derive full-grade), or the target of deletion (to derive zero-grade); Steriade opts for the latter, given the vowel’s unpredictable position within the root, and the consistent size of the full-grade (always a heavy monosyllable) as opposed to the zero-grade (sometimes heavy, sometimes not). Indeed the status of such subsyllabic constituents has been a topic of intense scrutiny in the literature (Clements and Keyser 1983, etc.), and as we have observed, current approaches to the syllable in the framework of moraic phonology have seen fit to formally (if not descriptively) do away with them. In this vein Steriade’s moraic instantiation of her analysis will turn out to be more relevant to the approach we will proceed to develop.

## 9.4 Optimality-Theoretic Formalization
Having sketched the basics of the account descriptively in 9.1 and in terms of moraic theory in 9.3, we turn now to a formal implementation in Optimality Theory (Prince and Smolensky 1993 [2004]). For a morphophonological approach which is sensitive to full-grade syllable structure, we must assume inputs with moraic content beyond that of a full

<!-- pdf-page: 290; source-page: 275 -->
(in the case of Proto-Indo-European, non-high) vowel. Specifically, consonants which find themselves in postvocalic position in full-grade forms maintain the moraicity associated with this position in the zero-grade. The vocalization process as conceived here is thus opaque, in that it is contingent on there being certain information in the input—the moraic status of relevant sonorants—which itself should form part of the output. To put it another way, we must assume that the analysis has a derivational component, in that there is a stage of syllabification which must take place prior to the syllabification we are interested in. While this would present a problem for classic Optimality Theory, which, as it is concerned with a direct mapping from phonological input to output, leaves no space for intermediate stages of development, nonetheless variations of the theory incorporating a derivational component have been proposed. Stratal Optimality Theory (Kiparsky 2000, 2003, McCarthy and Prince 1993b, et al.) is one such approach; building on the earlier framework of Lexical Phonology (Kaisse and Shaw 1985, Kiparsky 1985, Hargus and Kaisse 1993, et al.), the claim is that stages, or strata, of word- and phrase-formation can be identified and associated with distinct strata of constraint-based evaluation, which proceed from one to the next.11 For an example of its use in the case of Proto-Indo-European, one can look to the analysis of syllabification developed by Byrd (2010). In this framework, one stratum is concerned with the evaluation of output candidate forms of the stem (essentially, the product of derivational morphology), a second stratum is concerned with evaluation of output candidate forms of the word (essentially, the product of inflectional morphology),12 and a third stratum is concerned with evaluation of output candidate forms postlexically, that is, without regard to morphological category (130–131). Postlexical: *tert.i̯os → *ter.ti.i̯os 11 We note that another way of capturing analogy would be to introduce Output-Output constraints (Benua 1995, 2000), which are concerned with maximizing phonological identity across morphologically-related output forms. ### 9.4.1 Moraic Sonorant Vocalizes
In the absence of a full vowel, a moraic sonorant is preferentially vocalized over a non-moraic one, regardless of relative sonority. We account for this outcome with the (preliminary) ranking of constraints in (27). (27) Sonority-Sequencing, Dep-μ-IO, *Pk/Obstruent » *Pk/Nasal » *Pk/Liquid » *Pk/Glide » *Pk/Vowel

As we conceive of the preference for right-hand sonorant vocalization to tie directly to the moraic status of that segment, at first blush the analysis might simply and crucially hinge on faithfulness to input moraic structure— in other words, satisfaction of the constraint Max-μ-IO (26c.), omitted here. Alternatively, we conceive of this right-hand vocalization as driven by a limited allowance for derived moras, that is, moras that are inserted and lack correspondents in the input form, a situation calling for inclusion of the constraint Dep-μ-IO (26a.), as done here. ### 9.4.5 Sonorant Vocalization in Non-Zero-Grade Environments
Although we have so far developed the account of sonorant vocalization treating the phenomenon as exclusively an effect of the morphologically-conditioned transition to zero-grade ablaut, as already noted above in 9.2 there are also forms which can be analyzed as featuring syllabic sonorants, albeit outside of a zero-grade context. The aforementioned instrumental plural ending *-bhis, for instance, is reconstructed entirely as is, without an associated full-grade (likewise the reduplicated prefix seen in 9.4.3 above). On the face of it, the traditional account, being purely phonological in nature, is to be preferred to the morphophonological alternative, seeing as how it can easily accommodate forms such as *-bhis. ## 9.5 Conclusion
In this chapter we have sought to construct an alternative approach to sonorant
vocalization in Proto-Indo-European, in the interests of overcoming its intuitively unusual (but not typologically unattested) variable sensitivity to sonority: while sonority drives syllabicity as far which segments can be syllabic at
all (sonorants, not obstruents), it does not seem to play a role in determining
the target of vocalization, when two sonorants are in sequence. Inspired by
work on Sanskrit by Steriade (1988) and Calabrese (1999), we have centered the
analysis around the observations that, first, sonorants vocalize most often in
zero-grade environments, and second, a syllabic sonorant in such an environment typically is the one following a vowel in an associated full-grade form.
Conceptualized in the framework of moraic theory, and formalized in the
framework of Optimality Theory, we have analyzed the phenomenon as motivated crucially by a drive to minimize the insertion of moraic content. The data
have been successfully captured by the constraint ranking in (69), repeated
from (57) above. As we did for the final ranking in the analysis developed in
the previous chapter, we also include here justification for individual rankings
holding within the overall system.

So despite coming at the issue from two distinct perspectives, we are left with rankings that are not that different. The truly crucial distinction between our Optimality-Theoretic formalization of the traditional, phonological approach to Proto-Indo-European sonorant vocalization, and our formalization of the morphophonological alternative, is the fact that the morphophonological approach requires a derivational component to take place prior to the evaluation of the relevant data, such that ostensibly derived moraicity is part of the input to said evaluation. While we have proceeded in this chapter assuming the importance of input moraic structure to the success of the analysis, we have in fact shown in the development of the Optimality-Theoretic analysis in 9.4 that the presence or absence of input moraicity does not actually matter in the selection of optimal outputs, with one exception (see below). By and large, rather, it has been the interactions of the constraints in the assembled ranking which have led to the correct results, a ranking which excludes the one constraint one might think should play a crucial role, Max-μ-IO (‘Input moras should have output correspondents (no mora deletion)’) in (26c.). In fact, we have found no motivation for the inclusion of Max-μ-IO in the constraint ranking; as we progressed through the data, for no forms we considered were we compelled to rely on

it. Indeed for every piece of data we brought into the picture, we found either that the constraint ranking as developed up to that point could account for it, or if not, then that re-ranking of the constraints or a new constraint was necessary. But if an additional constraint was required, it was never the case that it could be Max-μ-IO. As Max-μ-IO is the only constraint we considered that is explicitly concerned with input moraicity, requiring faithful realization thereof, if we do not need it for a successful analysis, the obvious question is, do we actually need input moraicity of the sort we have assumed at all? The answer we were compelled to arrive at was yes, given the case of *per-u̯r̥ (cf. (33)). The entire burden for justifying inclusion of the derivational component in the analysis effectively rests on this form; is proposing the additional complication of input moraicity justified in view of one piece of data? To be sure, this same form was an issue in the phonological analaysis developed in the previous chapter, driving us to propose our mora-based Alignment constraint to account for it; so in either case *per-u̯r̥ motivates some crucial component of the analysis. What the essential difference between the competing analyses comes down to, then, is that one requires an additional constraint, while the other requires a derivational component (or an additional stratum, conceived in terms of Stratal Optimality Theory). Given only the data and phenomena we have considered in this work, it would seem that the phonological analysis is the more elegant approach to the Proto-Indo-European system, and has the added advantage (depending on one’s perspective) of adhering more closely to the traditional conception of Optimality Theory. As such, we will continue to maintain it for the remainder of the discussion to unfold. That being said, given independent justification for a derivational approach to syllabification in this language—such as Byrd’s (2010) analysis of Sievers’ Law—then one could prefer the morphophonological analysis, and if so, we expect that the constraints and rankings developed here should play a significant role in such an account.
