# Chapter 1. Introduction

## 1.1 Overview of the Volume
As its title suggests, this book is concerned with bridging gaps. In the immediate context, the gap in question concerns two posited aspects of Indo-European syllable structure: the heterosyllabic assignment of medial consonants (VC. CV) on the one hand, and the right-hand vocalization of sonorant consonants (CRR̥.C) on the other. If motivated by a drive to realize an ‘ideal’ Indo-European syllable, then these two phenomena offer ostensibly divergent views on what such a structure should contain: while the former suggests a tolerance for closed syllables and a dispreference for syllable-initial consonant clusters (cf. V.CCV), the latter suggests the mirror-image of these priorities (cf. CR̥R.C). The issue, then, is how these seemingly disparate structures, if we choose to maintain them both for Proto-Indo-European, are to be accounted for in one and the same system. This brings us to a second gap we aim to address here: that which has often separated Indo-European studies from current linguistic theory. This book seeks to advance the much-needed dialogue between these two fields initiated in our earlier work (Cooper 2009, 2011, 2012), so as to demonstrate the continued relevance of both Proto-Indo-European and the ancient Indo-European languages to contemporary linguistic theory, and, moreover, to reaffirm the value of the syllable as a unit of phonology, necessary for these languages’ formal representation. Highlighting the insights that Proto-Indo-European and syllable theory can reveal about each other, we hope this volume makes a solid contribution to the scholarly tradition of each. As a language reconstructed from careful study of languages both ancient and modern, Proto-Indo-European has typically, if understandably, eluded the notice of proponents of modern phonological theory. While the earliest attested daughter languages may fare somewhat better in this regard, no doubt due to their actually being documented, their position can hardly be considered robust. In the area of syllabification and syllable structure, the distance between the fields is particularly noticeable: the traditional account of Proto-Indo-European syllabification (as proposed in Schindler 1977b, reiterated in Mayrhofer 1986, Szemerényi 1996, Fortson 2004, Weiss 2009b, et al.) exemplifies an older, largely outdated theoretical approach, which, while achieving more-or-less descriptive adequacy, is at the same time narrowly

<!-- pdf-page: 17; source-page: 2 -->
focused on the identification of syllabic nuclei, to the exclusion of the remaining structure (onsets, codas) within and across syllable boundaries. Further, discussion of syllable ‘weight’, which in contemporary theory is directly tied to syllable structure as dictated by the position of syllable boundaries, is often discussed in the Indo-European literature in simple segmental terms (Seebold 1972), which may maintain descriptive adequacy but lack the explanatory grounding that invocation of theory would provide. Meanwhile the syllable, recognized already by the ancient Indo-European grammarians (Sanskrit akṣara ‘imperishable’, Greek συλλαβή ‘that which is held together’), but failing to garner significant mention in earlier work on generative phonology (e.g. Chomsky and Halle 1968), subsequently flourished as a means of accounting for a plethora of phenomena related to languages’ sound systems, including segment alternations, insertions, deletions, stress assignment, and rules for licit shapes of words and morphemes (see Blevins 1995, Zec 2007, Cooper and Zec 2013 for an overview). More recently, however, the unit has been challenged by claims that its empirical significance is in fact far more limited than has been conceived (e.g., Blevins 2003, Steriade 1999a, Côté 2000, etc.). Yet we believe it to be capable of shedding light on the workings of Proto-Indo-European—and vice versa—and as such, that its continued use in phonological theory is more than warranted.
## 1.2 Structure of the Volume
This work is divided into two parts, each of which tackles one aspect of syllable structure. Our focus in the first is the treatment of medial sequences of consonants, which as we have indicated has traditionally been analyzed, for both the ancient Indo-European languages, and by extension, Proto-Indo-European itself, as heterosyllabification, VC.CV. In Chapter 2 we study the evidence for and against this view as it applies to Vedic, reconciling the contradictory claims ultimately in favor of it; from there we extend the approach to longer sequences of consonants, and examine in depth one phenomenon in particular, the distribution of the so-called ‘perfect union vowel’, which, while suggesting the same basic prioritization of syllable-structural preferences, at the same time demonstrates a more active intolerance for certain complex syllable shapes. We then proceed in Chapter 3 to develop a formal analysis of medial consonant cluster syllabification for Vedic, one with the capability to predict both the general patterns as well as the idiosyncrasy associated with the perfect conjugation. In Chapter 4 we examine evidence from the history of Greek which, if analyzed from the perspective of syllable structure, can fill a

<!-- pdf-page: 18; source-page: 3 -->
gap in our understanding of Proto-Indo-European left open by consideration of Vedic. In Chapter 5, the final chapter of the first half of this volume, we devote some space to evaluating the extent to which a current framework of formal phonological theory is capable of generating the well-known, if typologically implausible, duo of syllabifications VR.OOV, VOO.RV; the approaches we lay out can serve to further elucidate the problematic nature of these patterns, which, while largely abandoned in current literature, nevertheless are still maintained in some quarters. The complementary focus of the second part of this volume is the phenomenon of sonorant vocalization, and in particular, the right-hand preference observed in the targeting of these segments for syllabicity. In Chapter 6 we lay the groundwork for this discussion by reviewing the descriptive generalizations and Schindler’s (1977b) rule-based account and presenting the findings of a small corpus study of sonorant syllabicity drawn from the Proto-Indo-European verbal domain. We move on to evaluate previous formal approaches to sonorant vocalization in Proto-Indo-European in Chapter 7, and highlight the nature of their insufficient empirical adequacy. In Chapter 8 we develop a new formal analysis, one which is more satisfying on at least three counts: explicitly encoding restrictions on consonant syllabicity; generating right-hand sonorant vocalization, a concept key to Schindler’s original rule yet subsequently insufficiently translated into more current theoretical frameworks; and coherently functioning in a framework in which medial consonants are heterosyllabic. In Chapter 9 we entertain an alternative account to that developed in Chapter 8, one which assigns a crucial formal role to morphological structure in the determination of which sonorants vocalize, and under which circumstances, but ultimately find it unjustifiably cumbersome. In Chapter 10 we assess a series of exceptions to Schindler’s generalization from within the proposed analysis, and also take steps toward situating the Proto-Indo-European system within a typology of languages with syllabic consonants. Finally, we conclude this work in Chapter 11, summarizing our findings and laying out some of the directions we envision for future work in this domain.
## 1.3 The Formal Framework
We will now give brief overviews of two theoretical conceptions we maintain, the first concerning the structure of the syllable and the representation of syllable weight, the second concerning the theoretical machinery we will use to model our claims about Proto-Indo-European syllabification.

### 1.3.1 Syllables, Moras, and Sonority
As this work is intimately concerned with the syllable structure of Proto-Indo-European, it will be important and useful to make clear exactly the conception of the syllable we maintain. This concerns not only the arrangement of segments within the syllable, but also the representation of syllable weight. We understand the syllable to be a phonological constituent “larger than the segment and smaller than the word” (Kahn 1976: 20). It serves both as an organizational unit governing the distribution of consonants and vowels within (and in some languages, across) words (phonotactics), and as a domain of quantity-sensitivity (weight), two functions relevant for Indo-European. With respect to subsyllabic constituency, our view of syllable structure can be captured by the schematized CVC syllable in (1) (motivated by McCarthy and Prince 1986 [1996], Steriade 1988, Hayes 1989, et al.): (1)

σ μ μ C V C A few clarificatory points concerning this structure are perhaps in order. First, the symbols C and V are meant to stand in for actual segmental material; they are not intended to represent a distinctive tier of structure superordinate to individual segments. Second, note the absence of any subconstituency within the syllable other than the mora (about which more below). Our view of syllable structure does not formally recognize onset, nucleus, coda, or rhyme as a distinct syllabic constituent (contra the hyper-articulated structures seen in early syllable-based works; see Cooper and Zec 2013 for an overview), since any phenomenon which might motivate formal recognition of such can also find explanation in the simpler structure given in (1) (see Zec 2007 for discussion). That being said, the terms onset, nucleus, coda, and rhyme find use as convenient shorthand for positions in the syllable, and as such will be used to refer to consonantal material in the left-hand margin of the syllable, the most sonorous segment (or peak) of the syllable, consonantal material in the right-hand margin of the syllable, and moraic segments, respectively. Further, we will also see fit to use the term appendix to refer to any segmental material contained within the syllable that is immediately dominated by the syllable node; more specifically, this will be relevant in the treatment of codas, and will provide a point of contrast from the situation which we argue holds in the languages we are concerned with, namely that codas are actually moraic (as in (1)). The mora is the unit of syllable weight (Trubetzkoy 1939); it is useful in differentiating the traditionally recognized binary distinction between ‘light’ and ‘heavy’ syllables, which can manifest itself in phenomena such as stress assignment or metrification: a monomoraic syllable is light, while a bimoraic one is heavy. (Trimoraic or ‘superheavy’ syllables are possible as well; these will feature in the discussion in Chapters 2 and 3.) What counts as moraic is a language-specific determination; in particular, in the case of Vedic and Proto-Indo-European, the two languages we will examine for which this will be relevant, the inventory of monomoraic syllables will include syllables of shape (C)V̆, while the inventory of heavy syllables will include syllables of shape (C)V̄, (C)VC; in all cases the onset is optional, and will not contribute to calculations of weight.1 Further, the structure in (1) can be distinguished from the perhaps less familiar proposal of Hyman (1985) et al. given in (2), in which the syllable is equally minimal in its subordinate constituency, but the assignment of moras takes a different tack. The syllable is a grouping of segments organized around a peak of sonority (relative loudness), a concept whose importance was recognized already by Sievers (1881), Jespersen (1904), and Saussure (1916); see more recently Hooper 1976, Steriade 1982, Selkirk 1984, Zec 1988, et al. For every syllable there is a peak, into which segments usually increase in sonority, and out of which segments decrease in sonority; this is known as the Sonority Sequencing Principle (Clements 1990). Sonority may also regulate the sets of syllabic and/or moraic segments in a language (Zec 2007; but see Cooper 2013b for discussion of exceptions to the former). The sonority of segments is usually defined according to manner class, with the scale in (3) being a traditional instantiation of the arrangement, or hierarchy: (3) Sonority Hierarchy Vowels > Glides > Liquids > Nasals > Obstruents The scale tolerates language-specific variation. Some languages have been argued to require a more fine-grained hierarchy, making further distinctions among natural classes beyond those in (3); in Imdlawn Tashlhiyt Berber as analyzed by Dell and Elmedlaoui (1985, 1988), for example, patterns of syllabification suggest a distinction is to be made between low vowels and high vowels / glides at one end of the scale (the language lacks mid vowels), and between voiced fricatives, voiceless fricatives, voiced stops, and voiceless stops at the other end (see the discussion in Chapter 10). Other languages have been argued to require idiosyncratic re-ranking of the classes in (3); in Hungarian as analyzed by Hankamer and Aissen (1974), the liquids are characterized as more sonorous than the glide j. For the present purposes, we will come to rely on the scale in (3), as well as the simplification in (4): (4) Sonority Hierarchy (Simplified) Sonorants > Obstruents The relatively more fine-grained hierarchy in (3) will be referenced in our analysis of sonorant vocalization in the second part of this volume; we will demonstrate how sonority-driven syllabicity, which would identify the most sonorous segment in a string as a syllable peak, is overridden by other principles, with the result being that the sonority differences between the sonorants (but not the obstruents) are in this case neutralized. The scale in (4) will serve in our examination of the syllabification of syllable margins, particularly our focus

on medial consonant syllabification in Vedic in Chapters 2 and 3. Note we do not mean to suggest the relevance in Indo-European of two distinct sonority hierarchies; rather, our use of the scale in (4) will serve as convenient shorthand for the more detailed version in (3); but we leave for future work in this area the formal derivation of the former from the latter, at least in the area of syllable-edge phonotactics. That sonority is expected to increase as one moves into the syllable and decrease as one moves to its termination has found conflict in situations in which segments falling or flat in sonority function as the onset of a syllable, or segments rising or flat in sonority function as the coda; for example, consider the position of s in English stay and German Stich ‘sting’ (Hall 2002: 33). One way that such ‘sonority reversals’ and ‘sonority plateaus’ have been dealt with in the literature is through the notion of extrasyllabicity: segments may be excluded from syllable structure, and incorporated instead directly into higher-level prosodic structure, such as the word.2 Extrasyllabicity is relevant in this context as it has been proposed to hold of s in Vedic (Kobayashi 2004: 41ff.), and of obstruents in Proto-Indo-European (Byrd 2010: 83ff.); in particular we will consider the analysis associated with the former claim in 2.2.3 in Chapter 2.
### 1.3.2 Optimality Theory
The phonological framework we will use to model the data is that of Optimality Theory (Prince and Smolensky 1993 [2004]), specifically, Correspondence Theory (McCarthy and Prince 1995).3 We introduce the system in 1.3.2.1 and comment briefly on our reliance on it in 1.3.2.2.
#### 1.3.2.1 Overview of the Theory
Optimality Theory is a constraint-based approach to phonology, in which the path from phonological input to output is no real path at all, in the traditional step-wise derivational sense. The theory emerges in part out of the recognition that different phonological processes in a single language can be interpreted as working toward the same end of satisfying some overarching generalization. Such coordinated operations, or conspiracies, fail to be formally accounted for in a simple rule-based derivational framework, absent explicit stipulation: the traditional schema for phonological rules, X → Y / Z (‘X becomes Y in the 2 In the case of English, see e.g. Selkirk 1982, Clements 1990; for German, see e.g. Giegerich 1985, Hall 1992; but cf. For example, Kisseberth (1970) illustrates processes of vowel epenthesis and consonant deletion in Yawelmani (Yokuts), which operate according to the following rules: (5) ø → V / C_C{C, #} (6) C → ø / CC+_ (7) C → ø / C+_C According to rule (5), a vowel is inserted between two consonants, provided the second is either before another consonant or word-final. Rules (6) and (7) target a consonant for deletion, the former if it follows a sequence of two consonants in an adjacent morpheme, the latter if it is the first of two consonants in a morpheme, following a consonant-final morpheme (note these two rules apply in different domains in the grammar, so their overlap is not an issue).4 Despite the different operations we see captured between these three rules (they cannot be collapsed into a single statement), Kisseberth connects them all by proposing for Yawelmani a derivational constraint on the licit output of phonological rules in this language: in particular, this constraint says that “strings containing the sequences CCC, #CC, CC# are not possible outputs of any phonological rule if these sequences were not present in the input” (304). According to the traditional conception of Optimality Theory, the answer to this question is no.6 Optimality Theory maintains constraints and their interactions as the source of phonological generalizations. There are three components to the theory: Gen, Con, and Eval. For a given input, Gen generates an infinite set of possible output candidates, or realizations. Con is the set of (in the strongest version of the theory, universal) violable constraints on linguistic structure, of which there are two basic types: markedness constraints, which place conditions on well-formedness; and faithfulness constraints, which require preservation of (some aspect of) input structure.7 A language-specific ranking of these constraints constitutes the basis against which the set of output candidates generated by Gen is assessed in the third component of the theory, Eval. For a pair of constraints X and Y, three possible relationships may hold: X may outrank Y; Y may outrank X; or X and Y may not be crucially ranked with respect to each other (we exemplify these rankings in the scenarios presented below). Generally, a candidate that satisfies a higher-ranked constraint, even at the cost of violating a number of lower-ranked constraints, will be preferred over one that satisfies a number of lower-ranked constraints, but violates a higher-ranked constraint. Finally, of course, there is also a Lexicon, the storehouse of underlying forms of morphemes, which contains all non-predictable aspects thereof and lacks language-specific restriction (‘Richness of the Base’). #### 1.3.2.2 Why Optimality Theory?
Our use of Optimality Theory as the means to model syllabification in ancient and reconstructed Indo-European can be justified from at least three perspectives. First, practically speaking, it is one of the (if not the) most prominent formal frameworks currently in use in phonological theory. If we conceive of Proto-Indo-European as an actual language spoken by actual speakers, then a theoretical approach that is actively employed to account for spoken languages today ought to be able to do the same in the analysis of this reconstructed system; the theory should likewise be capable of accounting for a ‘dead’ language

<!-- pdf-page: 31; source-page: 16 -->
such as Vedic. If it can, this outcome can afford us that much more confidence in our hypotheses about the workings of these ancient and reconstructed languages; but if it cannot, the questions necessarily raised as a consequence can be instrumental in helping us to refine our hypotheses about them, and/or about the theory itself. This interplay between theory and data, the crux of scientific investigation, will be observed several times over the course of the discussion to unfold; in most cases where the theory requires some adjustment to account for the data—in the exceptional syllabification practices of the Vedic perfect (Chapter 3), or the sonority-insensitive vocalization of right-hand sonorants and variable tolerance for complex onsets in Proto-Indo-European (Chapter 8)—we will argue such modification is principled and follows naturally from proposals already extant in the theoretical literature. In one situation, however—generating the combined syllabifications VOO.RV, VR.OOV in a single system (Chapter 5)—we will argue that the necessary changes are largely ad hoc and inconsistent with current theoretical assumptions; in other words, the difficulty of applying theory in this case would argue for a revision of this conception of Proto-Indo-European syllable structure.13 Secondly, the fact that Optimality Theory is concerned with optimization of linguistic structure makes it a particularly attractive framework for modeling syllabification. In Optimality Theory, competing priorities formalized as ranked constraints interact with each other so as to select the ideal output for a given input. Applied to our purposes, the relevant constraints will concern syllable well-formedness. We will see conspiracies towards achieving good Vedic and Proto-Indo-European syllables, which Optimality Theory formally accounts for in two ways. Not only does it identify, through markedness constraints, the disfavored structures to be avoided (such as complex onsets or superheavy syllables, or, more basically, an otherwise unsyllabifiable string of segments), but the theory also identifies, through constraint ranking, the contours of the ideal means of avoidance (insertion of i, vocalization of a sonorant—and which one, when multiple candidates present themselves). In short, Optimality Theory, given its ability to elegantly capture the operation of conspiracies, provides an attractive framework for the modeling of phenomena connected to prosodic optimization, and in particular, optimization of syllable structure. 13 To be sure, while the typological implausibility of this system has made it the subject of some doubt (see e.g. Byrd 2010), nevertheless it has been maintained as recently as Keydana’s (2004) Optimality-Theoretic analysis of Proto-Indo-European syllabification (though he does not formally capture VR.OOV in his proposed account; see Chapter 5, n. 3).

A final reason to use Optimality Theory for the phenomena under consideration lies in its predictive capacity. As we noted above, the ranking of constraints is a language-specific affair—the same constraints may be active in two different languages, but because of their respective positions in the associated constraint rankings, we can see different behaviors emerge. The assumption is that any given constraint ranking reflects a possible (if not actually attested) language—in other words, variations on constraint ranking can generate a factorial typology of possible language behaviors. So the analyses we build up will present us with an opportunity to evaluate our approach to the relevant phenomena from a typological perspective. In this volume we take the preliminary steps towards appreciating this goal, surveying three languages with systems of syllabic consonants that are in some way descriptively akin to that of Proto-Indo-European; we leave for future work in this area the application of the theoretical apparatus to cross-linguistic data to investigate the formal similarities (and differences).
## 1.4 A Note about Transcription and *
We conclude this introductory chapter with a brief note about the way in which linguistic data are transcribed and annotated throughout this volume. For the representation of Proto-Indo-European, Vedic, and Greek, we use the system of transcription standardly employed in the field of Indo-European linguistics, found in handbooks such as Meier-Brügger 2002, 2010. For the representation of data from unrelated languages, including Cairene and Iraqi Arabic (Chapter 8), and Micmac, Shuswap, and Imdlawn Tashlhiyt Berber (Chapter 10), we rely on the systems employed by the respective sources, as cited. Further, as the asterisk <*> has one meaning (reconstructed status) in historical linguistics, another meaning (ungrammaticality) in other subfields of linguistics—of relevance here, phonology—it is important to strive for clarity of presentation in a work such as this, which crosses sub-specialization. Therefore, we maintain the historical-linguistic use of <*> to indicate reconstructed status, and employ the dagger <†> to indicate ungrammatical (and/or unattested) status (although we do use <*> in the forms of various Optimality-Theoretic constraints and to indicate constraint violations, as seen in the previous section).
