## 2.2 The Syllabification of VCCCV Sequences
In this section we broaden the focus to consider the treatment of medial sequences consisting of three consonants. Building on the heterosyllabic approach we maintain for VCCV sequences, we show VCCCV syllabification to be entirely consistent with this treatment. In 2.2.1 we consider the position of superheavy syllables in Vedic; that such structures have been shown to be salient in this language offers some indication as to how VCCCV sequences are syllabified. In 2.2.2 we show how the attested distribution of VCCCV sequences can also be useful in this regard, as a source of insight into the nature of the principle governing their syllabification: that is, given the data, we argue for the relevance of strict sonority sequencing in Vedic. We then show in 2.2.3 how the behavior of one consonant in particular, the fricative s, which can be deleted between consonants, finds natural explanation under this view.

Calabrese’s data illustrate the two types of superheavy syllables commonly identified: those featuring a long vowel followed by at least one consonant (26a.), and those featuring a short vowel followed by at least two consonants (26b.), specifically here a sonorant followed by an obstruent. Of these two types, it is only the former that follows directly from the heterosyllabification of consonants we argued in favor of in 2.1. While a superheavy syllable containing two coda consonants—obviously the one we are more interested in, for the present purposes—is certainly compatible with this view, nevertheless it is not directly and exclusively predicted by it: a triconsonantal sequence syllabified as VC.CCV can be said to respect heterosyllabicity. Rather, to conflate V̄C(C). with V̆CC. in the Vedic system, one must make the following additional assumptions: first, that complex codas are preferred over complex onsets, and second, extrapolating from the moraicity of an initial coda segment (as suggested by metrical practice), that all coda consonants contribute to weight. While in a presentation such as Calabrese’s these assumptions must surely have been made, they have been only tacitly so; in his discussion we find lacking explicit justification for this treatment. From the metrical perspective, there is no practical distinction in weight, when a short vowel is followed by a simple coda (single consonant) or a complex one (two or more consonants), or a long vowel is followed by no coda, or a simple one: all four of these cases count as ‘heavy’. Yet the possibility of superheavy syllables in Vedic has been suggested through indirect evidence. The relevant configurations for realizing superheavy syllables (V̄CCV, V̆CCCV) are often the targets of various phonological processes, which alter their shape away from this treatment, to the extent that superheavy syllables are apparently regularly avoided in Rig Vedic cadences. Hoenigswald (1989: 559) cites four examples of such processes, including our focus in 2.3, the perfect union vowel; the others are Sievers’ Law, hiatus instead of contraction in a V#V sequence when the second vowel precedes a consonant cluster (as in sá ít C . . ., but séd V . . .; Wackernagel 1896: 315) and distraction of long vowels across two syllables, which according to Renou (1952) is clearly intended to avoid a vowel in a closed syllable or a group of three consonants.41 Hoenigswald also notes that the Rig Veda avoids non-thematic guṇa forms of *TERT- roots (e.g., dógdhi) and other categories with “overlength” (559). Further, to analyze the impact of these various phenomena on limiting superheavy syllables in the Rig Veda, Hoenigswald conducts a study of the cadences ( ͜ – ͜ x |, – ͜ –( ͜ ) x|) in the second book and the first sixty-four hymns of the tenth book, for a 41 “a visiblement pour objet d’éviter une voyelle longue entravée ou un groupe de trois consonnes” (31).

Based on the lower frequency of presumed superheavy syllables in this domain, as compared to outside it (that is, in the rest of the pāda), he tentatively concludes that overlength is actively avoided. Kobayashi (2004: 29–31), citing Hoenigswald, reiterates the processes but also conducts a more extensive version of the study, examining all ten books of two different editions of the Rig Veda; his results provide further confirmation that, if hypothesized to exist, superheavy syllables are avoided in the Rig Veda as “a general tendency” (31). Following Hoenigswald (1989) and Kobayashi (2004), then, we understand superheavy syllables to be a unit of prosodic structure that can be legitimately introduced in a discussion of Vedic syllabification, if for no other reason than that there is a calculated effort to avoid them in Rig Vedic cadences.42 But superheavy syllables do occasionally arise, as shown by the existence of examples such as Calabrese’s in (26), and as concerns VCCCV sequences in particular, suggest the syllabification VRO.OV. We will see further support for this treatment in the following subsection, in consideration of the broader distribution of triconsonantal sequences in the language.
### 2.2.2 VCCCV Sequences and the Strength of Sonority Sequencing
The heterosyllabic treatment of medial two-consonant sequences we argued
for in 2.1, when elevated to the level of three-consonant sequences, offers multiple, competing views of the syllabification of these more complex configurations. We noted this already in the previous subsection; we will consider it
further here, bringing additional distributional evidence into the picture to
determine which of these views is most appropriate for the Vedic system.

To take the example of VOOOV, either of the syllabifications VO.OOV or VOO. How we choose to syllabify these sequences can be perceived to be a function of how strict a sonority sequencing principle we employ for Vedic (see 1.3.1 in Chapter 1). In the strongest conception of the principle, as articulated by Selkirk (1984) et al., segments should rise in sonority as one moves toward the syllable peak, and fall in sonority as one moves away from it; under this view, complex onsets and complex codas are tolerated only when their members rise in sonority or fall in sonority, respectively. By comparison, the relatively weak version of sonority sequencing proposed by Blevins (1995) et al. allows for complex onsets and codas of flat sonority (a sonority plateau), provided the nucleus still stands out as the most sonorous part of the syllable. In neither conception of the principle are sonority reversals predicted to occur—that is, onsets that fall in sonority (e.g. .ROV-) or codas that rise in sonority (e.g. -VOR.)—though language-specific exceptions do occur.43 A comparison of the implications of these two approaches for the syllabification of the eight VCCCV sequences specified in (27) is presented below in (28). Depending on the version of the sonority sequencing principle maintained, these sequences can be syllabified as either VCC.CV or VC.CCV, or can be considered unsyllabifiable (i.e. require some additional assumptions for successful syllabic parsing); cases of the last type are included in the final column, labeled ‘Other’. The assignments are guided by a principle of onset-filling (Berg and Niemi 2000)—as extrapolated from the evidence of basic VC.CV syllabification—whereby an onset is minimal (but not absent) and as many segments as are allowed by sonority sequencing are assigned to the preceding coda. (In other words, a complex onset will surface only when a complex coda is not possible.) 43 The status of s in many (Indo-European) languages constitutes one well-known example of such, if one chooses to operate with a fine-grained sonority hierarchy differentiating fricatives (higher sonority obstruents) from stops (lower sonority obstruents); see 2.2.3 for examination of the status of s in Vedic, in the context of VCCCV sequences.

The overlap in attested distribution and predicted syllabification is highly suggestive of the relevance of strong sonority sequencing for Vedic: if weak sonority sequencing were operational, allowing for complex codas and onsets of flat sonority, we would expect to see examples of Vedic forms with VOOOV, VORRV, VRROV, VRRRV (syllabified VOO.OV, VRR.OV, VRR.RV, VO.RRV, as per (28)), but such data are apparently not found. With respect to VRORV, as sonority sequencing alone is an insufficient predictor of syllable structure (again, it is compatible with either VRO.RV or VR.ORV), we claim, absent explicit evidence in Vedic to the contrary, that these sequences are syllabified with a complex coda, i.e. VRO.RV.44 This would be the natural extension of the syllabification practice discussed above in 2.1 for biconsonantal sequences, a point that we will return to as we develop the formal analysis of Vedic medial syllabification in the next chapter.

Assuming either a stronger version of sonority sequencing or a more fine-grained sonority hierarchy will enable us to capture the facts. Given that we already have independent motivation for the stronger version of the sonority sequencing principle (as discussed in the previous subsection), it is simpler to rely on it here, than introduce any new assumptions.45, 46 As such, we can characterize the deletion of s as a response to a poor sonority profile for syllabification: between two obstruents, the sibilant cannot be tautosyllabified with either segment, given their equal sonority. On the other hand, s is preserved next to a sonorant, as it can group with that segment into a complex syllabic constituent: a coda with a preceding sonorant (ásvārṣṭām), an onset with a following one (ábhutsmahi).47 45 Independent support for the gradated sonority hierarchy might be drawn from sequences such as kṣ-, observed in the root kṣip- ‘throw’ and in dhārayatkṣit- ‘sustaining creatures’, attested twice in the Rig Veda (1.136.3, 10.132.2). We can conclude this discussion of the position of s by briefly remarking on the somewhat different approach taken by Kobayashi concerning sibilants (2004: 35ff.). Kobayashi distinguishes sibilants from other obstruents in Vedic in a number of ways. He points out that sibilants cannot occur in absolute final position (unless they precede an initial voiceless stop); while acknowledging that “distributional tendencies across a word boundary need not necessarily constrain medial strings” (41), he tentatively extends this restriction to all syllables, including word-internal ones. On the other hand, he speculates that a sibilant can be allowed in a coda, when it is geminated, in accordance with the Vājasaneyi-Prātiśākhya’s statement that all consonants, including fricatives, are doubled after r and h. Finally, he also notes that sibilants can violate the sonority sequencing principle, by preceding stops in onset position, as they are higher in sonority according to the universal scale of sonority (but note their patterning with other obstruents in the less fine-grained version used above). In order to account for such idiosyncratic behavior, Kobayashi analyzes sibilants occurring to the left of voiceless stops as extrasyllabic (see Chapter 1, specifically 1.3.1), that is, not part of a syllable proper, but rather licensed specifically by the following stop (sibilants occurring to the left of a sonorant behave as expected according to sonority sequencing). While for him this status applies word-internally as well, the evidence he adduces to bolster the claim—perfect stem reduplication, the inventory of possible word-final consonants as compared to other Indo-European languages, sandhi of final s before an initial sO cluster—is based only on facts and phenomena pertaining to word-initial or final positions. As we have already seen in the beginning of this chapter, however, what occurs initially (or finally) need not directly correspond to what occurs word-medially. As such it is difficult to be fully convinced by Kobayashi’s position.48 To be sure, Kobayashi’s extension of extrasyllabicity to word-internal syllable structure allows him to maintain a unified account of syllabification across the word, at edges and medially. However, this result comes at the cost of positing an additional layer in the syllabification process, to account for the behavior of sibilants with respect to weight-based phenomena such as meter. The resulting account would seem to be the theoretically more elegant one: if these three domains have to differ at some level anyway (at least with respect to metrical practice), then why posit any level of syllabification other than that at which they do so?
### 2.2.4 Summary
In view of the evidence for the concerted dispreference of long vowels followed by two consonants and short vowels followed by three consonants, which together can be analyzed as superheavy syllables, as well as based on the actual distribution of triconsonantal sequences in Vedic, which are exhaustively predicted by strong sonority sequencing, we propose the syllabifications VRO.OV, VO.ORV, and VRO.RV to hold for the language. Vedic syllabification, then, can be characterized as a system which in general favors simple onsets to the extent that complex onsets surface only when a complex coda is militated against by sonority sequencing (that is, in the case of VO.ORV). As we will see in the next section, however, this generalization does not extend into every domain.
