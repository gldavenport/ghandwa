# Chapter 10. Implications and Typology of the Phonological Analysis of Sonorant Syllabicity
## 10.0 Introduction
In this chapter we examine a sampling of the implications of the account of Proto-Indo-European nucleus selection and sonorant vocalization developed in Chapter 8. First, in 10.1 we reconsider the exceptional cases identified by Schindler (1977b), and examine how they may be accounted for under our approach. We then proceed in 10.2 to introduce data from multiple unrelated languages which also feature syllabic consonants, in the interests of situating the Proto-Indo-European phenomenon within a more explicit typology. We conclude in 10.3.
## 10.1 Schindler’s Exceptions Revisited

which evaluates output candidates from a purely phonological standpoint (as
opposed to the morphophonological approach entertained in Chapter 9), we
return now to the exceptional cases noted by Schindler (1977b), and assess
their treatment within this framework. These five cases are repeated in (1) for
convenience.

### 10.1.1 Nasal-Infix Presents: Evidence for Input Moraicity?
While we concluded in the previous chapter that because the need to posit input moraicity for sonorants rested on a single case (exemplified by *per-u̯r̥), the justification for a derivational component in the analysis was less than compelling, there is at least one additional set of data, the nasal-infix presents, which is ostensibly amenable to such a strategy. This will be confirmed when we encounter the limitations of the phonological approach to nucleus selection; while it can account for some forms, provided the appropriate conditions, there are others it cannot account for. Despite this unsatisfying outcome, we argue that the desired result can
indeed be obtained, if we make two adjustments to the analysis, one to our
understanding of one of the active constraints, the other to the constraint
ranking itself. Regarding the first of these modifications, we have to revise our
understanding of Sonority-Sequencing in Proto-Indo-European, the constraint that, as explicitly defined, militates against complex onsets of falling
or flat sonority (e.g. .RO-, .OO-) and complex codas of rising or flat sonority (e.g. -OR., -OO.), but upon which, for the present purposes, we have also
placed the burden of accounting for the idiosyncratic tolerance observed

word-initially in Proto-Indo-European for complex onsets like *ml-, *u̯r-, etc. In particular, if we take as significant the fact that only labial sonorants are seen to occupy the first position in initial .RR- complex onsets, rather than conclude that they alone exemplify a broader pattern, then because it does not abide by this generalization, the candidates in (2a–c.i.) can be interpreted as featuring an illicit syllabification. For now we hold that such illicit structure would be formally ruled out by Sonority-Sequencing; but as we noted earlier in Chapter 8 (n. 50), it remains the subject of future work in this area to identify exactly how best to model this aspect of the system in Optimality Theory. As for the adjustment to the constraint ranking, we need to revisit the positioning of Onset vis-à-vis Align-L, *ComplexCoda, and Coincide. Recall from our discussion in Chapter 8 that we posited no crucial ranking relationships holding between Onset with any of these three other constraints, but rather these constraints fell into their respective tiers in the hierarchy by virtue of their interactions with other constraints (see the crucial rankings in (110) in 8.5); for example, Onset cannot occupy the top tier because it must be dominated by Sonority-Sequencing and *Pk/Obstruent, nor can it occupy the second tier, because it must be outranked by Dep-IO as well (which itself must be outranked by *Pk/Obstruent). Chiefly, there are nasal-infix presents in which a root sonorant vocalizes, which is actually prevocalic in an associated full-grade. Given our current hypothesis, there should be no reason why this sonorant would be preferentially vocalized over the nasal of the infix. The nasal-infix present built to the root *u̯ebh‘wrap, weave’ (LIV 658; IEW 1114) provides a particularly interesting example in this regard: the strong form is reconstructed as *u-né-bh- (Ved. unap), the weak form as *u-n-bh- (Ved. aumbhan). ## 10.2 The Typology of Proto-Indo-European Sonorant Vocalization
In this section we take steps towards more explicitly situating the Proto-Indo-European phenomenon of sonorant vocalization within a cross-linguistic typology. Indeed while there are at least three components to the Proto-Indo-European phenomenon whose status can be considered from this perspec­tive—the existence of syllabic consonants in the first place; the ­ostensibly leftward directional character of the vocalization phenomenon; the coexistence of this phenomenon with a heterosyllabic treatment of con­sonants—we will be most concerned here with the second of these. In fact, in his recapitulation of this phenomenon in his work focusing on the eastern dialects of Shuswap, Kuipers seems to recognize as much, characterizing the syllabic positions of sonorants as follows: “if T is any obstruent, any resonant adjacent to a vowel (a nonsyllabic, i.e., consonantal position), or word-final pause, then we have TR̥T, TRR̥T, TR̥RR̥T, word-initially RəT, RR̥T, RəRR̥T” (1989: 12–13). Kuipers’ statements about sonorant syllabicity in Shuswap could just as easily describe the same phenomenon in Proto-Indo-European. Yet we acknowledge that the generalization about sonorant vocalization in Shuswap is posited by Kuipers in view of its particular set of sonorant phonemes, as he analyzes them; this set differs from that of Proto-Indo-European in lacking a rhotic, featuring an expansive set of velars and uvulars, and also apparently distinguishing between true high vowels and syllabic glides—the former unconditionally stressed (p. 22), the latter not (compare pəp̓íʔse ‘snake’ versus sxylap [sxiláp] ‘hour’). Still, if we assume that what have been identified as nasals, liquids, and glides do not depart significantly from the cross-linguistic ‘norm’ (admittedly illusory as such a notion may be), then comparison of Shuswap and Proto-Indo-European is arguably valid.34

## 10.3 Conclusion
In this chapter we have aimed to highlight a few of the implications the proposed analysis of Proto-Indo-European nucleus selection and sonorant vocalization holds for the language. We first of all revisited in 10.1 the five exceptional vocalization cases identified by Schindler (1977b), and assessed the extent to which they find explanation within our system. We focused on two issues in particular, the nasal-infix presents and the nature of *m; while for the idiosyncratic behavior of the former we have pointed to a possible analogical explanation (despite an ostensible connection to the morphophonological account explored in Chapter 9, and some success in capturing the phenomenon using the phonological account developed in Chapter 8), the idiosyncratic behavior of the latter we sought to incorporate into our phonological analysis through revision of the sonority hierarchy relevant for Proto-Indo-European, and by extension, our conception of the Peak family of constraints operative in the language. Following this exercise, in 10.2 we took steps to situate the Proto-Indo-European phenomenon within a typological framework, reiterating the previously cited similarity to the language Micmac and showing that the descriptively right-to-left nature of sonorant vocalization is apparently matched by at least one language, Shuswap, and tentatively paralleled by another, Imdlawn Tashlhiyt Berber.
