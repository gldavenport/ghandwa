## 1. Introduction

The reconstructed morphology of the Balto-Slavic nominal system is essentially that of late Indo-European. It looks like Sanskrit with an admixture of features shared with Germanic. The problem for Balto-Slavic has always been verbal morphology − both reconstructing the Balto-Slavic verbal system and deriving it from that of late Indo-European.

### 1.1. Overlapping morphological isoglosses

The dialect divisions of Slavic are irrelevant to the discussion of Balto-Slavic morphology, but the division between East Baltic (Lithuanian and Latvian) and West Baltic (Old Prussian) is not. For some features, Slavic agrees with one branch but not the other. Most of the clear innovations are shared by Slavic and East Baltic against archaisms in Old Prussian.

## 2. Nouns

### 2.1. Grammatical categories

Balto-Slavic had singular, plural, and dual number. Since the dual is well preserved, Balto-Slavic is useful for the reconstruction of the late Indo-European forms of this number.

Balto-Slavic had masculine, feminine, and neuter gender. This system is preserved in Slavic and Old Prussian. Balto-Slavic had all the cases of Indo-Iranian except the ablative, of which formal traces remain. Both Baltic and Slavic have athematic stems, <i>o</i>-stems, <i>u</i>-stems, <i>i</i>-stems, <i>ā</i>-stems, and a residue of <i>ī</i>-stems. Slavic has <i>ū</i>-stems, and Baltic shows historical descendants of such stems. Baltic has feminine <i>ē</i>-stems, which are not found in Slavic.

### 2.2. Features shared with Germanic

Balto-Slavic and Germanic share the use of <i>-m-</i> forms of the dative and instrumental where other languages have <i>-bh-</i>. The Germanic “dative” represents semantically a merger of the dative, locative, instrumental, and ablative. In some instances, a Germanic dative is cognate with a Balto-Slavic instrumental. Whether the endings are innovations, preservations, or a mixture of both will not concern us here. They occur in both the noun and the pronoun, so it is useful to combine the discussions of these.

#### 2.2.1. Instrumental singular

Baltic has <i>-mi</i> in the instrumental singular of <i>i</i>-stems, <i>u</i>-stems, and athematic stems, and in nonpersonal pronouns. In Slavic, *<i>-mi</i> in the instrumental singular is limited to masculine and neuter gender. Stang (1966: 209) points out that the north-western Lithuanian dialects which differentiate between a shortened long acute <i>ī</i> and an original short <i>i</i> indicate an original <i>*-mī</i>. Zinkevičius (1966: 230) confirms this, but suggests that the vowel quality might be from the influence of the plural. Prokosch (1938: 269) reconstructs the ending of the masculine dative singular of the non-personal pronouns in Old English and Old Norse as *<i>-mi</i>. The dative of <i>*to-</i>, Old English <i>þǣm</i>, Old Norse <i>þeim</i>, is cognate with OCS instrumental singular <i>těmĭ</i>. The crucial evidence is the <i>i</i>-umlaut in Old English.

#### 2.2.2. Dative plural

Lithuanian has <i>-ms</i>, Old Lithuanian and modern dialects, <i>-mus</i>. OP has <i>-mas</i> alongside obviously secondary <i>-mans</i>. Slavic has <i>-mǔ</i>, which could come from *<i>-mos</i> or *<i>-mus</i>. Germanic has *<i>-ms</i>, which should not come from *<i>-mus</i>, but might reflect an instrumental in <i>*-mis</i> (cf. Skt. <i>-bhis</i>) or (less likely) a dative in <i>*-mos</i> (cf. Latin <i>-bus</i>).

#### 2.2.3. Instrumental plural

Lithuanian has <i>-mis</i>, with dialectal evidence that this is a shortened *<i>-mīs</i> (Zinkevičius 1966: 231). Slavic has <i>-mi</i>, which could come from *<i>-mīs</i>, but not *<i>-mis</i>. Prokosch reconstructs the dative plural of Germanic pronouns as *<i>-mis</i>, reflecting an instrumental plural.

#### 2.2.4. Dative/instrumental dual

For this case, there are no dual forms in Germanic. The ending for all stems in Slavic is <i>-ma</i>. Lithuanian has <i>-m</i>. Vaillant (1958: 39) notes that the vowels found in Old Lithuanian are <i>-i</i> and (once) <i>-a</i>. He further argues that the dual pronominal possessives <i>mùma</i> ‘our’ and <i>jùma</i> ‘your’ are old forms of the dative, providing evidence that the original form was <i>-ma</i>. The only vowel that could give Slavic <i>a</i> and Lithuanian <i>a</i> in final position is acute <i>ā</i> < *<i>eH₂</i>.

### 2.3. Balto-Slavic innovations

2.3.1. The sole purely Balto-Slavic innovation is in the instrumental singular of the <i>ā</i>-stems. This is found in the nominal and pronominal system. The original ending should have been <i>*-eH₁</i>. That, added to the stem in <i>-ā- < *-eH₂</i>, eventually yielded acute long <i>ā</i>, identical to the nominative singular. Baltic and Slavic added a nasal at the end of the ending. One possible attestation in Old Prussian is <i>rānkān</i> ‘hand’ (Schmalstieg 1974: 59). In Slavic and in East Baltic, the original ending is clear in the definite adjective. Lith. <i>ger-ą́-ja</i> has an orthographic nasalized <i>a</i> which reflects the pronunciation in Old Lithuanian. The normal <i>ā</i>-stem instrumental singular in OCS is <i>-ojǫ</i>. It has the nasalized vowel, but the ending is from the pronominal system. The original <i>-ǫ</i> from *<i>-āN</i> can be found in the definite adjective ending <i>mlad-ǫ-jǫ</i>.

2.3.2. Both families show a tendency to replace athematic endings with those of <i>i</i>-stems, especially with consonant-initial endings. The motivation for this was the phonological change of syllabic nasals to <i>i</i> plus nasal in the accusative singular and plural (*<i>-im</i>, <i>*-ins</i>). Since the motivation exists in both families, there is no guarantee that the process was Balto-Slavic, particularly since both families have residues of consonant-initial endings added directly to consonant-final stems.

2.3.3. A few other endings are specific to Balto-Slavic but not unique. The genitive singular of the athematic paradigm has the variant *<i>-es</i> rather than *<i>-os</i> (Old Lith. <i>dukteres</i>, <i>akmenes</i>, OCS <i>dŭštere</i>, <i>kamene</i>). For the nominative plural of the <i>o-</i>stems, Balto-Slavic has *<i>-ai</i> from the original pronominal ending *<i>-oi</i>. This gives Slavic <i>-i</i>, with special phonology for the end of the word. Lith. <i>-i < -íe</i>, found in the adjective, is probably the regular reflex.

### 2.4. Slavic-East Baltic innovations

2.4.1. In the neuter nominative singular of <i>o</i>-stems, Slavic and East Baltic have a zero ending (Slavic <i>-o</i>; Lith., Latv. <i>-a</i>), opposed to OP <i>-an</i>, the reflex of <i>*-om</i>. The East Baltic forms are only adjectives used as predicates of phrases and the pronouns <i>kas</i> ‘something, anything’, and <i>vìsa</i>/<i>vìsas</i> ‘all’ and could well represent a different process. For Slavic, the most likely explanation is that the pronominal neuter ending in *<i>-od</i> has been coopted for nominal use.

2.4.2. Genitive singular of <i>o-</i>stems: Old Prussian has <i>-as</i>, while East Baltic and Slavic have <i>*-ā</i>, the reflex of the old ablative (*<i>-o-ad</i>). The OP ending is shared with Hittite and most likely Germanic, judging from ON Sg N <i>dagr</i> ‘day’, G <i>dags</i>, which looks like Verner’s Law has been utilized to distinguish two originally identical endings.

2.5. For the rest of the inflectional endings, the Proto-Balto-Slavic forms are generally those reconstructed for classical Indo-European.

## 3. Adjectives

3.1. Adjectival inflection was originally no different from noun inflection. That pattern is preserved in old Slavic, and in the feminine inflection of East Baltic.

The basic adjectival inflection preserved in both branches is that with <i>*o</i>-stem inflection for masculine and neuter, <i>ā</i>-stem inflection for feminine.

Baltic has productive <i>u</i>-stem inflection for adjectives. The corresponding feminines are <i>ī</i>/<i>jā</i> -stems. In Baltic, the <i>ī</i>/<i>jā</i> -stem inflection simply replaces the <i>u</i>-inflection, rather than being added to a non-syllabic <i>u</i>, as in Indo-Iranian, e.g. Lith. <i>saldùs</i>, <i>saldì</i>, gen. <i>saldžiõs</i> ‘sweet’. Slavic extends <i>u</i>-stem adjectives with a suffix <i>-k-o</i>/<i>ā</i>, as in OCS <i>sladŭkŭ</i>, <i>sladŭka</i> ‘sweet’. Both Baltic and Slavic show evidence of having had <i>i</i>-stem adjectives. Old Lithuanian has traces of <i>i</i>-stem inflection (Stang 1966: 260−261) as in <i>daugime</i> [locative] ‘many’, <i>didime</i> and, in the comparative, <i>didęsnime</i> ‘bigger’. OP <i>arwi</i> [neuter nominative singular] ‘true’ is probably an <i>i</i>-stem. OCS has a few indeclinable adjectives ending in <i>-ĭ</i> (Vaillant 1958: 539): <i>svobodĭ</i> ‘free’, <i>različĭ</i> ‘various’.

3.2. The masculine/neuter of active participles had athematic inflection (Stang 1966: 262−267). Distinctive athematic forms are found in the masculine/neuter nominative singular, the accusative singular, and in Slavic, the nominative plural masculine. Elsewhere, in both Baltic and Slavic, one finds a stem extension in *<i>-jo-</i>. In dative absolutes, the old athematic dative/locative in *<i>-i</i> is found in East Baltic, and in Old Lithuanian the original athematic dual form in <i>-e</i> is still attested. Feminines have <i>ī</i>/<i>jā</i>-stem inflection in both Baltic and Slavic. Examples are: OCS present active participle, nominative singular masculine <i>bery < *beronts</i>, feminine <i>berǫšti</i> (with analogical <i>-j-</i> in the nominative), nominative plural masculine <i>berǫšte</i> (once again with <i>-j-</i>extension); past active participle masculine/neuter singular <i>nesŭ < *nesus(s)</i>, feminine <i>nesŭši < *nesusī</i>. Lithuanian has masculine <i>reñkąs < *renkants</i>, feminine <i>renkantì < *renkantī</i>, gen. <i>renkančiõs</i>. The Old Prussian inflection has merged with <i>i-</i>inflection outside of the nominative singular.

3.3. Baltic and Slavic added the pronoun <i>j-</i> to adjectives to form definite noun phrases. This may be an areal feature, or it may have been inherited from Balto-Slavic. It is technically syntax. The <i>j-</i> pronoun was still a clitic with flexible position in Old Lithuanian (Zinkevičius 1996: 119).

### 3.4. Comparison

The Indo-European comparative suffix *<i>-j(e</i>/<i>o)s-</i> is used in Slavic in unproductive comparatives. These forms had athematic inflection similar to that of the participles, with <i>ī</i>/<i>jā</i>-inflection in the feminine. OCS <i>bol’e < *boljes</i> ‘better’. The rest of the forms have zero-grade, which in Slavic is realized as <i>*-jĭs-</i>. The feminine nominative singular is <i>bol’ĭši</i>. The rest of the paradigm has a stem <i>bol’ĭš-</i> from <i>*boljisj-(o</i>/<i>ā)</i>. The productive comparative suffix has an <i>ě</i> < *<i>ē</i> preceding the *-<i>jes-</i>/<i>-jis-</i>, as in <i>starěiš-</i> ‘older’.

Old Prussian has a suffix spelled <i>-is-</i> (often losing the vowel) in adverbs <i>tālis</i>/ <i>tāls</i> ‘farther’, <i>toūls</i> ‘more’, <i>mijls</i> ‘lieber’ (Endzelīns 1971: 174). Endzelīns also cites the Latvian adverbs <i>labis</i> ‘better’ and <i>vairs</i> ‘more’ as possibly from the same source.

The comparative suffix in Lithuanian is <i>-esnis</i>, which may reflect <i>*-jes-n-</i>.

## 4. Numerals

### 4.1. Cardinal Numbers

For the most part, Baltic and Slavic start from the system of cardinal numbers in PIE, and innovations belong to the histories of the individual families.

4.1.1. Thousand: Gothic <i>þūsundi</i> and Slavic (OCS) <i>tysǫšti</i>, and <i>tysęšti</i> (probably reflecting <i>o</i>-grade and zero-grade ablaut) are feminine <i>ī</i>/<i>jā</i>-stems. OP accusative <i>tūsimtons</i> has probably been influenced by a presumed <i>*simtan</i> ‘100’. Lith. <i>tū´kstantis</i>, Latv. <i>tũkstuôt(i)s</i> go back to <i>*tūkstant-</i>. Endzelīns (1971: 183) cites an Old Latvian form without the <i>k</i>, which indicates that <i>k</i> was inserted before the <i>st</i>, a typical but not regular Baltic sound change. Zinkevičius (1996: 136) points out attested forms in Old Lithuanian with athematic endings (<i>tūkstantes)</i>. This would indicate an East Baltic *<i>tūstant-</i>. The East Baltic forms are still deviant. Stang (1966: 282), citing Kalima, argues that an older East Baltic <i>*tūšant-</i> is indicated by early Finnic borrowings. Trubachëv (1973) has suggested that <i>st</i> is a variant <i>k̑</i>reflex of PIE palato-velar.

The older etymology *<i>tūs-k̑m̥ tom</i> has difficulties: 1. The expected reflexes of <i>*sk̑</i> are not found. This could be eliminated with the assumption that the reflex of <i>*k̑</i> was restored at the morpheme-boundary. Then Germanic *<i>s-h</i> > <i>s</i>, while in Baltic and Slavic the <i>*s</i> could disappear before the continuant from the palato-velar. 2. Baltic <i>*m</i> does not normally assimilate to a following <i>*t</i> (Lith. <i>šim˜tas</i> ‘100’, <i>samda</i> ‘hire’). 3. The <i>o</i>-grade ablaut in East Baltic and (optionally) in Slavic is in need of an explanation. Vaillant (1958: 647−648) accounts for the lack of <i>m</i> and the ablaut by assuming an old -<i>nt</i>-participle from a stem <i>*tū-st</i>- ‘fatten’ (with the common -<i>st</i>- present suffix). Endzelīns assumes a secondary confusion with a participle. Any argument for an original participle must explain the failure of Slavic <i>*s</i> to change to <i>x</i> after the <i>ū</i>. If it is not a borrowing, the Slavic <i>s</i> should reflect a palato-velar. To get a confusion with a participle, the form should end in *<i>nt</i>, rather than *<i>mt</i>. The participial solution is attractive, at least as a reinterpretation. It allows for both an athematic <i>-nt-</i> stem and a derived feminine <i>-ī</i>/<i>jā</i>stem with zero grade ablaut. One way to get an <i>*nt</i> is to assume that the compound was originally indeclinable, and that <i>*mt</i> > <i>nt</i> in word-final position. There are no exceptions that could not be due to analogy. The second element of the compound could have been *<i>-k̑omt</i>, as in the Greek words for the decads. If the <i>m</i> assimilated, this could easily have been taken as a neuter participle.

4.1.2. Ten: The closest thing to a purely Balto-Slavic innovation in the number system is the use of the athematic *<i>dek̑m̥t</i>- for ten, replacing the indeclinable <i>*dek̑m̥</i>. The -<i>t</i>-stem exists in Indo-Iranian as well, in the meaning ‘group of ten.’

4.1.3. Six: Balto-Slavic, like Indo-Iranian, shows the reflex of the “ruki” rule in the initial continuant (Lith. <i>šešì</i>, OCS <i>šestĭ</i>, Sanskrit. <i>ṣaṭ</i>, Avestan <i>xšvaš</i>), which indicates a proto-form <i>*kswek̑s</i>. A variant form <i>*w(e)k̑s</i> is indicated by the Lithuanian derivative <i>ušės</i> ‘six-week confinement for childbirth’, which is related to a similar zero-grade form in the OP ordinal <i>uschts</i>/<i>usts</i>. The Lithuanian form may be borrowed from Old Prussian.

4.1.4. East Baltic and Slavic, as opposed to Old Prussian, apparently share a change of the initial consonant of ‘nine’ from <i>n</i> to <i>d</i>, e.g. OCS <i>devętĭ</i>, Lith. <i>devynì</i>. OP <i>newīnts</i> ‘ninth’.

### 4.2. Ordinal Numbers

The Baltic and Slavic words for ‘first’ show different suffixes (OCS <i>prŭvŭ</i>, Lith. <i>pìrmas</i>: <i>*pr̥H-wo-</i>, <i>*pr̥H-mo-</i>). For ‘second’, Proto-Baltic has <i>*anteras</i> (Lith. <i>añtras</i>/<i>añtaras</i>, Latv. <i>ùotrs</i>/<i>uotars</i>, OP <i>antars</i>; cognate with Sanskrit <i>ántaraḥ</i> Gothic <i>anþar</i>). Slavic (OCS) <i>vŭtorŭ</i> is presumably a distortion of the same form (Vaillant 1958: 654).

The other ordinals were originally formed by adding the theme vowel <i>-o-</i> (-<i>ā</i>- for feminine) with zero grade in the preceding syllable (Szemerényi 1996: 227). There was a later spread of a suffix -<i>tó</i>-, and a secondary tendency to make the ablaut agree with the cardinal. Balto-Slavic shows both tendencies but never added the <i>-to-</i> suffix to a stem that ended in <i>-t-</i>.

Three ordinals assume different forms in Old Prussian as opposed to Slavic and East Baltic. For ‘third’ Slavic <i>*tretĭjĭ</i> (OCS <i>tretii</i>), Lith <i>trẽčias</i>, and Latv. <i>trešais</i> share the initial sequence *<i>tret-</i> at least, and may share the entire stem <i>*tretij-</i>. They are opposed to OP <i>tirtis</i>, from *<i>tr̥tijas</i>. The latter form has a Sanskrit cognate <i>tr̥tī́yaḥ</i>. For ‘sixth’ OP has <i>uschts</i>/<i>usts</i>, as opposed to OCS <i>šestǔ</i>, Lith. <i>šẽštas</i>. For ‘ninth’ Slavic and East Baltic have replaced the initial <i>*n</i> by <i>d</i>: OP <i>newīnts</i>, OCS <i>devętǔ</i>, Lith. <i>deviñtas</i>.

The Balto-Slavic stems for ‘fourth’ and ‘fifth’ are *<i>ketvr̥t</i>- (Lith. <i>ketvir˜tas</i>, Old East Slavic <i>četvĭrtŭ</i>) and <i>*penkt</i>- (Lith. <i>peñktas</i>, OCS <i>pętŭ</i>). For ‘seventh’, PIE *<i>septmos</i> yields Old Prussian <i>sep(t)mas</i>, Old Lith. <i>sekmas</i>, and OCS <i>sedmŭ</i>. The word for ‘eighth’ has <i>m</i> from ‘seventh’: Balto-Slavic *<i>ak̑(t)mas</i>, Old Lith. <i>ašmas</i>, OP <i>asmas</i>, OCS <i>osmŭ</i>. This could be a Balto-Slavic innovation, although Indo-Iranian also has *-<i>m</i>-: Sanskrit <i>aṣṭamáḥ</i>, Avestan <i>astəma</i>-.

From dialectal IE <i>*dek̑m̥tos</i> we find OP <i>dessīmts</i>, Lith. <i>dešim˜tas</i>, OCS <i>desętŭ</i>; cf. Greek <i>dékatos</i>.

For ‘hundredth’ Lithuanian normally uses <i>šim˜tas</i> (no different from the cardinal). But an alternative form <i>šimtàsis</i> is a formal match for Latv. <i>sìmtais</i>, Rus., Ukrainian <i>sotyj</i>, Belorussian <i>soty</i>, Czech <i>sty</i> (Slavic <i>*sŭt-ŭ-jĭ</i>) ‘hundredth’.

### 4.3. Collective numerals

Both Baltic and Slavic have extensive sets of collective numerals, used for groups and with <i>pluralia tantum</i>. For numbers ‘two’ to ‘four’, Lithuanian and Slavic have similar stems, but they may differ in ablaut. Insofar as the Sanskrit forms are legitimate cognates, the lack of Brugmann’s Law indicates <i>e</i>-ablaut. We find Lith. <i>dvejì</i>, OCS <i>dŭvojĭ</i> ‘two’, Sanskrit <i>dvayáḥ</i> ‘twofold’; Lith. <i>trejì</i>, OCS <i>trojĭ</i> ‘three each’, Sanskrit <i>trayáḥ</i> ‘threefold’; Lith. <i>ketverì</i>, OCS <i>četvorŭ</i> ‘fourfold’, Sanskrit <i>catvaram</i> ‘quadrangular area’.

## 5. Gendered pronouns

### 5.1. Interesting archaisms

The inflectional system of demonstrative, interrogative/indefinite, and relative pronouns in Indo-European was a single system with two variants − sometimes called <i>o</i>-stem and <i>i</i>-stem inflection. The notion of “stem”, however, is not as clear in pronouns as it is in nouns. Both types exist in Slavic. Example paradigms (for <i>onŭ</i> and <i>sĭ)</i> can be found in Diels (1963: 206−209). The Slavic stem variation is very close to the proto-system postulated by Szemerényi (1996: 205−207), except for the merger of gender in the oblique plural. A presumed original *<i>o</i>/<i>e</i>-ablaut has been reduced to a unified -<i>o</i>- in the <i>o</i>-stem pronouns, but the <i>i</i>-stem pronouns, which always seem to have been suppletive *<i>i</i>/<i>e</i>-stems (cf. Skt. <i>imam</i> but <i>asmai</i>), maintain this distinction, while the non-singular is characterized by an extension in <i>*-o-i-</i>. The <i>i</i>-stems have ablaut variants in *<i>-i-</i> and <i>-ei-</i>. Outside of the nominative and accusative cases, <i>-e-</i> and <i>-ei-</i> correspond to <i>-o-</i> and <i>-oi-</i> in distribution.

In Indo-European, the anaphoric pronoun *<i>is</i> and the interrogative-indefinite *<i>kʷis</i> were “<i>i</i>-stems.” In Slavic, the demonstrative <i>sĭ < *k̑is</i> ‘this’ has this inflection. The interrogative/indefinite has <i>i</i>-inflection in the neuter <i>čĭ-to</i>. There is no plural of <i>čĭ-to</i>, and the anaphor <i>*is</i> merged with the relative *<i>jos</i>, so the only complete example of the Slavic <i>-i-</i>/<i>-e-</i>/<i>-ei-</i> inflection is that of <i>sĭ</i>.

Modern East Baltic has lost the <i>-i-</i>/<i>-e-</i>/<i>-ei-</i> paradigm outside of the nominative/accusative. The interrogative *<i>kʷi-</i> was replaced everywhere by the competing form *<i>kʷo-</i>, and *<i>i-</i> was inflected as <i>jo-</i>. The anaphor *<i>is</i> merged with *<i>jos</i>. Old Prussian has no descendant of <i>is</i>, has only <i>kas</i>, <i>ka</i> as interrogative, and has the reflex of <i>*k̑j-</i> in <i>schis</i> ‘this’. This, along with the archaic look of the Slavic paradigm, might lead us to view the Slavic paradigm as reflecting the oldest Balto-Slavic stage. However, Stang (1966: 233) points out that Daukša’s <i>Postilla</i> (from 1599) has <i>iime</i> seven times for the locative of <i>jìs</i>, versus 77 times for the regular <i>iame</i>. There is also a dialectal <i>šimè</i> for the locative of <i>šìs</i>. Old Latvian has dative <i>šim</i> in competition with <i>šam</i>. If we trust Old Prussian orthography, we find <i>i</i> in masculine dative singular <i>schismu</i>, feminine dative singular <i>schissai</i>, and genitive singular <i>schisses</i>. These are all forms which in Slavic have <i>je-</i>, <i>se-</i>, and for which Indo-Iranian has <i>-a-</i>. If there is no internal Baltic explanation for these forms, they complicate the reconstruction of the paradigm.

### 5.2. Balto-Slavic-Germanic innovations

#### 5.2.1. For <i>-m-</i> endings, see (2.1)

### 5.3. Possible Balto-Slavic Innovations

#### 5.3.1. The merger of the relative <i>jo-</i>/<i>ja</i>̄<i>-</i> and the <i>-i-</i>/<i>-e-</i>/<i>-ei-</i> anaphoric pronoun

Although the Slavic forms are ambiguous, both Slavic and East Baltic can be said to have masculine nominative, accusative singular *<i>jis</i>, <i>*jim</i>; feminine nominative singular <i>*jī</i>, with the rest of the forms for the masculine and neuter from <i>*jo-</i>, the feminine from <i>*jā</i>.

5.3.2. The suppletion in the demonstrative <i>*so</i>, <i>sā</i>, <i>tod</i> has been eliminated and replaced with consistent <i>to-</i>. This pronoun is not directly attested in Old Prussian, but the third person singular verbal ending <i>-ts</i> is surely from <i>tas</i> (Stang 1966: 232).

5.3.3. A lexical innovation in Balto-Slavic pronouns is the word for ‘all’, which has pronominal inflection, as it does, for example, in Sanskrit: OCS <i>vĭsĭ</i>, Lith. <i>vìsas</i>, Latv. <i>viss</i>, OPr. <i>wissa</i>.

5.4. Slavic and East Baltic eliminated the <i>s</i> of the -<i>sm-</i> in the dative-locative of the masculine and neuter pronominal declension. Slavic also eliminated the <i>s</i> from the -<i>sj-</i>in the corresponding feminine forms. East Baltic generalized the noun endings in the feminine, so it is impossible to tell if it once shared this change.

## 6. Personal pronouns

The history of the personal pronouns is difficult, and the proto-forms for Balto-Slavic are uncertain. The following forms have cognates outside of Balto-Slavic and are thus good candidates for Balto-Slavic forms:

### 6.1. Singular

In the nominative, OCS first person <i>jazŭ</i> is phonologically regular from <i>*eg̑ -Hom</i>, found in Sanskrit <i>aham</i>. Old Lith. <i>eš</i>, OP and Latv. <i>es</i> are phonologically regular from <i>*eg̑</i> found in Gothic <i>ik</i>, if we assume that final devoicing preceded Winter’s law in Balto-Slavic. OCS second person <i>ty</i>, Lith. <i>tù</i>, Latv. <i>tu</i>, and OP <i>tōu</i> all come from PIE <i>*tū</i>.

For the accusative, OCS <i>mę</i>, <i>tę</i>, and reflexive <i>sę</i> are cognate with OP <i>mien</i>, <i>tien</i>, <i>sien</i>. Sanskit <i>mā´m</i> and <i>tvā´m</i> are potentially cognate, indicating <i>*mēN</i>, <i>tēN</i>, <i>sēN</i>.

OCS genitive <i>mene</i>, Lith. dialectal <i>manè</i>, Avestan <i>mana</i> are potentially cognate, if we ignore the leveling of /a/ in the Lithuanian stem. In the second person and reflexive, OCS shows secondary <i>b</i> in <i>tebe</i>, <i>sebe</i>, while Lith. <i>tavè</i>, <i>savè</i>, Latv. dialectal <i>tev</i>, standard <i>tevis</i> have the expected <i>v</i> found in Sanskrit <i>táva</i>, Avestan <i>tauua</i>. The Slavic <i>b</i> can be from the dative. The Balto-Slavic forms are probably <i>*teve</i>, <i>seve</i>.

In the dative, the OCS clitics are <i>mi</i>, <i>ti</i>, <i>si</i>. Old Lith. <i>mi</i>, <i>ti</i>, Modern Lith. <i>si</i>, Latv. <i>si</i> are almost certainly cognate, indicating Balto-Slavic <i>*mei</i>, <i>tei</i>, <i>sei</i>. Sanskrit <i>me</i>, <i>te</i>, and Prakrit <i>se</i> are potential cognates, but the vowel in the Sanskrit diphthong is ambiguous. Greek has an <i>o</i> in <i>moi</i>, <i>soi</i>, <i>hoi</i>.

For tonic datives, there is no good candidate for a first person Balto-Slavic form. For the second person and the reflexive, we find OCS (South Slavic) <i>tebě sebě</i>, Old East Slavic (North Slavic) <i>tobě sobě</i>, OP <i>sebbei</i>, Old Lith. <i>tevi</i>, <i>tevie</i>, <i>savie</i>, Latv. <i>sev</i> < *<i>sevi</i>, cf. Latin <i>sibī</i>, Oscan <b><i>sífeí</i></b>. The East Baltic <i>v</i> is clearly secondary. We can reconstruct the consonants as <i>*tVbh-</i>, <i>*sVbh-</i>, but the vowels are unclear.

### 6.2. Plural

Szemerényi (1996: 217) makes the interesting suggestion that <i>*mes</i> is the original first plural nominative, and Greek <i>ámmes</i> < <i>*ṇs-mes</i> is a reduplicated <i>*ms-mes</i>. Whether Szemerényi is correct or not, the best candidates for Balto-Slavic nominatives are the Baltic forms <i>mes</i> (Lith. <i>mẽs</i>, Latv. <i>mẽs</i>, dialectal <i>mes</i>, OP <i>mes</i>), and <i>jūs</i> (Lith., Latv. <i>jū˜s</i>, OP <i>ioūs</i>). The Slavic <i>my</i> and <i>vy</i> must be secondary. <i>Vy</i> is from the accusative (cf. OP <i>wans</i>, which would correspond to Slavic <i>vy</i>) and <i>my</i> has been influenced by <i>vy</i>.

In the oblique cases, Slavic shows the closest relationship to other Indo-European languages. Like Latin, it used the stems <i>*nōs</i>, <i>*wōs</i>. The genitive plurals <i>*nōs-som</i>, <i>*wōs-som</i> and the locatives <i>*nōs-su</i>, <i>*wōs-su</i> yielded <i>nasŭ</i>, <i>vasŭ</i>, which were parsed as <i>na-sŭ</i>, <i>va-sŭ</i>. The oblique paradigms were then built on the stems <i>na-</i>, <i>va-</i>. Old Prussian has an oblique stem <i>nou-</i> < *<i>nū-</i>, perhaps originally the same as Slavic, but influenced by the second plural (<i>iou-</i>, <i>iū</i>-). The Old Prussian second plural, and the East Baltic first and second plural paradigms were rebuilt, influenced by the nominative cases in processes that belong to the history of Baltic.

## 7. Verbs

7.1. The verbal system was reorganized, so that the aorist-present opposition became a past-present opposition. Stem-formants associated with the present/imperfect stem, such as the <i>n</i>-infix or the *-<i>je</i>/<i>o-</i> suffix, came to signify a present stem. The <i>s</i>-aorist, preserved in Slavic, came to signify a simple past.

7.2. Baltic morphology has been crucially used to argue for a new reconstruction of the thematic paradigm, beginning with Toporov (1961). Watkins (1969) continued with an integrated view of the thematic paradigm, the middle, and the Hittite <i>ḫi-</i>conjugation. This view has been accepted to the point that the reconstructed thematic paradigm in Beekes (1995: 232) is essentially identical to the Lithuanian thematic inflection in the singular, except for the fact that Lithuanian has generalized *<i>-o-</i> as the thematic vowel. Crucially, the third singular of the thematic inflection is reinterpreted as having a zero ending. The second singular in *<i>-ei</i> < *<i>eHi</i> is also accepted as original. The zero ending in the third person is attested in Slavic as well, but it had previously been interpreted as secondary.

7.3. Both Baltic and Slavic preserve athematic presents: Old East Slavic <i>estĭ</i>, Old Lith. <i>esti</i> ‘is’; Old East Slavic <i>ěstĭ</i>, Old Lith. <i>ėsti</i> (<i>*ēd-</i> from Winter’s Law) ‘eats’; Old East Slavic <i>dastĭ</i>, Old Lith. <i>dúosti</i> ‘gives’. Old Lith. <i>dẽsti</i> ‘puts’, probably reflects a Balto-Slavic form. Old Lithuanian and dialectal Lithuanian have many athematic presents which have no counterparts in other Indo-European languages (Stang 1966: 310). Some of these may come from old perfects.

### 7.4. Balto-Slavic-Germanic features

7.4.1. Indo-European had a class of <i>n</i>-infixed athematic verbs, which are best preserved in Sanskrit. Most commonly these verbs are associated with root aorists without the infix. In the active these verbs are transitive, generally factitive verbs indicating a change of state of the object. Most Indo-European languages have reflexes of the active verbs. In Germanic, Slavic, and Baltic, however, they are primarily intransitive change-of-state verbs. East Baltic has a large class of verbs with an <i>n</i>-infix in the present, but with thematic inflection. Slavic has a few forms with <i>n-</i>infixed presents, again with thematic inflection. Most of the Slavic forms have an <i>-n-</i> suffix with thematic inflection. The associated Slavic aorists are typically thematic with no infix. With a few unobvious exceptions, Germanic has only suffixed forms, and Gothic is the only language that preserves (surface-level) thematic inflection. The most archaic forms, with cognates in more than one language, have zero-grade vocalism. Several scholars have suggested that one could account for both the ablaut and the meaning by assuming that the Balto-Slavic and Germanic forms developed from the middle voice (Jasanoff 1973, 1978: 85 fn. 57; Darden 1996; Praust 1998: 79, 126). Watkins (1969: Chapter 8) provides a possible mechanism. He argues that the original ending of the third singular middle was <i>-e</i>, like that of the perfect. He further accepts the hypothesis that the third singular of the thematic inflection was <i>-e</i> with a zero ending. Using the identity in the third singular as the basis for analogy, he proposes a shift from athematic middle to thematic inflection. He uses this to derive thematic aorists, but the same arguments may apply to present stems. This process, applied to the athematic <i>n</i>-infixed class and to the associated root aorists attested in Sanskrit, could produce the thematic <i>n</i>-infixed presents of Baltic and the thematic zero-grade aorists of Slavic, which are the most likely Balto-Slavic forms.

Gorbachev (2007) accepts the middle solution for Balto-Slavic, which has no surviving middle, but he points out that the similar meaning and ablaut in Germanic cannot be easily explained, since Germanic does have an overt reflex of the middle − the Gothic passive (third singular *-<i>toi)</i>. His alternative solution is to start from what Jasanoff (2003) calls a “proto-middle”, with *-<i>e</i> as the third singular, and allow for a dialectal bifurcation between the proto-middle and the middle in the dialects underlying Balto-Slavic and Germanic. One could also propose that a third singular passive in *<i>-toi</i> separated from a middle in *<i>-e</i> in Proto-Germanic.

7.4.2. Balto-Slavic has a class of factitive-iteratives with infinitive/past-tense stems in *<i>-ī-</i>, e.g. (Rus./Lith.) <i>prosit'</i>/<i>prašýti</i> ‘ask’; <i>gonit'</i> ‘drive’/<i>ganýti</i> ‘take care of cattle’; <i>brodit'</i>/<i>bradýti</i> [dialectal] ‘wade’. The present tense forms differ. The Baltic forms have a present stem with *<i>-ā-</i> ([3rd. sing.] Lith. <i>prãšo</i> < <i>*prošā-</i>), while Slavic has a present in *<i>-ī-</i> (Rus. <i>prosit</i>). Neither present-stem suffix can be derived from the other, nor can either be derived phonologically from the *<i>-eje-</i> found in other Indo-European languages. Most research has focused on the present in <i>-ī-</i> (see Schmalstieg 2000: 130−136 for a survey). However, if we believe that this is a Balto-Slavic class, and we take as original the present stem that could not be derived by paradigmatic analogy, we would normally pick the Baltic present. Germanic Class II weak verbs, with an *<i>-ā-</i> suffix, are normally denominal, but have a few deverbal formations (Guxman et al., Vol IV: 180). Several of them have Balto-Slavic cognates or parallel formations among the factitive iteratives. These include: Old Saxon <i>frâgon</i> (denominal), Lith. <i>prašýti</i>, Rus. <i>prosit'</i> ‘ask’; Old Saxon <i>giwaldon</i>, Lith. <i>valdýti</i> ‘rule’; Old High German <i>lehhôn</i>, Gothic <i>bilaigōn</i>, Lith. <i>laižýti</i> ‘lick’; Old Norse <i>vaga</i>, Old English <i>wagian</i>, Old High German <i>wagōn</i>, Rus. <i>vozit'</i> ‘transport’; Old High German <i>sagōn</i> ‘say’ (from de Vries 1977), Lith. <i>sakýti</i> ‘say’, Rus. <i>sočit'</i> ‘seek’; Old High German <i>manōt</i> ‘understands’, Lith. <i>manýti</i> ‘think of’, Latv. <i>manît</i> ‘take note of’. There is also Slavic <i>kupiti</i> ‘buy’, apparently borrowed from Gothic <i>kaupōn</i> ‘conduct business’.

If the Baltic present formation is original, then it is the past/infinitive stem in <i>-ī-</i> that needs explanation.

### 7.5. Balto-Slavic innovations

7.5.1. Balto-Slavic formed past active participles with the suffix <i>*-us-</i>, which is used to form the perfect participle in Indo-Iranian and Greek (cf. also Gothic <i>ber-us-j-os</i> ‘parents’, originally ‘those having borne’, which is built to the weak stem of the preterite, i.e. the PIE perfect). Aside from the exclusive employment of the zero-grade of the original suffix *<i>-wos-</i>/<i>-us-</i>, the Balto-Slavic innovation consists of the emancipation of this form from the perfect and its implementation as a general past participle.

7.5.2. Balto-Slavic has a set of verbs with infinitive/past-tense stems with the suffix <i>*-ē-</i>and present tense with the suffix <i>*-ī-</i> in Slavic, short <i>-i-</i> in Baltic. Examples are (Old East Slavic: Lith.) <i>bŭděti</i> / <i>bŭditŭ</i>: <i>budė́ti</i> / <i>bùdi</i> ‘be awake’, <i>sěděti</i>: <i>sėdė́ti</i> ‘sit’. They are primarily stative. The infinitive stem can easily be related to the statives formed with <i>-ē-</i> in Germanic and Latin (Old High German <i>habēn</i>, Latin <i>sēdēre</i> ‘sit’) and perhaps to the suffix forming the Greek aorist passive. The present tense of these verbs has been claimed to come from the Indo-European perfect (Kurylowicz 1964: 80−84; Jasanoff 1978: 101−112; Darden 1998). Jasanoff (2003: 158−159), however, now traces them to athematic middles.

The clearest example in Slavic of an inherited perfect is <i>vědě</i> from <i>*woida-i</i> ‘I know’. It has the present-marking particle <i>-i</i> added, as does Latin <i>vīdī</i>. In Slavic, the rest of the paradigm is that of a regular athematic active. The infinitive <i>věděti</i> has a suffix <i>-ě-</i>. The forms of this verb in Old Prussian (Stang 1966: 313) show a mixture of athematic and <i>-i-</i> conjugation forms: second singular <i>waisei</i>, first plural <i>waidimai</i>, second plural <i>waiditi</i>. Endzelīns (1928) suggested that the /i/ in this case comes from the third plural *<i>-int</i> from -<i>n̥t</i> as is apparently the case in the Slavic 3<sup>rd</sup> plural <i>vědętǔ</i>. We could assume that Balto-Slavic, like Greek and Germanic, generalized <i>-nt-</i> to replace the original ending with <i>r</i>. For Baltic, we simply assume that the short <i>i</i> spread throughout the paradigm.

For Slavic, we have evidence from <i>vědě</i> that the particle <i>i</i> was added in the present. Starting from the third person singular and plural, we get singular *<i>-e-i</i>, plural *<i>-inti</i>. After Slavic <i>ei > ī</i>, we have <i>-ī</i>, <i>-inti</i>. Since long vowels were shortened before sonorant plus stop (Osthoff’s Law), <i>-inti</i> could be morphophonemically interpreted as <i>-ī-nt-i</i>, and the paradigm could be rebuilt based on a suffix-vowel <i>-ī-</i>.

Many of the verbs in this class have prototypical perfect meaning − a state resulting from previous action. In modern Lith., many can be glossed with the modern Lithuanian perfect − ‘be’ plus the past active participle. Other than resultant state, the range of meanings includes verbs of perception (Old East Slavic <i>viděti</i> ‘see’, <i>sŭmotrěti</i> ‘look’, <i>slyšati < *slūx-ē-</i> ‘hear’, Lith. <i>paveiždė́ti</i> ‘look’, <i>girdė́ti</i> ‘hear’), emotional state (Old East Slavic <i>bojati</i> ‘fear’, Lith. <i>mylė́ti</i> ‘love’), and verbs for the production of sounds and visual sensations (Old East Slavic <i>zvĭněti</i> ‘ring’, <i>svĭtěti</i> ‘shine’, Lith. <i>spindė́ti</i> ‘shine’). This is the same range of meanings as we find in the Greek perfect.

The ablaut of the roots in Balto-Slavic cognates is predominantly zero-grade. Between obstruents, as in <i>sěděti</i>, <i>sėdė́ti</i>, we find <i>*ē</i>, but the length here could be due to Winter’s law, which lengthens vowels before simple voiced stops. In an analogous formation with a voiced aspirate, Slavic has no length: <i>ležati</i> (*<i>legh-ē-)</i> ‘lie’. If these are indeed old perfects, the dearth of <i>o</i>-grade of the root is surprising.

In Old Lithuanian and in dialects, we find evidence that many of these verbs were inflected as athematic active verbs (Stang 1966: 310−318; Schmalstieg 2000: 88−103). Stang suggests that some of these verbs come from perfects, primarily because of their meaning. He also points out (1966: 315) that many of the athematically inflected forms cannot be phonologically old: <i>stovmi</i> ‘I stand’, <i>sėdmi</i> ‘I sit’, <i>girdmi</i> ‘I hear’, <i>žydmi</i> ‘I bloom’ all have consonants that would not be allowed before <i>m</i>. If <i>-mi</i> had replaced a vocalic ending, these configurations could make sense. It would also make sense to think that the original paradigm might have contained some forms that were identical to those of the athematic active. The perfect was athematic, and it had a vocalic ending in the first singular.

Since the parent language had neither short nor long -<i>i-</i> as a theme vowel, it makes sense to think that the original paradigm was athematic. Given the lack of cognates elsewhere, it is unlikely to have been an athematic active paradigm. It could have been an athematic middle, perfect, or the middle of the perfect.

Ultimately, it is the meaning that makes the perfect hypothesis attractive, and it is the ablaut that makes the middle origin attractive.

7.5.3. Slavic and East Baltic have a present passive participle in <i>*-(o)m-os</i>/<i>-ā</i>. The <i>-m-</i>is added to the present tense stem when there is a vocalic suffix. Athematic verbs take <i>*-om-</i>. Examples are: OCS <i>nesomŭ</i>, <i>xvalimŭ</i>, <i>vědomŭ</i>; Lith. <i>nẽšamas</i>, <i>gir˜dimas</i>, <i>sãkomas</i>, <i>ẽsamas</i>; Latvian <i>lìekams</i>, <i>darãms</i>, dialectal <i>gulims</i>. Old Prussian has one attested form with <i>-manas</i>: <i>poklausīmanas</i> (Stang 1966: 445−446) (but see Ambrazas and Schmalstieg, this handbook, 6.1). The Old Prussian form has cognates in Sanskrit <i>-māna-</i>, Greek <i>-menos</i> (normally reconstructed as *<i>-mh̥₁no-</i>). Vaillant (1966: 114) suggests a sound change <i>*mn > m</i> for Balto-Slavic, where medial laryngeals were regularly lost, but there is no other evidence for this sound change.

7.6. One of the problems for the claim of Balto-Slavic unity is the fact that in some cases the same form that represents an <i>s</i>-aorist in Slavic can represent an <i>s</i>-future in East Baltic. Both had athematic inflection. Brugmann (1888: Vol. 4, 365−366) argues that the Baltic future comes from the injunctive of the <i>s</i>-aorist. The aorist was an aspect, not a tense. The aorist indicative could only be used with past reference. However, the aorist injunctive could be used with various modal meanings, some with future reference. Kuryłowicz (1964: 111), who firmly believed that a formal distinction was necessary to separate the modal from the indicative uses, proposed that lengthening applied to the aorist indicative, but not to the injunctive, creating the formal distinction that he wanted. Kuryłowicz’s hypothesis is supported by evidence from Indo-Iranian, and it may fit the facts of Balto-Slavic. However, in general, the older the <i>s</i>-aorist looks, the more likely the Slavic stem is to be distinct from the stem of the Baltic <i>s</i>-future. This is discussed in detail in Darden (1995).

The overwhelming majority of the stems for which the Baltic <i>s</i>-future has potentially the same shape as the Slavic <i>s</i>-past belong to two classes. One huge class includes suffixed stems − denominals, iteratives, factitives − that in Indo-European had no aorists, much less <i>s</i>-aorists. There is, however, a small class of verbs that originally had root aorists, verbs like <i>*dō-</i> ‘give’, <i>*dhē-</i> ‘put’, <i>*bhū-</i> ‘be’. One member of this group, <i>* bhū-</i>, is the only stem that has a reflex of the <i>s</i>-future in Slavic: the Church Slavic future participle <i>byšęštĭ</i>/<i>byšǫšti</i>.

This may be a reasonable account for the development of Baltic and Slavic, but it does not solve the whole problem for Balto-Slavic. If the <i>s</i>-future really started as an injunctive use of the <i>s</i>-aorist, we still need an explanation for the initial differentiation of the stems.

### 7.7. The Baltic past and the Slavic imperfect

The Baltic past tense system was totally reorganized. There are two past tense suffixes, *-<i>ē</i>- and *-<i>ā</i>- (Lith. -<i>ė</i>- and -<i>o</i>-). In the formation of the Slavic imperfect, the element <i>-ax-</i> < *-<i>ās-</i>, followed by thematic inflection, is added to a stem that ends in <i>-a-</i> < <i>*ā</i> or <i>-ě-</i> < <i>*ē</i>. One view of the Slavic imperfect is that it is formed with preterite iterative suffixes <i>-ājā-</i>, <i>-ējā-</i>, and thus has nothing necessarily to do with Baltic (Kuryłowicz 1959). Stang (1942: 81−85) argued that the *<i>as-</i> was once a separate word. In early texts that show no tendency to lose <i>j</i> between vowels, there was no <i>j</i> between the vowel of the stem and the -<i>aš-</i>. Vaillant (1966: 63−68) slightly improves the argument. Once the <i>aše</i> is treated in this way, there is an obvious temptation to relate the Slavic stems in <i>*-ē-</i> and <i>*-ā-</i> to the past-tense suffixes in Baltic. This is an attractive idea, but it is very difficult to prove. Nevertheless, Slavic does have a suffix <i>-ě-</i> that specifically derives an imperfective stem, and there is an <i>-a-</i> that differentiates the past-infinitive stem from the present stem in a substantial class of verbs.

Lithuanian cognates for the Slavic verbs with the apparent suffix <i>-a-</i> in the past-infinitive stem lack the suffix in the infinitive. The present tense of these stems is most often directly cognate with the Slavic present. Examples are Lith. [infinitive] <i>piẽšti</i>, [present] <i>piẽšia (*peišjo)</i> ‘draw’; OCS [infinitive] <i>pĭsati</i>, [aorist] <i>pĭsax-</i>, [present] <i>pišetŭ (*peisjetu)</i> ‘write’; Lith. <i>giñti</i>, <i>gẽna</i>, OCS <i>gŭnati</i>, <i>gŭnax-</i>, <i>ženetŭ</i> ‘drive’; Lith. denominal suffix <i>-auti</i>, <i>-auja</i>, OCS <i>-ovati</i>, <i>-ovax-</i>, <i>-ujetŭ</i>.

With only two suffixes, we would expect fifty percent agreement with random distribution. We get considerably less than that − primarily because of the verbs discussed in the previous paragraph. Lithuanian regularly has <i>*-ē-</i> pasts associated with -C-<i>ja-</i>presents. Lithuanian is the only language with reliable information, and it seems to have undergone a reorganization to make the past suffixes predictable.

### 7.8. Common losses

Similar losses are not good arguments for common development, but are certainly possible results of common development. Along with Germanic, Baltic and Slavic lost the subjunctive, but retained the form of the optative. Both Baltic and Slavic lost the middle voice. A similar function is expressed through reflexive verbs in both families, but that is typologically so common that it cannot be argued that this was a common development.
