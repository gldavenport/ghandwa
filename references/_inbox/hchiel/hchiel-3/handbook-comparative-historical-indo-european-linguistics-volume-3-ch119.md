## 1. Introduction

In the historical period, speakers of Celtic and Italic languages were in close proximity. Lepontic inscriptions are attested in Northern Italy from the sixth century BCE, and Celts sacked Rome in the 4<sup>th</sup> century BCE. The expansion of the Roman empire led to closer contact between Latin and Celtic languages. Some of the latter were eventually altogether replaced by Latin, while Celtic loanwords can be identified in Latin, especially in the semantic field of horses and chariots (e.g. Lat. <i>uerēdus</i> ‘breed of horse’, cf. W. <i>gorwydd</i> ‘horse’). In addition to this contact, there is a longstanding hypothesis that the Italic and Celtic languages shared a prehistoric linguistic unity (i.e. an “Italo-Celtic” subgroup in the Indo-European family tree. It will be assumed here that Latin-Faliscan and the Sabellic languages, consisting primarily of Oscan, Umbrian, and South Picene, descend from a shared proto-language called Proto-Italic). However, unlike some other Indo-European subgroups such as Indo-Iranian or Balto-Slavic, the existence of Italo-Celtic has never reached the status of established fact. Compare, for example, Ringe, Warnow, and Taylor (2002), who posit an Italo-Celtic subgroup (although they admit the evidence is slender), with the criticisms of Isaac (2004: 54 ff.), who calls the Italo-Celtic hypothesis obsolete. The only yardstick for the sub-grouping of languages is the existence of shared innovations which are unusual enough not to have been parallel developments, but must reflect an earlier linguistic unity. The basic list of innovations put forward as evidence for the proposed Italo-Celtic subgroup has changed remarkably little since the discussion of Watkins (1966; with earlier literature), although approaches have differed in the light of new evidence and new theoretical perspectives.

## 2. Shared lexemes

Shared lexemes are the least reliable evidence for a proto-family, since the possibility of borrowing between languages is so high (especially when the languages were in close physical proximity in historical times). Collections of the (relatively few) lexemes unique to Italic and Celtic can be found in Meillet (1922: 37 f. and 1977: 34 ff.) and Weiss (2009: 466). The most significant are the prepositions discussed by Stüber (2012): *<i>dē˘</i> with ablatival rather than directive function (Lat. <i>dē</i>, OIr. <i>dí-</i> ‘from’, Lat. <i>in-de</i> ‘thence’, OIr. <i>dé</i> ‘from him’) and *<i>trāns</i> (Lat. <i>trāns</i> ‘across’, MW. <i>tra</i> ‘beyond’).

## 3. Shared phonological features

Phonological evidence for Italo-Celtic is slight (for examples see Meillet 1922 and 1977, Watkins 1966, and Schrijver 2006: 48 ff. − note that Schrijver’s proposed Italo-Celtic sound change *<i>CRHi̯V-</i> > *<i>CRīi̯V-</i> cannot be accepted: Zair 2009: 214). Most of the sound changes are too trivial to be considered evidence for shared development. The distant assimilation *<i>p … kʷ</i>- > *<i>kʷ… kʷ-</i> (*<i>penkʷe</i> > Lat. <i>quīnque</i>, OIr. <i>cóic</i> ‘five’) is phonetically unsurprising, and may have taken place independently in Italic and Celtic: *<i>perkʷu-</i> gave Lat. <i>quercus</i> ‘oak’, in which *<i>-kʷu-</i> > *<i>-ku-</i> took place after the assimilation, while the presumably Celtic name of the Hercynian forest (preserved in Greek authors as Ἑρκύνια) suggests that *<i>-kʷu-</i> gave *<i>-ku-</i> before the assimilation in Celtic (but see Weiss 2009: 465 f.). Another sound change which has sometimes been taken as evidence for Italo-Celtic (e.g. Kortlandt 1981) is the shortening of long vowels in some words in Italic and Celtic (Lat. <i>uĭr</i>, OIr. <i>fer</i> ‘man’ < *<i>u̯ĭros</i>, cf. Ved. <i>vīrá-</i>). But this occurs also in Germanic (Goth. <i>wair</i> ‘man’ < *<i>u̯ĭros</i>), and Italic and Celtic do not always agree in the forms which show shortening (Lat. <i>uīuus</i> ‘alive’ < *<i>gʷīu̯os</i>, OIr. <i>béu</i> ‘alive’ < *<i>gʷĭu̯os</i>). The conditioning environment for the change remains unclear (for one suggestion see Schrijver 1991: 225 ff., 334 ff., 512 ff.; but compare Zair 2012a: 132 ff., Matasović 2012), and it has been argued that it is analogical rather than phonological (Ringe 1988: 420). A more plausible isogloss is the development of *<i>CR̥HC-</i> to *<i>CRāC-</i> (Lat. <i>plānus</i> ‘flat’ < <i>pl̥h₂no-</i>, OIr. <i>lán</i> ‘full’ < *<i>pl̥h₁-no-</i>). But again the process does not seem to be exactly the same in Italic and Celtic (cf. the difference in vowel length between Lat. <i>strātus</i> ‘strewn’, OIr. <i>srath</i> ‘grass, valley’ < *<i>str̥h₃to-</i>; Zair 2012a: 69 ff.). Even leaving this aside, *<i>CR̥HC-</i> to *<i>CRāC-</i> could be a parallel development rather than a shared innovation, because a similar result is found in Greek (στρωτός ‘spread’ < *<i>str̥h₃to-</i>), although with colouring of the epenthetic vowel by the following laryngeal.

## 4. Shared morphological features

The main evidence for Italo-Celtic consists of shared morphology. There are four major similarities between Italic and Celtic which might be considered to imply a relationship: the superlative in *<i>-is-m̥mo-</i>, the <i>ī-</i>genitive of <i>o-</i>stem nouns, the “<i>ā</i>-subjunctive”, and the medio-passive verbal endings in *<i>-r</i>. Despite the superficial similarities, a direct connection between the Latin future in <i>-bō</i> (originally a periphrastic construction formed with the aorist subjunctive of the root *<i>bʰuH-</i> ‘be, become’) and the Irish <i>f-</i>future is very unlikely (despite Bammesberger 1979; for recent attempts to explain the <i>f-</i>future, see McCone 1991: 176 ff., Jasanoff 1994: 215 ff.). By far the most plausible of these is the first. Both Italic and Celtic inherited a superlative suffix *<i>-(t)m̥mo-</i>, restricted to pronominal and adverbial stems (Lat. <i>ultimus</i>, Osc. <b>últiumam</b> acc. sg. ‘farthest’ < *<i>ol-tm̥mo-</i>, W. <i>eithaf</i> ‘farthest’ < *<i>eks-tm̥mo-</i>). But the productive suffix in both families comes from *<i>-is-m̥mo-</i> (Lat. <i>pigerrimus</i> ‘laziest’ < *<i>pigr̥samo-</i> < *<i>pigr-is-m̥mo-</i>, OIr. <i>sinem</i>, OW. <i>hinham</i> ‘oldest’ < *<i>sen-is-m̥mo-</i>). The reason for the geminate *<i>-s-</i> in <i>-issimus</i>, the most common reflex of this suffix in Latin, is obscure (“expressive” gemination?). Cowgill (1970) compares the Old Irish double superlatives in <i>-i</i>/<i>a</i>/<i>e</i>/<i>mem</i> in the Milan Codex. The superlative suffix found in Germanic, Indo-Iranian, and Greek is *<i>-is-to-</i>: OE. <i>swētest</i>, Gk. ἥδιστος, Ved. <i>svā´diṣṭha-</i> < *<i>su̯eh₂d-is-to-</i>. This *<i>-is-m̥mo-</i> suffix is a clear-cut isogloss between Italic and Celtic, although in principle it could be a shared archaism, having been replaced by *<i>-is-to-</i> in the other three languages (the remaining Indo-European subgroups do not have morphological superlatives). For this reason Ringe, Warnow, and Taylor (2002: 105) do not include the superlative in their computation of Indo-European trees. What is more probable is that Proto-Indo-European did not have a morphological superlative category, and that some (proto-)languages created superlative endings (transparently based on the zero grade of the comparative suffix *<i>-i̯ō˘s-</i>). The Italic and Celtic superlatives could then be parallel creations, since *<i>-(t)m̥mo-</i> was already available (it appears in all the languages which show morphological superlatives). But *<i>-is-m̥mo-</i> stands a good chance of being a shared innovation.

The <i>o-</i>stem genitive in *<i>-ī</i> is rather more doubtful. It is found in Latin, Faliscan (e.g. <i>Louci Teti</i>), Gaulish, Lepontic, Irish, and the Brittonic languages (Gaul. <i>Segomari</i>, Lep. <b>aśKoneTi</b>, Ogam Irish MAQQI, OIr. <i>maicc</i> ‘of a son’, W. <i>Dineirth</i> (place name) ‘Bear’s Retreat’ < *<i>dūnom artī</i>). To posit *<i>-ī</i> for Proto-Celtic is plausible, but not certain, since Celtiberian shows a gen. sg. in <i>-o</i>, of debated origin, and early Lepontic also has a gen sg. in <i>-oiso</i>, perhaps from *<i>-osi̯o</i> (on the Celtiberian and Lepontic genitive singulars, see Eska 1995). Similarly it cannot be definitively reconstructed for Proto-Italic, since the Sabellic languages have generalized the <i>i-</i>stem genitive singular in *<i>-ei̯s</i> in the <i>o-</i>stems: Osc. <b>sakarakleís</b>, U. <b>katles</b>, SP. <b>kaúieis</b>. A problem with positing an Italo-Celtic innovation is that the <i>ī-</i>genitive may not be restricted to Italic and Celtic, but may be a Western European areal feature. It is probably found also in Venetic (e.g. <b>.u.r.k.li</b>), which may, however, be an Italic language. The poorly-understood Messapic, attested in ancient Italy, but neither Italic nor Celtic, has genitive singulars in <i>-ihi</i> (i.e. /-ī/?). However, in the <i>o-</i>stem gen. sg. <i>-aihi</i> the *<i>-ī</i> seems to be added to the thematic vowel rather than replacing it.

It has also been suggested (e.g. Klingenschmitt 1994: 375 ff.) that the Italic and Celtic gen. sg. in *<i>-ī</i> should be compared with the Tocharian A and B gen. sg. in <i>-i</i> found only in nouns referring to family-members (Toch. A. <i>pācri</i>, B. <i>pātri</i> ‘of a father’), personal and demonstrative pronouns, and personal names borrowed from Sanskrit with nom. sg. in <i>-e</i>. A similar system may persist in Albanian, where <i>o-</i>stem words for family members preceded by possessive pronouns perhaps preserve traces of an original <i>ī-</i>genitive, e.g. Old Geg <i>timett</i> ‘of my father’ < *<i>tosi̯o mei̯osi̯’ attī</i>. If correct, the Tocharian forms would show that the preform of the <i>i-</i>genitive was *<i>-ih₁</i>. However, the Tocharian <i>i-</i>genitive is found only in athematic forms, and should perhaps be better traced back to the athematic dat. sg. in *<i>-ei̯</i> (Pinault 2008: 487 ff. with references).

If the connection with Tocharian and Albanian is not maintained, a plausible source for the *-<i>ī</i> genitive is the so-called “<i>vr̥kī´ḥ</i>” suffix *<i>-ih₂</i>, which is used to derive nouns with the meaning ‘belonging to, pertaining to’ from <i>o-</i>stems, e.g. Ved. <i>rathī´-</i> ‘charioteer’ ← <i>rátha-</i> ‘chariot’. The same suffix is probably the basis of the genitival adjective *<i>-ii̯o-</i> < *<i>-ih₂-o-</i> (Ved. <i>pítriya-</i> ‘paternal’). If this derivational origin of the <i>ī-</i>genitive is correct, it would explain why we still find traces of the inherited <i>o-</i>stem gen. sg. *<i>-osi̯o</i> (cf. Ved. <i>-asya</i>) in early Latin and Faliscan inscriptions (Lat. <i>Popliosio Valesiosio</i>, Fal. <i>Kaisiosio</i>) and in Lepontic (<i>χosioiso</i>).

With this picture in mind, it should be noted that the shared innovation between Italic and Celtic cannot be said to be the creation <i>de nihilo</i> of the <i>ī-</i>genitive, but rather the reinterpretation of forms in <i>-ī</i> as genitival alongside inherited *<i>-osi̯o</i> (but the eventual complete integration of *<i>-ī</i> into the <i>o-</i>stem paradigm occurred only within the recorded history of Latin, Faliscan, and Lepontic). Although it is striking that this happened only in Celtic and Italic, the possibility of a parallel rather than shared development becomes more plausible: indeed, it is possible that it occurred only in Latin and Faliscan, and that the Sabellic languages never possessed a gen. sg. in *<i>-ī</i>; the same may perhaps be true of Irish, British, Gaulish, and Lepontic, leaving out Celtiberian.

Yet more uncertain is the status of the so-called “<i>ā-</i>subjunctive”. A formant *<i>-ā-</i> is the usual subjunctive marker in the 2<sup>nd</sup>, 3<sup>rd</sup>, and 4<sup>th</sup> conjugations of the regular verb in Latin (2.sg. <i>moneās</i>, <i>regās</i>, <i>audiās</i>), and is found in Faliscan (<b>douiad</b> ‘may [s]he give’), Oscan (<b>pútíans</b> ‘may they be able’) and Umbrian (<b>habia</b> ‘[s]he should hold’). Other subjunctive formations in Latin and Oscan can be traced back to the Indo-European optative (OLat. <i>siet</i> < *<i>h₁s-i̯eh₁-</i> ‘[s]he should be’, Lat. <i>amet</i> ‘[s]he should love’, Osc. <i>deiuaid</i> ‘let him/her swear’ < *<i>-āē-</i> < *<i>-eh₂-i̯eh₁-</i>), so it is usually assumed that the suffix *<i>-ā-</i> is originally optative, replacing the thematic optative suffix *<i>-o-i̯h₁-</i> (e.g. Gk. φέροιμι).

In the Celtic languages, an “<i>ā-</i>subjunctive” is attested primarily in Irish (e.g. OIr. ·<i>bera</i> ‘[s]he should carry < *<i>bʰer-ā-</i>). It is the usual subjunctive marker in weak verbs and in primary verbs is in complementary distribution with the <i>s-</i>subjunctive, which is largely restricted to stems ending in a dental or velar obstruent (e.g. <i>geiss</i> ‘[s]he should pray’ < *<i>gʷed-se</i>/<i>o-</i>). There are possible cases of “<i>ā-</i>subjunctives” in Celtiberian (<b>asekati</b>, <i>sistat</i>), but the context and meaning of these forms is too uncertain for them to be strong evidence. Gaulish has one possible example of an “<i>ā-</i>subjunctive” in <i>lubiías</i> ‘you should love (?)’ (but see Schumacher 2004: 53 fn. 46). The Brittonic languages, however, have a different system. Relic forms in Welsh such as MW. <i>gwnech</i> ‘(s)he may do’ < *<i>u̯reg-se</i>/<i>o</i> point to the existence of an <i>s</i>-subjunctive identical to that of Irish, but the productive formation is the “<i>h-</i>subjunctive” in forms like MW. <i>carho</i> ‘(s)he should love’, <i>dycko</i> ‘(s)he should bring’ (where *<i>-h-</i> has caused devoicing of the preceding voiced stop, cf. present <i>dwg</i>), MB. <i>maruhynt</i> ‘they will die’. This is traceable to a sequence *<i>-Vse</i>/<i>o-</i>, which would also give the Irish “<i>ā-</i>subjunctive” if the first vowel were *<i>-ā˘-</i>, because intervocalic *<i>-h-</i> < *<i>-s-</i> would be lost and *<i>-ā˘e</i>/<i>o-</i> would contract to *<i>-ā-</i>. This possibility was observed by Rix (1977), who compared the Insular Celtic *<i>-se</i>/<i>o-</i> and <i>-ā˘se</i>/<i>o-</i> suffixes to the desiderative suffix *<i>-h₁se</i>/<i>o-</i> seen in forms like Gk. τενέω ‘I will stretch’. This suffix is added only to roots ending in a liquid or nasal, *<i>-se</i>/<i>o-</i> being added after an obstruent. Alternatively, McCone (1991: 55 ff.) derives both Insular Celtic subjunctive suffixes from the subjunctive of <i>s-</i>aorists; the suffix *<i>-ā˘se</i>/<i>o-</i> is due to reanalysis of the sequence *<i>CeRH-se</i>/<i>o-</i> in laryngeal-final roots: thus *<i>CeRH-se</i>/<i>o-</i> > *<i>CeR-ăse</i>/<i>o-</i> → *<i>CeR-ăse</i>/<i>o-</i>. The expected result of both Rix’s and McCone’s theories would be a Celtic *<i>-ăse</i>/<i>o-</i> subjunctive; the only evidence for *<i>-āse</i>/<i>o-</i> is found in the Brittonic paradigm of the verb ‘to be’, where e.g. MW. <i>bwyf</i> ‘I should be’, points to *<i>bu̯āse</i>/<i>o-</i>. The lengthening of *<i>-ăse</i>/<i>o-</i> to *<i>-āse</i>/<i>o-</i> must be explained analogically.

Although McCone’s explanation in particular has gained support, the case for a shared Italic and Celtic “<i>ā-</i>subjunctive” has been taken up by Jasanoff (1994), who argues that the Brittonic suffix *<i>-ā˘se</i>/<i>o-</i> is the result of a secondary addition of the <i>s-</i>aorist subjunctive suffix *<i>-se</i>/<i>o-</i> to original *<i>-ā-</i>. Jasanoff points out serious flaws in McCone’s treatment of the Brittonic evidence, but it is nonetheless still possible to derive the Brittonic forms from an original rather than secondary *<i>-āse</i>/<i>o-</i> (Schumacher 2004: 49 ff.; Zair 2012b). A major plank in Jasanoff’s argument is MW. <i>el</i> ‘(s)he should go’, which cannot go back to *<i>elā˘se</i>/<i>o-</i>, but could come from *<i>elā-</i>. However, some old root aorist subjunctives are preserved in Celtic (McCone 1991: 115 ff.; Schumacher 2004: 48 f., 413 ff.). It is therefore possible that MW. <i>el</i> is also by origin the subjunctive of a root aorist, from *<i>pelh₂-e</i>/<i>o-</i> (for the root see LIV 470 f.). Alternatively, Peter Schrijver (p.c.) suggests a way to derive <i>el</i> from *<i>pel-ā˘se</i>/<i>o-</i>. According to him, Celtic subjunctives took the secondary endings, final *<i>-t</i> was lost early in the dialects which became Gaulish, Irish, and Brittonic, and subsequently final *<i>-e</i> fell together with final *<i>-i</i>, which was then also lost (Schrijver 2007). If all this is correct, original *<i>pelh₂-se-t</i> would give *<i>elaset</i> > *<i>elase</i> > *<i>elas</i> > MW. <i>el</i> with regular loss of final syllables.

The existence of a shared Italo-Celtic “<i>ā-</i>subjunctive” cannot be discounted, but the apparent similarities may be merely an historical accident.

Both Italic (Lat. <i>amātur</i> ‘[s]he is loved’, Osc. <b>sakarater</b> ‘[s]he is consecrated’, U. <b>herter</b> ‘[s]he should’) and Celtic (OIr. pass. <i>léicthir</i> ‘[s]he is left’, dep. ·<i>labrathar</i> ‘[s]he speaks’, OW. <i>cephitor</i> ‘it is obtained’, Celtib. <b>ne-bintoṛ</b> ‘let them not be struck [?]’, Gaul. <i>nitixsintor</i> ‘[?]’) have mediopassive verbal endings characterized by final <i>-r</i>. This is not a shared innovation, since Hittite (<i>artari</i> ‘[s]he stands) and Tocharian (<i>klyauṣtär</i> ‘[s]he is heard’) also have forms in <i>-r</i>. This suffix is found only in primary endings in Hittite and Tocharian, and was probably originally a primary marker equivalent to the *<i>-i</i> found in active primary endings, and generalized also to middle endings, by the other Indo-European languages, e.g. Arcadian Gk. -τοι, Ved. <i>-te</i> < *<i>-to-i</i> (for a summary of the Indo-European mediopassive endings see Weiss 2009: 387 ff.). Although the <i>-r</i> endings <i>per se</i> are not an innovation, there has been a tendency to look for shared Italo-Celtic aspects in their development. Cowgill (1970: 142) considers the spread of *<i>-r</i> as a voice marker into originally secondary endings to be a shared innovation. But in fact the only possibly shared secondary verbal category is the “<i>ā-</i>subjunctive”, so this is hardly a strong argument. Furthermore, if the Irish and British imperfect endings reflect the original secondary mediopassive endings (for which see Schrijver 1992), it seems likely that the distinction between primary and secondary mediopassive endings lasted as far as Proto-Celtic. The creation of 1pl. mediopassive *<i>-mor</i> in place of *<i>-me(s)dʰh₂</i> (Ved. <i>-mahe</i>, Gk. -με[σ]θα) is a better possibility, if it in fact took place in both Proto-Italic and Proto-Celtic (it is only actually attested in Latin <i>-mur</i> and Irish abs. <i>-m[m]ir</i>, conj. <i>-m[m]ar</i>). But the spread of *<i>-r</i> throughout the paradigm is unsurprising (note a similar process in Toch. 1pl. <i>-mtär</i>), and the similar basis in 1pl. indicative *<i>-mos</i> → *<i>-mor</i> may be coincidental.

Jasanoff (1997) has argued for an Italo-Celtic 3pl. primary mediopassive ending *<i>-ntro</i>, formed by blending the two 3pl primary mediopassive endings in *<i>-ro(r)</i> and *<i>-ntor</i> posited by Jasanoff for PIE. The Italic evidence for such a form comes primarily from the Sabellic languages. In Umbrian, beside the secondary passive endings in *<i>-(n)tor</i> found in the subjunctive (<b>emantur</b> ‘they should buy’) are also found primary endings in the present/future/future perfect (<b>herter</b> ‘it is appropriate’). In Oscan, the primary endings have been generalized (<i>uincter</i> ‘is convicted’, <b>sakraitír</b> ‘it should be consecrated’). In Old Irish, the 3<sup>rd</sup> person deponent endings differ from the passive in, among other things, never undergoing syncope of the preceding vowel (<i>gainithir</i> ‘is born’, <i>gainitir</i> ‘they are born’; cf. <i>léicthir</i> ‘is left’, <i>suidigtir</i> ‘they are placed’). One way to explain the Irish facts is to reconstruct 3sg. and pl. endings *<i>-(n)tro</i>, which may also explain the Oscan and Umbrian endings, if these reflect *<i>-(n)tro</i> > *<i>-(n)tr̥</i> > *<i>-nter</i>. However, the Sabellic evidence is more likely to reflect *<i>-(n)tir</i>, as suggested by the spelling variation in U. <b>herter</b>, <i>herti</i>, <i>hertei</i> (thus Meiser 1992, who proposes an independent development in Sabellic). The sequence of analogical changes required by Jasanoff to get from his proposed starting point to the attested Sabellic and Irish facts is in any case so complex as to require pre-existing faith in the existence of Italo-Celtic rather than to be evidence for it.

## 5. Conclusion

This concludes the possible cases of Italo-Celtic isoglosses. Despite the continuing debate, the question of whether there was ever a single Italo-Celtic language family remains open. Although there are a number of apparent similarities, very few can be shown reliably to reflect shared innovations. Only the *<i>-is-m̥mo-</i> superlative seems nearly unassailable; in the next rank of plausibility are the striking reinterpretation of *-<i>ī</i> as an <i>o-</i>stem<i>-</i>genitive, and perhaps the 1pl. passive ending *<i>-mor</i>. Whether this is enough to posit an Italo-Celtic subgroup is uncertain; if such a family did exist, it may best be seen as a “drowned” subgroup, the result of “a rather short period of common development followed by a long period of divergence” (Cowgill 1970: 114).
