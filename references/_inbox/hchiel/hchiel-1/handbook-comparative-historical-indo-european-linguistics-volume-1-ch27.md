## 1. Introduction

The present chapter will present only the main issues in the morphology of the grammatical categories indicated above with emphasis on historical grammar.

Old-Indo-Āryan [OIA] morphological elements are largely inherited from Proto-Indo-European [PIE] via Proto-Indo-Iranian [PII] (Proto-“Aryan”) and agree very well with those of Old Iranian (Avestan [Av.] and Old Persian [OPers.]). The oldest forms are represented in the poetic language of the *R̥gveda* [RV] (ca. 1200 BCE), followed by the *Atharvaveda* and other Vedic *mantra*s (ca. 1000 BCE−), almost all of which are in verse. The earliest prose is attested in the *brāhmaṇa*s of the *Yajurveda*-*Saṁhitā*s (ca. 800 BCE−), the *Brāhmaṇa*s (7ᵗʰ c. BCE−), and the oldest *Upaniṣad*s (6−5ᵗʰ c. BCE). The language of these texts is “Vedic”, and has been transmitted for the most part with accent. The succeeding stage of OIA is “Classical Sanskrit”, as dealt with in the standard grammar of Pāṇini (ca. 380 BCE). The “Epic Sanskrit” of the *Mahābhārata* and *Rāmāyaṇa* shows popular features. Vedic, especially the language of the *R̥gveda*, is the major focus for reconstructing PII and PIE, and for comparative IE linguistics in general.

## 2. Nouns

### 2.1. Inflectional patterns

We begin with a discussion of the inflectional patterns of the noun as inherited from PIE. The term “dynamic” refers to an inflectional pattern characterized by accented *-*e*-(rarely, **-o-*) vocalism in a particular position (indicated by □; other elements are in unaccented zero or *-*o-* grade). The dynamic scheme is based on a trisyllabic structure A-B-C which generally corresponds to root + suffix + ending, the canonical form of an Indo-European word. A formation without suffix (consisting of root + ending) is regarded as being of the form B-C. The “strong form” in a paradigm (e.g. nom. sg. in nouns) has *-*é-* (rarely, **-ó-*) in A or B; the “weak form” (e.g. an oblique case) has *-*é*- in A, B, or C. The place of *-*é*- in a weak form does not recede beyond that of the strong form. An *acrodynamic* pattern shows A-B-C in both strong and weak forms; a *proterodynamic* pattern shows A-B-C in strong forms and A-B-C in weak forms; the accented syllable in an *amphidynamic* pattern alternates between A-B-C and A-B-C in strong and weak forms, respectively; and a *hysterodynamic* type opposes A-B-C in strong forms to A-B-C in weak ones. The reconstructed endings (C), however, do not illustrate the whole range of possible ablaut grades, but some have only a full-grade (e.g., dat. sg. *-*ei̯*, nom. pl. *-*es*) or zero-grade form (e.g., acc. pl. *-*m̥s*, *-*ns*). These inflectional schemes also apply to the verbal formations.

### 2.2. Grammatical categories

Like PIE and other older languages, OIA distinguishes three genders: masculine [m.], feminine [f.], and neuter [n.]. Nouns consist of substantives and adjectives. The latter are declinable in three genders. Words designating male and female beings are m. and f., respectively; there are suffixes that allow derivation of feminines from masculines (“motion suffixes”) (cf. 2.6). Inanimate objects and concepts are m., f., or n., largely depending on the suffixal stem class to which they belong. Words for trees are m. (: *vr̥kṣá-* m. ‘tree’), herbs are f. (: *óṣadhi-*), and fruits n. (: *phála-*). Animals are represented either by m. (*śván-* ‘dog’ < **k̑u̯én-*, f. *śunī́-* ‘she-dog’) or f. (*gáv-* ‘cow’, ‘cattle’ < **gʷóu̯-*). OIA has three numbers: singular [sg.], dual [du.], and plural [pl.]. The du. is used to refer to two persons or objects in general, whether or not they constitute a pair. There are eight cases: nominative [nom.] for the subject; vocative [voc.] for addressing a person (or thing); accusative [acc.] for the direct object or goal; dative [dat.] for the indirect object or benefactive; genitive [gen.] to indicate possession or a partitive construction (or more generally, an adnominal structure); instrumental [inst.] for means, path, or association; ablative [abl.] to indicate separation; and locative [loc.] to indicate location. The vocative is unaccented unless it stands in sentence-initial position, where it always takes initial accent regardless of the location of its lexical accent.

### 2.3. Thematic and athematic inflections, terminations, and endings

The nominal stem carries the lexical meaning, and is in most cases bound to one of three genders. Every inflected form terminates with an ending which indicates at the same time number, case, and in part, gender. Stems fall into two overarching inflectional categories: thematic and athematic, depending upon whether or not a vowel -*a-* (< PIE *-*e*/*o-*) precedes the endings. Neuters are inflected like masculines except for having their own endings in the nom. = acc. of all three numbers.

Many nouns belong to the thematic type (-*a-* stem; -*a-* < PIE *-*e*/*o-*), e.g. *devá-*‘heavenly; god’: sg. nom. *devás*, voc. *deva*, acc. *devám*, inst. *devéna* (also *devénā*, *devā´*), abl. *devā´t*, dat. *devā´ya*, gen. *devásya*, loc. *devé*; du. nom.-acc. *devā´* or *deváu*, voc. *devā* or *devau*, inst.-dat.-abl. *devā´bhyām*, gen.-loc. *deváyos*; pl. nom. *devā´s* (also *devā´sas*), voc. *devās* (also *devāsas*), acc. *devā´n*, inst. *deváis* (also *devébhis*), abl.-dat. *devébhyas*, gen. *devā´nām*, loc. *devéṣu*. #### 2.3.1. Notes on the locative singular

A variety of forms occurs in the loc. sg. in the athematic inflections. The stems without ablaut have -*i* simply added in general (translations cited are those of the nom.), e.g. *uparátāti* ‘prominence’, *mánasi* ‘thinking’, *viśí* ‘settlement’. Otherwise, the suffix appears in the long-grade, e.g. *vásau* (*vásu*- ‘good’; cf. Av. *vaŋhāu*), *giráu* (-*ā´* + *u̯*) ‘mountain’ (cf. Yav. *gara*), *śúcā*, *°au* ‘pure, bright’; in *-*e*- full-grade, e.g. *dásyavi* ‘Dasyu, enemy’ (cf. YAv. *daŋ́hō* < **dahi̯au̯* ‘land’), *rā´jan(i)* (*rā´jñi* ŚB+) ‘king’, *pitári* ‘father’, *gávi* ‘cow’, *dyávi* (more frequently *diví*) ‘heaven’; or zero grade: *tanū´*, *tanúvì*, °*vī̀* (cf. YAv. *tanuui*) f. ‘body’, *br̥hatí* ‘high’, *nadī́* ‘river’ (*vr̥kī́*- type). Various forms occur in ‘earth’: *kṣám*-*i* (full-grade, cf. YAv. *zəmi*); *jmay*-*ā´* (zero grade + PIE *-*ai̯*, cf. Gk.. *khamaí*, YAv. *zəmē*); *kṣā´m*-*an(i)* (*-*o-* grade + *-*en*), *jm*-*án* (zero grade + *-*en*).

### 2.4. Nominal stem classes

#### 2.4.1. *-a-* and *-a*̄*-* stems, ablaut, root nouns, etc.

-*a*- stems show no ablaut, and -*ā*- stems (*-*eh₂-*) always have full-grade in the suffix (“mesodynamic”, if one speaks of the ablaut-scheme); however, some forms of the *ā*-stem paradigm are influenced by -*ī*- stems, e.g. inst. sg. -*áyā* (PII **-ái̯ā*) (cf. 2.4). -*a*-stem inflection has influenced some case-forms of so-called vowel stems (-*i*-, -*u*-, -*ī*-, -*ū*-, -*[t]ar*).

Ablaut is observed in stems in -*ánt-*/-*at-*, -*vánt-*/-*vat-*, -*mánt-*/-*mat*-, -*vā´ṁs-*/-*vás-*/-*úṣ-*, *án-*/-*n-*/-*a-*, *-ván-*/-*va-*/-*un-*, -*mán-*/-*mn-*/-*ma-*, -*añc*-/-*ac*-(/-*īc*-), -*tár-*/-*tr-*/-*tr̥-*, -*ár-*/-*r-*/-*r̥-*, some root nouns and elemental vocables, and partially in stems in -*ay-*/-*i-*, -*av-*/-*u-*, -*yā-*/-*ī-*, etc. The nom., voc., and acc. in the sg., the nom./voc. in the du. and pl., and the loc. sg. are strong cases in PIE, and this holds true as well for Indo-Iran. Neuter nouns predominantly show weak stem-forms in the nom.-acc. in the sg. and du., but strong forms in the nom.-acc. pl., partly with a long-grade which goes back to the collective. Many stems and forms have lost their postulated ablaut.

The root noun *díś-* ‘direction’ has columnar accent without ablaut. The accent position moves in the case of *vā´c*- ‘voice, speech’ between stem (root) and ending: nom. *vā´k*, gen. *vāc*-*ás*, but no change of vowel quantity occurs. Contrast Av. *vāc*-/*vac*-. The ablaut remains in -*hán-* ‘slaying (someone, something)’: nom. *vr̥tra-hā´*, gen. -*ghnás* < **gʷʰḗn-s*, **gʷʰn-és*. *pā´d-*/*pad-* ‘foot’ preserves its hysterodynamic (or amphidynamic) inflection: sg. nom. *pā´t*, acc. *pā´d-am* (whence a new stem *pā´da-*), gen. *pad-ás*, loc. *pad*-*í*, du. nom.-acc. *pā´d-ā*, pl. acc. *pad-ás*. The patterns seem therefore to be lexically conditioned. *dyáv-*‘heaven’ preserves its hysterodynamic pattern: sg. nom. *dyáu-s*, acc. *dyā´m* (Stang’s law in PIE), gen. *div-ás*, loc. *dyáv-i*, *div-í*, pl. nom. *dyā´v-as. gáv-* ‘cow’ points to an acrodynamic scheme: *gáu-s*, *gā´m* (Stang), *góṣ* < **gáu̯-s*, *gáv-i*; pl. *gā´v-as*, acc. *gā´s* (after sg. *gā´m*), *góṣu* (cf. Gk. *bousí*). Amphidynamic *náv-* ‘ship’ has fixed vocalism in the root syllable already in PIE: *náu-s*, *nā´vam* (**náh₂-u̯-m̥*, cf. Hom. *nē˜a*), *nāv-ás* (**nah₂-u̯-és*, *nēós*), pl. *nā´v-as* (**náh₂-u̯-es*, *nē˜es*), *nā´v-as* (**náh₂-u̯-m̥-s*, *nē˜as*), *náubhis* (*naũpʰi*) ‘ship’.

#### 2.4.2. -*ı*̄-́ stems

There are two main types of -*ī́-* stems. The *vr̥kī́-* ‘she-wolf’ type, consisting of root nouns and derived feminines from masculine -*a-* stems, as well as some masculine nouns, e.g. *rathī́-* ‘charioteer’, is based on the hysterodynamic inflection, e.g. nom. *dhī́-s* ‘thought, reflexion’, *dhiy-é*, *°*-*ás*, du. *abhi-śríyā*, pl. *dhíy-as*, *dhī-nā´m*. The *devī́-* type, including mostly derived -*ī́-* feminines, goes back to a proterodynamic pattern: nom. *devī́* ‘goddess’ (**dév-ī-*0̸), dat. *devyái* (< **dai̯u̯-i̯áH-ai̯*), abl.-gen. *dev-yā´-s*, du. nom.-acc. *devī́*, pl. nom.-acc. *devī́s* (instead of **déviyas* and **deviyás* or the like, respectively), gen. *devī́nām* (for **devyā´m*). These two types have influenced each other and developed into Classical inflections: monosyllabic, sg. nom. *dhīs*, acc. *dhiyam*, inst. *dhiyā*…, gen. *dhiyas*/*dhiyās*, loc. *dhiyām*/*dhiyi*, du. nom.-acc. *dhiyau*, pl. gen. *dhiyām* beside *dhīnām*, loc. *dhīṣu*; and polysyllabic, sg. nom. *devī*, acc. *devīm*, inst. *devyā*, … loc. *devyām*, du. nom.-voc.-acc. *devyau*, pl. nom. *devyas*, acc. *devīs*. *strī́-* ‘woman’ follows mainly *devī́-*: sg. nom. *strī́*, dat. *striyái*, gen. *°iyā´s*, loc. *°iyā´m*, but sg. acc. *°íyam* and pl. nom. acc. *°íyas* already in the RV; also the accent positions in *°ībhís*, *°ībhyás* (YSᵖ), *°īṣú* (AV+), *°īṇā´m* (AV+) are those of *vr̥kī́*-; in Classical Sanskrit, the pl. acc. is *°īs* beside *°iyas*.

#### 2.4.3. -*i*- and -*u*- stems

-*i*- and -*u*- stems often show full grade in the suffix: sg. nom. m. f. *śúc-i-s* ‘shining pure’, *mádh-u-s* ‘sweet’, n. *śúc-i*, *mádh-u*, dat. m. f. n*. śúc-ay-e*, *mádh-av-e*, gen. *bhū´res* ‘many’, *mádhos* (n. also *mádhunas*), loc. *agnā´*, *agnáu* ‘fire’, m. f. *mádhau* (m. n. also *mádhav-i*, n. *mádhu[ni]*), voc. m. f. *śúce*, *mádho*; pl. nom. m. f. *śúc*-*ay-as*, *mádh-av-as*, acc. m. *agnī́n* (< **-i-ns*), f. *bhū´mīs* (also as nom., -*ī-s* after -*ā-s*), ‘earths’, m. *mádhūn* (< *-*u-ns*), f. *íṣ-ūs* ‘arrows’, *dhe-nū´s* ‘milch cows’ (after -*ā-s*), only once *mádhvas*, n. nom.-acc. *śúcī˘*, *°īni*, *mádhū˘*, *°ūni*; also full-grade endings, e.g. in gen. sg. m. n. *mádh-₍ᵤ₎v-as*. Fem. -*i*-stems have also *devī́-* forms, e.g. sg. gen.-abl. *bhū´myās*, loc. *bhū´myām* (no **bhū´mes*, **bhū´mā*/*au*), dat. -*yai* (and -*vai*); this phenomenon increases in the younger language; also those of the *vr̥kī́*-type occur, e.g., du. *śróṇyau* ‘both parts of the hip’, *paṅktyàu* ‘two sets of five’ YSᵖ.

Some exceptional conservative inflections: *rayí*- ‘wealth’ (amphidyn.): nom. *rayís*, inst. *rāyā´*, pl. nom. *rā´yas*, acc. *rāyás* < **réh₁-i-s*, **reh₁́-̥i̯-éh₁*, **réh₁-i̯-es*, **reh₁-i̯-m*s; *ví-*‘bird’ (**h₂éu̯-i-s*, gen. **h₂u̯-éi̯-s*) with generalized weak forms of proterodyn. inflection: sg. nom. *vís* (as in usual -*i*- stems) or *vés*, gen. *vés*, pl. nom. *váyas*; *ávi-* ‘sheep’ (amphidyn.): *áv-i-s*, gen. *áv-y-as*; *arí-* ‘member of a tribe, chief; enemy’: *ar-í-s*, gen. °-*y-ás*, pl. nom. °-*y-ás*, but dat. sg. usually *ar-áy-e*; *páti*- ‘husband’ (:: ‘lord’) dat. *pátye* (:: *pátaye*), gen. *pátyur* (after -*tar*-:: *pátes*), inst. *pátyā* (:: *pátinā*), loc. *pátyau* (:: -*patau*); hysterodyn. sg. nom. *sákh-ā*, acc. °-*āy-am*, dat. °-*y-e*, pl. nom. °-*āy-as*, acc. °-*īn*, dat.-abl. °-*ibhyas* ‘follower, attendent’; proterodyn. sg. nom. acc. *dā´r-u*, gen. abl. *dr-ós* ‘wood’, *jā´n-u* ‘knee’. *krátu*- ‘mental power’ is based on the acrodyn. type: sg. nom. *krá-tu-s*, acc. -*m*, gen. abl. -*tv-as*, dat. -*tv-e*, only rarely gen.-abl. -*tos*, dat. -*tave*. Forms of both types occur in *vásu-* ‘adj. good, n. good’ and *paśú-* m. (Av. *pasu*-), *páśu-* n. ‘cattle’.

#### 2.4.4. -*tar*-, -*ar*-

Stems in -*tar*-/-*tr̥*- have generalized forms partially of acrodynamic and partially of hysterodynamic type: e.g., sg. nom. *hó-tā* (acro. root + hystero. suffix), acro.: acc. °*tāram*, dat. °*tre*, pl. nom. °*tāras* ‘Hotar-priest’; sg. nom. *sto-tā´*, acc. °*tā´ram*, dat. °*tré*, pl. nom. °*tā´ras* ‘praiser’ preserves only hysterodynamic accent positions. The stems for family relations have preserved a hysterodynamic paradigm except for gen. sg. -*ur* < *-*r̥s* of acrodynamic origin: e.g. sg. acc. *pitáram*, du. nom.-acc. °*tárā*, -*au*, pl. nom. °*táras* ‘father’ with -*tár*- < *-*(h₂)tér*-; *bhrā´tar*- ‘brother’ has preserved acrodynamic accent but not structure. Acc. pl. -*tr̥̄́n*/-*tr̥̄ n* goes back to PII *-*tr̥*-*ns*; gen. pl. is analogic -*tr̥̄ṇā´m*. − *devár*- ‘husband’s brother’ is inflected like *pitár*-: *devár*-*am*, -*as*, *devŕ̥ṣu. svásar*- ‘sister’ (gen. *svásur*, pl. *°sāras*, *°srām*/*°sr̥̄ṇā´m*) follows the acrodynamic -*tar*- pattern, likewise *nánāndar*- ‘husband’s sister’. *nár*- ‘man’ follows the *pitár*- type, but the cluster *nr*-before a vowel has been avoided (instead, *nár*-): acc. sg. *nár*-*am*, pl. nom. *nár*-*as*, acc. *nr̥̄́n*, loc. *nŕ̥ ṣu*, and dat. sg. *nár*-*e*, pl. gen. *nár*-*ām*, *nr̥ṇā´m*, cf. nom. sg. Gk. *anḗr* = Av. *nā* (*nára*-*s* supplies this case in OIA); f. *nā´rī-* with *devī́*- inflection.

#### 2.4.5. *r*/*n* heteroclites

-*ar*/*r*- (*r* < PIE **r* or **l*) and -*an*/*n*- stems build well-known neuter heteroclitic paradigms: hysterodyn. sg. nom.-acc. *ū´dh*-*ar* ‘udder’, gen. °-*n*-*as*; amphidyn.: nom.-acc. *yák*-*r̥*-*t* ‘liver’, gen. *yak*-*n*-*ás*; *áh*-*ar* ‘day’, °-*n*-*as*, °-*ān*-*i*; only -*n*- forms occur in ‘water’: abl. gen. sg. *ud-n*-*ás*, nom.-acc. pl. *udā´*, and loc. sg. °-*án(i)*. The word for ‘sun’s light’ shows hysterodynamic structure: *svàr* (*súv-ar*), dat. *sūr*-*é* (for **sū*-*n*-*é*), gen. *sū´r*-*as* (with artificial weak stem *súu̯r*-), PIE proterodynamic **sáh₂u̯-l̥*, **sh₂(u)u̯*-*én*-*s*. Sg. nom.-acc. *párur*, gen. °*uṣ*-*as*, loc. °*uṣ*-*i*, and abl. *párvaṇ*-*as*, loc. *°aṇ*-*i* ‘joint, joint-piece’ come from acrodynamic **pér*-*u̯r̥*, **pér*-*u̯on*/*un*/*u̯n̥*-; sg. nom.-acc. *dhánuṣ*, instr. °*uṣ*-*ā*, pl. nom.-acc. °*ūṁṣi*, inst. °*ur*-*bhis*, and sg. inst. *dhánvan*-*ā*, *°van*-*as* ‘bow’ from proterodynamic **dʰén**u̯r̥*, **dʰn̥*-*u̯én*-*s*.

The m. -*van*- stems (added also to root-nouns for clarifying the agent meaning) have associated adjectives in -*var*-*a*-, f. -*var*-*ī*-: *ī́ś*- ‘lord, master’, *īś*-*vará*- ‘capable of’; *śák**van*-, °-*varī*- ‘capable, skillfull’; *ádhvan*- ‘course, way’, *adhvará*- ‘(ritual) process’, *pī́van*-, *°varī*-, Ep. *pīvara*- ‘swelling, fat’.

#### 2.4.6. Nouns for body parts with -*i-* and -*an*-

Neuters designating body parts frequently show suppletive paradigms with -*i-* and -*an*-/ -*n*-, which might go back to local particles: sg. nom.-acc. *ákṣ*-*i* ‘eye’, abl. *akṣ*-*ṇ*-*ás*, du. *akṣ*-*ī́* (whence *akṣī́bhyām*), pl. nom.-acc. *akṣ*-*ā´ṇ*-*i*, inst. °-*á*-*bhis*; sg. nom.-acc. *sákth*-*i* ‘thigh’, pl. *sakth*-*ā´n*-*i*, du. acc. *sakthíyā´* (extended from **sakthī́*/**sákthī* < **°*-*i*-*h₁*). -*as*-stem with -*an*- suffix: nom. *śíras* ‘head’, gen. *śīrṣ*-*ṇ*-*ás*, abl. °-*a*-*tás*, loc. °-*án*, nom.-acc. pl. °-*ā´(ṇi)*: *k̑*r̥h₂*-*és* or **̥h₂*-*os*, *k̑r̥h₂*-*s*-*n*-*és*, **k̑r̥h₂*-*s*-*n̥*-*tós* (cf. #### 2.4.9. -*ín-*

The suffix -*ín*-, which appears in both primary and secondary formations, shows columnar accent without ablaut; the nom. sg. m. ends in -*ī*. Examples of primary formations are *arcín*- ‘radiating’, *javín*- ‘speedy’. This type frequently occurs as a final member of compounds, e.g., *abhy*-*ā*-*vartín*- ‘returning towards’, *uktha*-*śaṁsín*- ‘saying official speeches’. Examples of secondary formations from nouns (very productive, especially from -*a*- stems) include *keśín*- ‘characterized by hair’ (: *kéśa*-), *ūrmín*- ‘(always) waving’ (: *ūrmí*-), *hiraṇín*- ‘decorated with gold’ (**hiraṇ*-). -*ín*- is used to indicate that someone/ something possesses something or some quality as its essential, habitual, or characteristic aspect, whereas -*vant*- (with variant -*mant*-) designates also an incidentally possessed feature.

#### 2.4.10. *pa*´*ntha*̄*-*

The stem variations *pánthā*-, *path*-, *pathí*- ‘path’ are due to phonological developments from hysterodynamic **pont*-*éh₂*-*s*, *pont*-*éh₂*-*m̥*, **pn̥t*-*h₂*-*és*, *pont*-*oh₂*-*és*, *pn̥t*-*h₂*-*m̥s*, **pn̥t*-*h₂*-*bʰís*, with the change of **th₂* to *th* according to a general rule of OIA paradigmatic levelling of the aspirate. ### 2.5. -*tara-*, -*tama*-; -*(ı̄)yām˙ s-*, -*iṣtṭha-*

Comparatives and superlatives are expressed in two series. 1. The inherited secondary suffixes -*tara-* (PIE *-*tero*-) and -*tama-* (*-*tm̥-h₂-o-*) are added to nominal stems. Forms of this Indo-Iranian gradation system are in the RV rather limited, but increase more and more in the younger language: *tavás-tara-*, -*tama-* ‘stronger, -est’, *pitŕ̥-tama-* ‘most fatherly’; from pronominal stems *í*-*tara-* ‘another’, *ka-tará-*, -*tamá-* ‘who/what of two/ among many’; with pronominal inflection *út-tara-* ‘upper, further’, -*tamá-* ‘upmost’. *aśvatará-*, *°tarī́-* ‘mule, she-mule’ (AV+) can be interpreted as ‘one rather a horse (than a donkey)’. There are also forms with -*ra-* and -*má-* from prepositions and adjectives in -*a*: *ádhara-*, *adhamá-* ‘lower, -est’, *paramá-* ‘farthest, highest’, *madhyamá-* ‘central’. 2. The primary suffixes are -*(ī)yāṁs-*/*(ī)yas-* and -*iṣ-ṭha-*. The nasal in °*yān*/°*yāṁ*, °*yāṁs**am*, °*yāṁs*-*au*, °*yāṁs*-*as*, °*yāṁsi* is introduced from -*vant*-, as in the case of -*vā´ṁs-*/-*úṣ-*. Forms without *ī* are preserved in *jyā´-yāṁs-* ‘having priority, older’, *bhū´-yāṁs-* ‘more’ (and limited almost to the RV: *vás-yāṁs-* ‘better’, *náv-yāṁs-* ‘newer’, etc.), and in the forms °*e-yāṁs-* such as *śréyāṁs-* ‘excellent’, *préyāṁs-* ‘dearer’; but the majority show -*ī-* already in the RV, where *bhávīyāṁs-*, *vás-īyāṁs-*, *náv-īyāṁs-* etc. occur alongside the forms just mentioned. The suffix goes back to *-*i̯ōˇs* and its Sievers’ variant *-*ii̯ōˇs-* in the strong cases (with accented root), and *-*(i)i̯es-* in the weak cases (accented endings); f. *-*(i)i̯es-ih₂-*. Zero-grade *-*is-* is found in *máh-iṣ-ī-* ‘the first wife of a king’ (*‘the greater [wife]’), *mah-iṣ-á-*, -*iṣ-ī́-* ‘buffalo’ (*‘a bigger [bull, cow]’). The superlative suffix is built to this *-*is-* with *-*th₂o-* (beside *-*to-* in Gk., and presumably in Iranian; also in ordinal numbers). The suffixes are added directly to the root, e.g., *svād-ú*- ‘sweet’, *svā´d-īyāṁs-*, -*iṣṭha-* (cf. Gr. *hēd-ú-s*, *hēd-ī́ōn*, *hḗd-isto-s*;), *bhū´-ri*- ‘many’, *bhū´-yāṁs-*, °*y-iṣṭha*-; *a-śrī-rá*/*lá*- ‘not beautiful’, *śré-yāṁs-* ‘more beautiful, excellent’, *°ṣṭha-* (also /*śráyiṣṭha*-/, < **k̑réi̯H-isth₂o-*).

However, the inst. sg. is °*yáyā* beside °*yā´* as in the pronominal or -*ī*- stem inflection, and dat. °*yā´yai*, gen. °*yā´yās*, loc. °*yā´yām* after the -*ī*-stem; thus already in PII. [m., n.] *sthā´*-*s*, inst. *sthā´*, gen. *sth*-*ás*.) 2. is used also for derivation from many -*a*- stems (predominantly substantives in the *vr̥kī́*- and adjectives in the *devī́*- inflection), and always from -*u*- (from weak forms) as well as consonant stems (mostly in *devī́*- inflection), cf. also 2.4.2: *urvī́*- ‘wide’ (: *urú*-), *pūrvī́*- ‘many’ (**pl̥h₁-u̯*-*ih₂*-: *purú*- < **pl̥h₁-ú*-), *bhágavatī*-, *nā´rī*- ‘woman’ (: *nár*-). ## 3. Numerals

### 3.1. Cardinals

The cardinals ‘2’ to ‘1,000’ consist of inherited material. ‘1’ to ‘4’ are inflected by genders and cases. *éka*- ‘one’ follows the nominal -*a*- inflection with some (historical and inner-Indic) pronominal forms, e.g., in the RV: m. sg. nom. *ék-as*, acc. °*am*, inst. °*ena* (of pron. origin:: Av. *aēuuā˘*), gen. *°asya*, loc. *°asmin* (pronom.), pl. ‘some people’ nom. *éke* (pronom.), dat. *ékebhyas*; n. nom.-acc. *ékam*, pl. **ékā* in *ékam*-*ekā śatā´*; f. sg. nom. *ékā*, acc. *°ām*, inst. *°ayā*, gen.-abl. *°asyās* (pronom.). *d₍ᵤ₎va*- ‘2’ is inflected in the dual: m. nom. *dvā´*, *dváu*, acc. *dvā´*, inst. *dvā´bhyām*, gen.-loc. *dváyos*; n. *dvé*; f. *dvé*, *dvā´bhyām. tráy*-/*tri*- ‘3’ is inflected in the pl.: m. nom. *tráyas*, acc. *trī́n*, inst. *tribhís*, dat.-abl. °*bhyás*, gen. *trayāṇā´m* (YSᵖ+; *trīṇā´m* RV 1×), loc. *triṣú*; n. nom.-acc. *trī́(ṇi)*; f. *ti*-*sra*-/*ti*-*sr̥*-: nom.-acc. *tisrás*, inst. *tisŕ̥ bhis*, dat.-abl. *°bhyas*, gen. *tisr̥̄ṇā´m*, loc. *tisŕ̥ṣu*; multiplicative adv. *trís* serves sometimes for the cardinal. *catvár*-/*catru*- ‘4’: m. nom. *catvā´ras*, acc. *catúras*, inst. *°bhís*, gen. *°ṇā´m*; n. nom.-acc. *catvā´ri*; f. nom. acc. *cátasras*, inst. *catasŕ̥bhis*.

‘5’ to ‘7’, and ‘9’ to ‘19’ are adjectives without gender-difference; nom.-acc. has no ending: *páñca*-, *ṣáṣ*-, *saptá*-, *náva*-, *dáśa*- (nom.-acc. *dáśa*, inst. *daśábhis*, gen. *daśānā´m*, loc. *daśásu* RV). ‘20’ … ‘50’ are feminine collective substantives: *viṁśatí*-, *triṁśáti*-, *catvāriṁśát*-, *pañcāśát*-; inflected in the sg., but also pl. acc. in *tisráḥ pañcāśátas* ‘150’. ‘60’ … ‘90’ are f. collectives with -*tí*-: *ṣaṣtí*- ‘60’, *aśītí*- ‘80’ etc., sg. and pl.: gen. *saptatīnā´m*, *navatīnā´m*, acc. *navatī́s*. ‘100’ is n. collective *śatá*-, inflected as a nominal -*a*- stem in sg., du., and pl.; an indeclinable use is known only in the instrumental case, five times in the RV, e.g., *śatám ūtíbhis* ‘with 100 helps’. ‘1,000’ is *sahásra*- n. collective nominal -*a*- stem; attested in the sg. and pl.; there is also an indeclinable use of *sahásram*, many times in the instrumental. The counted things are expressed in the gen. sg., gen. pl., or appositionally in the same case.

Numbers intermediate between 20 and 100 are, in principle, expressed by dvandva (copulative) compounds formed by prefixing the accented unit to the decade, e.g., in the RV *tráyas*-*triṁśat*- ‘33’, *cátustriṁśat*- ‘34’. For ‘36’, a complexive compound with -*á*-is used: *ṣaṭtriṁśá*-. Other examples in the RV: *ékaṃ ca* … *viṃśatíṃ ca*, *tríḥ saptá*-, and *triṣaptá*- ‘21’, *trí*-*ekādaśá*- ‘33’, *tríḥ ṣaṣṭís* ‘180’, *saptatíṃ ca saptá ca* ‘77’, *catúrbhis* … *navatíṃ ca* (inst.) ‘94’, *navatír náva*, *náva* … *navatím* ‘99’, etc.

## 5. Verbs

### 5.1. Categories of verbal inflection

Every finite form terminates with a personal ending which designates a combination of person (1ˢᵗ, 2ⁿᵈ, or 3ʳᵈ), number (sg., du., or pl.), and diathesis (“voice”, active or middle/ mediopassive). Many verbal stems are inflected in the act. When middle endings are used in addition to them, they have a function of direct-reflexive (or reflexive: ‘asks himself’), indirect-reflexive (or affective: ‘asks someone in his own interest’, ‘gives something to himself’), possessive-affective (‘washes his hands’, ‘asks about his own obligation’), reciprocal (‘ask each other’), patientive (“passive”: ‘is asked’), or the like. Middle inflections are used also for instances where a subject’s state or its change is expressed. Some verbs are inflected only in the middle (*media tantum*).

There are four categories of endings: primary, secondary, perfect, and imperative. “Secondary endings” are fundamental, indicating only person, number, and diathesis, whereas “primary endings” designate in addition the present time (‘is asking’). Perfect endings indicate, together with the perfect stem, a state of the subject which it has attained as the result of a verbal action (‘has seen [and now knows]’). Special imperative endings exist for only 2ⁿᵈ and 3ʳᵈ persons.

#### 5.1.1. Aktions-, Verhaltens-, and Rektionsart

The verbal root conveys the lexical meaning of the verb. By means of suffixes, infixes, or reduplication, “tense stems” are formed. These include, in the first place, the present and aorist stems (aspect-stems). The future is morphologically only a special type of present. Some noematic categories belonging to the lexical meaning of the verb are relevant for the morphology:

1. Aktionsart [action type], i.e.,

a. the action signalled by a root or verb is *punctual* (without time extension), *momentary* (with short duration), or *durative* (with longer duration);

b. it is *terminative* (i.e. has a beginning or ending);

c. it is *iterative* (involves repetition), *inchoative* (in its initial phase; said of a pres.), or *ingressive* (already in process; said of the aorist and perfect);

2. Verhaltensart, i.e.,

a. a verb designates an action (*facientive* or *agentive*), change of state (*fientive*), or state (*statutive* or *essive*);

b. related to (2a), the action designated may or may not involve intention;

c. it may be *factitive* (e.g. in the causative) or *patientive* (e.g., in the passive);

3. Syntactic governance (rection): the action may be *transitive* or *intransitive*.

#### 5.1.2. Aspect and suppletion

The present stem is originally used for the imperfective aspect, i.e. the view of a verbal action in its ongoing phase. The aorist stem signals perfective aspect, the view of a verbal action in its closed phase, i.e. to express merely whether the action happens without regard for any of its internal phases. The perfect system designates a state of the subject which it has attained as the result of a verbal action (*nacto-stative*). If a root’s meaning has a punctual Aktionsart (“aorist-roots”), the root can serve as an aorist stem without any marking: athematic root aorist. Otherwise, an aorist stem is formed in the ways given in 5.5. A root having a lexical meaning with a distinctively durative Aktionsart (“present-root”) can function as a present stem: athematic root pres. If not, a present stem is formed in ways indicated in 5.1.3.

In extreme cases, suppletion (suppletive paradigm) occurs. The root *han* ‘smite, slay, destroy’ serves directly as a present stem: *hán*-*ti* ‘slays’, *ghn*-*ánti* ‘they slay’. Even if a repeated action is meant, no special formation is necessary, because the root itself includes such a meaning (noeme) which emerges as a relevant component from the context. *han* cannot build an aorist because of its extremely durative Aktionsart. *vadhⁱ*, a counterpart with a punctual Aktionsart, provides the aorist function: athematic root aorist *á*-*vadhī*-*t*. Likewise, the root *ay*/*i* ‘go’ has present forms *é*-*ti*, *y*-*ánti*, *ái*-*t*, *ā´y*-*an* ‘goes, they go; s/he, they went’, but it has no aorist. The latter is supplied by *gā*: root aorist *á*-*gā*-*t*, *á*-*gur. gā* means properly ‘stride’, i.e. ‘spread the legs’ and has its own present, *jí*-*gā*-*ti* *‘spreads the legs repeatedly’ in the RV. *as*/*s* ‘exist, be’ (*ás*-*ti*, *s*-*ánti*) has only the present stem, and *bhavⁱ*/*bhū* (**bhu̯ā*) ‘come into being, become’ provides the aorist for it: *á*-*bhū*-*t*, *á*-*bhūv*-*an*; the latter has its own pres. *bháv*-*a*-*ᵗⁱ*, the opt. of which is usually supplied by *s*-*yā´*-*t* from *as*. These phenomena are required by the logic rooted in the principle of stem formation. Some other examples of this principle: instead of *dá*-*dā*-*ti* ‘gives’, one finds also or preferably present *prá*-*yacchati* (*yam* ‘stretch and hold out [the arms]’); a factitive of *é*-*ti* ‘goes’ is provided by *náyⁱ-a*-*ti* ‘leads’ or *é*-*tave kr̥*‘make (someone) to go’. Some such cases are due to particular conditions: in the case of imperfect 2ⁿᵈ and 3ʳᵈ sg. of *ad* ‘eat’ (ind. *át*-*ti*), phonologically ambiguous **ā´t* is avoided and supplied with *ā´va*-*ya*-*s*, -*t*, supposedly of denominative origin. Meanwhile, *ad* ‘eat’ (< ‘bite, chew’ with iterative-durative Aktionsart) shows a suppletive aorist *ghas* ‘eat, consume, devour’.

#### 5.1.3. Present stem formations

There are many formations for the present stem. Athematic root presents from roots with durative Aktionsart correspond roughly to the 2ⁿᵈ class of traditional Pāṇinian grammar [A1]. In other instances, the present stem must be marked in order for the verb to function in the imperfective aspect; a marked stem-formation serves thus to modify (extend) the duration of the action which the verb’s root originally signifies: [B1] with a thematic vowel -*a*- (*-*e*/*o-*) appended to the accented full-grade root, when no change of the Verhaltensart is involved (1ˢᵗ class), e.g. *bhavⁱ-a-ᵗⁱ* ‘come into being, becomes’; [B2] with a thematic vowel -*á*- appended to the zero-grade of the root, predominantly for signalling a pushing or urging action of short duration, or for a factitive meaning (6ᵗʰ class), e.g. *tud-á*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘push’, -*tirá*- ‘make traverse’, *krīḍ-á*- ‘play, frisk’; *dáś*-*a*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘bite’ (*daṁś*) with secondary accent; [B3] with a themat. vowel -*a*- appended to the long-grade root to signal the terminative or iterative-durative function, e.g. *cā´y*-*a*- ‘perceive’, *dhā´v*-*a*- ‘run (towards)’; [B4] with the suffix -*ya*- predominantly built to the zero-grade root, when a fientive meaning (a natural process, non-intentional action, etc.) is additionally expressed (4ᵗʰ class), e.g. *dī́v*-*ya*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘gamble’, *páś*-*ya*- ‘see’, *gā´*-*ya*- ‘sing’ with full-grade root; *vac* -*yá*-*ᵗᵉ* ‘totter’ (*vañc*), *mriyá*-*ᵗᵉ* ‘die’, and *dhriyá*-*ᵗᵉ* ‘get ready (to do something)’ with passive accent; (*ava*)-*d*-*yá*-*ᵗⁱ* to *dā* ‘divide, separate’; *gr̥bhā*-*yá*- ‘grasp’ < **gr̥bʰ*-*n̥H*-*i̯é*- extended from the nasal-infix stem *gr̥bh-nā´-ti*; ### 5.2. Endings

The active endings in the “primary” series (for the present indicative and some subjunctive forms) are, in the order sg. 1ˢᵗ, 2ⁿᵈ, 3ʳᵈ; du. 1ˢᵗ, 2ⁿᵈ, 3ʳᵈ; pl. 1ˢᵗ, 2ⁿᵈ, 3ʳᵈ: -*mi*, -*si*, -*ti*; -*vas*, -*thas*, -*tas*; -*masi*/-*mas*, -*tha*/-*thana*, -*anti*/-*nti*/-*ati*. The more fundamental “secondary” series shows: -*m*/-*am*, -*s*, -*t*; -*va*, -*tam*, -*tām*; -*ma*, -*ta*/-*tana*, -*an*/-*n*/-*ur*. The primary middle endings are: -*e*, -*se*, -*te* (“stative” -*e*); -*vahe*, -*āthe* (°*ethe* in thematic), -*āte* (°*ete*); -*mahe*, -*dhve*, -*nte*/-*ate* (“stative” -*[i]ré*); secondary: -*i*, -*thās*, -*ta*; -*vahi*, -*āthām* (°*ethām*), -*ātām* (°*etām*); -*mahi*, -*dhvam*, -*anta*/-*nta*/-*ata*/-*ran*/-*ram*. 1ˢᵗ sg. act. -*mi* is added to thematic stems, where historically -*ā* < *-*oh₂* is expected (cf. OAv. ind. -*ā*); this latter is preserved in the subjunctive.

### 5.3. Moods

With the help of moods a speaker adds his attitude to the contents of what he utters: indicative [ind.], injunctive [inj.], and strongly modal: subjunctive [subj.], optative [opt.], and imperative [iptv.]. The indicative serves for reporting in general. The indicative present designates the action in progress or process. The indicative aorist with secondary endings expresses a concluded action with the help of the augment (*á*-). It designates an immediate, actual past, or states facts of the remote past. The Middle Indo-Aryan preterite is based on the aorist in the latter function, which occurs largely in speaking. There are also special usages such as ingressive (the action or state has already started) or designation of antecedence in time. The preterite of the present is the imperfect [ipf.]; it designates with augment and secondary endings the non-actual or historical past. The perfect indicative reports an attained state.

The injunctive is formed from present, aorist, or sometimes perfect stems, with secondary endings, so that it designates in itself only a person and a number (originally the aspect of its stem as well). Its principal function is simple reference (Erwähnung, “memorative”), used concretely to express general circumstances or the truth without limitation to some period, to represent data or someone’s qualification, to refer to a myth or affair that everybody knows, etc. With *mā´* ‘not, lest’, the inj. is used for the prohibitive sentence: the injunctive aorist for a preventive function (‘don’t X’), the injunctive present for the inhibitive (‘stop … X-ing’).

The augment is often lengthened before a root beginning with a laryngeal, e.g. *ā´naṭ* < **é*-*h₂nek̑*-*s*/-*t* ‘you have attained,... has attained’; thus a “vr̥ddhi” appears before a verb beginning with a vowel, e.g. *ā´s*-*an* < **é*-*h₁s*-*ent* ‘they were’ (*as*), *ái*-*t* < **é*-*h₁ei̯*-*t*, *ā´y*-*an* < **é*-*h₁i̯*-*ent* ‘went’ (*ay*).

#### 5.3.1. Subjunctive

The subjunctive is formed from the full-grade stem with the suffix -*a*- (*-*e*/*o*-); in the intensive and zero-grade thematic present, however, it is formed from zero-grade or weak stems. Its functions are prospective (future, expectation) and voluntative (speaker’s will, intension). Secondary and primary endings are used; in some cases, there are also elements making the form clearer. Act.: sg. -*āni*/-*ā*/-*a*-*m*, -*a*-*si*/-*a*-*s*, -*a*-*ti*/-*a*-*t*; du. -*ā*-*va*, -*a*-*thas*, -*a*-*tas*; pl. -*ā*-*ma*, -*a*-*tha*/-*a*-*thana*, -*a*-*nti*/-*a*-*n*; mid.: sg. -*ai*, -*a*-*se*/-*a*-*sai*, -*a*-*te*/ -*a*-*tai*; du. -*ā*-*vahai*, -*aithe*, -*aite*; pl. -*ā*-*mahe*/-*ā*-*mahai*, -*a*-*dhve*/-*a*-*dhvai*, -*a*-*nte*/-*a*-*nta*.

The forms in -*anta* in the 3ʳᵈ pl. mid. of the thematic present are originally injunctives but are used also as subjunctives in the RV, e.g. *bhájanta*, *tiránta*, *janáyanta* etc.; °*ā˘ntai* appears in Vedic prose. A new formation for the 2ⁿᵈ sg. mid. such as *nayāsai*, *paśyāsai* occurs from the AV onward, and 3ʳᵈ sg. *°āntai*/-*antai* YSᴾ Br.

Hypermarked forms such as *várdhₐās*, *áva-padāti*, *marāti* occur, and their number increases slightly from the AV on. Short-vowel subjunctives are formed in the reduplicating and -*nā*- presents, and athematic root-aor., e.g., *dadh*-*a*-, *dad*-*a*-, *jah*-*a*- (cf. OAv. *zaz*-*a*-), *min*-*a*-, *aśn*-*a*-, aor. *dh-a*-. The category subjunctive ceases to exist in Post-Vedic and only the 1ˢᵗ persons survive integrated into the imperative. The subjunctive aorist is sometimes inflected in the active even when forms in the other categories of the same verb are inflected throughout in the middle (a similar case is observed also in the indicative perfect).

#### 5.3.2. Optative

The optative is formed in athematic inflections with suffix -*yā´*-/-*ī*- (<*-*i̯éh₁*-/*-*ih₁-*) in a hysterodynamic pattern with secondary endings: act. sg. -*yā´*-*m*, -*yā´*-*s*, -*yā´*-*t*; du. -*yā´*-*va*, -*yā´*-*tam*, -*yā´*-*tām*; pl. -*yā´*-*ma*, -*yā´*-*ta*, -*y*-*úr*; mid. sg. -*ī*-*yá*, -*ī*-*thās* (attested only in the perfect), -*ī*-*tá*; du. −; pl. -*ī*-*máhi*, −, -*īran*/-*īyan*/-*īrata*. The thematic inflections show the terminations act. sg. °*eyam*, °*es*, °*et*; du. °*eva*, °*etam*, −; pl. °*ema*, °*eta*, °*eyur*; mid.: sg. °*eya*, −, °*eta*; du. °*evahi*, *−*, −; pl. °*emahi*, −, °*erata*. The functions of the optative are potential (possibility that the action happens) and cupitive (speaker’s wish); also the prescriptive usage is common: ‘should do’, with *ná* ‘should not, may not do’.

The aorist optative has received an Indic renovation through 3ʳᵈ sg. -*yās* instead of -*yāt*. Its background is given by the fact that in PII the active of the sigmatic aorist optative is supplied from the root aorist. After the proportion, e.g., root aor. inj. 2ⁿᵈ sg. *yauṣ* (< **yauṣ*-*ṣ*):: 3ʳᵈ sg. *yauṣ* (< **yauṣ-ṭ*) = *yu-yās*:: *x* (*yav* ‘keep [oneself] away’), the 3ʳᵈ sg. -*yāt* is replaced by -*yās*, which incidentally has the appearance of a sigmatic aorist and could be interpreted as *-*yā*-*s-t*. “Precative” forms are then built on this remodelled 3ʳᵈ sg. -*yās*: act. sg. 1ˢᵗ *bhū-yās-am*, pl. 1 *kri-yās-ma*; in the middle with its weak form -*īṣ-* from the sigmatic aorist: 3ʳᵈ sg. °*s-īṣ-ṭa*, 2ⁿᵈ sg. °*s-īṣ-ṭhās*. The 3ʳᵈ sg. -*yās* is transformed again back into -*yāt* in the YSᵐ. The active forms survive YSᵖ+, sometimes also in potential meaning.

The type *yeṣam* (*yā* ‘move, travel’), *jéṣma* (*jay*/*ji* ‘win’) is an analogic formation after the thematic aorist optative of the type *khyéṣam* (YSᵐ), **khyéṣ*, **khyéṣ*, **khyéṣma*, and **khyéṣur*, which has arisen beside **khyéyur*. In the background of **jéṣur*, a trace of the PIE acrodynamic root aorist optative **jéyur* is postulated (cf. OAv. *var əzimā*-*cā*, Gk. *theĩmen*, etc.), which was remodelled to **jéṣur*, whence *jéṣam*, **jéṣma*. The type *gaméyam*, -*éma*, -*émahi* from *gam* ‘go’, *vanéma* (OAv. *vanaēmā*) ‘we may win’ belongs to the athematic root aorist. Its origin lies in the analogy *vidhéma* (20×):: *vidh* = *x*:: *gam*, where *x* = *gaméma*. The root *vidh* had become independent from *vi*-*dhā* already in PII, presumably through this very *vi*-*dhéma* (many times in the RV; PII **u̯i*-*dʰá[H]-i[H]-ma*, possibly a relic of an acrodynamic root aorist; cf. thematic aorist *vidhánt*- = OAv. *vīdaṇt*-, subj. *vidhāti* = OAv. *vīdₐāⁱtī*, *vīdₐāt*). *gaméma* has opened the way for the thematic aorist *gama*- RV X+.

#### 5.3.3. Imperative

The imperative endings are: act. sg. 2 -*dhí*/-*hí* in athematic stems, 3 -*tu* (built to a strong stem); du. 2 -*tam*, 3 -*tām*; pl. 2 -*ta*/-*tana* (sometimes built to a strong stem), 3 -*antu*/ -*ntu*/-*atu*; mid. sg. 2 -*sva*, 3 -*tām*, du. 2 -*āthām* (athematic), -*ethām* (thematic), 3 -*ātām* (athematic), °*etām* (thematic), pl. 2 -*dhvam*, 3 -*atām*/-*ntām*. There are also forms with -*ām* in the 3ʳᵈ sg. mid.: *duhā´m* ‘let... give milk’ RV+, *śáyām* ‘let … lie’ AV, cf. 5.4.1.4, perf. *vidām* ‘let... find’ AV, cf. 5.6.

The 2ⁿᵈ singular imperative is a bare stem form in the thematic inflection, and -*dhi* (developed into -*hi* in some phonological enviroments) is added in the athematic inflection. The 3ʳᵈ sg. act. -*tu* and pl. -*antu*/-*ntu*/-*atu* are analyzed as injunctive forms + -*u*; -*tām*, -*atām*/-*ntām* in the middle as injunctive + -*am*. The forms in the dual and the 2ⁿᵈ plural are in both diatheses in principle nothing but the injunctives, e.g., 2 pl. *bhára*-*ta* ‘bring’ = Gk. *pʰérete*, Goth. *baíriþ*; 2 du. *s*-*tam* ‘be’, pl. *s*-*ta* AV+. However, the 2ⁿᵈ pl. present imperative shows in athematic inflection sometimes a strong stem form as opposed to the injunctive: e.g., *juhóta(na)*:: inj. *juhutā* (*hav*/*hu* ‘pour an offering, libation’), *yunákta* ‘yoke’, *kr̥ṇóta(na)* beside *kr̥ṇutá* ‘make’, *śr̥ṇota(na)* beside *śr̥ṇutá* ‘hear, listen to’, *punāta* beside *punītá(na)* ‘make pure’, etc.

The indicative and injunctive of the athematic root aorist has, in contrast to the athematic present, a strong stem also in three persons in the dual, and in the 1ˢᵗ and 2ⁿᵈ pl. in the active; thus the zero-grade stems in the imperative are rather remarkable: e.g., 2 du. *kr̥tám* beside *kartam* (AV), *gatám*/*gantám*, *jitam* ‘win’, *śrutám*; 2 pl. *śrutā*/*śrótā* (OAv. *sraotā*), *kr̥ta*/*kárta*, *gata*/*gánta*.

Imperatives in -*tāt* (imperative II) are employed for all persons and numbers in both diatheses for instances where the action is conceptualized as taking place at a fixed time: “imperativa posterior”. This inherited formation is built theoretically to each stem. − Interjections *hánta* ‘now then!’, *éta* ‘go!’ appear to be older forms of the imperative 2ⁿᵈ pl.; the paradigmatic forms are *hatá* and *hantana* ‘slay ye!’, *itá*, *itana*, and *étana* ‘go!’.

### 5.4. Present system

Present stems are inflected in the indicative, imperfect, injunctive, subjunctive, optative, and imperative, and form a participle, theoretically all in both active and middle. On thematic present stems, cf. 5.1.3, their inflection proceeds with the addition of the personal endings in 5.2; the thematic vowel -*a*- is lengthened before *m* or *v* of the endings.

### 5.5. Aorist

Aorist stems are inflected in the indicative, injunctive, subjunctive, optative, and imperative, and form a participle, theoretically all in both active and middle. The indicative is restricted to the past.

#### 5.5.1. Athematic root aorist

Athematic root aorists have full grade of the root in the active indicative and injunctive, except 3ʳᵈ plural. The subjunctive has a thematic full-grade stem. The optative is built on zero grade of the root with the suffix -*yā´*- (in the active except 3ʳᵈ pl.) or -*ī-* (with accented endings). On the acrodynamic root aorist and the type *gaméma*, *vanéma*, cf. 5.3.2. The stems are strong in the imperative in 3ʳᵈ sg. active, predominantly also in the dual and plural in the active, cf. 5.3.3. These schemes go back to PII. The ablaut of *á*-*bhū*-*t*, *bhū´*-*t* ‘has come into being, become’ is an old exception. From the RV, e.g.:

#### 5.5.2. Thematic root aorist

#### 5.5.3. Rudiplicated aorists

The main body of reduplicated aorists consists of forms with zero-grade root and long reduplicated syllable with -*ī˘*- or -*ū˘*-, inflected thematically, functioning as the aorist of the causative or other factitive present; productive also in the later language: inj., e.g., 3 sg. *jī́*-*jan*-*a*-*t*, 3 pl. -*a*-*nta* (*janáya*-*ᵗⁱ/ᵗᵉ* ‘beget’), 1 sg. *cu*-*krudh*-*a*-*m*, 1 pl. -*ā*-*ma* (*krodháya*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘make angry’), sg. 2 *rī*-*radh*-*a*-*s*, 3 -*t*, du. 2 -*tam*, pl. 2 -*ta* (*randháya*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘subdue’).

This category includes also athematic stems, often with full-grade root (some suppose these to be of perfect origin): 2 sg. *a*-*jī*-*gar* (*jāráya*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘wake’); opt. pl. 1 *cu*-*cyuv*-*ī*-*mahi*, 3 -*ī*-*rata* (*cyāváya*-*ᵗᵉ* ‘agitate [onself]’); subj. 3 sg. *jū*-*juv*-*a*-*t* (*junā´*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘impel’), *á*-*pū*-*po*-*t* (*punā´*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘purify’), *á*-*śi*-*śre*-*t*, 3 pl. -*śray*-*ur* (*śráy*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘make lean’:: *śráy*-*ᵗᵉ* ‘lean’; root aorist *á*-*śre*-*t* ‘has made lean’); subj. 3 sg. *pī́*-*par*-*a*-*t* (*pāráya*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘carry over’); sg. 2, 3 *dī*-*dhar*,subj. 2 sg. *dī*-*dhar*-*a*-*s*, -*t* beside inj. sg. 1 *dī*-*dhar*-*a*-*m*, 3 -*a*-*t* (*dhāráya*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘hold, support’); *a*-*śi*-*śnat*, subj. sg. 2 *śi*-*śnath*-*a*-*s*, 3 -*t* beside inj. sg. 3 *śi*-*śnath*-*a*-*t* (*śnatháya*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘pierce’).

*va*-*vr̥t*- (: *vartáya*- ‘make … turn’; opt. sg. 1*vavr̥tyām*, 3 -*yāt*, pl. 3 -*yur* etc., subj. 3 sg. *vavártat[i]*, iptv. 2 pl. *vavr̥ttana*, mid. 3 pl. *avavr̥tran*, -*ranta*, etc.) reminds us of Hom. *pe-pitʰ-eĩn* ‘persuade’ (: *peítʰō)*. Some incentive, therefore, may have existed in PIE for the formation of a factitive reduplicated aorist. However, the system [aorist with long syllable reduplication with -*ī˘*- or -*ū˘*-:: causative -*áya*-] is built only in Old Indo-Aryan. In Iranian, merely two stems of this sort are known, both with intransitive meaning: Av. *vaoc*-*a*- ‘speak’, OAv. *nąs*-*a*- ‘perish, disappear’.

The inherited aorist *vóc-*/*vóc-a-* < **u̯e-u̯kʷ*-*(e)*-, cf. Gk. *eip-(e)-*, from *vac* ‘speak’ is productive, occurring also in the later language and Middle Indo-Aryan, e.g., 3 sg. *ávocat*, pl. 1 -*āma*, 3 -*an*, inj. sg. 2 *vócas*, 3 -*at*, pl. 3 -*an*, subj. sg. 1 *vocā*, 3 *vócā˘ti*, opt. sg. 1*vocéyam*, 2 -*és*, pl. 3 -*éyur*, iptv. sg. 2 *vocā*, 3 *-atu*, pl. 2 -*ata*; mid. sg. 3 *ávocata*, inj. 1 sg. *voce*, subj. 1 du. *vocāvahai*, 3 pl. *vócanta*, opt. 1 sg. *voceya*, 1 pl. -*emahi*.

Another pattern involves formations which pair with intransitive or iterative presents, predominantly with zero-grade root: *apaptat*, *paptas*, -*ta*, *páptan*, (iterat. *pataya*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘fly’); athematic *ádudrot*, subj. *dudrávat* (*dráva*-*ᵗⁱ*, *dravayanta* ‘run, rush’); thematic and athematic formations exist side by side in inj. *tatánat*, subj. *tatanas* (< **te*-*tn̥h₂*-*e*-*t*: *stanáya*-*ᵗⁱ* < **stn̥h₂*-*éi̯e*- or **stonh₂*-*éi̯e*- ‘thunder’). The root vocalism of *neś*-*a*-*t* (*náśya*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘perish’:: *nāśáya*-*ᵗⁱ* with aor. *anīnaśát*) is that of the secondary weak form of the perf. as against OAv. *nąs*-*a*-.

#### 5.5.4. Sigmatic aorists

Sigmatic aorists consist of forms marked by -*s-*, -*iṣ-*, and secondarily -*siṣ-* and -*sa-*. Their inflection is acrodynamic with long-grade root in the active and full-grade in the middle and moods. The -*iṣ-* aorist is only a variation of the -*s*- aorist built to *seṭ* roots (roots ending with a laryngeal, which appears as -*i*-); if a root ends with -*ā* or -*ī* (-*ayⁱ*), its sigmatic aorist appears as an -*s*- aorist in -*ā*-*s-* or -*ai*-*ṣ-*/-*e*-*ṣ-*, respectively. The 2ⁿᵈ and 3ʳᵈ sg. **a*-*yā-s* from *yā* ‘travel’ (attested *a*-*yā-s-am*, -*ur*) is differentiated into **a*-*yās-īs* and *a*-*yās-īt*, which serves at the same time to distinguish them from ipf. *a*-*yā-s. a*-*yās*-*iṣ-am*, -*iṣ-ur* have been produced from these forms to hypercharacterize them as -*s-* aorists, and this has given rise to the -*siṣ-* aorist (originally from *yā* and *gā* ‘sing’ in the RV, later to an additional eight roots ending in -*ā*).

The extension of **a*-*yā*-*s* to 2ⁿᵈ **a*-*yā*-*s-īs*, 3ʳᵈ -*s-īt* is based on the proportion athematic root aorist *a*-*kram*-*am*, -*ur*:: *a*-*kramī*-*s*, -*t* = *a*-*yās*-*am*, -*ur*:: *x*. This formation has become standard after the RV, where the 2ⁿᵈ and 3ʳᵈ sg. active end -*sīs*, -*sīt* in the -*s*-aorist, and -*īs*, -*īt* in the -*iṣ-* aorist. Athematic root aorists such as sg. 1 *a*-*kram*-*am*, 2 *a*-*kramī*-*s*, 3 °*ī*-*t*, pl. 3*a*-*kram*-*ur* (*kramⁱ* ‘stride’) have been reformed into -*iṣ-* forms: *a*-*kramiṣ-am*, (*a*-*kramī*-*m*), (*a*-*kramī*-*s*, *a*-*kramī*-*t*), du. 3 *a*-*kramiṣ-ṭam*. The ablaut grade of the root reveals their origin. This is also the case with 1ˢᵗ sg. middle *ā´vr̥k*-*ṣ-i* (*varj* ‘twist, wrench off ’); this form is due to the reinterpretation of athematic 3 sg. *a*-*vr̥k*-*ta* (beside 2/3 sg. *vark*, 3 pl. *avr̥jan*, etc.) as underlying **a*-*vr̥k*-*ṣ*-*ṭa*. Athematic root aorists from *aniṭ* roots, e.g. 2/3 sg. *á*-*tan*, 3 pl. *á*-*tn*-*ata* (*tan* ‘stretch’) are reformed into a more explicit *a*-*tān* in the RV, and further -*s-* aorist forms are built later.

The starting point of the -*sa-* aorist is to be sought in *á*-*dhuk*-*ṣ-an* ‘they have milked’, which could be segmented as °*kṣa-n. ádhukṣan* would have been built to the middle -*s-*aorist *a*-*dhuk*-*ṣ-ata* after the model -*an*:: -*ata*, e. g. in the athematic root aorist indicative instead of -*ṣ-ur*; *adhukṣata* reminds us of *a*-*yuk*-*ṣ-ata*, which has been remodelled with -*ṣ-ata* from athematic root aorist **ayujata* in a similar way to *ā´*-*vr̥k*-*ṣ-i*, cf. 3ʳᵈ sg. *yukta*. The new thematic inflection has spread to stems with the constellation °*kṣa*- after *i*, *u*, *r̥*(7 roots in the RV, 10 more after the RV, almost entirely forms in the indicative or injunctive active).

So-called -*si* imperatives such as *yāsi*, *jéṣi*, *yaṁsi*, *yákṣi* are the result of haplology from the subjunctive -*s-* aorist. Forms such as *párṣi*, *yaṁsi*, *darṣi* (*dar ⁽ ⁱ ⁾* ‘pierce’), and *ā*-*sátsi* (*sad* ‘sit down’) still serve as subjunctives in the RV in relative clauses. This development must have happened in Proto-Indo-Iranian, cf. OAv. *dōišī* < **dai̯ć*-*ši* < **dai̯ć*-*ša*-*ši* (the sole example in Iran.), but has gained productivity only in Vedic.

#### 5.5.5. Medio-passive aorist

A medio-passive aorist exists in the 3ʳᵈ sg. and pl., used in patientive or fientive meaning. The singular forms consist of the root in full-grade *a* or *ā* < PIE **o* (Brugmann), and the ending -*i*; the plural consists of the zero-grade root and -*ran* or -*ram*: *á*-*kār*-*i* ‘has just been made’; *á*-*gr̥bh*-*ran* ‘they have just been grasped’; *á*-*darś*-*i* ‘has become visible’, *dárś*-*i*, *á*-*dr̥ś*-*ran*, -*ram*; *pā´d*-*i* ‘falls’, *a*-*pad*-*ran*; *á*-*bodh*-*i* ‘has been awakened’, *á*-*budh*-*ran*, -*ram*, etc. The formation in the singular goes back to PII, cf. OAv. *cəuuīšī* < **cōišī* < **čai̯ši* (**kai̯š*/*ciš* ‘assign’), (*a*-)*vācī* (*=* Ved. *[a*-*]vāci*), *srāuuī* (Ved. *śrā´vi*). The coupling of plural formations with -*ran*, -*ram*, which occur in the 3ʳᵈ pl. of the athematic root aorist (*ajuṣran*, *áviśran*, *ásthiran*, *aspr̥dhran*, cf. 5.5.1), athematic “stative” root present (*aśeran* RV, *áduhran* AV+, cf. *aśerata* YSᵖ+, *duhrate* RV+, cf. 5.4.1.4), reduplicated aorist (*avavr̥tran*, *-ranta*, cf. 5.5.3), preterite of the perfect (*ájagmiran*, *ácakriran*, cf. 5.6), and optative -*ī*-*ran*/-*īrata* (5.3.2), is probably a strictly OIA phenomenon.

#### 5.5.6. Periphrastic aorist

Periphrastic aorists occur in Vedic prose, MSᵖ etc.: *vidā´m akar*, *akran*, and *akar* after *abhyutsādayā´m*, *prajanayā´m*, *ramayā´m*, *pratiṣṭhāpayā´m*, *svadayā´m*; also precative *pāvayā´ṃ kriyāt*.

### 5.6. The perfect

The perfect is marked with reduplication, ablaut, and its indicative endings. The reduplication occurs originally with *a* (PIE **e*), but is assimilated to *i* or *u* with *ī˘* or *ū˘* in the root syllable: *ca*-*kar*-/-*kr̥*-/-*kr*-, *bi*-*bhay*-/-*bhī*-, *śu*-*śrav*-/-*śru*-. Only *ved-*/*vid-* ‘know’ lacks reduplication, a circumstance that existed already in PIE. The inflection follows the hysterodynamic pattern (*-*o-* in active singular, otherwise zero): sg. 1 *ca*-*kár*-*a* (<**kʷe-kʷór*-*h₂a*), 3 *ca*-*kā´r*-*a* (<**kʷe-kʷór*-*e*, *ā* after Brugmann), 3 pl. *ca*-*kr*-*úr*, mid. 1/3 sg. *ca*-*kr*-*é*. *ba*-*bhū´v*-*a* ‘has become’ has preserved -*a*- redupl. and unique (non-)ablaut. Connecting -*i*- appears, e.g., in *āv*-*i*-*tha* ‘you have helped’, *uvoc-i*-*tha* ‘you have spoken’, *ūd*-*i*-*ma* ‘we have uttered’, etc. Roots with the constellation *CaC* build secondary forms with weak stems in *CeC*, e.g., *tep*-, *debh*-, *nem*-, *pec*-, *ped*-, etc., and *pet*-, *yej*-, *rebh*-/ *lebh*- beside *pa*-*pt*-, *īj*-, *rā˘rabh*- in the RV; later, more forms of this sort occur. The starting point are forms such as *yet-* from *yat* ‘take one’s position’: **i̯a*-*i̯t*- < **i̯e*-*i̯t*-, cf. OAv. *yōiθəmā* < **i̯ai̯tma*, and possibly *sed-* < **sazd*-.

The endings are: act. sg. -*a* (*-*h₂e*), -*tha* (*-*th₂e*), -*a* (*-*e*); du. (-*vá*), -*áthur*, -*átur*; pl. -*má* (*-*mé*), -*á*, -*úr* (*-*́r̥*); mid. sg. -*é*, -*sé*, -*é*; du. (-*váhe*), -*ā´the*, -*ā´te*; pl. -*máhe*, -*dhvé*, -*ré*. 1ˢᵗ pl. *-*mé* is the same as the secondary ending in other paradigms. Also *-*té* is reconstructed for 2 pl. beside *-*é*, cf. Gk. *íste*, Goth. *wituþ* ‘ye know’ < **u̯id-te*; no form is attested in OIran. *-*ŕ̥* is possibly a back formation after the proportion *-*to*:: *-*t* = *-*re*:: *x* from *-*ré*, which is, extended with *-*i̯*, integrated in the mid.; 1 sg. -*é* and 3 sg. -*é* seem to go back to PIE *-*h₂e*-*i̯*, *-*e*-*i̯*; other middle endings are nothing but the primary endings used in the present indicative. Roots ending in -*ā*have in the 1/3 act. forms in -*au*: *dadhau* ‘I have,... has set’, the only exception is *paprā´* ‘has filled’ RV I 69,1.

The perfect also has moods: e.g., subj. act. sg. 2 *cíketas(i)*, 3 *dadhárṣati*, pl. 3 *tatánan*; mid. sg. 1 *śaśvacái*, 3 *tatápate*, pl. 3 *cākánanta*; opt. sg. 1 *jagamyām*, 3 *vidyā´t*, pl. 3 *jagmyur*; mid. sg. 2 *vāvr̥dhīthā´s*, 3 *jagrasīta*, pl. 1*vavr̥tīmahi*; iptv. sg. 2 *jāgr̥hí*, *mumugdhí*, 3 *ciketu*, *mumoktu*, *jāgratu*, *bibhyatu*, pl. 2 *jāgr̥tá*, *vitta*, *mamattána*, *yuyóta*; mid. only in Ved., e.g. 2 sg. *dadhiṣvá*, 2 pl. -*dhvam*, 2 sg. *mimikṣvā´*, thematized *māmahasva*, 3 pl. °*antām*, etc. RV; *vitsvá* and 3 sg. *vidām* (cf. 5.4.1.4) AV+.

The proper function of the perfect is to designate a state of the subject which it has attained as the result of a verbal action, thus a kind of present. The preterite to the perfect is expressed by means of the augment and secondary endings, thus the imperfect from the perfect stem, known as “pluperfect”, e.g., act. sg. 1 *acacakṣam* ‘I looked at’, 2 *ájagan* ‘you went’, *abubhojīs* ‘you bent’, 3 *ájagan*, *aciket* ‘noticed’, *avivyak* ‘embraced’, *abibhet* ‘feared’, *ájagrabhīt*; *amumuktam* ‘you both released’; 3 pl. *ajaganta*, *acikayur*; mid. du. 2 *ápaspr̥dhethām*, pl. 3 *ájagmiran*, *ácakriran*, *átitviṣanta*. From the root *ved* ‘know’ sg. 1 *avedam* RV, 3 *ávet* RV, 3 pl. *ávidur* YSᵖ, whence a new present sg. 1 *védmi*, 3 *vetti*, pl. 1 *vidmasi*, 3 *avidan*, etc. AVP RVKh Br.+. The forms without augment are used as injunctives.

Perfect participles (act. -*vā´ṁs*-/-*úṣ*-, f. -*úṣī*-, 2.2.12, mid. -*āná*-, f. -*ānā´*-) are used also as predicates instead of finite forms; this usage is attested already many times in the RV and enregistered by Pāṇini (cf. III 2,105−109).

Periphrastic formations appear from the AV onward: *gamayā´ṃ cakāra* (: caus. *gamáya-ti*), likewise in YSᵖ, Br. from causative, desiderative, intensive, and denominative stems, sometimes with normal perfects in parallel passages; the perf. of *veda* ‘knows’: *vidā´ṃ cakāra*, *cakr̥ma* YSᵖ, *vidā´ṃ cakartha*, *cakrur* Br. The construction with *as* is younger and rare. That with *bhū* is far more exceptional, attested only in Epic and Classical Sanskrit.

### 5.7. Secondary present systems

#### 5.7.1. Future

The future is a kind of present formed with suffix -*syá*-, added to *-*e-* full-grade roots, e.g. *vakṣyá*- (YAv. *vaxšiiete*), *dhāsyá*- YSᵖ+. Another form -*iṣyá*-, having no correspondents in Old Iranian, originates as a variant after a *seṭ* roots, or is rather to be explained from *-*h₁si̯e-* after resonants, just as desiderative *-*h₁se* /*o-* (5.7.2): e.g., *bhavi*-*ṣyá*-*ti* (YAv. *būšiiaṇt*-), *jani*-*ṣya*-*te* (YAv. *ząhiiamna*-), *man*-*iṣyá*-, *kar*-*iṣyá*-. Examples contravening this distribution are found from the AV onwards: *naś*-*iṣya*- (*naś* ‘perish, disappear’), *svapiṣyā´masi* AV (*svap* ‘fall asleep’). Older forms of the root *sav* ‘press out’ (YAv. *haošiiaṇt*-) are *sav*-*iṣyāmi* MSᵖ, *abhi*-*ṣav*-*iṣyánt*- ŚB, later *so-ṣyati* ChUp, *so-ṣyant*- ŚrSū; this could speak for the second of the explanations proffered above. The active participles are very productive in the RV, often in the meaning ‘going, intending to do’. The imperfect is attested in the RV only in *ábhariṣyat* ‘he was going to bear off’; the same formation serves as “conditional” for irrealis assumptions from the time of Vedic prose. The subjunctive *kariṣyā´s* occurs in the RV and *ātapsyāni* ‘I will be going to heat’ in the JB. Some future stems are built on present or causative stems from the AV onward.

The future II with nomen agentis -*tár*- is employed in cases where the time of the action is designated or meant pregnantly, attested YSᵖ+: 3 sg. *śvó vraṣṭā* ‘it will rain tomorrow’ MSᵖ, *bhavitā´* ŚB, 3 pl. *ā*-*dātāras* AB, 1 sg. *ā*-*gantāsmi* AB. Mid. forms are also found: *prayoktā´se* ‘I will apply myself’’ TSᵖ (on the analogy act. *°s*-*mi*:: mid. °*s*-*e*), *śayitā´se* ‘you will lie’ ŚB (on the analogy act. *°si*:: mid. °*se*), *yaṣṭā´smahe* ‘we will sacrifice’ TBᵖ, 1 sg. *yaṣṭāhé* TĀᵖ (-*h*-*e* from re-analyzing, e.g. *yoddhāham* ‘I shall fight’ Rāmāyaṇa < *yoddhā* + *aham* as *yoddhā*-*h*-*am*). Active forms increase in the later language, some traces being present also in Pāli. Traditional grammar gives a paradigm: sg. 1 *dātā´smi*, 2 *°tā´si*, 3 *dātā´*; du. 1 *dātā´svas*, 2 *°tā´sthas*, 3 *°tā´rau*; pl. 1 *°tā´smas*, 2 *°tā´stha*, 3 *°tā´ras*; in the later tradition also middle (scarcely attested) sg. 1 *dātā´he*, 2 *°tā´se*, 3 *dātā´*; du. 1 *dātā´svahe*, 2 °*tā´sāthe*, 3 *°tā´rau*; pl. 1 *°tā´smahe*, 2 *°tā´dhve*, 3 *°tā´ras*.

#### 5.7.2. Desiderative

The desiderative stem for designating the desire of the subject is built with -*í*- reduplicated zero-grade root and the suffix -*sa*- (*-*se*/*o*-): *rí*-*rik*-*ṣa*- (*rec*), *dí*-*dr̥k*-*ṣa*- (*darś*), *dí*-*dā*-*sa*-, *ī́*-*p-sa*- AV+ (*ap*/*āp*), *í-yak*-*ṣa*- < **Hí*-*Hn̥k*-*ṣa*- (*naś*, younger *í*-*nak*-*ṣa*-); with long -*ī́*-: *bī́*-*bhat*-*sa*-*ᵗᵉ* ‘get disgusted’ (*bādh* ‘push away’), with assimilated -*ú*-: *dú*-*duk*-*ṣa*- (*dogh* ‘milk’). The PIE suffix *-*h₁se*/*o*-, employed with roots ending in resonants, is well reflected in Old Indo-Aryan: *jí*-*gī*-*ṣa*- (*jay*/*ji*), *ví-vā*-*sa*- < **u̯í*-*u̯n̥*-*h₁se*- (*van*), *jí*-*ghāṁ**sa*- < **gʷʰí*-*gʷʰn̥*-*h₁se*- (*han*), *mī́*-*māṁ*-*sa*- (*man*). Indicative, optative, and participles are attested in both diatheses, imperfect, subjunctive, and imperative only in the active. The -*iṣ-* aorist occurs, e.g., *a*-*cikits*-*īs*, *īrts*-*īs* AV, *airts*-*īs* (*rādh*) ŚB, *aipsīt* TBᵖ, *aipsiṣma* JUB.

### 5.7.3. Intensive

The intensive stem, employed essentially for expressing repetition, is built with full or strengthened reduplication: *nā´*-*nad*-, *śā*-*śvas*-, *cā´*-*kaś*- (*kāś*), *dé*-*diś*-, *yó-yavī* /*yuv*- (*yav* ‘keep away’), *yó*-*yu*- (*yav* ‘hold fast’), *nó-navī*-/*no-nav*-/*no-nuv*-/*no-nu*-/*návī*-*no*- (*navⁱ*/ *nū* ‘bellow’), *dávi*-*dyot*-/*dávi*-*dyut*- (*dyot*/*dyut* ‘flash’), etc. The ablaut follows the pattern of the athematic present (5.4.1). Indicative, imperfect, injunctive, and subjunctive occur in both diatheses, imperative only in the active; optative only *pári*-*veviṣyāt* AV, *ā´*-*vevīran* TSᵖ, ⁺*jojūyur* TBᵖ in Ved.; perf. *davidhāva*, *nonāva*, *marmr̥jmā´*, -*nonuvur*, *badbhadé*, -*sasré* RV, some other forms in YSᵖ Br.; on periphrastic perfects, cf. 5.6; caus. *varīvar-jáyantī*- AV (*dādhārayati* JB is a nonce form built on perf. *dādhā´ra*, which supplies the 3 sg. of pres. *dhāráya*-*ᵗⁱ*); a participle is common in active and middle; gerdv. *carkŕ̥ tya*-, *vitantasā´yya*-, *marmr̥jén₍ᵢ ₎ya*- RV; some verbal adjectives in -*tá*- appear in the Brāhmaṇas.

A formation with -*yá*-, inflected only in the middle, is used in the same function as the athematec type. Only some forms occur in the RV, e.g., *coṣkūyá*-*se*, -*te*, -*māna*-; *marmr̥jyá*-*te*, -*nte*, -*māna*-; 2 du. *ūhᵢyā´the* (instead of *-*yethe*), subj. 3 Sg. *ūhᵢyā´te* (*vah*); some more forms occur after the RV; and Pāṇini teaches this type as standard. *vanīvāhyéta* ŚB belongs to the causative, marked as such with *ā* in the root.

#### 5.7.4. Causative

The causative is a well-represented category. Its formation goes back to a PIE present stem with suffix -*éi̯e*- added to the root in **-o-* grade. Thus the stem shows, in its original distribution, -*ā*- in open and -*a*- in closed syllables (including those closed with a laryngeal): *cātáya*-, *chādáya*-, *bhājáya*-, *gāmaya*, etc., against *janⁱáya*-, *jarⁱáya*-, *damⁱáya*-, etc.; in addition, *cetáya*-, *bhojáya*-, *darśáya*-, etc. But from an early period levelling pressure existed in this living category, so that we have, on the one hand, *gamáya*- (2× in RV), *yaváya*-, *namáya*- (substitute for *náma*-*ᵗⁱ*), etc. and, on the other hand, *tārⁱáya-* AV, *āmⁱáya*-, *bhāvⁱáya*- AV. The indicative, imperfect, injunctive, subjunctive, and imperative are well attested both in active and middle; the optative is rare (not found in the RV), attested only in the active.; participles are common in the active, rare in the middle. Some future forms occur: *dhārayiṣyáti* and *vāsayiṣyáse* RV; on the perfect cf. 5.6; the reduplicated aorist functions as the aorist to the causative (5.5.3), but -*s*-aorist forms also occur: *dhvanayīt* RV, *ailayīt*, *vyathayīt* AV, and some others appear in the Br.; pass. *bhāj*-*ya*-*māna*- AV; nominal derivatives are very common. Roots ending in -*ā* build stems with -*páya*-: *glā*-*páya*-, *dhā*-*páya*-, *vā*-*páya*-, *sthā*-*páya*-, *snā*-*páya*-; after the RV with short *a* in the root: *jña*-*páya*-, *śra*-*páya*-, *sna*-*páya*-; in addition *kṣe-páya*- beside *kṣay*-*áya*-, etc.; reduplicated aorists are formed from some of these stems: *jīhipat* (: *ví*-*hāpayati* AV), *á*-*tiṣṭḥipat*, -*as* (: *sthāpaya*-) RV.

#### 5.7.5. Passive

The passive stem is formed with -*yá*- added to the root in zero grade and inflected only in the middle (cf. 5.1.3, [B4]): *uc*-*yá*-, *kri*-*yá*- (*kar*/*kr̥*), *sū*-*yá*-, *dī*-*yá*- (*u* and *i* are always long before -*ya*-), *han*-*yá*-, *pac-yá*-, etc. Indicative and participial forms are relatively common; imperatives appear in a fair number of instances; the imperfect is attested only in some 3ʳᵈ person forms in the RV, e.g. *anīyata*, *ámucyata*, *apacyanta*; injunctive only in *acyanta* (*añc*); subjunctive is rare, only *bhriyāte*, *bhriyanta* occur in the RV.

#### 5.7.6. Denominatives

Denominatives in various functions are formed from nouns by means of -*yá*-.

−Some have adopted the accent of the -*áya*- present type: *mantráya*-*ᵗᵉ* ‘think out, speak’, *kāmáya*-*ᵗᵉ* ‘desire, love’ (whence a new root *kamⁱ*), *artháya*-*ᵗᵉ* ‘aim’, etc.

−The 3 sg. in particular is common in both diatheses, and participles are well attested: from -*a*- stems: *deva*-*yánt*- ‘seeking the gods’, *aghā*-*yá*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘cause mischief’, *aśvā*-*yánt*-‘desiring horses’, *adhvarī*-*yá*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘perform the sacrificial process’ (also *adhvar*-*yá*-*ᵗⁱ*), *putrī*-*yá*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘desire sons’;

−*gopā*-*yá*-*ᵗⁱ* (*gopā´*- ‘herdsman’, whence a new root *gop*/*gup*); *kavī*-*yánt*-, -*yámāna*- ‘being a seer (*kaví*-)’; *r̥jū*-*yánt*-, -*yámāna*- ‘being honest’ (*r̥jú*- ‘straight’), *asū*-*yánt*- ‘feeling pain, unwilling’ (*asrá*- ‘painful’ Br.); *gav*-*yánt*- ‘desiring cows’; *bhiṣaj*-*yá*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘act as a doctor, heal’, *aviṣ-yánt*- ‘desiring provisions (*avasá*-)’; *taviṣ-yá*-*ᵗᵉ*, *taviṣī*-*yá*-*ᵗⁱ*/*ᵗᵉ* ‘be mighty’ (*tavás*-, *taviṣá*-);

−forms in °*anyá*-: *vr̥ṣaṇ*-*yá*-*ti* ‘acts as a bull’, -*yántī*- ‘desiring a bull’, *udan*-*yánt*- ‘irrigating’; *kr̥paṇyá*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘desire to get, long for’ (*kr̥páṇa***-** ‘misery’); autonomous *huvanya*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘call’, etc.; *iṣ-aṇyá*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘urge’ could go back to PIE **(h₁)is(h₂)*-*n̥*-*i̯é*- together with Gk. *iaínō* ‘I heat, cheer’, beside *iṣ-ṇā´*-*ti* < **(h₁)is-né-h*₂-, thematized Gk. *ináō* (*ī*°) ‘I clear out’.

The *-*i̯é-* formation goes back to PIE, e.g., *saparyáti* ‘attends to, venerates’ (many indicative forms, also imperfect, participle *saparyánt-*, gerundive. *°éṇya*-; *°yú*-), Lat. *sepeliō* ‘I bury, entomb’ < **sepel-i̯é-*. The suffix is properly added to the weak(est) form: *uruṣyá*-*ᵗⁱ* ‘search for breadth; bring someone into breadth, into security’ < **u̯r̥H*-*us*-*i̯é*-(36× RV; later rare):: *várivas*- < **u̯érH*-*u̯os*- ‘wideness’, whence analogic *varivasyá*-*ᵗⁱ* (9× RV); *ī́rṣyantī*- ‘being jealous’ TSᵖ (with independent accent; *°yā´*-, *°yú*- AV+), YAv. *ar əšiiaṇt*- ‘jealous’ < **(H)r̥Hs-i̯é-*, whereas *irasyá*-*ᵗⁱ*, *°yā´*- RV is a younger formation. The -*iṣ-* aorist occurs in *ūnāyīs* (*ūná*- ‘lacking’), *pāpayiṣṭa* TSᵐ (*pāpá*- ‘bad’), *á*-*saparyait* AV. Nouns in -*yā´*- and -*yú*- are very common.

### 5.8. Nominal forms of the verb

#### 5.8.1. Infinitives

Infinitives show a multiplicity of formations in the RV (dat. -*dhyai*, -*e*, -*tave*, -*táye*, -*tyái*, -*aye*, -*ase*, -*se*, -*mane*; abl.-gen. -*as*, -*tos*; acc. -*am*, -*tum*; loc. -*sáni*). These are later reduced radically in number, and only -*tum* remains in Epic and Classical Sanskrit; in Vedic prose (YSᵖ, Br.) besides productive -*tum*: *sā´ḍhyai* ‘in order to win’ MSᵖ; *dr̥śé*, *ārabhé*, and *ávitave*, *sártave* ŚB; a fair number of forms in *-tavái* (< *-*tave* + *vái*), e.g., *yā´tavái*, *kártavái*, *mánthitavái*; several forms in -*am* with *ná śak* ‘be unable’; a good number in -*tos* with *īśvará*- ‘able’, *ā´* ‘till’ and *purā´* ‘before’; and some forms in -*as* with *īśvará*-. All go back to case forms of predominantly -*tu*- and root nouns. Some (e.g., in the case of -*dhyai*) are built to marked verbal stems.

#### 5.8.2. Participles

Participles (part.) are made from every verbal stem. In the active of the present and aorist, the suffix -*ánt*-/-*nt*-/-*at*- (2.4.12) is employed, with f. -*ī*- or -*ī́*- (*devī́*- type) built to the weak form. In the middle, -*māna*- is employed for thematic, -*āná*- for athematic stems; f. -*mānā*- or -*ānā´*-/-*ānā*-. These go back to PIE *-*m̥h₁nó*-, from which -*āná*- is derived directly; an expected *-*am(ī˘)na*- < *-*o-mh₁no*- (cf. Av. -*amna*-/-*əmna*-) in the thematic stems has been remodelled into -*a*-*māna*-, levelled from -*āná*-. The suffix for the perfect is -*vā´ṁs-*/-*úṣ-* (2.4.13) in the active, and -*āná*-in the middle.

#### 5.8.3. Verbal adjectives

Verbal adjectives, commonly known as perfect passive participles, are formed with -*tá*-, or in certain cases with -*ná*-, added to the root in zero grade, if this is phonologically realizable. They have predominantly patientive meaning, but in the case of fientive/intransitive verbs they lack such a value, e.g. *ga*-*tá*- ‘gone’ (*gam*). If the root ends with a laryngeal, a variety of forms appear: *hitá*-, -*dhitá*- (*dhā*); *gītá*- (*gā*); -*tta*-, (*tvā´*-)*dāta*-, remodelled *dat*-*tá*- (*dā*); *gr̥bhītá*- (*grabhⁱ*), *līná*- (*layⁱ*), *gīrṇá*- (*garⁱ*). Bartholomae’s law plays a role in, e.g., *buddhá*- (*bodh*), *dugdhá*-(*dogh*), *ūḍhá*- (*vah*). Some verbal adjectives have become independent, e.g. *pūrṇá*-‘full’, *ūná*- ‘lacking’, *tr̥ṣṭá*- ‘harsh, hoarse’, and new forms are made: *prātá*- ‘filled’, -*vātá*- ‘extinguished’, *tr̥ṣitá*- ‘thirsty’. The formation -*tá*-*vant*-, attested sometimes in its proper meaning in the RV, is in Classical Sanskrit used as “perfect participle active”. In Vedic, however, this usage is found only for *aśitā´*-*vant*- AV IX 6,38 (prose): *aśitā´vaty átithau* ‘when the guest has eaten’.

#### 5.8.4. Gerundives

Gerundives (predominantly “future passive participles” from transitive verbs) have 6 formations, 3 of which occur almost only in the RV: -*ā´yᵢya*-, -*en₍ᵢ₎ya*-, and -*t₍ᵤ₎va*-. -*ya*-(very often /-*iya*-/) is the most productive throughout OIA. Those in -*tavyà*- (-*tavíya*-) and -*anī́ya*- are common in the later language, attested only from the AV onward.

#### 5.8.5. Gerunds

The gerund (absolutive, “conjunctive participle”) has five formations: -*tvā´*, -*tvā´ya* (RV, some forms also in YSᵐ and in verse in ŚB−AB), -*tvī́* (the commonest form in the RV), -*yā˘* (-*yā* is dominant in the RV, later only -*yă*,), and -*am*. The first four suffixes, probably of instrumental origin, are, in most cases, added to a weak root-form. -*am*, nearly always added to compounded verbs, denotes an action accompanying that of the finite verb, simultaneously or collaterally, often in a repetition; it has no certain occurrence in the RV. The stem shows *-*o-* grade, and the forms apparently originate in the accusative singular of a noun. Those in -*tvā´* and -*ya* survive also later, and are standard from Vedic prose through Epic and Classical Sanskrit, for simplices and compound verbs, respectively. The use of gerunds is not as common in Vedic as in later Sanskrit.
