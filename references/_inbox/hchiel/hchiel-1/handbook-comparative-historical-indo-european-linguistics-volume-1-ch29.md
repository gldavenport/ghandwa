## 1. Introduction

The Indo-Aryan (IA) branch of Indo-European is roughly to be differentiated into Old (OIA), Middle (MIA), and New Indo-Aryan (NIA). The native term *Sanskrit* denotes the OIA language in contrast to the native term *Prākrit*, which corresponds to MIA. Vedic Sanskrit is the oldest layer of OIA, taking its name from the text corpus of the Veda. From a linguistic point of view, Classical Sanskrit is a grammatically reduced form of Vedic Sanskrit; it is the language of classical Indian literature, and is still in use today as a literary language. The different varieties of IA have influenced each other in the past and continue to do so in the present. Due to the time span of about three millennia, and also due to the great number of different literary genres, there exists a vast quantity of Sanskrit texts, as a further consequence of which the Sanskrit lexicon is notoriously rich.

Lexicographical works based on Sanskrit and MIA languages were compiled already in antiquity; for the history of native Indian lexicography see Vogel (1979), Patkar (1981), and Katre (1991). The main modern reference work and basis for any research on the Sanskrit lexicon is still the so-called (*great*) *St. Petersburg Dictionary* (*Petersburger Wörterbuch* = PW) by Otto von Böhtlingk and Rudolf von Roth, which appeared in seven volumes between 1855 and 1875 (an interesting insight into its making can be gained from the edition of Böhtlingk’s letters to Roth by Brückner et al. 2007). It covers the literature from Vedic times to the early Classical period and gives text quotations, grammatical information, and references to native Indian lexicographical works, but is less extensive with respect to special terminology of Indic arts and sciences. A revised edition of this monumental and groundbreaking dictionary is the so-called *small St. Petersburg Dictionary* (= pw) edited by Böhtlingk between 1879 and 1889, which lacks most of the text references and quotations PW provides, but contains additional entries. The supplement to pw by Schmidt (1924−1928) is another important work to consult. From PW a one-volume excerpt was distilled, the *Sanskrit-Wörterbuch* by Cappeller ([1887] 1966), the English version of which (Cappeller [1891] 1972) is richer with respect to entries than the German version. The main Sanskrit-English dictionary is the one-volume manual by Monier-Williams (= MW), which is largely based on PW as well, but lacks text quotations. The *Practical Sanskrit-English dictionary* by Apte (¹1890, revised edition by Gode/Karve 1957−1959) contains quite a few lemmata to be found neither in PW nor in its offsprings, among them many items only to be found in works of Indian lexicographers, but its main focus is Classical Sanskrit. An on-going, highly ambitious project is the *Encyclopaedic dictionary of Sanskrit on historical principles* known as the *Poona Sanskrit Dictionary* (= PD), which has been appearing in fascicles since 1976; its aim is to present a thesaurus of all Sanskrit literature from Vedic times up to the 18ᵗʰ century CE. The last fascicle appeared in 2013. Apart from these comprehensive manuals, there exists a great number of specialized dictionaries focusing on (1) various text corpora, such as Grassmann’s dictionary of the Rigveda ([1873] ⁶1996) or the SWTF restricted to the Buddhist literature of the Turfan manuscripts, (2) special languages such as the Buddhist Hybrid Sanskrit Dictionary by Edgerton (= BHSD), (3) specific subjects of Indian arts and sciences, such as Renou (1957), which is on grammatical terminology. A bibliography of lexicographical works on IA languages can be found in Heidermanns (2005: 1247 ff.); see also the overviews by Katre (1991) on OIA, Strandberg (1991) on MIA, and Singh (1991) on NIA lexicography.

## 2. Inherited vocabulary

The main bulk of the Sanskrit lexicon is inherited from PIE; therefore Sanskrit, as a very early attested *Großkorpussprache*, is one of the basic sources for the reconstruction of the PIE lexicon. A rough scan of ²LIV reveals that about 60% of the PIE verbal roots listed there are said to have avatars in IA. The standard reference work for OIA etymology is *Etymologisches Wörterbuch des Altindoarischen* (EWAia) by Mayrhofer. Some part of the inherited lexicon, interestingly enough including terms pertaining to the religious-ritual sphere, certainly goes back to Indo-Iranian (IIr.) times but lacks both direct cognates in languages other than Iranian and evident PIE etymologies (e.g., the name of the ritual plant Skt. *sóma-* = Av. *haoma-* or the divine name Skt. *Índra-* = Av. *Iṇdra-*‘name of a demon’).

Despite the fact that Sanskrit is an exceedingly well-attested language, some apparently inherited words are found in MIA only, see Oberlies (1999: 44 f.); on the other hand, some Vedic words are missing in Classical Sanskrit, and then show up again in MIA, see Oberlies (2001a: 9 ff.) and (2001b: 368 f.).

## 3. Loan words

In general, loans do not play a very significant role in any layer of the IA language group, but it must be said that the percentage of loan words increases from OIA to NIA. Generally speaking, one has to distinguish between (more or less) obvious loans from foreign languages of non-IE origin, and evident loans from different layers of IA itself. With regard to the first kind, even such a prominent advocate of a robust presence of loans from non-IE local substrates in the Rigveda as Kuiper estimated that such loans amounted to 4% of the RV lexicon only (1995: 261). Therefore, one can argue that (Rig)Vedic Sanskrit was a highly conservative language, which is certainly not confined to the lexicon. The same can be said of Sanskrit of later periods. Even Sanskrit of today as well as NIA languages like Hindī prefer calques over loans when it comes to denoting modern concepts, e.g., ‘television’ is rendered by *dūradarśana-* in Sanskrit and *dūrdəršən* in Hindī.

### 3.1. Loan words of non-IE origin

The question of non-IE loan words in OIA is connected with the problem of the origin of the speakers of IA on the Indian subcontinent; for a general overview on this topic, see, e.g., Bryant (2001) and the papers in Bryant (2005); Witzel (most recently 2006) has devoted many studies to identifying different layers of such loan words in OIA and their implication for Vedic (pre)history. He and other scholars assume that those lexemes of OIA shared with Iranian (cf. 2 above) that lack evident IE etymologies are to be connected with the urban civilizations of Central Asia of the 2ⁿᵈ millennium BCE, notably the culture reflected in the Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex (BMAC); see, e.g., Witzel (1999: 341 f.), Lubotsky (2001), and Pinault (2006); Lubotsky actually put forth a list of 124 lexical items that can be assigned to the religious sphere (e.g., **anću*-‘Soma plant’ or **ućig*- ‘sacrificing priest’), animals (**[H]uštra*- ‘camel’), and material objects (**išt[y]a-* ‘brick’); for some of these items also archaeological evidence can be put forth, and, according to Pinault (most recently 2006), some of them have also been borrowed into Tocharian.

Generally speaking, the obvious non-IA candidates for borrowings in the IA lexicon are Dravidian, Austro-Asiatic, and probably Tibeto-Burman as well as protoforms of isolated languages of South Asia such as Burushaski (on which see Witzel 1999: 339); in addition, one has, of course, also to reckon with the existence of substratum languages otherwise unattested; note that according to Masica (1978: 134 ff.), around 30% of the agriculture terms of Hindī come from such an unknown source.

For lexical items of Vedic Sanskrit that may have been borrowed from Austro-Asiatic or Dravidian see above all various publications by Emeneau (most recently 1974) and Kuiper (most recently 1991). According to Kuiper, such loans belong to the spheres of natural objects and agriculture including animals, plants, and agricultural products (e.g., *ṛbīsa-* ‘(ashen) pit, oven’, *odaná-* ‘kind of porridge’, *kṛkalasá*- ‘chameleon’, *píppala*-‘Ficus religiosa’, *lāṅgala*- ‘plough’), magical/religious practice, music (e.g., *āṅgūṣá-*‘song of praise’), and anthroponymy.

This strategy applied in order to account for words that seem to lack good IE etymologies and which often also look somewhat suspicious with respect to phonology and/or morphology, has not met with general approval, however, and has failed to convince especially Das (1995: 208) who claimed that there is “*not a single case* in which a *communis opinio* has been found confirming the foreign origin of a Rigvedic (and probably Vedic in general) word”. Since the possible non-IA donor language (group)s are not attested as early as Vedic Sanskrit is (or may not be otherwise attested at all), scholars defending the loan-word theory have to rely on internal reconstruction, and therefore are forced to move on less firm grounds indeed. For such reasons, also Mayrhofer in EWAia only cautiously accepts non-IE etymologies, and he explicitly states (1985: 123) that as far as Vedic words (in contrast to post-Vedic ones) are concerned, any interpretation in terms of OIA phonological and morphological rules is “principally” to be preferred over explanations as loans from non-IA or even from MIA (for such loans see 3.3 below).

Other scholars take a mediative position; e.g., Oberlies (1994) admits that about 250 out of Kuiper’s 383 appellative examples may reasonably be interpreted as loans from non-IA languages, and that the same may hold for about 100 personal names; on the latter see also esp. Witzel (1999: 354 ff.). As for the semantic fields of religion and music/musical instruments, to judge from Oberlies’ lists there are in the end not too many examples, and some of the suggested loans can easily be interpreted as onomatopoeic (e.g. *karkarí-* ‘a kind of lute’, *gárgara-* ‘a musical instrument’, see EWAia, s. v., which for the latter compares the hapax *gárgara-* ‘whirlpool’; for *aghaṭí-* ‘sound of cymbals’, see the ref. in Oberlies 1994: 336); the basic meaning of *bákura*- ‘bagpipe’ seems to be ‘bag’, so that the term may have referred to an animal or a plant in the first place.

### 3.2. Loan words from other IE languages

OIA has also some old loan words from Iranian languages and Greek (see AiGr I: LVI), and possibly also Nūristanī (Hoffmann 1986; on the disputed pedigree of Nūristanī see Degener 2002).

### 3.3. Dialectisms or Prakritisms

Many Vedic words of a non-standard shape can be plausibly explained as derivations from IE and/or IA protoforms if one is willing to assume that those protoforms underwent phonological changes typical of MIA rather than of OIA, i.e., that the respective words are borrowings into Vedic Sanskrit from other OIA dialects and/or other sociolects, i.e., from rather informal styles, which would have shared many phonological traits with MIA. Therefore, it would be quite appropriate to call such borrowings “Prakritisms”; on this question see esp. Oberlies (1994) with ref.; on Vedic dialect variants in general, see, e.g., the papers in Caillat (1989); note that even Kuiper (1991: 28), who otherwise strictly opposed the idea of Prakritisms in the Rigveda, did not deny the existence of different registers in the Vedic language.

### 3.4. Sanskritization of MIA and NIA

MIA and NIA languages show a higher percentage of loans from non-IA languages than Sanskrit. In some semantic fields those loans even outnumber the inherited lexemes; e.g., according to Masica (1978: 131), about 80% of the agricultural terminology of Hindī is of non-IA origin. Further, MIA and NIA languages also borrowed (and still borrow) a huge number of lexical items from Classical Sanskrit (“Sanskritization”). Indian grammarians distinguish between *tatsama* lexemes (“the same as that [i.e., Sanskrit]”) and *tadbhava* lexemes (“originating from there [i.e. Sanskrit]”); see Masica (1991: 64 ff.) and von Hinüber (2001: 90) contra Kahrs (1992). *tatsama* lexemes are direct borrowings from Classical Sanskrit, i.e., learned loan words, whereas *tadbhava* lexemes are regular MIA or NIA outcomes of the respective Sanskrit terms by sound law. NIA languages therefore possess a certain number of *tadbhava* and *tatsama* doublets, with *tatsama* words belonging to the culturally more prestigious or more formal register. Classical Sanskrit terms, especially those pertaining to arts, sciences, and religion, were in addition also borrowed into non-IA languages of the Indian subcontinent and (South/) East Asia, notably Austro-Asiatic, Burmese, Chinese, Cambodian, Dravidian (see Emeneau and Burrow 1962), Japanese, Indonesian (see Gonda [1952] 1973), Mongolian, Thai, Tibetan, and into now extinct languages of Central Asia such as Tocharian (see Adams, this handbook, 3.1).

## 4. Specific vocabulary

Sanskrit is very rich in specific vocabulary from the spheres of science, philosophy, medicine, astrology, grammar, etc., due to the vast amount of such specialized literature written in Sanskrit. Hence, one encounters an abundance of synonyms and quasi-synonyms, and words taking on different meanings in different registers and in different cultural contexts. To a certain extent this holds for the vocabulary of religious works as well, because formation and meaning of certain terms were a mark of different schools, see von Hinüber (2002) on Buddhist Hybrid Sanskrit.

## 5. Word formation

Vedic Sanskrit still makes regular use of vowel gradation and accent shift inherited from PIE in word formation. Much as in PIE, the base of the verbal system is the root (Skt. *dhātu*), to which different affixes are added for the purposes of mood stem and tense stem formation. Beside inherited patterns of primary verbal stem formation and denominative present stem formation, there exist also productive patterns for the formation of various deverbative present stems (causatives/factitives, desideratives, intensives) that basically can be built from any verbal root, which cannot be said of any other branch of IE. The most productive nominal stems are the thematic ones, i.e., those having suffixal -*a*- or having suffixes ending in -*a*-. As for feminine derivation, stem-final -*a*- of adjectives is replaced mostly by -*ā*- and that of substantives mostly by -*ī-*/*-iy-* (the so-called *vṛkī-* inflection), whereas to athematic noun stems *-ī(-)*/*-yā*- (the so-called *devī*- inflection) is attached for that purpose. In general, Sanskrit has kept or made productive various inherited ways of forming action nouns, agent nouns, adjectives (including adjectival abstracts and gradation forms), and diminutives, and has gone much beyond the inherited limits in forming nominal compounds (both endo- and exocentric). In fact, compound formation remains to this day one of Sanskrit’s main devices for creating new lexical items.
