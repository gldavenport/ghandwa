# 4. Linnaean linguistics

# ‘Bear’, ‘horse’, ‘wolf’ and the Indo-European phylogeny from a zoographical perspective

<i>Stefan Höfler</i>

Austrian Academy of Sciences & University of Vienna

## Abstract

Taking inspiration from the biological taxonomy of mammals, this paper explores the diversification of the Indo-European language family through a zoographical lens. It investigates shared innovations of phonology, morphology, and semantics in zoonyms across language branches. The aim is to uncover evidence for early splits within the family tree. The study primarily centers on Anatolian versus Core Indo-European and features an extensive discussion of <i>*h₂ŕ̥tk̑o-</i> ‘bear’ (Hittite <i>ḫartakka</i>-) vs. <i>*h₂ŕ̥k̑þo-</i> (Vedic <i>ŕ̥kṣa</i>-, Greek ἄρκτος, Latin <i>ursus</i>, etc.), <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>- ‘horse’ (Hittite */ekkus/, Luwian /azzu-/) vs. <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>- (Vedic <i>áśva</i>-, Latin <i>equus</i>, Tocharian B <i>yakwe</i>, etc.), and <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘lion’ (Luwian <i>walwa/i-</i>) vs. <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘wolf’ (Vedic <i>vŕ̥ka-</i>, Lithuanian <i>vil͂kas</i>, Tocharian B <i>walkwe</i>, etc.). Thorough analysis of these examples will determine their relevance within the proposed scenarios.

## 1. Introduction

The way individual members of a language family are distributed on a phylogenetic tree is reminiscent of models that have been in use in biology for over 200 years.[^1] The hierarchy of parent languages, branches, sub-branches, and individual idioms can be compared to the system of taxonomy established by the Swedish botanist, zoologist, and physician Carl Linnaeus (1707–1778) who devised a classification of organisms into kingdoms, classes, orders, genera, and species.[^2] The greatest change to this system was the widespread acceptance of evolution as the driving force behind biological diversity and the formation of species subsequent to Charles Darwin’s (1809–1882) publication of <i>On the origin of species</i> in 1859. It became evident that the Linnaean classification reflects the phylogeny of organisms, i.e., their descent by evolution. Interestingly, a similar model for the genealogy of the Indo-European languages had already been claimed some decades earlier.[^3]

Without taking the analogy too far, a simple comparison of a traditional phylogenetic tree of the class <i>Mammalia</i> (mammals)[^4] with a tree of the Indo-European language family[^5] shows superficial similarities between the two models in how orders and subfamilies branch off and bifurcate. The ramification of the mammal tree depends (or, in pre-DNA times, depended) on the evaluation and integration of shared innovations and retained archaisms. The order <i>Monotremata</i> (monotremes), for example, is generally considered the first (extant) group to branch off. Monotremes (such as the platypus and the echidna) retain a couple of archaisms vis-à-vis the other mammals:[^6] they lay eggs and only have one orifice for urinating, defecating, and reproduction (a “cloaca”), both traits of which they share with reptiles. The remaining (extant) mammals, subsumable under the subclass <i>Theria</i> (comprising the marsupials and the placental mammals) have a number of innovations in common that distinguish them from monotremes.[^7] For one thing, they developed a placenta (in rudimentary form in marsupials) that facilitated nutrient exchange between mother and foetus and allowed them to give birth to live young without a shelled egg, and for another, they developed teats, i.e., projections from the mammary glands from which milk is ejected for the purpose of feeding the offspring.[^8]

In linguistics, too, the combination of shared innovations and retained archaisms plays a vital role in establishing a family tree. To be sure, archaisms can be deceiving both in mammals and in language families, as some seemingly archaic features turn out to be secondary developments, such as the loss of external ears (“pinnae”) in whales and some types of seals, or the re-formation of a secondary cloaca in some non-monotreme mammals such as the tenrecs. Within the Indo-European languages, a comparable case is, for instance, the gender system of most Scandinavian languages with a common and a neuter gender, similar to the archaic system of the Anatolian languages. On the other hand, while a certain trait might indeed be a retained archaism, it does not necessarily provide diagnostic evidence for classification. This is because there is a chance that the archaism was retained independently in several subgroups. The fact that Latin, along with certain other branches, preserves (at least in residual forms) the inherited optative, does not move the Italic branch closer to optative-retaining Indo-Iranian and Greek and away from optative-less Celtic. A series of Italo-Celtic isoglosses[^9] (such as for example the superlative suffix <i>*-ism̥mo-</i>) warrant a closer affinity of these two branches and therefore outweigh a single instance of a unilaterally preserved archaism.[^10]

For linguistic subgrouping, shared innovations are therefore the most significant factor. Only a non-trivial change of a feature <i>x</i> to <i>y</i>, that is shared by a group of branches and cannot be explained as a parallel development that happened separately and independently in each branch, can be used for ascertaining a closer genealogical relationship of the group that shares this innovated feature <i>y</i>. In theory, the isogloss under consideration can be from the following areas: phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, and the lexicon. Since this study will be focused on zoonyms, evidence from syntax will not play a role. All the other areas, however, can be exploited in the following ways.

A. SHARED INNOVATIONS IN SOUND: If a zoonym <i>X</i> in one language branch α is different from the etymologically related zoonym <i>X</i>ʹ in the remaining languages by presenting a phonological trait that seems to be more archaic, while all other languages present a more advanced stage, it might be evidence of an early split-off of language branch α. The sound change leading to <i>X</i>ʹ in the remaining languages would then be a shared common innovation of these languages. Unless the sound change is trivial in nature, this scenario is more economical than assuming that all remaining languages underwent the same sound change independently.

B. SHARED INNOVATIONS IN FORM: If a zoonym <i>X</i> in one language branch α is different from the etymologically related zoonym <i>X</i>ʹ in the remaining languages by exhibiting morphology that seems more archaic, it might be evidence of an early split-off of language branch α. The remodelling of <i>X</i>ʹ in the remaining languages would then be a shared common innovation of these languages.

C. SHARED INNOVATIONS IN MEANING: If a zoonym <i>X</i> in one language branch α has a different meaning ‘X’ from the etymologically related zoonym <i>X</i> with a meaning ‘Y’ in the remaining languages whereby the change of ‘X’ to ‘Y’ or the other way round is not trivial, it might be evidence of an early split-off of language branch α. The semantic change ‘X’ >> ‘Y’ in the remaining languages would then be a shared common innovation of these languages.

D. SHARED INNOVATIONS IN LEXICON: If for a meaning ‘Z’ one language branch α has a zoonym <i>X</i> but the remaining languages all have an etymologically unrelated zoonym <i>Y</i>, it might be evidence of an early split-off of language branch α. The lexical replacement <i>X >> Y</i> for ‘Z’ in the remaining languages would then be a shared common innovation of these languages.

It must be stressed from the outset that shared innovations in the latter two dimensions are rather unreliable, since semantic change on the one hand and lexical replacement on the other rarely follow strict systematic rules and are seldom non-trivial. Evidence from phonology is much more useful, especially when it pertains to weird and typologically rare sound changes. The more idiosyncratic a specific change, the less likely it is to have occurred independently. Morphological innovations are generally considered the best indicator for genetic subgrouping, as changes in this component are typically least predictable.[^11] As a rule, a combination of shared phonological and morphological traits therefore proves to be most felicitous in establishing a linguistic phylogeny.

The goal of this contribution is quite modest. Outlining a fine-grained diversification of the family tree would not be possible based on zoonyms alone. I will therefore limit myself to trying to establish whether animal names furnish evidence to corroborate an early split-off hypothesis of the Anatolian branch, as this bifurcation is the most widely accepted amongst scholars of Indo-European linguistics.[^12] In order to refer to the individual proto-languages at the consecutive stages I will employ the terminology proposed by Olander (2019), in particular the terms “Proto-Indo-European” or “PIE” (proto-language of all the Indo-European languages), “Proto-Indo-Tocharian” or “PIT” (proto-language of the remaining 11 branches[^13] after the split-off of Anatolian), and “Proto-Indo-Celtic” or “PIC” (proto-language of the remaining 10 branches after the split-off of Tocharian that is generally[^14] – though not universally[^15] – assumed to be the second one to branch off). I will examine one significant and much-discussed example for each mentioned scenario. The example for A will be <i>*h₂ŕ̥tk̑o-</i> ‘bear’ (Hitt. <i>ḫartakka-</i>) vs. <i>*h₂ŕ̥k̑þo-</i> (Ved. <i>ṛ́kṣa-</i>, Gk. ἄρκτος, Lat. <i>ursus</i>, OIr. <i>art</i>, etc.), for B the <i>u</i>-stem <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>- ‘horse’ (Hitt. ANŠE.KUR.RA-<i>uš</i> /ekkus/) vs. thematic <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>- (Ved. <i>áśva</i>-, Lat. <i>equus</i>, etc.), for C the alleged case of <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo- ></i> Luw. <i>walwa/i-</i> ‘lion’ vs. Ved. <i>vṛ́ka-</i>, Lith. <i>vil͂kas</i>, etc., all ‘wolf’, and for D the potential replacement of Proto-Indo-European <i>*u̯l̥p-</i> ‘wolf’ by Proto-Indo-Tocharian <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘dangerous one’ > ‘wolf’. All examples will be examined carefully to assess their validity for the proposed scenarios.

## 2. Phonology – the word for ‘bear’

In terms of phonological innovations, the most intriguing zoonym in PIE is the word for ‘bear’.[^16] It is attested (directly or indirectly through a potential derivative) in almost every branch except for Germanic, Slavic, and (presumably) Tocharian: Hitt. <i>ḫartakka-</i>, Ved. <i>ṛ́kṣa-</i>, YAv. <i>arša-</i>, Pers. <i>xirs</i>, Gk. ἄρκτος (f.), Lat. <i>ursus</i>, OIr. <i>art</i>, Welsh <i>arth</i>, Arm. <i>arǰ</i>, OAlb. <i>ar</i> all continue the simplex; in Lithuanian we find <i>irštvà</i> f. ‘bear’s den’, an apparent derivative of (*)<i>iršas</i> that might still be attested in the phrase <i>piktas kaip iršas</i> ‘angry as an <i>iršas</i>’.[^17]

Prior to the discovery and the subsequent decipherment of Hittite, the sound correspondence Ved. <i>-kṣ- ~</i> Av. <i>-š- ~</i> Gk. -κτ- ~ Lat. <i>*-ks-</i> (<i>ursus < *orksos</i>) in this and a handful of other words (e.g., Ved. <i>tákṣaṇ-</i> ~ Av. <i>tašan-</i> ~ Gk. τέκτων ‘carpenter’ ~ Lat. <i>texere</i> ‘fit together, weave’) used to be explained by assuming a ‘<i>thorn</i> cluster’, i.e., a cluster of a (palato-, labio-, or plain) velar stop followed by a dental fricative <i>*þ</i>.[^18] Accordingly, the ‘bear’ word was set up as <i>*ŕ̥k̑þo-</i> (thus, for instance, in IEW: 875; in laryngealistic terms <i>*h₂ŕ̥k̑þo</i>-) and the root of the ‘carpenter’ as <i>*tek̑þ-</i>. The correspondence set Ved. <i>kṣám- ~</i> Gk. χϑών ~ Lat. <i>humus</i> furnished evidence for the voiced-aspirated counterpart <i>*g̑ʰþ</i> (or <i>*g̑ʰðʰ</i>) qua <i>*g̑ʰþóm</i>- ‘earth’.

The discovery of Anatolian, however, has challenged this view. The word for ‘earth’ is attested in Hittite as nom. sg. <i>tēkan</i>, gen. <i>taknaš</i>, which has led to a revised reconstruction of the word as an ablauting <i>m</i>-stem nom. sg. <i>*dʰég̑ʰōm</i>, gen. <i>*dʰg̑ʰm-é/ós</i>,[^19] i.e., with the dental and the palato-velar in reverse order. Tocharian, too, exhibits a dental plus velar cluster in the word for ‘earth’ (A <i>tkaṃ</i>, B <i>keṃ</i> < <i>*dʰg̑ʰom-</i>).[^20] This suggests that thorn clusters started out as tautosyllabic sequences of a dental and a dorsal stop (<i>*-TK-)</i> that were preserved in Hittite and Tocharian but metathesized to <i>*-KÞ-</i> in the other Indo-European daughter languages.[^21] This dichotomy between Anatolian and Tocharian on the one hand, and the remaining branches on the other, lends itself to a chronological interpretation of the phenomenon. Instead of assuming that the ten remaining branches each developed a metathesis in <i>*-TK-</i> clusters independently, the more economical hypothesis would be to interpret the thorn metathesis as a common innovation of Proto-Indo-Celtic after Anatolian and Tocharian had left the family.[^22] The PIE word for ‘bear’ can therefore be reconstructed as <i>*h₂ŕ̥tk̑o-</i> (reflected by Hitt. <i>ḫartakka-</i> /<i>ḫartka-</i>/), which after the secession of Anatolian and Tocharian underwent metathesis to <i>*h₂ŕ̥k̑þo-</i> (reflected by Ved. <i>ṛ́kṣa-</i>, Gk. ἄρκτος, Lat. <i>ursus</i>, etc.).[^23] Note that the syllabification of this word needs to have been <i>*h₂ŕ̥.tk̑o</i>- (and <i>*h₂ŕ̥.k̑þo</i>-, respectively) for the thorn cluster to be in tautosyllabic position.[^24] This might look counterintuitive at first glance, but since <i>*tk̑</i>- (<i>*k̑þ-</i>) was a possible word onset in PIE (and PIC) as shown, for instance, by the root <i>*√tk̑ei̯ > *√k̑þei̯</i> ‘settle’ (Ved. <i>kṣéti</i>, Gk. κτίζω, Lat. <i>situs</i>, etc.), it follows that it must have also been a possible syllable onset.[^25]

The exact phonetic reality behind *-<i>KÞ-</i> is a matter of debate. Scholars today are quite certain that the second element was not actually a thorn (i.e., a dental fricative) as proposed by the Neogrammarians.[^26] Since the daughter languages realize the second element of the cluster as either a sibilant (Ved. <i>-kṣ</i>-, Lat. <i>*-ks-</i>) or a dental stop (Gk. -κτ-, PCelt. <i>*-xt-</i>), recent suggestions have identified it as a dental affricate (<i>*</i>-<i>TK- > *-TsK- > *-KTs-</i>; cf. Melchert 2003; Byrd 2015: 24) or – with convincing arguments – as a palatalized dental (<i>*-TK- > *-T(ʲ)K(ʲ)</i>-[^27] <i>> *-KTʲ</i>-; cf. Jasanoff 2018). For our purposes, the exact phonetics behind the sound change are of lesser concern. Whatever it was, the metathesis in and of itself is not a trivial change that could easily have happened independently in the individual branches, nor is *-<i>KÞ-</i> the predictable outcome of a cluster <i>*-TK-</i>. In addition, while metatheses generally are a sporadic phenomenon, the thorn metathesis affected all tautosyllabic <i>*-TK-</i> clusters throughout the language.[^28] It can therefore be used as evidence for phylogenetic subgrouping and fittingly draws a line between Anatolian and Tocharian on the one hand, and the remaining ten branches on the other.

Apart from being an adequate example for a shared non-trivial innovation of Proto-Indo-Celtic, the ‘bear’ is also an apt illustration of the warning issued in the previous section that retained archaisms cannot be used for subgrouping. As Kümmel (2018) argues, the anlauting <i>x-</i> /x/ in Persian <i>xirs</i> ‘bear’ is the reflex of <i>*h₂</i>-, preserved in some varieties of Western Iranian as <i>x-</i> or <i>h-</i>. Compare Middle Persian (MP) <i>xā́yag</i> ‘egg’ (<i>*h₂ōu̯i̯o-°</i>), MP <i>xām</i> ‘raw’ (<i>*h₂ohxmó-</i>; cf. Av. <i>āma-</i>), MP <i>hesm</i> ‘firewood’ (<i>*h₂eidʰsmo-</i>; cf. Av. <i>aēsma-</i>), etc. In view of the almost ubiquitous loss of any word-initial and word-internal laryngeal in almost all other languages, this archaism is indeed stunning. The only language branch that systematically preserves a consonantal reflex of word- initial <i>*h₂</i> in general and in <i>*h₂ŕ̥tk̑o-</i> in particular is Anatolian (Hitt. <i>ḫartakka-</i>). However, it would be fallacious to embrace the idea of a closer connection of Western Iranian to the Anatolian branch based on this shared trait. The retention of such an archaism might be an intriguing feature, but it is not diagnostic of phylogenetic closeness.

## 3. Morphology – the word for ‘horse’

There is arguably no animal more closely associated with the Proto-Indo-Europeans than the horse. As residents of the Eurasian steppe, the speakers of PIE were probably one of the first people to tame and domesticate the wild horse sometime in the 5th or 4th millennium BCE.[^29] The word is attested – directly or indirectly – in every branch of Indo-European except for Slavic. The continuants include Hitt. *<i>ekku-</i>, CLuw. *<i>azzu-</i>, HLuw. <i>á-zú</i>-, Lycian <i>esb(e/i)-</i>, Ved. <i>áśva-</i>, YAv. <i>aspa-</i>, OPers. <i>asa</i>-, Greek ἵππος, Mycen. <i>i-qo</i>,[^30] Lat. <i>equus</i>, Venet. acc. sg. <i>ekvon</i>, OIr. <i>ech</i>, MW <i>*eb</i> (in <i>cyf-eb</i> ‘in foal’, <i>eb-awl</i> ‘foal’), Gaul. PN <i>Epo-redorix</i>, Goth. <i>*aiƕs</i> (in <i>aiƕa-tundi</i> f. ‘bramble’), OE <i>eoh</i>, ON <i>jór</i>, Toch. B <i>yakwe</i>, A <i>yuk</i>, Arm. <i>ēš</i> ‘donkey’, Lith. <i>ašvà</i> f. ‘mare’, OPruss. <i>aswinan</i> ‘mare’s milk’, Alb. <i>sasë</i> ‘horsetail rush, Equisetum’.[^31] All these forms can be traced back to <i>*h₁ék̑u-</i> or <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o-</i>. The <i>u</i>-stem <i>*h₁ék̑u-</i> seems to be limited to Anatolian, whereas all other languages require or are in line with a thematic masculine<i>*h₁ék̑u̯o-</i>.

In Hittite, ‘horse’ is never spelled out but always written in sumerograms ANŠE.KUR.RA(ᴴI.A/MEŠ) (literally ‘donkey of the mountain(s)’). Sometimes, however, the scribes made use of phonetic complements to indicate case endings, thereby revealing a <i>u</i>-stem inflection. Compare the nom. sg. ANŠE.KUR.RA-<i>uš</i> (OS; OH/NS) and the acc. sg. ANŠE.KUR.RAᴴI.A-<i>un</i> (OH/NS).[^32] The situation is similar in Cuneiform Luwian, where we have one attestation of a nom. sg. ANŠE.KUR.RA-<i>uš</i>.[^33] In Hieroglyphic Luwian, however, we find not only a similarly written nom. sg. (ANIMAL)EQUUS-<i>sa</i>₄, and an acc. sg. (EQUUS)<i>zú-na</i>, but also a fully spelled-out dat.-loc. sg. (EQUUS.ANIMAL)<i>á-zú-wa/i</i> /azzuwi/, and a dat.-loc. pl. (EQUUS)<i>á-zú-wa/i-za</i> /azzuwanz/,[^34] indicating that the Luwian word indeed was <i>azzu-</i>, the regular outcome of PIE <i>*h₁ék̑u-</i>.

To account for the discrepancy between the <i>u</i>-stem in Anatolian and the thematic stem elsewhere, Kloekhorst (2008: 10) writes:

There is no known phonological development through which PIE <i>*h₁eḱu̯o-</i> could yield PAnat. <i>*h₁eḱu-</i> and in view of the productivity of the <i>o</i>-stem inflection in Anatolian it is unlikely that PIE <i>*h₁eḱu̯o-</i> would have yielded PAnat. <i>*h₁eḱu-</i> through secondary developments. We therefore must conclude that the PAnat. <i>u-</i>stem <i>*h₁eḱu-</i> reflects the original state of affairs and that the thematicization as visible in the non-Anatolian IE languages (which is a trivial development) must be regarded as a common innovation of them.

Instead of assuming that the <i>u</i>-stem was thematized in each of the remaining eleven branches independently, it would seem more economical to hypothesize that this thematization happened only once in the predecessor of these eleven branches, i.e., at a time when the Anatolian branch had already split off.[^35]

But at closer inspection, this is not the best possible interpretation. It is disfavored by the following factors. For one thing, thematization of nouns in Proto-Indo-Tocharian is not a trivial process. Unlike what the above-mentioned quote would suggest, there is no systematic transfer of <i>u</i>-stems to the thematic declension in PIT (because if so, there would be no <i>u</i>-stems in Indo-Tocharian). Instead, Anatolian <i>u</i>-stems systematically correspond to Indo-Tocharian <i>u</i>-stems: compare adjectives like Hitt. <i>panku-</i> ‘all, entire’ (: Ved. <i>bahú-</i> ‘many, much, numerous’, παχύς ‘thick’), substantives such as Hitt. <i>ḫaššu-</i> c. ‘king’ (: Av. <i>ahu-</i> ‘lord’, ON <i>áss</i> ‘god’), or neuters such as Hitt. <i>genu-</i> ‘knee’ (: Ved. <i>jā́nu-</i>, Gk. γόνυ, Lat. <i>genū</i>, etc.).

In general, we cannot observe any large-scale transfer of nouns from athematic to thematic inflection at any stage of PIE or PIT.[^36] When thematization of athematic nouns does happen, it is an <i>einzelsprachlich</i> phenomenon and viable only if there is phonological overlap of case endings (in so-called <i>Scharnierform</i>s). As such, it is not limited to post-Anatolian branches of Indo-Tocharian, but occurs in Hittite as well, where the (post-consonantal) athematic acc. sg. ending <i>*-m̥</i> fell together with the thematic acc. sg. ending <i>*-o-m</i> as Hitt. <i>-an</i>,[^37] thus opening the door for inflectional fluctuations.[^38] Something similar happened in Vedic where the ending <i>*-m̥ > *-a</i> was recharacterized by <i>-m</i> as Ved. -<i>am</i> and fell together with thematic <i>-am < *-o-m</i>. As a result, both languages independently exhibit what one could call a sporadic “thematization” of consonant stems. Compare Old Hittite <i>pā́t-</i>, <i>pat-</i> c. (acc. sg. GÌR-<i>an</i> [OH/NS]) and New Hittite <i>pata-</i> c. ‘foot’[^39] and Ved. <i>pád-</i> m. (acc. sg. <i>pā́dam</i>) and <i>pā́da-</i> ‘foot’.[^40] This metaplasm, however, is not a universal development. In Baltic, where <i>*-m̥</i> gave <i>*-im</i> and fell together with the <i>i</i>-stem acc. sg. <i>*-i-m</i>, consonant stems are not “thematized” but remade as <i>i</i>-stems instead. Compare, for instance, the <i>i</i>-stem Lith. <i>žvėrìs</i>, Latv. <i>zvêrs</i>, OPruss. acc. pl. <i>swīrins</i> ‘animal’ vis-à-vis the root noun Gk. θήρ, Lesb. φήρ ‘beast’.[^41] And in Gothic, the inherited root noun <i>*fōt-</i> ‘foot’ became a <i>u</i>-stem Goth. <i>fotus</i> based on case forms like acc. sg. <i>fotu</i> < PGmc. <i>*fōtun < *pōd-m̥</i>.[^42] Such a phenomenon remains sporadic unless (as in the case of consonant stems in Baltic) the whole category is moribund.[^43]

When it comes to PIT <i>u</i>-stems (and, in a parallel fashion, <i>i</i>-stems), a thematization of the suffix <i>*-u-</i> (and <i>*-i-</i>) and its allomorph <i>*-eu̯</i>- (and <i>*-ei̯-</i>) to precisely <i>*-u̯-o-</i> (and <i>*-i̯-o-</i>) would not only be highly unexpected. Apart from the ‘horse’ word under debate, I am not aware of any examples that could be interpreted in such a way in any of the daughter languages. The thematization of athematic (or, more precisely, consonant) stems that we see in some individual languages, and which is made possible by largely <i>einzelsprachlich</i> (or at least post-PIE or post-PIT) sound developments, can therefore not be used to explain a purported PIT change of <i>*h₁ék̑u-</i> to <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o-</i>.[^44]

In purely descriptive terms, however, there exists a “thematization” of athematic stems in PIE (and PIT) and it is in fact well attested. But this only applies if “thematization” is understood as a derivational process rather than as a functionless inflectional extension. As such, this process derives adjectives from substantives by adding <i>*-ó-</i> to the stem (or, one of the stems) of the base word.[^45] Examples of this derivational mechanism can easily be adduced: compare <i>*léu̯k-es-</i> n. ‘light’ (Av. <i>raocah-</i> n. ‘light, day’, Ved. <i>rókas-</i> n.) → <i>*luk-s-ó-</i> ‘having light’ (Ved. <i>rukṣá-</i> ‘shining’), or Gk. ἔρυμα n. ‘fence, defence, guard’ (<i>*</i>-<i>mn̥</i>) → Gk. ἐρυμνός ‘fenced, fortified, strong’ (<i>*-mn-ó-</i>). For <i>u</i>-stem bases, one can cite <i>*módʰu-</i>, <i>*médʰu-</i> n. ‘alcohol’ (Toch. B <i>mot</i>,[^46] Ved. <i>mádhu-</i>, Gk. μέθυ, OIr. <i>mid</i>, MW <i>medd</i>, etc.) → <i>*medʰu̯-ó-</i> ‘having alcohol’ (OIr. <i>medb</i> ‘intoxicating’, MW <i>meddw</i> ‘intoxicated’[^47]), or <i>*k̑óru-</i>, <i>*k̑éru-</i> n. ‘horn’ (cf. Av. <i>sruuā</i>- ‘horn’, Gk. κορυ(-)δός ‘crested lark’, PGmc. <i>*heru(-)taz</i> ‘hart’) → <i>*k̑r̥u̯-ó-</i> ‘having horns’ (MW <i>carw</i> ‘stag’; with a new full grade Lat. <i>ceruus</i> m. ‘stag’ <i>< *k̑eru̯o-</i>).[^48]

The fundamental drawback of this finding for the interpretation of <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o-</i> as a thematized <i>*h₁ék̑u-</i> is that there is no change in meaning between the Anatolian ‘horse’ and the Indo-Tocharian ‘horse’. If the <i>u</i>-stem <i>*h₁ék̑u-</i> meant ‘horse’, the thematic <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o-</i> would have to mean ‘having a horse’, but it doesn’t. On the other hand, if <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o-</i> meant horse, the <i>u</i>-stem <i>*h₁ék̑u-</i> reflected by Hitt. <i>*ekku-</i>, Luw. <i>azzu-</i> would have to mean something other than ‘horse’, yet it doesn’t. Both <i>*h₁ék̑u-</i> (Hitt. <i>*ekku-</i>, Luw. <i>azzu-</i>) and <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o-</i> (Ved. <i>áśva-</i>, YAv. <i>aspa-</i>, Lat. <i>equus</i>, etc.) simply mean ‘horse’. Of course, this does not exclude the possibility of analyzing <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o-</i> as a possessive derivative based on a <i>u</i>-stem <i>*h₁ék̑u-</i> denoting some property characteristic of a horse, it only excludes (in any plausible way, at least) that the latter is what is reflected by Hitt. <i>*ekku-</i>, Luw. <i>azzu-</i> ‘horse’.

Indeed, according to Schindler,[^49] <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>- goes back to precisely such a possessive formation, viz. <i>*h₁(e)k̑</i>-<i>u̯-ó-</i> ‘having swiftness’, derived from an acrostatic <i>u</i>-stem <i>*h₁ók̑-u</i>, *<i>h₁ék̑-u</i>- ‘swiftness’ (preserved as the first member <i>*h₁k̑-u-°</i> in the compound Lat. <i>acu-pedius</i> ‘swift-footed’; furthermore the basis – in one way or another[^50] – of the adjective <i>*hxō(hx)k̑ú-</i> (vel sim.) ‘swift’ > Ved. <i>āśú</i>-, Gk. ὠκύς, Lat. comp. <i>ōcior</i>, OW <i>di-auc</i> ‘lazy’) and substantivized by accent retraction. If this interpretation is correct (and indeed, it does seem to have gained some acceptance[^51]), it would mean that to uphold the idea of Hitt. <i>*ekku-</i> and Luw. <i>azzu-</i> ‘horse’ continuing a PIE <i>u</i>-stem, we would have to conclude that the adjectival abstract <i>*h₁ók̑-u</i>, *<i>h₁ék̑-u</i>- ‘swiftness’ was inherited into Anatolian and only there developed a metaphorical meaning ‘horse’, while in Proto-Indo-Tocharian it served as the derivational base for an exocentric derivative ‘swift’ that came to be substantivized as the word for ‘horse’. This hypothesis is, of course, not very attractive.

Slightly better and a little more refined is the idea that both the Anatolian and the Indo-Tocharian ‘horse’ are possessive derivatives of the acrostatic <i>u</i>-stem <i>*h₁ók̑</i>-<i>u</i>, *<i>h₁ék̑</i>-<i>u</i>- ‘swiftness’; the latter in the manner described above (i.e., via <i>*h₁(e)k̑</i>-<i>u̯-ó-</i> ‘having swiftness’), while the former would constitute an internally (i.e., without overt suffixation) derived adjective, ideally of proterokinetic inflection, quasi <i>*h₁ék̑</i>-<i>u</i>-, <i>*h₁k̑-éu̯</i>- ‘having swiftness’ (cf. <i>*dlóu̯k-u-</i>, <i>*dléu̯k-u-</i> ‘sweetness’ → <i>*dléu̯k-u-</i>, <i>*dluk-éu̯</i>- ‘sweet’ > Gk. γλυκύς, Lat. <i>dulcis</i>[^52]), of which Anatolian would have generalized the strong stem allomorph. The Anatolian ‘horse’ <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>- and the Indo-Tocharian ‘horse’ <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>- would then be isofunctional derivatives of <i>*h₁ók̑</i>-<i>u</i>- ‘swiftness’, one older and internally derived (<i>*h₁ók̑</i>-<i>u</i>-,<i>*h₁ék̑</i>-<i>u</i>- → *<i>h₁ék̑</i>-<i>u</i>-, <i>*h₁k̑-éu̯</i>-) and one more recent and externally derived (<i>*h₁ók̑</i>-<i>u</i>-,<i>*h₁ék̑</i>-<i>u</i>- → *<i>h₁(e)k̑</i>-<i>u̯-ó</i>-). Compare the existence of both an internally derived Gk. γλυκύς (< <i>*dléu̯k-u-</i>, <i>*dluk-éu̯</i>- ‘sweet’) and an externally derived Gk. γλυκκός (< <i>*dluk-u̯-ó-</i> ‘sweet’; cf. γλυκκόν· γλυκύ Hsch.) within Greek.

This scenario requires us to assume that at some stage of post- Anatolian Proto-Indo-Tocharian, the speakers had an old word for ‘horse’, <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>-, that they still analysed as ‘swifty’ (i.e., as being derived from the <i>u</i>-stem <i>*h₁ók̑</i>-<i>u</i>, *<i>h₁ék̑</i>-<i>u</i>- ‘swiftness’), and that they decided (for whatever reason) to re-derive a new word from the underlying <i>u</i>-stem abstract that had the same meaning, ‘swifty’, but was created by a different morphological process (overt suffixation and subsequent accent retraction).[^53] This is a rather complicated, albeit not entirely implausible scenario. To be sure, there must have been a time when these two ways of forming denominal possessives (i.e., the internal and the external mechanism) existed side by side, with the external option slowly gaining ground, so that there were a certain number of isofunctional doublets (as, for example, Av. <i>raocah-</i> adj. ‘light’ <i>< *leu̯k-és-</i> vs. Ved. <i>rukṣá-</i> ‘bright’ < <i>*luk-s-ó-</i>; both derived from an <i>s</i>-stem <i>*léu̯k-es-</i> n. ‘light’; cf. also Gk. γλυκύς and γλυκκός from above) that potentially encouraged a re-derivation of internally derived adjectives from their underlying bases by use of overt suffixes (such as <i>*-ó-</i>). What complicates the matter slightly is that despite having arguably been the fully lexicalized PIE and PIT standard word for ‘horse’ for some time, we must assume that the formation <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>- was still transparent enough for the speakers of Proto-Indo-Tocharian to perform such a re-derivation. Another drawback to this analysis is that while the above-mentioned example of an internally derived <i>s</i>-stem simplex adjective (Av. <i>raocah-</i> adj. ‘light’ <i>< *leu̯k-és-</i>) belongs to a residual class that nowhere shows any signs of productivity, the <i>u</i>-stems (both substantival and adjectival) are a well-established class in most ancient IE languages. It is therefore quite difficult to justify the motivation for replacing a perfectly fine <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>- ‘swift’ (and/or ‘horse’) by its isofunctional counterpart <i>*h₁(e)k̑u̯ó-</i> ‘swift’ (and/or <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>- ‘horse’).[^54]

One last complication that has only been skated over so far concerns the probability of the reconstruction of an adjective <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>- ‘swift’ (the purported source of PAnat. <i>*ek̑u-</i> ‘horse’) in the first place. Strictly speaking, there is only evidence for a <i>*hxō(hx)k̑ú-</i> (vel sim.) ‘swift’ (as in Ved. <i>āśú</i>-, Gk. ὠκύς, Lat. comp. <i>ōcior</i>, OW <i>di-auc</i> ‘lazy’). This form, however, is hardly reconcilable with a purported <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>- ‘swift’, unless one invokes a series of additional hypotheses, all of which would be hard to argue for based on the attested facts alone. One would have to explain the unique substitution of a well-formed <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>- ‘swift’ (the evidence for which is limited to the etymological interpretation of the alleged PIE <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>- and PIT <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>- ‘horse’) by an <i>ō</i>-grade <i>*h₁ōk̑ú</i>- or a reduplicated <i>*h₁o-h₁k̑-u-</i>[^55], neither of which is easy to account for morphologically.[^56]

Note that the explanation of the ‘horse’ as derived from the <i>abstract</i> ‘swiftness’ does not encounter these problems: starting from a root <i>*√h₁ek̑</i> and an abstract <i>*h₁ók̑u</i>- ‘swiftness’, the adjective can be interpreted as a compound <i>*(h₂)o-h₁k̑u-</i> ‘having swiftness to it’ (see note 50). Departing from a different root altogether, namely <i>*√h₁eh₃k̑</i> or <i>*√h₃eh₁k̑</i> (again see note 50) with an adjective <i>*h₁</i>/<i>₃eh₃</i>/<i>₁k̑-ú-</i> and an abstract <i>*h₁</i>/<i>₃óh₃</i>/<i>₁k̑u-</i>, it seems natural to assume that in the expected possessive derivative <i>*h₁</i>/<i>₃h₃</i>/<i>₁k̑u̯-ó-</i> ‘swift’, <i>*h₃</i> (in whichever position) was lost through expected cluster reduction to give <i>*h₁k̑u̯-ó-</i>, which was then substantivized to <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>-.

All attempts to justify the existence of a PIE <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>- ‘horse’ next to a PIT <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>- ‘horse’ therefore require costly assumptions and a concatenation of uneconomical hypotheses, and should be considered in earnest only if there is no other explanation available. But in fact, there is a more convincing alternative at hand to account for the <i>u</i>-stem in Hitt. <i>*ekku-</i>, Luw. <i>azzu-</i> ‘horse’. One could assume (as indeed has been done, among others, by Starke 1995: 120; Sasseville 2017) that an already PIE <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>- ‘horse’ was inherited into Anatolian and only on the way to Hittite and Luwian was remade into a <i>u</i>-stem. The reason for this could be phonological, as a sporadic syncope of -<i>(u)wa-</i> sequences to <i>-u-</i> is a common phenomenon in both Hittite (cf. <i>šanḫuwanzi</i> ~ <i>šanḫunzi</i> ‘they roast’, <i>kuwaliu- ~ kuliu-</i> ‘blue (?)’, etc.[^57]) and Luwian (cf. <i>wanattiš ~ unattiš</i> ‘woman’, <i>walipna/i-</i> <i>~ ulipna/i-</i> ‘wolf’[^58]). For this to work for Hitt. <i>*ekku-</i>, Luw. <i>azzu-</i> ‘horse’, we need to assume that the syncope of -<i>(u)wa-</i> to <i>-u-</i> happened in the nom. sg. *<i>ek̑waš ></i> *<i>ek̑uš</i> already in Proto-Anatolian and led to a reclassification as a <i>u</i>-stem, which is, however, not a very attractive hypothesis given that this syncope is a synchronic phenomenon in both Hittite and Luwian and leads to a situation where syncopated and unsyncopated forms occur alongside each other and “do not seem to show any particular chronological distribution” (Melchert 1984: 53 for Hittite).

The alternative to this phonological scenario is to consider an analogical origin for the <i>u</i>-stem. While in the above-mentioned examples phonological overlap of certain case endings (“<i>Scharnierform</i>s”) led to a thematization of consonant stems in Indo-Iranian, the reverse effect (a “de-thematization”) seemingly resulted in a transfer of certain former thematic stems to an athematic inflection in Hittite. A clear example are the Old Hittite (OH) adjectives in <i>-zz(i)ya-</i> (< <i>*-ti̯o-</i>) that merge with <i>i</i>-stems on the way to New Hittite (NH), e.g., OH <i>ḫantezziya-</i> ‘in front, first’ > NH <i>ḫantezzi</i>-.[^59] Though this development was probably aided by the fact that <i>-(i)ya-</i> sequences, too, undergo a sporadic syncope to <i>-i-</i>,[^60] the main factor that paved the way for the change in stem class was certainly the fact that the case endings of (<i>sc</i>. non-ablauting) <i>i</i>-stems and thematic stems in <i>-iya-</i> are identical in all cases of the paradigm except for the nom. and acc. sg.[^61] The same is true, <i>mutatis mutandis</i>, for consonant stems in <i>-il-</i> and <i>-ul-</i> and thematic stems in <i>-ila-</i>, <i>-ula-</i>; in fact, according to Rieken (2008), the extraordinarily large group of consonant stems in <i>-il-</i> and <i>-ul-</i> in Hittite finds an explanation by assuming that they represent former thematic stems (i.e., formations in <i>*-i-lo-</i> and <i>*</i>-<i>u-lo-</i>) that had been “de-thematized” already in pre-Hittite times.[^62] It is not difficult to see that a similar scenario would work for non-ablauting <i>u</i>-stems and thematic stems in -<i>wa-</i> as well.[^63] They, too, share the same set of endings outside the nom. and acc. sg. in both Hittite and in Luwian,[^64] and this situation probably goes back to Proto-Anatolian. Given these premises, it is not hard to imagine that the inherited thematic word for ‘horse’ <i>*ek̑wa-</i> was transformed into a <i>u</i>-stem <i>*ek̑u-</i> already in Proto-Anatolian, nor is it unthinkable – given the predictability of the process – that this transformation happened independently in the respective prehistories of Hittite and Luwian.[^65] The latter option is actually made somewhat more plausible when an up-to-now neglected Anatolian continuant of <i>*h₁ék̑u(o)-</i> is added to the discussion, namely Lycian <i>esb(e/i)-</i> ‘horse’, which according to Sasseville (2017) and Schürr (2019: 564–565) also continues the thematic stem. The only case form securely attested (apart from the poss. adj. nom. sg. c. <i>esbehi</i>) is the abl./instr. <i>esbedi</i> with the ending <i>-edi</i> matching other former thematic stems.[^66] On the other hand, it has been argued that <i>esbedi</i> and <i>esbehi</i> need to be segmented as <i>esb-edi</i>, <i>-ehi</i> with <i>-edi</i>, <i>-ehi</i> simply reflecting the expected endings (cf. the CLuw. counterparts -<i>āti</i> and <i>-ašša/i-</i>) and <i>esb-</i> continuing the <i>u</i>-stem PAnat. <i>*ek̑u-</i> (or rather <i>*ek̑u̯</i>-).[^67] At present, our knowledge about the synchronic nominal system of Lycian and its diachronic developments is too limited to ascertain definitively the (former) stem class of the Lycian substantive.[^68] For our purposes, however, either option would be in line with the two scenarios outline above: a (former) <i>u</i>-stem <i>esb-</i> would confirm that the remodelling of the stem in <i>*-wa-</i> to <i>*-u-</i> happened already in Proto-Anatolian; a (former) thematic stem <i>esbe/i-</i>, on the other hand, would virtually guarantee that the Hittite and the Luwian <i>u</i>-inflection are secondary. Either way, Anatolian can indeed have inherited a thematic <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o-</i> of PIE age. The consequence of this is that the word cannot be used to demonstrate an early split-off of Anatolian.[^69]

## 4. Semantics and Lexicon – the word for ‘wolf’

The third case study, the word for ‘wolf’, serves as a potential example for a shared innovation in both semantics and the lexicon. The word <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> is represented by Ved. <i>vṛ́ka-</i>, Av. <i>vəhrka-</i>, Pers. <i>gorg</i>, Lith. <i>vil͂kas</i>, Latv. <i>vìlks</i>, OCS <i>vlьkъ</i>, Czech <i>vlk</i>, Gk. λύκος, Lat. <i>lupus</i>,[^70] PGmc. <i>*wulfaz</i> (Goth. <i>wulfs</i>, ON <i>úlfr</i>, OE <i>wulf</i>, OHG <i>wolf</i>),[^71] Alb. <i>ujk</i>, Toch. B <i>walkwe</i>, but it is missing from Anatolian, at least in a meaning ‘wolf’. It has been claimed, however (first by Lehrman 1978; see also Lehrman 1989), that <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> lives on in CLuw. <i>walwa(/i)-</i> ‘lion’ (which was subsequently equated with Lyd. <i>walwe-</i> by Wallace 1986), and some have contended (e.g., Mallory & Adams 2006: 138) that the meaning ‘lion’ is the original one. One could therefore hypothesize that PIE <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> in the first instance meant ‘lion’ (reflected by CLuw. <i>walwa(/i)-</i>) and that Proto-Indo-Tocharian shifted the meaning of the word to ‘wolf’, preserved in Toch. B <i>walkwe</i>, Ved. <i>vṛ́ka-</i>, and so on.

Kloekhorst (2008: 951), however, levelled criticism against the assertion of a CLuw. <i>walwa(/i)-</i> ‘lion’ for being based on weak evidence: <i>walwa(/i)-</i> is only attested as an element in names and according to him, it cannot be unambiguously identified as the spelled-out version of UR.MAḪ ‘lion’. While this scepticism has subsequently been countered with rebuttal by Melchert & Yakubovich (2013: 313, referring to Hawkins) and by Oettinger (2014: 312 with note 25)[^72] who draws attention to the fact that Lyd. <i>walwe-</i> is found on several coins in combination with a lion’s head (see also Dale 2015 especially 162–163; Sasseville 2021b), the phonological reservations of Kloekhorst’s criticism seem substantial: <i>*-kʷ</i>- is not expected to yield Luw. <i>-w-</i> in this position, at least judging from the example he mentions, namely CLuw. <i>papparkuwa-</i> ‘to cleanse’ < *<i>pr̥kʷ</i>- (cf. Hitt. <i>parkui-</i> ‘pure, clean’), representing a comparable phonological context.

However, with Hitt. <i>tarku-</i>, CLuw. <i>taru-</i> ‘dance’ <i>< *terkʷ</i>- ‘twist’ (cf. Lat. <i>torquēre</i>) and Hitt. <i>šākuwa-</i>, CLuw. <i>tāwa/i-</i> ‘eye’ <i>< *sókʷo-</i> ‘seeing’ (?) (cf. Goth. <i>saiƕan</i> ‘see’, etc.), there are two famous examples that seem to guarantee a change <i>*</i>-<i>kʷ</i>- > PAnat. <i>*-gʷ</i>- (> Luw. <i>-w-</i>) in medial position.[^73] In addition, the assertion that Luw. <i>walwa(/i)-</i> contains <i>*-kʷ</i>- (and not simply <i>*-u̯</i>-) is all but guaranteed by the hybrid Luwo-Hittite personal name ᵐ<i>Ura-walkui-</i> in an attractive interpretation as ‘big lion’ (cf. HLuw. MAGNUS-LEO- = <i>*Ura-walwi-</i>).[^74] The element is also extant in the names <i>Walkuwa-</i>, <i>Walkui-</i>, which might just mean ‘Lion’ (quasi <i>Leo</i>).[^75] The reconstruction of a PAnat. <i>*walkʷa-</i>, <i>*walgʷa-</i> ‘lion’ seems therefore unavoidable.

Postulating a simple chronological difference between an alleged PIE <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘lion’ and a PIT <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘wolf’ is, however, not very attractive. In principle, nothing precludes that the original meaning was ‘wolf’ rather than ‘lion’ and that Anatolian innovated on its part. The change ‘wolf’ > ‘lion’ is just as plausible (or implausible, for that matter) as a change ‘lion’ > ‘wolf’. In fact, if we assume a PIE “<i>Urheimat</i>” somewhere in the Pontic Steppe, we can be quite certain that the speakers of PIE did not come in close contact with lions. Even though the historical habitat of the lion stretched north until the Caucasus, it did not reach beyond this mountain range. Anatolia, on the other hand, was populated by lions up until the late 19th century. If anything, this favours the view that the speakers of Anatolian re-used an old word for ‘wolf’ to designate a hitherto unknown and unnamed large predator. Of course, it does not really help this scenario that wolves are quite common in Anatolia up to this day, which makes the repurposing of an old word for ‘wolf’ rather unattractive.

In addition, the historical phonology of Anatolian renders this account almost impossible. Judging from examples like Hitt. <i>ūrki</i>- c. ‘track, trace’ (<i>*u̯r̥g-i-</i>), Hitt. <i>ḫulana-</i> c. ‘wool’ (<i>*h₂u̯l̥h₁n-°</i>) Hitt. <i>ḫurkil-</i> n. ‘perversity’, CLuw. gen. adj. acc. pl. c. <i>ḫurkilaššinza</i> ‘id.’ (<i>*h₂u̯r̥g/g̑(ʰ)-°</i>), the expected Proto-Anatolian outcome of a PIE <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> should have been <i>*ulkʷa-</i> (or <i>*ulgʷa-</i>, see above) and not <i>*walkʷa-</i> (<i>*walgʷa-</i>).[^76] Melchert’s (1994: 127) scenario of complementary sandhi variants (<i>-C# #uRC- vs. -V# #wR̥C</i>-) that would have been generalized in different ways (i.e., the post-consonantal variant for all above-mentioned words and the post-vocalic variant only in the word for ‘lion’) is hardly convincing. Keeping in mind the problems that an identification of <i>*walkʷa-</i> (<i>*walgʷa-</i>) ‘lion’ and <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘wolf’ poses on the semantic side, we might prefer to take up a suggestion made by Oettinger (2014: 313) that the Anatolian forms in reality reflect a different ablaut grade in the root.[^77] Indeed, a thematic stem with an <i>o</i>-grade <i>*u̯olkʷo-</i> would explain the Anatolian facts effortlessly and it can be accounted for on a formal level, too.

Lehrman (1978: 228–230) connected PIE <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘wolf’ etymologically with the adjective Ved. <i>avṛká-</i> ‘safe’, which lends itself to an interpretation as a compound with a meaning ‘not *<i>vṛká-</i>’, implying a simplex *<i>vṛká-</i> ‘harmful, dangerous (vel sim.)’. The same element seems to be extant in <i>vṛkátāt-</i> ‘danger (?)’, though this abstract is a <i>hapax</i> at RV 2.34.9 and could rather mean ‘wolfishness (vel sim.)’. In any event, PIE <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘wolf’ may be analysed as a substantivized adjective ‘the dangerous one’, derived through accent retraction (see above) from an underlying <i>*u̯l̥kʷó-</i> ‘dangerous’ (~ Ved. *<i>vṛká-</i> ‘harmful, dangerous (vel sim.)’). The latter might even be directly attested in OIr. <i>olc</i> ‘evil, bad, wrong’, reflecting a strangely vocalized <i>*ulkʷo- < *u̯l̥kʷó-</i> (instead of expected <i>*u̯likʷo-</i>), which is reminiscent of OIr. <i>olann</i> f. ‘wool’ <i>< *ulanā</i> instead of <i>*u̯lanā</i> (cf. MW <i>gwlan</i> ‘id.’) from <i>*h₂u̯l̥h₁néh₂</i>-.[^78]

If the underlying root <i>*√u̯elkʷ</i> ‘harmful, dangerous’ had an adjectival profile similar to <i>*√(h₁)reu̯dʰ</i> ‘red’ and <i>*√leu̯k</i> ‘bright’, we would not be too surprised to find a zero-grade thematic adjective <i>*u̯l̥kʷ-ó-</i> of the likes we see in <i>*h₁rudʰ-ó-</i> (Lith. <i>rùdas</i>) and <i>*luk-ó-</i> (Ved. <i>rucá-</i>) next to a synonymous <i>o</i>-grade adjective <i>*u̯olkʷ-o-</i> parallel to <i>*h₁rou̯dʰ-o-</i> (PGmc. <i>*raudaz</i>) and <i>*lou̯k-o-</i> (Lith. <i>laũkas</i>). In fact, this <i>*u̯olkʷ-o-</i> ‘dangerous, harmful’ might not only be the source of the ostensible taboo term PAnat. <i>*walkʷa-</i>, <i>*walgʷa-</i> ‘lion’ (qua ‘dangerous one’), it might also underlie the Hittite word <i>walkuwa-</i> c. that is found in two separate texts, of which only one – the Old Hittite tale of the city Zalpa (KBo 22.2) – furnishes enough context to allow a determination of its meaning. After giving birth to 30 sons, the Queen of Zalpa asks [<i>k</i>]<i>ī=wa kuit walkuwan</i> <i>ḫāšḫun</i> ‘What is this <i>walkuwa-</i> that I have born?’ Since Otten’s (1972) edition of the text, <i>walkuwa-</i> has been interpreted as ‘bad omen, portentous thing’ and it is easy to see how this meaning could have developed from a substantivization of an adjective ‘dangerous, harmful’.[^79] To distinguish the ‘lion’ word from this formation, one could even surmise that the former represents an inner-Anatolian substantivization of the adjective <i>*u̯olkʷ-ó-</i> ‘monstrous’ (the type <i>*k̑ok-ó- ></i> Ved. <i>śāká</i>- ‘mighty’), while the latter continues an inherited corresponding abstract <i>*u̯ólkʷ-o-</i> m. ‘monstrosity’ (: <i>*k̑ók-o- ></i> Ved. <i>śā́ka</i>- m. ‘might’).[^80]

In this light, the (probably) taboo-motivated use of a substantivized adjective meaning ‘dangerous, harmful’ in both PIE and Proto-Anatolian to refer to a large predator (the ‘wolf’ here and the ‘lion’ there) seems entirely plausible.[^81] In fact, the respective analyses of the two terms seem to substantiate each other’s plausibility reciprocally: if an adjective derived from <i>*√u̯elkʷ</i> ‘harmful, dangerous’ was able to serve as the term for a predator in PIE, it seems very likely that the avatar of another adjective of the same root could serve as the term for a different predator in Proto-Anatolian. There is thus no need and, indeed, no reason to assume that the Anatolian word for ‘lion’ represents an example of semantic change, be it from ‘lion’ to ‘wolf’ in Proto-Indo-Tocharian, or from PIE ‘wolf’ to Proto-Anatolian ‘lion’.[^82]

The only question that this raises, however, is whether it is a plausible scenario to assume that Proto-Anatolian had both an <i>*ulkʷa-</i>, <i>*ulgʷa-</i> ‘wolf’ (inherited from PIE <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> but subsequently apparently lost) and a <i>*walkʷa-</i>, <i>*walgʷa-</i> ‘lion’ (newly created within Proto-Anatolian). The latter presupposes the existence of an inherited adjective <i>*u̯olkʷ-ó-</i>, which might have still been close enough to the continuant of <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> to allow the speakers an interpretation of the latter as ‘dangerous one’. It is questionable whether this association would have encouraged rather than prevented the creation of a superficially very similar formation PAnat. <i>*walkʷa-</i>, <i>*walgʷa-</i> ‘dangerous one’ > ‘lion’ for the purpose of naming a different animal. In other words, it might be worthwhile to ask ourselves whether Anatolian did in fact inherit a word <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> in the meaning ‘wolf’ from PIE in the first place. There are some clues at hand that indicate that it did not.

The word for ‘wolf’ in Hittite is usually written in sumerograms as UR.BAR.RA. As with the ‘horse’ discussed earlier, we sometimes find phonetic complements attached to it as in dat. sg. UR.BAR.RA<i>-ni</i>. These forms indicate that the stem of the word ended either in <i>-n-</i> or <i>-na-</i>, which has led to the identification of UR.BAR.RA<i>-n(a)-</i> with <i>ulip(a)n(a)-</i>, a word referring to some kind of predator in other texts. The attested forms of this word present a couple of difficulties on their own: [^83] the acc. sg. <i>ulipanan</i> (NH) is ambiguous in terms of stem class of the underlying word, as is the nom. pl. (or sg.?) <i>ú-li-ip-ni-eš</i>. The nom. sg. <i>ú-li-ip-za-aš(-ša-an)</i> (NH), on the other hand, looks like an error for *<i>ú-li-ip-pa</i>!<i>-aš</i> and could, then, represent an <i>n</i>-stem nom. sg. (cf. <i>ḫārāš</i> ‘eagle’ quasi <i>*h₃érōn</i>+s; acc. sg. <i>ḫāranan</i>). In this case, however, it would be remarkable that the form is spelled with a geminate <i>p</i> while the two other instances point to a lenis consonant. Rieken (2021) therefore prefers to read *<i>ú-li-ip-na</i>!<i>-aš</i>, i.e., a stem in <i>-na-</i> (<i>ulipna-</i>). The latter is also presupposed by the Cuneiform-Luwian cognate <i>ulipn(i)-/walipn(i)-</i> ‘wolf’.[^84] If the identification is correct, it follows that Anatolian either replaced the inherited PIE word <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘wolf’ by a new term, or that it did not inherit such a word at all. While the former scenario would constitute an obvious but inconspicuous case of lexical replacement, the latter would potentially entail the conjecture that PIE did not possess a <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> in the meaning ‘wolf’ at the time the Anatolian branch split off. Hittite <i>ulip(a)n(a)-</i> might, then, reflect the original PIE word for ‘wolf’, which was replaced in Proto-Indo-Tocharian by the taboo term <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘dangerous one’. Under this scenario, the lexical replacement of the word for ‘wolf’ would reflect the phylogenetic position of Anatolian on the language tree.[^85]

In purely theoretical terms, neither scenario would seem superior or more plausible than its alternative unless it could be shown that Hitt. <i>ulip(a)n(a)-</i> does in fact continue an older word for ‘wolf’. And indeed, the term looks suspiciously similar to another PIE zoonym <i>*u̯l̥p-</i> that is found in a number of animal names denoting different types of predators: compare Lat. <i>uolpēs/uulpēs</i>, gen. sg. <i>uolpis</i> f. ‘fox’, Av. <i>urupi-</i> m. ‘marten’, <i>raopi-</i> m. ‘fox’,[^86] Lith. <i>vilpišỹs</i> m. ‘wildcat’, and Middle Persian <i>gurbag</i> ‘cat<i>’ < *u̯l̥paka-</i>. Against this backdrop, Hitt. <i>ulip(a)n(a)-</i> could be interpreted as either <i>*u̯l̥p-ōn-</i> or <i>*u̯l̥p-no-</i>.[^87] This <i>n-</i> (or <i>*-no-</i>) stem, however, is somewhat difficult to reconcile with the variety of suffixes (<i>*-(e)i-, *-i-k̑-°, *-(o)-ko-</i>) that the other languages point to. There is no indication that the Anatolian <i>n-</i> (or <i>*-no-</i>) stem is in any way more pristine than the <i>i</i>-stem seen in Latin and Avestan, nor is it in fact likely that words for smaller predators such as ‘fox’, ‘marten’, ‘wildcat’ continue or were derived from an earlier word for ‘wolf’ that was supposedly placed under a taboo. Intriguing as this scenario might be, at present it cannot be substantiated.[^88] The mere fact, however, that Anatolian continues a different word for ‘wolf’ than the remaining languages, while it uses similar lexical material and a parallel naming motivation to designate the ‘lion’ that the other branches employ to refer to the ‘wolf’, might be counted as a noteworthy feature that sets Anatolian apart from the rest of the languages, though its diagnostic value is of course limited.

## 5. Conclusion

Zoonyms constitute a very stable and certainly quite important part of the core lexicon and can therefore play a significant role in ascertaining archaisms and innovations on several levels. The word for ‘bear’ <i>*h₂ŕ̥tk̑o-</i>, for instance, is one of the most widely attested words containing a tautosyllabic *-<i>tk̑</i>- sequence, which is preserved in Anatolian (Hitt. <i>ḫartakka-</i>) and Tocharian (where the word for ‘bear’ is not preserved) but was metathesized to a so-called thorn cluster in the remaining branches (<i>*h₂ŕ̥tk̑o-</i> > <i>*h₂ŕ̥k̑þo-</i>). As such, it can be regarded as an important piece in the discussion of phonological changes that purportedly divide Anatolian (and Tocharian) from the rest of the family.

The word for ‘horse’ has sometimes been claimed to represent an example of morphological change. Hitt. <i>*ekku-</i> and Luw. <i>azzu-</i> are thought by some to continue a PIE <i>u</i>-stem <i>*h₁ék̑u</i>-, which was subsequently thematized in Proto-Indo-Tocharian to give <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>- (Ved. <i>áśva</i>-, Lat. <i>equus</i>, etc.). This postulation, however, runs into a plethora of difficulties as I have tried to show in Section 3. The converse development seems therefore preferable: Proto-Anatolian inherited a thematic <i>*h₁ék̑u̯o</i>- (perhaps preserved in Lyc. <i>esb(e/i)-</i>), which was reanalysed as a <i>u</i>-stem in Hittite and Luwian, possibly through a combination of inflectional overlap of stems in <i>*-u-</i> and <i>*-wa-</i> in all cases except for the nom. and acc. sg., and a general tendency towards syncope of <i>*-(u)wa-</i> sequences to <i>*-u-</i> in these two languages. Even though changes in morphology are generally the best indicator for phylogenetic subgrouping, the word for ‘horse’ does not lend itself to such a purpose.

Lastly, the word for ‘wolf’ was scrutinized as a possible example for both semantic and lexical change. The supposition that the PIE word <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> originally meant ‘lion’ and is continued in this meaning in Hitt. <i>walkuwa/i-</i>, Luw. <i>walwa/i-</i>, Lyd. <i>walwe-</i>, while Proto-Indo-Tocharian underwent a semantic shift from ‘lion’ to ‘wolf’, could not be substantiated, nor could, in fact, the converse scenario, i.e., that PIE <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘wolf’ shifted to ‘lion’ only in Anatolian. The formal interpretation of <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> as a substantivization of the adjective <i>*u̯l̥kʷó-</i> (OIr. <i>olc</i> ‘evil, bad’, Ved. <i>a-vr̥ká-</i> ‘safe’) paved the way for analyzing PAnat. <i>*walkʷa-</i>, <i>*walgʷa-</i> ‘lion’ as a similar albeit not identical formation, namely the substantivization of an adjective <i>*u̯olkʷo-</i> based on the same root <i>*√u̯elkʷ</i> ‘harmful’ (or ‘rapacious’; see note 82). Since Anatolian exhibits a different word for ‘wolf’, however, viz. Hitt. UR.BAR.RA<i>-n(a)-</i> (= Hitt. <i>ulip(a)n(a)-</i>, Luw. <i>walipna/i-/ulipna/i-</i>?), which is reminiscent of certain words for ‘fox’ (Lat. <i>uolpēs</i>, Av. <i>raopi-</i>) and ‘(wild)cat’ (Lith. <i>vilpišỹs</i>, Middle Persian <i>gurbag</i>) in the other branches, one last speculation was entertained according to which Anatolian would preserve an older word for ‘wolf’ (<i>*u̯l̥p</i>-), which was replaced by the taboo formation <i>*u̯ĺ̥kʷo-</i> ‘dangerous one’ after the split-off of the Anatolian branch in Proto-Indo-Tocharian. However, this scenario was deemed inconclusive, as it cannot be demonstrated beyond doubt that PAnat. <i>ulip(a)n(a)-</i> really continues an archaic PIE word for ‘wolf’.[^89]
