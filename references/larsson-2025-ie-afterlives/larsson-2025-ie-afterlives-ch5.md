# 5. Indo-European motifs in the Greek underworlds

Jan N. Bremmer

University of Groningen

## Abstract

In this chapter, I examine the Indo-European legacy of the underworld in ancient Greece. The Indo-Europeans did not know of a land beneath the earth nor did they have a standardised concept of the underworld. Nevertheless, several Greek underworld motifs did originate from the Indo-European heritage. These include the idea of a journey to the hereafter, a river marking the border between this world and the next, a guardian dog, a kind of palace with strong gates and a beautiful meadow. However, Orphic poets sometimes turned ancient motifs into their opposite.

## 1. Introduction

Can we say anything about Indo-European motifs in Greek conceptions of the underworld? The question may not look very promising, as we do not have many studies that would help us to find our way. Admittedly, there are plenty of contributions on the Greek underworld, on Homer in particular. The latter’s descriptions, especially in the Odyssey, have proved to be an inexhaustible source for contributions on its real and supposed contradictions. From Erwin Rohde’s (1845–1898) famous Psyche (1894) to the greatest Hellenists of my time, Martin West (1937–2015) and Walter Burkert (1931–2015), scholars have tried to find their way in the jungle of opinions, both of Homer and of modern scholars. However, there has been little interest in possible Indo-European roots. The “Oriental turn”, as initiated by Burkert (1992) and continued by West (1997), directed the interest of students of the afterlife to the East Face of Helikon, to quote the title

<!-- page: 62 -->

of a famous book by West, that is, to the world of Gilgamesh and other Akkadian poems and epics, such as the Atrahasis, instead of to possible roots of Greek concepts.[^1] Admittedly, Martin West (2007) turned to Indo-European myth and poetry in a later book, but this study has had remarkably little influence in Greek studies. The most recent studies of the Homeric ideas about the afterlife do not even pay attention to a possible Indo-European heritage (Matijević 2015; Gazis 2018; Lye 2024; Sekita 2024). Yet, given West’s unique position, I will use his book as a kind of guide but also as a collection of studies in which the Greek evidence, rather surprisingly, does not always play the important role we would have expected from this eminent Hellenist, even though he has of course noted the most obvious passages.

## 2. Casting lots

Let us start with the fact that this interest in Mesopotamian literature has turned up a motif regarding the underworld that is not mentioned in earlier studies of the Greek underworld. As Burkert notes, the beginning of Atrahasis, an Akkadian epic first published only in 1969, relates that the three main gods of the pantheon ‘grasped the flask of lots by the neck, they cast the lots; the gods made the division: Anu went up to heaven, Enlil took the earth, for his subjects, and the bolts, the bars of the sea were set for Enki, the far-sighted’. This passage, as Burkert notes, has a surprising resemblance to words of Poseidon in the Iliad: “For when we threw the lots I received the grey sea as my permanent abode, Hades drew the murky darkness, Zeus, however, drew the wide sky of brightness and clouds; the earth is common to all and spacious Olympus.”[^2] Burkert points out that the combination heaven-sea- underworld is unique to this passage and the triad of gods of this passage is not worshipped anywhere in the Greek world. And indeed, when we look at the Indo-European evidence, we frequently find the pair ‘Sky (Heaven) and Earth’ (West 2007: 181–183; Olsen 2021; García Ramón 2023), but the underworld and the sea are not part of it. In fact, there is no Indo-European evidence of a land below the earth as we find it conceptualised and imagined in Greek religion and mythology. In other words, the Homeric poet had adopted, probably indirectly,

<!-- page: 63 -->

the beginning of Atrahasis because it suited his own ideas of the world of the dead, which may well have been influenced by Mesopotamian literature, such as the Gilgamesh epic.

On the other hand, we know that dividing property among brothers by lots was not unusual in Greek mythology. Odysseus mentions the custom in one of his fictitious tales (Od. 14.208–14.209),[^3] and we find it also in the myth of the Heraclids, the myth that legitimated the territorial claim of the Spartans in the Peloponnesus. Herodotus (6.52) apparently suggests that the story was a part of epic poetry, although he clearly does not know of any particular poet. In any case, tradition seems to have told how the Heraclids conquered the Peloponnese and then divided the area between them by casting lots, which Euripides recounts in different ways in his tragedies.[^4] In the Temenidai, Oxylus divides the Peloponnese into three parts, with the eldest son, Temenos, receiving the best part (the Argolid), Kresphontes receiving Messenia, and the sons of Aristodemus receiving Sparta. In the Temenos, however, Kresphontes gets Messenia by lot and the sons of Aristodemus get Sparta. The procedure cannot have been unusual, as we also hear of casting of lots over Argos by the three sons of Phoroneus (Hellanikos FGrH 4 F 36 = 36 Fowler: Demont 2000: 309–315). In fact, this division by lots would have a long life and could still be found in modern Greece until recent times.[^5] Burkert does not mention the Greek parallels, but they are clearly old and might help to explain why the Homeric poet was attracted to this theme of the Atrahasis, as the custom of throwing lots must have been familiar to him.

## 3. Sceptics and euphemisms

Given, then, that the passage fitted into the Greek way of thinking and living, it also directs our attention to the fact that a world of the dead as a separate world below that of the living does not seem to have been an Indo-European concept. That does not mean that the Indo-Europeans did not have ideas about life after death. However, we have to consider the possibility that ancient motifs survived in different contexts. That becomes more likely when we realise that we have a variety of sources regarding early Greek beliefs, much more than for

<!-- page: 64 -->

any other Indo-European tradition. As I have noted in a contribution on Indo-European initiation (Bremmer 2024), the Greek evidence is often overlooked by people starting from Indo-Iranian traditions. Yet we can also immediately say that the more sources we have, the more we can see that there was no canonical thought about life after death. How could there have been? Among the Indo-Europeans, there was no professional priesthood or a holy book to tell people what to believe. All there might have been were travelling poets – bards, if you like – who might well have put forward different ideas.

We cannot even know whether there were sceptics of the kind we hear of in fifth-century Athens, where the author of the Derveni Papyrus complains that people do not fear the terrors of Hades (§14 Kotwick). He might not have been an outlier in his opinion, as a character in Euripides’ Meleagros (F 532 Kannicht) states that ‘after death every man is earth and shadow: nothing goes to nothing’. We also find a reflection of this scepticism in Plato’s Republic (330–331), where Kephalos says to Socrates:

> [W]hen a man begins to realise that he is going to die, he is filled with anxiety and concern about matters that did not occur to him before. The tales that are told of the world below and how the men who have done wrong here must pay the penalty there, though he may have laughed them off before, then begin to torment his soul with the doubt that there may be some truth in them.

In short, by all accounts we can only be sceptical about our full knowledge of the ideas about the fate of mortals after death.

Our knowledge of Greek ideas is also hindered by the average Greek’s preference for using euphemisms rather than explicit descriptions of what awaited them in the afterlife. Thus, Persephone was referred to as the ‘daughter of Demeter’ rather than by her own name;[^6] indeed, Plato (Cratylus 404CD) notes that ‘many people’ are in awe of the name Pherephatta.[^7] We even find such a euphemism in the Odyssey, where Heracles tells Odysseus about the Athenian king Eurystheus: “and once he sent me here to fetch the dog, since he could not think of any more difficult labour for me. I took him and led him out of Hades, and Hermes and the owl-eyed Athena accompanied me” (Odyssey.

<!-- page: 65 -->

11.621–11.626; see also Iliad VIII.362–369).[^8] What strikes us here is that the dog is nameless, whereas from Hesiod onwards it was almost unanimously agreed that it was Cerberus, the watchdog of the underworld (see below).[^9] The example shows that there is no fixed rule but that we must be aware of the fact that the underworld was not always spoken of in clear terms.

## 4. Indo-European motifs

Let us now turn to motifs that might be of Indo-European origin. We will start with the fact that for the Indo-Europeans death and sleep were closely connected. As West notes, “‘[s]leep’, ‘the eternal sleep’, ‘the sleep of death’, etc., is widely used as a poetic metaphor or euphemism for death”. He presents examples from the Rig Veda where ‘put to sleep’ is four times used of putting to death. For Lucretius, he notes that the dead man is leto sopitus (3.904, cf. 920–921, 466, Catullus 5.6, etc.), although he omits Verg. Aen. 6.278 consanguineus Leti Sopor (imitated by Val. Flacc. Arg. 8.74), and for Anglo-Saxon deaðes swefn, ‘the sleep of death’ (Genesis B 720). For ancient Greece, West notes that in the Iliad a slain warrior κοιµήσατο χάλκεον ὕπνον, ‘fell into the bronze sleep’ (XI. 241), whereas another simply εὕδει, ‘sleeps’ (XIV.482). However, he fails to mention that in Greek mythology Sleep is the brother of Death.[^10]

West (2007: 388) also notes that the Indo-Europeans spoke of a journey to the underworld from which there would be no return. Unfortunately, he did not annotate this idea properly, but Circe says about Odysseus’ comrades: “Rash men, who have gone down alive to the house of Hades to meet death twice, when the other mortals die only once” (Od. 11.21–11.22: σχέτλιοι, οἳ ζώοντες ὑπήλθετε δῶµ᾽ Ἀίδαο, δισθανέες, ὅτε τ᾽ ἄλλοι ἅπαξ θνῄσκουσ᾽ ἄνθρωποι). In fifth-century Athens, according to tragedy, the souls of the dead were believed to have to travel some distance, as in the Ajax, nowadays to be dated to the 440s just like the

<!-- page: 66 -->

Antigone (Finglass 2011: 1–11), the protagonist says: “For I will go to that place where I must travel” (690) and Antigone, too, uses the word ‘travel’ (poreuô) with reference to her descent into the underworld (891–893).[^11]

When the dead travel, they arrive at the edge of the world of the living, which is marked by a river or even a number of rivers. It seems typical of Greece that we hear that it is impossible to cross the river if one has not been properly buried (Il. XXIII.71–74; see also Od. 11.66, 72–73). In other words, we have here an interesting combination of myth and ritual or, perhaps better, beliefs and ritual, which seems quite unique for the early period. In the passage, the famous dream visit of Achilles by Patroclus, the river is not mentioned by name. It may well be that we have here another example of the euphemistic trend I have just mentioned. Later authors, such as Porphyry (F 377 Smith), identified the river in Achilles’ dream as the Acheron, but that is not necessary, the less so as the infernal rivers are rarely mentioned by name in Homer (but see Od. 10.513–10.514), although the Acheron is already noted as an infernal river by Sappho and others.[^12] In fact, there is only one passage in which all four rivers of the underworld – Acheron, Pyriphlegethon, Cocytus and Styx – are mentioned (Od. 10.513–10.515). The most famous infernal river is probably the Styx (Bremmer 2025), which is also mentioned as the boundary between the world of the living and the underworld in a less commented- upon passage, in which Athena refers to Heracles’ descent into the underworld to fetch Cerberus (Il. VIII.366–369). What seems typical of the Greek infernal rivers is that they are described as being full of whirlpools, raging (Pind. fr. 129 Maehler) and making lots of noise – presumably to make them more terrifying. In other words, they reflect the state of the rivers in the winter, but not those in the summer, when most rivers would have been reduced to small streams of water (Suárez de la Torre 2019; Falbo 2022a, 2023).

Such a horrendous river cannot be crossed without help. It is therefore not surprising that many Indo-European traditions know of a ferryman (never a ferrywoman) of the dead, as do several other eschatological traditions, as in Sumer and Egypt (Matijević 2015: 52). West records several traditions, with special attention to the Slavic and Baltic ones. Our oldest Greek source mentioning the ferryman with the name Charon, the late archaic or early classical epic Minyas (fr. 1 Davies/

<!-- page: 67 -->

Bernabé), which is a lost Boeotian epic perhaps dating from the early fifth century (Debiasi 2010: 266–279), pictures him as an old man, an image still followed by Vergil in his Aeneid (6.299). However, not every Indo-European tradition knew of a ferryman. The Greek evidence is hardly early, and the Romans had no independent tradition either (Wagenvoort 1956: 102–131). Instead of a ferryman, the Iranians had the famous Cinvat Bridge, which already appears in the Gathas of the Avesta (Yasna 46.10, 46.11, 51.13) and is evidently very old (Tafazzoli 1992). From Iran, this bridge even found its way into the Christian Apocalypses, probably via Jewish sources.[^13] From there it may have also entered other, later Christian traditions. West notes that an old north English (= Yorkshire) song sung at wakes referred to “the bridge of dread, no brader than a thread”, but he gives no source, probably because his own source, the Deutsche Mythologie of the Brothers Grimm, was too abbreviated at this point.[^14] Other traditions are certainly much later and in the Slavic and Baltic countries already influenced by Christianity. Bruce Lincoln has argued that the old age of Charon is a typical feature of Indo-European origin. and speculates that the old age of the ferryman personifies the fact that life ebbs away in old age.[^15] However, there seems to be no evidence for this. It is rather that youth would be out of place in an area characterised by gloom, misery and darkness. In any case, the Indo-Europeans clearly did not have a uniform tradition about the crossing. People had to get to the other side, so to speak, but they must have imagined various ways of doing so, perhaps depending on the local ecology.

When people got to the other side, what did they see? I would like to draw our attention to three important ancient features. First, the entrance to the underworld was guarded. Apparently, people imagined it as a kind of house or palace which, presumably, in real life was also guarded by an animal. In Homer, as we have seen, this animal was a dog, who still remains anonymous (Il. VIII.368, Od. 11.623), but in later literature, as said, was called Cerberus. It is perhaps somewhat difficult to imagine him as terrifying in our times of poodles and golden retrievers, but Servius’ (pseudo-)etymology of Cerberus as

<!-- page: 68 -->

kreo-boros, ‘meat devouring’ (in Aen. 6.395), suggests that we should not take him too lightly. The mention of dogs on the battlefield in the Iliad right at the beginning of the poem, where it is said that the anger of Achilles drove many steadfast lives down to Hades and made their bodies prizes for dogs and for all birds, should make us realise that the early Greeks did not think of dogs as just nice pets. They are more like the dogs in the Edda, which are scavengers of warriors’ corpses. Nevertheless, according to Hesiod (Th. 769–773), Cerberus welcomes new arrivals in a very friendly manner but devours those who try to escape from the underworld.

In the Avesta (Vd. 13.9,19.30), we hear of two dogs guarding the Cinvat Bridge, and in the Vedas Yama’s two dogs guard the way to the other world and seize the dead.[^16] It seems therefore that the Indo-Europeans originally thought of two dogs at the entrance to the other world. Greece – but also Nordic mythology, where Odin meets a dog standing on the road to Hel (Baldrs draumar 2.7–3.4) – seems to have reduced this duo to only one dog. Interestingly, however, Greek tradition was clearly not satisfied with a single dog but gave Cerberus two, three or even more heads (Woodford and Spier 1992). In other words, even the single dog counted for at least two. In this connection, we must also mention the Pythagorean expression ‘dogs of Persephone’ (Aristotle, fr. 159 Gigon), which may well have served as a kind of Cerberus (Riedweg 2021: 64). I therefore assume that the Indo-European tradition originally had two dogs at the entrance, a number that was varied by later traditions.

Once the dogs had been safely passed, it became possible to enter the ‘house of Hades’ by passing through its gates. In the Iliad, the latter action is twice equivalent to ‘to die’ (V.646, XXIII.71; Theognis 1.427). It is no pleasure to enter the ‘gates of Hades’, as they are clearly seen as something awful (Il. IX.312, XIV.156; Plato, Hippias Minor 363a4). Given that most people die, it is not surprising that the gates are called ‘wide’ (εὐρυπυλές: Il. XXIII.74): they must receive many people, just as Hades himself is called Polydektes or Polydegmon (‘Receiver of Many’: Hymn. Hom. Cer. 9, 17) and Polyxeinos (‘Host to Many’: Aeschylus, fr. 228 Radt; Callimachus, fr. 285 Pfeiffer). The image of gates is old, as an Indian funeral text mentions ‘the fearful path (death as a journey!) to Yama’s gates’; in Nordic mythology Hel’s walls are extremely

<!-- page: 69 -->

high and her gates strong (Gylfaginning 34), and Baltic mythology also knew of gates as entrances to the other world, but alternated gates with doors.[^17] Latin mythology, on the other hand, basically only knows of doors and its occasional mention of gates is probably influenced by Greek literature (Horsfall on Verg. Aen. 6.106).

Vergil also uses the picture for his image of the underworld in the Aeneid: “facing him [Aeneas] there was a great gate, and columns of massive adamant, such that no human force, not even the gods of heaven have the strength to tear them down” (6.552–6.554, transl. Horsfall). It is somewhat puzzling that these gates are now located in Tartarus, the deepest part of the underworld, and are said to be made of adamant, the legendary, hardest metal of antiquity, which is rarely mentioned in early Greek literature (Hesiod, Theog. 161, Op. 147), but is not unusual in Latin poetry and early Christian writings.[^18] We must probably think of a Hellenistic model, as the term is used by Theocritus (2.33–2.34: τὸν ἐν ῞Αιδα κινήσαις ἀδάµαντα), the Sicilian poet who worked in Alexandria and who may well have derived it from a lost epic model. In any case, Homer in his Iliad also refers to the ‘iron gates and bronze threshold’ (VIII.15) of Tartarus, which lies far below Hades.

The gates lead to the ‘House of Hades’, clearly a formulaic expression that is mentioned several times. Although it appears in various forms in the Iliad and Odyssey, singular and plural,[^19] it must be old, since it has Hittite, Indian and Irish parallels.[^20] It is interesting to note that in our earliest Orphic Gold Leaf, that of Hipponion (c. 400 bce), the underworld is also called the Ἀίδαο δόµους εὐήρεας (Orphicorum Fragmenta 474, ed. Bernabé = 1.2, ed. Graf/Johnston). There is some uncertainty about the translation of this expression, which Graf and Johnston render as ‘the well-built house of Hades’, Bernabé and Jiménez as ‘the well-built abode of Hades’, Edmonds as ‘the well-built halls of Hades’ and Riedweg as ‘the palace of Hades’. However, the expression ‘House of Hades’ is clearly used here metaphorically for the underworld,

<!-- page: 70 -->

so that Edmonds’s ‘halls of Hades’ may be the most appropriate. In any case, the adjective εὐήρεας, which in Homer is normally used for an oar, is clearly a later misreading of an original εὑρήσεις, as Richard Janko (2016: 110–111) has shown. It remains interesting that the Orphic poet of these lines used such a traditional expression, which was losing its popularity after the fifth century and tragedy – perhaps to add further solemnity to his instructions; or he may have taken it from one of the poems about Orpheus’ katabasis, where it would have been quite appropriate, given that Orpheus actually visited Hades and Persephone.

We have finally arrived in the underworld, but it seems that the dead did not have to stay in some kind of prison. Different traditions have a much nicer idea about life after death. It was, I think, Paul Thieme (1905–2001) who was the first to collect material on the existence of a meadow in the afterlife (Thieme 1952: 47–50). Naturally, as an Indologist, he adduced passages from the Rig Veda and the Avesta that indicated the existence of a pasture for cattle in the other world. However, his starting point was two verses from the Odyssey, which mention a ‘meadow of asphodel’ (11.539, 573), although he should also have noticed ‘the asphodel meadow, where the souls live, the shades of the dead’ (24.13). Subsequently, Hittitologists have also found evidence of an underworld meadow and have noted that ‘to go to the meadow’ was a Hittite expression for ‘dying’. West also mentions a connection between cows and the dead in a Latvian daina.[^21]

Although the connection between the meadow and the underworld is clear, its role in Greek mythology and beliefs deserves more attention than Thieme and West have given it. To start with, what does this asphodel mean? Later authors, beginning with the Homeric Hymn to Hermes, interpret the asphodel meadow as a beautiful and fragrant meadow full of flowers and the asphodel as a nutritious and medicinal plant with healing properties. Both ancient and modern scholars have struggled with the appropriateness of the plant for a meadow, though, and the exact meaning of the expression still seems a problem.[^22] However, there can be no doubt of the fact that a meadow was an important element of the underworld, which remained important

<!-- page: 71 -->

well until the end of the fifth century, with particular prominence in Eleusinian and Orphic Mysteries, and as such was also appropriated by Plato in his own images of the underworld.[^23]

A thrênos by Pindar mentions ‘the meadows with red roses’ (fr. 129 Maehler) and the chorus in Aristophanes’ Frogs sings of the ‘rose-rich, flowery meadows’ (448–449), but it is especially in typically Orphic literature that we find references to a meadow in the underworld.[^24] Although most of the references are relatively late, going from Plutarch to Proclus, a fourth-century Gold Leaf from Thurii (Orphicorum Fragmenta 487, ed. Bernabé = 3, ed. Graf/Johnston) mentions the ‘holy meadows and groves of Persephone’ and a contemporary one from Thessalian Pherae exhorts the initiate: ‘enter the holy meadow’ (Orphicorum Fragmenta 493, ed. Bernabé = 27, ed. Graf/Johnston). Fritz Graf (1974: 79–94) has analysed in detail all the passages, except the last one, which was discovered after the publication of his book, and argues persuasively that these mentions of underworld meadows originally derive from Orphic literature and were taken over in simplified form from there into the Eleusinian Mysteries. Graf does not exclude the influence of Pythagorean traditions, but in the – admittedly small – corpus of Pythagorean passages about the afterlife meadows do not play any role at all.[^25] As with the ‘House of Hades’, then, we can see that the Orphic poets preserved parts of the traditional Indo-European otherworld imagery in their specific poetry. Unfortunately, this poetry has been irretrievably lost, except for some fragments, and we can only guess the extent to which it also used the other motifs we have discussed.

In this respect, there is an interesting transformation to be noticed in the Orphic poets. There can be no doubt that the Greek underworld, as in other Indo-European traditions, was originally imagined as an area of obscurity and darkness.[^26] In this context, it is appropriate to note a new interpretation of the obscure expression ἀάατον Στυγὸς ὕδωρ (Il. XIV.271), which the linguist Alexander Nikolaev (2015; see also Oettinger 2020) has explained as ‘the sunless water of the Styx’. In other words, the sun does not shine in the underworld, as indeed

<!-- page: 72 -->

is emphasised in several Greek passages (Aeschylus, Septem 856–860; Euripides, Alcestis 436–437) but which is also clear from the Greek and Indo-European expression ‘to leave the light of the sun’ for ‘to die’ (Nikolaev 2015: 219–220; Oettinger 2020: 164–166). However, this is not the case in the Orphic tradition. It is precisely here that in the underworld a presence of the sun is emphasised, as we can see in the Orphically coloured passages of Pindar (fr. 129 Maehler) and Aristophanes (Frogs 454–455).[^27] Evidently, the Orphic poets not only adopted ancient motifs, but they were also able to turn them into their opposite.

## 5. Conclusion

The evidence discussed seems sufficient to confirm West’s inclusion of ancient Greece within the Indo-European evidence. However, we cannot help noticing that he does not ask how we should imagine the chain of tradition and its possible transformations. When we look at our oldest evidence, for example, about the meadow, it is clear that the Indo-Iranian traditions imagined a beautiful meadow for their cows. But there is nothing of this visible in the Greek evidence. Admittedly, Hades receives the epithet ‘famous for his colts’, but there is no mention of meadows in this respect. Evidently, the link between meadow and cows in Greek epic poetry was dropped over time, but the existence of the meadow itself remained attractive enough for dry and hot Greece to survive this change. Subsequently, it was reinterpreted within the context of a pleasant hereafter in the Orphic tradition. The Orphic images, like the Jenseitsvorstellungen of Homer, were transmitted via poetry, be it epics or laments. So we have to imagine a continuous poetic tradition that adapted traditional Indo-European material to the changing ecological conditions in Greece, but it also seems to have transferred the hereafter to a world beneath the earth – perhaps under Mesopotamian influence, as already suggested. Unfortunately, the Mycenaean evidence has not given us any information about poets, so in the end we remain about this process in a darkness like that of the underworld![^28]
