# 7. The love life of the dead

Norse Valkyries from an Indo-European perspective

Riccardo Ginevra

Università Cattolica del Sacro Cuore, Milano

## Abstract

Old Norse Val-kyrja is a reflex of Proto-Germanic *wala-kuzjōn-, which is easily interpreted as ‘slain-chooser, she who chooses the slain’. In view of the likely origin of Valkyries as female death-demons and in view of the compound’s great antiquity, however, it is also possible that *wala-kuzjōn- originally meant ‘slain-taster’, displaying the older semantics (still attested in Gothic and Old English) of the Proto-Indo-European root *ǵeu̯s- ‘taste’ that underlies Proto-Germanic *keus-. The development from corpse-tasting she-demons to splendid warrior- women who love mortal heroes may be linked to the technical formula of “own-choice” marriages in Germanic (‘to choose a man as husband’, as attested in Old Norse and Old English). The Valkyries and their “chosen” Einherjar may thus be Norse counterparts to several Indo-European “Heavenly Maidens” who are said to “choose as husbands for themselves” characters who were originally not gods and were only accepted among them later (e.g. Sūryā in the Indic tradition and Helen in the Greek one).

## 1. Introduction: afterlives of the “Hel type” vs. afterlives of the “Valhǫll type” in Indo-European traditions[^1]

Mythological texts composed in ancient Indo-European (IE) languages mention two main destinies that await human beings after death, both

<!-- page: 96 -->

being well attested in Norse mythology: we may correspondingly refer to these two afterlife models as the “Hel type” and the “Valhǫll type”.

On the one hand, together with other undesirable states such as illness and danger,[^2] death could be thought of as the opposite of functional (healthy and safe) life.[^3] Since life was conceptualized as involving several activities that are habitually more or less co-present in the life of a human being (at least within an idealized conceptualization), the dead (and characters who were in other non-functioning states) were imagined as unable to do such things (Ginevra 2020a: 117–122 and passim), for example being unable to properly stand (as in (1) in Vedic Sanskrit and (2) in Homeric Greek), to see (as in (1)–(3), the latter in Homeric Greek as well), to speak (as in (2) in Greek and (4) in Middle Welsh), and/or to eat properly (as in (5) in Hittite and (6) in Old Norse). This negative afterlife model is the one evoked by Old Norse (ON) Hel (6), the name of an undesirable underworld and of its ruling goddess in Norse mythology.[^4] Within this model, the dead were also generally imagined to be unable to have social relations

(as a further essential aspect of life); for example, mothers would not recognize their children (as in (2) in Greek and (7) in Hittite).

2. “I see here the ghost of my dead mother; she sits in silence near the blood and cannot bring herself to look upon the face of her own son or to speak to him. Tell me, my lord, how may she recognize that I am he?” (Odyssey 11.141–11.144).[^6]

3. “and declares that not for long shall I look on the bright light of the sun [i.e. that I will die soon]” (Iliad 5.119–5.120).[^7]

4. “Then they threw the corpses into the cauldron […] and they [i.e. the corpses] would get up the next morning fighting as well as before except that they could not talk” (Branwen ferch Llŷr 376–378).[^8]

On the other hand, death could also be conceived as a journey towards a most desirable afterlife, in which several aspects of functional (healthy and safe) life – such as eating and drinking – were recreated, or even improved upon. As is well known, this positive afterlife model is the one evoked by ON Val-hǫll ‘Hall of the Slain’, name of a

heavenly mansion (as in (8) and (9)) where those who die as warriors in battle go to become so-called Einherjar, Odin’s soldiers. The same conception underlies not only the famous ancient Greek “paradises” called Ēlúsion ‘Elysium’ (as in (10)) or Makárōn Nē̂soi ‘Isles of the Blessed’ (as in (11)) or Leukḗ ‘White (Island)’ (as in (12)), mythical places mentioned in the sources as the locations where ancient heroes such as Menelaus and Achilles (and a few others)[^12] went after death, but also the Old Indic Indra-loka- ‘World of Indra’, where the souls of righteous kings who die in battle go according to Sanskrit epic mythology (13). Within this “life-like” model of the afterlife, the dead were apparently assumed to have social relations, both with each other (the Einherjar enjoy each other’s company in (9), either Menelaus or Achilles shall be husbands of Helen in (10) and (12)), and with the gods (the Einherjar are adoptive sons of Odin in (8), Menelaus shall keep being treated as Zeus’ son-in-law in (10)).

The aim of this contribution is to argue that the latter “Valhǫll model” for the afterlife, according to which the dead could be provided with a

## 2. Norse Valkyries and their possible Mediterranean background

The Norse “desirable afterlife” Valhǫll is inextricably linked to a well-known group of figures in Scandinavian mythology: the Valkyries, warrior-women who have inspired a number of renowned artworks (e.g. Richard Wagner’s Die Walküre) and academic studies in modern times. These divine warrior-women and psychopomps were not only thought to choose which fallen warriors were to spend the afterlife with Odin until the End of Time (the so-called Ragnarǫk) but also imagined as entertainers and waitresses for the dead Einherjar (as in (14)).

14. “There are still others, whose function it is to wait in Val-hall, serve drink and look after the tableware and drinking vessels. […] These are called valkyries. Odin sends them to every battle. They allot death to men and govern victory. Gunn and Rota and the youngest norn,[^19] called Skuld, always ride to choose who shall be slain and to govern the killings” (Gylfaginning 36).[^20]

The Norse being called val-kyrja and her counterpart within Anglo-Saxon culture, called wæl-cyrġe (also wæl-cyriġe, wæl-cyrie), however, apparently had a darker side as well: in some sources they are clearly conceived as demonic beings with a connection to harm and death, being associated with witches in both Old Norse (scass in (15)) and Old English (OE) sources (wiccean in (16), tessa in (17)) and identified with terrible deities of classical myth (the Erinyes or Furies Tisiphon and Allecto; the war-goddess Bellona) in OE glosses (17); the OE term

or, more precisely, its derivatives are used to refer to Gorgons in (18) and (19).

15. “You were a harmful creature, a witch [scass], a valkyrie, horrible, unnatural, with All-Father; all the Einheriar had to fight, headstrong woman, on your account” (Helgakviða Hundingsbana I 38).[^21]

16. “[we must with all our strength and all our might] shun all evil-doing, namely [the deeds of] slayers of their kin and murderers, and perjurers and witches and sorceresses [wiccean & wælcyrian], and adulterers, and incestuous deeds” (LawCn 1020 16 (15))[^22]

19. þa deor habbad eahta fet ond welcyrian eagan “The animals have eight feet and Valkyries’ eyes” (Marv 34, 6; OE translation of a Latin original hae bestie oculos habent Gorgoneos).

As argued by Matthias Egeler (2011), these demonic features of the Valkyries find close parallels in various further European traditions, which attest female characters, often appearing as a group, who share close associations with death, war, birds (of carrion), and who display both erotic and lethal influences on men: the Old Irish Bodbs and Morrígains; the Greek Sirens, Harpies, Erinyes and Keres; the Roman Furies; and the Etruscan Vanth. On the basis of these striking correspondences, Egeler proposes that both the Norse Valkyries and the Irish Bodbs and Morrígains may reflect the diffusion into the early Germanic and Celtic cultural environments of the same (erotically characterized, lethal, bird-like) female death-demon(s) motif attested in the Mediterranean area, a diffusion that may have taken place within the context of the historically well-attested contacts

<!-- page: 102 -->

in antiquity between Graeco-Roman Southern Europe and Celto-Germanic Continental and Northern Europe. As explained in the next section, Egeler’s reconstruction may find some support in one of two possible etymologies of the ON term valkyrja.

## 3. Old Norse val-kyrja ‘slain-chooser’ or ‘slain-taster’?

ON val-kyrja and OE wæl-cyrġe are currently analyzed as reflexes of a Proto-Germanic (PGmc) verbal governing compound *wala-kuzjōn- (de Vries 1962: 641; Kroonen 2013: 569), with:

• a first element ON val° and OE wæl° reflecting the PGmc noun *wala- ‘the slain on the battlefield, battlefield’ attested by ON m. val-r ‘id.’, OE n. wæl ‘id.’, OHG n. wal ‘battlefield’;

• a second element ON °kyr-ja and OE °cyr-ġe reflecting a PGmc agentive feminine noun *°kuz-jōn- ‘she who chooses’, which may in turn be traced back to the PGmc verb *keus-a- (preterite 3pl *kuz-un) attested e.g. by ON kjós-a ‘to choose’ (preterite 3pl kur-u) and OE ċēos-an ‘id.’ (preterite 3pl cur-on).

The PGmc verbal governing compound *wala-kuzjōn- belongs to the same type with zero grade of the verbal root (m. *CC-jan-, f. *CC-jōn-; Casaretto 2004: 249) as Gothic arbi-numja ‘heir, who takes the inheritance’ (*°num-jan- ‘who takes’, from *nem-a- ‘to take’) and ON erfi-nyti ‘heir, who enjoys the inheritance’ (*°nut-jan- ‘who enjoys’ from *neut-a- ‘to enjoy’). This is a productive type in Germanic (Meid 1967: 26) and most likely a very ancient one as well, which may have originated from the recharacterization of inherited Proto-Indo-European root compounds (Schindler 1972; Benedetti 1988; Scarlata 1999) by means of the suffixes m./f. *-a/ōn- and *-ja/ōn-.

The meaning ‘to choose’ of ON kjósa and OE ċēosan suggests a synchronic interpretation of the historical reflexes of PGmc *wala-kuzjōn-, ON val-kyrja and OE wæl-cyrġe, as ‘slain-chooser, she who chooses the slain on the battlefield’. This is strongly supported, at least for ON valkyrja, by the ON phrase kjósa val ‘to choose the slain’, which occurs with the Valkyries as subject in (14) above, partially repeated below as (20). The same phrase is also attested with the Einherjar as grammatical subject in (9) above, partially repeated below as (21), as well as with the love-goddess Freyja as subject in (22), matching the fact that in the OE version of Aldhelm’s De laude virginitatis 47

<!-- page: 103 -->

only the name of the Roman love-goddess Venus is translated with OE wælcyrġe, whereas the generic gydene ‘goddess’ is used not only for this goddess but also for other female deities such as Vesta, Proserpina, the Nymphs and the Furies.

20. “These are called valkyries. Odin sends them to every battle. They allot death to men and govern victory. Gunn and Rota and the youngest norn, called Skuld, always ride to choose who shall be slain [kjósa val] and to govern the killings” (Gylfaginning 36).[^23]

21. “All the Einheriar in Odin’s courts […] they choose the slain [val þeir kiósa] and ride from the battle; then they sit the more at peace together” (Vafþrúðnismál 41).[^24]

22. “Half the slain she [i.e. Freyja] chooses [hálfan val / hon kýss] every day, and half Odin owns” (Grímnismál 14).[^25]

Egeler’s proposal that the Norse Valkyries originated as reflexes of Mediterranean female demons of death and battle, however, together with the great antiquity of the PGmc compound *wala-kuzjōn- (as clear from the Grammatischer Wechsel attested by the Verner root variant *kuz- vs. *keus-) allow for the hypothesis that PGmc *wala-kuzjōn- originally had another meaning, namely ‘she who tastes the slain on the battlefield’ (as already suggested by de Vries 1962: 641):

• the analysis of the first element would not be any different, whereas

• the meaning of second element would reflect the older semantics of the Proto-Indo-European root *ǵeu̯s- ‘to taste’ (LIV²: 166–167) that also underlies PGmc *keus-, a meaning that is directly attested or indirectly reflected by its cognates in other IE languages, e.g. Hittite ku-kus-zi ‘taste’, Ved. joṣ-áya- ‘enjoy, take pleasure in’, Ancient Greek γεύ-ο-µαι ‘taste’.

While the meaning ‘to taste’ is not attested for the ON and OE verbs, it must have been inherited from Proto-Indo-European to

<!-- page: 104 -->

Proto-Germanic, as it still occurs in Gothic, where the verb kausjan (reflex of an iterative PGmc formation *kaus-ja-) is clearly used with the sense ‘to taste’ to translate Ancient Greek γεύοµαι in (23).

23. “For I say unto you, That none of those men which were bidden shall taste of my supper [kauseiþ þis nahtamatis meinis = γεύσεταί µου τοῦ δείπνου]” (Luke 14:24).[^26]

While Norse Valkyries are never described as corpse-eaters (Egeler 2011: 83), the reconstruction of an original meaning ‘slain-taster, she who tastes the slain’ for the PGmc compound *wala-kuzjōn- may find some comparative support in a very similar term for a carrion-eating bird with which Valkyries were closely associated: the raven, which in (24) is referred to by means of the OE compound wæl-cēasiġa. This term is almost identical to OE wæl-cyrġe and ON val-kyrja: it attests the same first element wæl°, as well as a second element, °cēas-iġa, that reflects a weak masculine agent noun *°kaus-jan- ‘taster, chooser’ (although we are most likely dealing here with a late remodeling of an older formation, as suggested by the absence of i-Umlaut in the root). The latter almost exactly matches the weak feminine *°kuz-jōn- underlying ON °kyr-ja and OE °cyr-ġe, the only difference between them being the grammatical gender (masculine *-jan- vs feminine *-jōn-) and the apophonic grade of the PGmc root (full-grade *kaus- in *°kaus-jan- vs. zero-grade *kuz- in *°kuz-jōn-). As a literal meaning of ‘slain-chooser’ would make no sense to refer to a raven, ‘slain-taster’ is the most likely semantic interpretation of this OE compound, which must thus be identified as a further fossil reflex of the inherited semantics ‘to taste’ of this PIE root in a Germanic language.

24. “The warbirds cried, eager for battle, dew-feathered ones over the bodies of the slain troops, the dark taster of the slain [wælceasega]” (Exodus 158).[^27]

## 4. From corpse-tasting demons to man-choosing maidens

If the original meaning of PGmc *wala-kuzjōn- was indeed ‘slain-taster’, however, a question arises: how did the Valkyries end up

<!-- page: 105 -->

becoming pleasant and fascinating ‘choosers of the slain’ as we know them from Norse mythology? This change required (as a terminus post quem) the semantic shift of PGmc *keus- from ‘taste’ to ‘choose’, as attested in Old Norse and Old English, where the meaning ‘to choose’ seems to have definitely replaced ‘to taste’ (still attested in Gothic, with a fossil reflex in Old English, as just seen). This late semantic shift allowed for the reinterpretation of *wala-kuzjōn- ‘slain-taster’ as ‘slain-chooser’, prompting an association of these female death- demons with two distinct but interconnected themes of IE heritage: (4.1) the social institution of “own-choice” marriage and (4.2) the corresponding mythological theme of the “own-choice” marriage of Heavenly Maidens.

### 4.1. Choosing a man as husband in Germanic and Indo-European

Once PGmc *keus-a- ‘to taste’ had acquired the meaning ‘to choose’, the (once-gruesome) semantics of the compound *wala-kuz-jōn- ‘(female) slain-taster’ could be reinterpreted as ‘(female) slain-chooser’. The latter, however, given that slain warriors were most likely men at the time, could also be understood as ‘she who chooses the slain men’, and thus linked to an inherited Germanic formula [woman – choose (*keusa-) – man as husband (*wira-) – (for herself)],[^28] which may be reconstructed on the basis of its (etymologically identical) reflexes in Old Norse and Old English, namely:

• the ON phrase [woman – choose (kjósa) – man as husband (verr) – (for herself)], as in (25)–(26);

• the OE phrase [woman – choose ([ġe]ċēosan) – man as husband (wer) – (for herself)], as in (27)–(29).

25. “Who would you choose as husband for yourself [þú þér at ver kjósa]?” (Kormákr Ǫgmundarson Lausavísur 19V, Kormáks saga 20).[^29]

26. “She [i.e. the goddess Skaði] was to choose herself a man [as husband] [hon skal kjósa sér mann] out of the Æsir and choose by the feet and see nothing else of them. Then she

<!-- page: 106 -->

saw one person’s feet that were exceptionally beautiful and said: ‘I choose that one [Þenna kýs ek]; there can be little that is ugly about Baldr.’ But it was Njǫrðr of Noatun” (Skáldskaparmál G56).[^30]

27. “Each [widow] shall stay 12 months without husband [werleas]; then she shall choose whom she wants [as husband]” [ceose syððan þæt heo sylf wille] (Aethelred’s Laws, 37 21.1 Liebermann).[^31]

28. “If she chooses a man [as husband] [wer geceose] within a year” (L. TH. 416,8).[^32]

29. “Either they came back or they would choose other men [as husbands] for themselves [hi him wolde oðerra wera ceosan]” (Old English Orosius 1, 10.29.7).[^33]

It may be worth noting here that OE oðerra wera ceosan ‘choose other men [as husbands]’ from the last (29), an OE translation of Paulus Orosius’ Historiae adversum paganos, has no counterpart in the Latin original, and was thus introduced by the translator, by whom it must have been perceived as idiomatic.[^34]

A similar situation is that of the following (30), a series of five occurrences of the phrase wer (ġe)ċēosan in the Old English translation of the Late Latin romance Apollonius of Tyre: within these passages, OE wer (ġe)ċēosan ‘to choose a man (as husband)’ is used twice to literally translate Latin marītum ēligere ‘id.’, but it is also used twice to simply translate Latin nūbere ‘to marry’; furthermore, in its last occurrence, OE wer (ġe)ċēosan actually has no counterpart in the Latin original, just like in (29), once more suggesting that the phrase must have been used by OE speakers as an idiomatic expression (although the possibility that the OE translation may be based on a slightly different version of the romance must be taken into account as well).

The ON and OE reflexes of the inherited Germanic collocation [woman – choose (*keusa-) – man as husband (*wira-) – (for herself)] thus seem to have been used as a technical formula to refer to a marriage in which the woman apparently had some say in the choice of male (as in the case of widows), a relatively peculiar type of marriage in most IE-speaking communities, whose social systems were highly patriarchal.

<!-- page: 108 -->

As noted by Dumézil (1973: 33) with regard to (26) – in which the goddess Skaði ‘chooses a husband for herself’ – this liberal procedure closely resembles the Indic tradition of the svayaṃ-vara ‘own-choice’ wedding, as attested e.g. in (31), where the princess Sukanya must svakaṃ patiṃ vr̥- ‘choose her own husband’ among three characters who look exactly the same.

31. “A little while later they all climbed out of the lake, all young and divinely beautiful, with shining earrings, wearing the same outward appearance. And increasing the love of her heart, they all said to her, ‘Beautiful young woman, choose [vṙṇīṣva] one of us for marriage [patitve], whomever you desire.’ When she saw them all stand there looking the same, the princess decided with heart and mind, and chose her very own husband [vavre svakaṃ patim]” (Mahābhārata 3.123.17–3.123.19).[^35]

The own-choice marriage formula [WOMAN – CHOOSE (*keusa-) – MAN as HUSBAND (*wira-) – (for HERSELF)] attested in ON and OE texts may thus be identified as a Germanic counterpart to the (already Vedic) Sanskrit svayaṃvara formula [WOMAN – CHOOSE (vr̥-) – MAN AS HUSBAND (páti-) – (for HERSELF)], whose traditional and ritual character has been demonstrated by Jamison (2001). Once the PGmc verb *keus-a- acquired the innovative semantics ‘to choose’, the feminine compound *wala-kuzjōn- may have easily been linked to this own-choice marriage formula and correspondingly reinterpreted as ‘she who chooses the slain (as husband for herself)’. This would explain why, in heroic poetry as well as in legendary texts, Valkyries are often said to be lovers of male heroes who are fated to die, as in the famous Valkyrie-hero couples Sigrún–Helgi, Sváva–Helgi and Sigrdrífa–Sigurðr.

### 4.2. The IE mythological theme of the “own-choice” marriage of Heavenly Maidens

The reinterpretation of the compound *wala-kuzjōn- as ‘she who chooses the slain (as husband for herself)’ may also explain why

<!-- page: 109 -->

Valkyries developed from death-demons into heavenly maidens, being referred to as [maidens – of Odin] in Old Norse (as in (32)).[^36]

32. “Now Herjann’s [i.e. Odin’s] maidens are counted up, valkyries ready to ride over the earth” (Vǫluspá 30.9–30.12).[^37]

Since in many respects Odin is a Norse counterpart to IE sky-gods (see e.g. West 2007: 173; Ginevra 2020b: 7–57), this aspect of the Valkyries may be linked to the fact that, within IE mythology, “own-choice” marriage seems to be a peculiar feature of “Heavenly Maidens”, i.e. daughters of the Sun-god or Sky-god, such as the Vedic Sanskrit goddess Sūryā and the Ancient Greek heroine Helen.

The “husband-chooser” par excellence of Vedic myth, as famously noted by Jamison (2001), is the sun-maiden called Sūryā- (lit. ‘She-Sun’), also known as the “Daughter of the Sun” (most likely just an aspect of Dawn, originally):[^38] according to passages like (33), she āvr̥ṇīta yuvā́m pátī ‘chose you two [i.e. the two Aśvins] as husbands’.

33. “Having come to marriage [patitváṁ] to you for a partnership with you, the noble young girl [Sūryā] chose you two [Aśvins] as her husbands [avr̥ṇīta yuvā́m pátī]” (Rigveda 1.119.5cd)[^39]

In her contribution, Jamison (2001: 313–314) also draws attention to further thematic and phraseological parallels between Sūryā’s own-choice wedding and the wedding of Helen, the only mortal daughter of the Greek sky-god Zeus and most likely a sun-maiden (cf. West 2007: 230–232), whose wedding may be analyzed as a svayaṃ-vara- ‘own-choice’ procedure as well, given that Helen’s earthly father Tyndareus allowed her to ἑλέσθαι µνηστήρων ἕνα ‘choose one of the suitors’, as in (34).

<!-- page: 110 -->

34. “He (Tyndareus) allowed his daughter (Helen) to choose one of the suitors [ἑλέσθαι µνηστήρων ἕνα], him to whom the sweet breezes of Aphrodite were carrying her. She chose Menelaus [ἡ δ᾿ εἵλεθ᾿ … Μενέλαον], and how I wish she had never chosen him!” (Euripides, Iphigenia at Aulis 68–71)[^40]

These two IE examples of mythological own-choice marriage are extremely relevant to the topic of this investigation: in the passages (33)–(34) quoted above, both the Vedic sun-maiden Sūryā and the Greek daughter of the sky-god Helen may be regarded as acting like Valkyries, as they “choose as husbands for themselves” characters who were originally not divine: the chosen husbands of Sūryā, the Aśvins, were originally considered too close to the human beings by the gods (35), who only admitted them among themselves after a quite literal exchange of favors (36), whereas Helen’s chosen husband Menelaus was actually born mortal and was able to avoid death exclusively thanks to his wife, as seen above in (10), repeated here as (37).

35. “The seer [Cyavana] himself answered them [i.e. the Aśvins]: ‘In Kurukṣetra yonder the gods perform a sacrifice and exclude you two from it: in that respect you are incomplete, in that respect imperfect!’ And the Aśvins departed forthwith, and came to the gods, as they were performing a sacrifice, after the chanting of the Bahiṣpavamāna. They said: ‘Invite us thereto!’ The gods said: ‘We will not invite you: you have wandered and mixed much among men, performing cures’” (Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa 4.1.5.13–4.1.5.14).[^41]

36. “They [i.e. the Aśvins] said: ‘But surely ye worship with a headless sacrifice!’, ‘How with a headless [sacrifice]?’, ‘Nay, invite us, and we will tell you!’, ‘So be it!’, so they [i.e. the gods] invited them [i.e. the Aśvins]. They drew this Āśvina cup for them; and those two [Aśvins] became the Adhvaryu

<!-- page: 111 -->

priests of the sacrifice, and restored the head of the sacrifice” (Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa 4.1.5.15).[^42]

37. “But for yourself, Menelaus, fostered by Zeus, it is not ordained that you should die and meet your fate in horse-pasturing Argos, but to the Elysian plain and the ends of the earth will the immortals convey you, where dwells fair-haired Rhadamanthus, and where life is easiest for men. No snow is there, nor heavy storm, nor ever rain, but always Ocean sends up blasts of the shrill-blowing West Wind that they may give cooling to men; for you have Helen to wife, and are in their eyes the son-in-law of Zeus” (Odyssey 4.561–4.569).[^43]

These two Indic and Greek narratives find parallels in (26) above, where the Norse goddess Skaði is also said to have performed an “own-choice’ marriage, more precisely with Njǫrðr, another mythological character who did not number among the gods originally, and only came to be admitted among them later on in his life, as told in (38). In the same passage of Snorri’s Edda where Skaði’s “own-choice” procedure is told, the goddess is also described as a warrior-maiden, highly reminiscent of the Valkyries (39).

38. “Njǫrðr is not of the race of the gods. He was brought up in the land of the Vanir, but the Vanir gave him as hostage to the gods” (Gylfaginning 23).[^44]

39. “But Skaði, daughter of giant Þjazi, took helmet and mail-coat and all weapons of war and went to Asgard to avenge her father. But the Æsir offered her atonement and compensation, the first item of which was that she was to choose herself a husband out of the Æsir” (Skaldskaparmál g56).[^45]

<!-- page: 112 -->

As shown by these correspondences between the marriages of the Aśvins, Menelaus, and Njǫrðr (as well as several others), the luck of being “chosen” by a Heavenly Maiden (or by a goddess in general) seems to be often combined with the luck of becoming immortal and/or inhabiting a paradisal afterlife. This frequent association between a divine “own-choice” wife and a “Valhǫll-type” afterlife may also be the reason why the Ancient Greek passage (12) above, repeated below as (40), not only mentions the hero Achilles as spending the afterlife in the paradisal White Island but also has him married to Helen in place of Menelaus. According to a further – apparently puzzling – tradition (Cingano 2023: 33–37),[^46] dating at least from the 6th century bce but prominently attested in a later passage of Apollonius of Rhodes’ Argonautica (41), in the paradisal Elysian plain Achilles is destined to be married to Medea, daughter of Aeetes and granddaughter of the Greek sun-god Helios (and thus – at least genealogically – a Heavenly Maiden).

40. “In time he was healed and returned from White Island, where, he used to declare, he saw Achilles, as well as Ajax the son of Oileus and Ajax the son of Telamon. With them, he said, were Patroclus and Antilochus; Helen was wedded to Achilles” (Pausanias, Description of Greece 3.19.13).[^47]

41. “When your son [i.e. Achilles] comes to the Elysian field – he whom the Naiads are now tending in the dwelling of Cheiron the Centaur, though he longs for your milk – it is his fate to be the husband of Aeetes’ daughter, Medea” (Apollonius of Rhodes, Argonautica 4.811–4.815).[^48]

I propose that these Heavenly Maidens and their originally non-divine “chosen” lovers, attested in several IE traditions, reflect the same inherited IE mythological theme that also developed into the Valkyries and their “chosen” heroes within the Norse tradition. While the semantic shift of the PGmc verb *keus-a- from ‘to taste’ to ‘to choose’ may explain

<!-- page: 113 -->

why PGmc *wala-kuzjōn- (most likely originally meaning ‘slain-taster’ and referring to a female death-demon of the Mediterranean type) eventually came to mean ‘slain-chooser’, further aspects of the mythology of the Valkyries are best explained as the result of convergence with the IE theme of the Heavenly Maiden’s own-choice marriage with a worthy hero, accepted among the gods after death.

## 5. Conclusion: love is the only cure for death – or why you should marry into a divine family

The results of this investigation may be summarized as follows. Textual traditions in Old Norse and in other historical IE languages attest two main models of the afterlife:

42. An undesirable afterlife, based on the idea that death is the opposite of life, where the dead are not able to perform any activities linked with life (eating, standing, seeing), and where they also have no social relations at all – the “Old Norse Hel” type.

43. A desirable afterlife, where several aspects of life are recreated or even improved upon (e.g. eating and drinking), and where social relations are indeed possible – the “Old Norse Valhǫll” type.

Valkyries were – and still are in contemporary popular culture – a fundamental element of the paradisal Norse afterlife, Valhǫll. This may have not always been the case, however: as argued by Egeler (2011), the Valkyries most likely developed from a Mediterranean belief in monstrous she-demons associated with death, battle, carrion-birds, which also underlies well-known Celtic and classical figures such as the Irish Bodbs, the Greek Sirens, the Roman Furies and the Etruscan Vanth.

Even though ON val-kyrja was clearly synchronically interpreted (e.g. by the Icelandic mythographer Snorri Sturluson) as ‘slain-chooser, she who chooses the slain’ (its second element being analyzed as an agentive noun linked to ON kjósa ‘to choose’, pret. 3pl kuru ‘they chose’), in view of the Valkyries’ likely origin as female death-demons it is at least conceivable that the ancient compound *wala-kuzjōn- (underlying both ON val-kyrja and OE wæl-cyrġe) originally meant ‘slain-taster’, displaying the inherited semantics of the Proto-Indo-European

<!-- page: 114 -->

root *ǵeu̯s- ‘to taste’ (as still attested by Gothic kausjan). An almost exact match for this reconstruction would be the OE compound wæl-cēasiġa ‘slain-taster’ (*wala-kausjan-), referring to the ‘raven’, a bird closely associated with the Valkyries (but unlikely to be a “chooser” of slain heroes).

As is well known, however, the Valkyries eventually developed from slain-tasting she-demons into splendid warrior-women, who are frequently mentioned as lovers of mortal heroes in poetic and mythological sources: after the semantic shift of PGmc *keus-a- from ‘to taste’ to ‘to choose’, the fact that slain warriors were mostly men allowed for a reinterpretation of *wala-kuzjōn- as ‘she who chooses the slain (as husband for herself)’, by convergence with the technical formula of “own-choice” marriages in Germanic, [woman – choose (*keus-a-) – man as husband (*wir-a-) – (for herself)], well attested in both Old Norse (with kjósa and verr) and Old English (with ċēosan and wer), and with quite close counterparts in several other IE traditions, such as the Indic and the Greek ones.

In the latter two traditions, “own-choice” marriages seem to have been a peculiar feature of mythological “Heavenly Maidens”, such as the Vedic Sanskrit daughter of the sun-god, the sun-maiden Sūryā, and the Ancient Greek daughter of the sky-god, Helen of Troy. Within their respective traditions, these Heavenly Maidens were literally said to “have chosen as husbands for themselves” characters who were originally not divine and who were accepted only later among the gods: Sūryā chose the two Aśvins (originally regarded as incomplete and imperfect by the gods because they spent too much time among mortals) and Helen chose the hero Menelaus (a mortal).

This mythological theme about Heavenly Maidens literally “choosing” originally non-divine characters as husbands for themselves, who consequently get to live with them in heaven forever, may thus be reflected in Norse mythology by the Valkyries, who are both Heavenly Maidens (described in the kennings as “maidens of Odin”, the supreme god and in many respects a Norse counterpart to IE sky-gods) and literal “choosers” of noble dead warriors (the Einherjar) who get to live with them in Valhǫll. The same inherited IE motif may also underlie the “own-choice” marriage of the Norse warrior-maiden Skaði (a Valkyrie-like divine character), who in Old Norse texts is said to have (inadvertently) chosen as her own husband Njǫrðr, a character who was not a member of the main divine clan (the Æsir) by birth. It must

<!-- page: 115 -->

be noted that, while passages like (39) describe Skaði as the daughter of a giant (Þjazi), she is also listed among the female members of the Æsir (Faulkes 2007a: 114) and referred to as “the shining bride of the gods” (Grímnismál 11.5). Further research may thus attempt to analyze Skaði’s mythology within the “IE Heavenly Maiden” framework proposed in this contribution, as well as investigate how the ON phrase kjósa val ‘to choose the slain’ (originally a “own-choice” marriage formula apparently reserved to women) eventually came to be used with the male warriors Einherjar and with the love-goddess Freyja as grammatical subjects, as seen above (21)–(22).

In addition to the Greek-Indic parallels noted by Jamison (2001: 313–314; cf. also Janda 2005: 346–348), the reconstruction of IE mythological themes involving “own-choice” marriage of a Heavenly Maiden has already been proposed by McCone (2020: 137–143) with regard to a sovereignty myth mainly attested in Ancient Greek, Old Indic and Old Irish. One of the main proposals of this study, however, is that a Proto-Indo-European “own-choice” marriage procedure may be reconstructed not only on the basis of thematic (i.e. mythological) data (as already argued by McCone) but also of linguistic (i.e. phraseological) material, given that the same formulaic phrase [woman – choose – man as husband – (for herself)] evoking the “own-choice” marriage procedure is attested in at least three IE branches: Germanic (Old Norse and Old English), Indic and Ancient Greek.

Finally, I hope to have shown that analyzing and comparing IE linguistic structures dealing with the “love life” – and existential conditions in general – of the dead in the afterlife may be a powerful means to learn more about IE customs and social institutions for the living. For instance, one is left to wonder whether the idea that marriage into the heavenly family was the only way for mortals to get into a paradisal afterlife may have mirrored real-life conceptions of social mobility,[^49] which may have allowed lower-born warriors to achieve a better social standing through marriage with a higher-status “own-choosing” woman. At least for the time being, however, only the dead know the answer to this question.
