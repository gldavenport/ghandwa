# 1. Exploring the afterlife in Indo-European traditions

Jenny H. Larsson

Stockholm University

## Abstract

This chapter showcases the interdisciplinary work in the volume, work characterized by the use of data from various sources – archaeological as well as textual – in order to address both mythological concepts of the afterlife and ritual activities involving mourning and handling of the diseased.

## 1. Death and afterlife across Indo-European cultures

Rituals and mythological concepts related to the afterlife are a consistent theme in ancient Indo-European cultures. Descriptions of funerary practices and ideas about the afterlife are found in numerous texts from various early Indo-European communities. The exploration of the myths and religious imagery of early Indo-European traditions holds significant promise for future progress. The advancement is contingent upon a meticulous focus on textual sources, undertaking a linguistic reconstruction of names, epithets, poetic formulae and phraseology. By examining these elements within the framework of Indo-European comparative linguistics, and applying the established sound laws, we can to a large extent rule out parallels due to coincidences or borrowings between languages. This methodological approach is a central part of this volume.

At the same time, the archaeological record is often particularly informative when it comes to the handling of the dead. Although different in nature, both types of data provide valuable insights into the beliefs and concepts about the afterlife in ancient societies. In order to improve our understanding of these issues, we need to utilize methodologies

from different fields, such as archaeology, comparative mythology and historical linguistics.

## 2. Indo-European poetic motifs and mythological concepts surrounding the afterlife

A significant portion of this volume is dedicated to an in-depth exploration of Indo-European poetic motifs and mythological concepts related to the afterlife.

Peter Jackson Rova, in his contribution, emphasizes the oral and aural nature of early Indo-European poetic traditions. Ancient Indo-European poets utilized language and poetry not only as artistic expressions but also as vehicles for social and eschatological transformation and to shape perceptions of the afterlife. By examining passages from Greek, Iranian, Vedic and to some extent Germanic traditions, Jackson Rova argues that the ancient poets envisioned poetry as a means of creating spaces that transcend ordinary existence and place the subject within the realm of imperishable fame and divine dwelling.

Building on the proposals by Jackson Rova in the preceding chapter, Martin Schwartz examines the intricate poetic and symbolic structure of the Gathic texts. Focusing on Yasna 45.3 as a key example, he explores phonic mystagogy, audiovisuality and the symbolic use of initial sounds in Zoroastrian sacred poetry. The chapter suggests that these texts were crafted to be both heard and visually envisioned by their audience, highlighting the interplay of sound and structure in conveying spiritual messages.

In the next chapter, Laura Massetti explores intriguing parallels between the Greek figure Orpheus and the Vedic R̥bhus. Building on her previous work (Massetti 2017), she presents comprehensive phraseological data supporting the etymological connection between the two names, both derived from the common PIE root *h₃erbʰ- ‘to turn, change sides, join’. Massetti situates these figures within the Indo-European poetic tradition, by highlighting their shared capacity to use poetry and ritual to bridge the mortal and divine realms. This chapter thus contributes to a deeper understanding of the “eschatology of glory-through-poetry” and how ancient poets envisioned and articulated the afterlife through their craft.

Jan Bremmer further explores the Indo-European motifs in Greek conceptions of the underworld. He points to a continuous poetic tradition, where the inherited Indo-European mythological material has,

<!-- page: 3 -->

to some extent, been adapted to the changing ecological conditions in Greece, and where the notion of the afterlife appears to have been transformed, perhaps influenced by Mesopotamian beliefs, to envision an underworld located beneath the earth.

In the chapter about the god of the night sky as a guide to the afterlife, Michael Janda explores the significance of the god Dionysus in relation to Indo-European mythology and the constellations, particularly Orion. Janda also explores the idea of Dionysus as a psychopomp, a role that is supported by traditions associating Orion with guiding souls in the afterlife. The chapter posits that the constellation Orion influenced a variety of myths and religious beliefs, including those involving Dionysus.

Riccardo Ginevra proposes a new reconstruction of the development of the Old Norse Valkyries from female death-demons of likely Mediterranean origin to heavenly maidens in love with mortal heroes. The chapter proposes that their name originally meant ‘slain-taster’ and was later reanalysed as ‘slain-chooser’ after the technical formula of “own-choice” marriages in Germanic and Indo-European (“to choose a man [as husband]”), making the Valkyries a Norse counterpart to Indic and Greek “Heavenly Maidens” (who often choose their own husbands in myth) and shedding light on how conceptualizations of the afterlife may mirror actual customs and social institutions.

Together, these chapters offer different perspectives on the eschatological themes and figures that permeate Indo-European mythology associated with the afterlife. By integrating linguistic, mythological and poetic evidence, they provide a deeper understanding of how ancient Indo-European societies conceptualized the afterlife and the transition from life to death.

## 3. Funerary practices and ritual mourning

Another prominent theme of the book is the exploration of rituals and funerary practices performed by different Indo-European speaking communities. Here the intersection between textual evidence and archaeological findings occupies a central position.

Birgit Anette Olsen examines Indo-European concepts of the afterlife, focusing on the notion that the deceased are in some way bound to the underworld. Olsen discusses three Proto-Indo-European roots related to tying, binding, and covering, *sh₂ai̯- ‘tie, bind (also magically)’, *deh₁- ‘bind’ and *u̯elhu̯- ‘wrap, cover’. This suggests a common Indo-European view of the afterlife where the deceased are both

<!-- page: 4 -->

physically and metaphorically tied to the underworld. Through a detailed linguistic analysis, supported by mythological and archaeological evidence, Olsen demonstrates how language and myth intertwine to shape Indo-European conceptualizations of the underworld and the fate of the deceased.

The contribution by Anders Kaliff and Terje Oestigaard also focuses on the funerary practices of the early Indo-Europeans. They examine the Bronze Age Håga grave mound, located outside Uppsala, Sweden, noted for its remains of intricate cremation rites. In the chapter, they argue that cremation in Scandinavia from the Bronze Age onwards is closely tied to Indo-European traditions and that it is significantly linked to advances in metallurgy, the handling of high temperatures, and the frequent use of fire in various rituals. These rituals included not only funerary practices but also agricultural and seasonal rites, highlighting the multifaceted significance of fire in Indo-European cultural and religious contexts.

In the final chapter, Anders Richardt Jørgensen analyses the two roots *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’ and *k̑eh₂d- ‘to mourn; to be emotionally distraught’. He suggests that the original meaning of the root ‘to fall’, was metonymically extended to apply specifically to ‘falling down as the result of strong emotional distress’, a cross-culturally very common ritualized behaviour found in relation to the death of a relative. Accordingly, it is suggested that this behaviour may have been characteristic of ritualized mourning among the early speakers of Indo-European.

## 4. Conclusion and a way forward

The present volume is intended as a step towards creating an interdisciplinary interface that allows drawing conclusions from different types of data and methodologies. The interdisciplinary approach allows for a richer, more nuanced exploration of the complex interplay between language, ritual and belief systems and enables us to examine how Indo-European peoples sought to understand and ritualize the mysteries of the afterlife.

Looking ahead, we hope that this volume will also set the stage for future research. By highlighting the intersection between textual evidence and archaeological findings and by including contributions from various disciplines, we hope to encourage further interdisciplinary collaboration of this kind in order to enrich our understanding of human cultural heritage across time and space.
