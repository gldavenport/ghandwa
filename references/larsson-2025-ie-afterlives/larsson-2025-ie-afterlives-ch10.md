# 10. Mourning among the speakers of Proto-Indo-European

*k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’ and ‘to mourn’

Anders Richardt Jørgensen

Uppsala University

## Abstract

This chapter argues for the identity of the two roots *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’ and *k̑eh₂d- ‘to mourn; to be emotionally distraught’. It is suggested that the original meaning ‘to fall’ was metonymically extended to apply specifically to ‘falling down as the result of strong emotional distress’, a cross-culturally very common ritualized behaviour found in relation to the death of a relative. It is furthermore proposed that the suppletive paradigm of Vedic non-present śad- ‘to fall’ (from *k̑eh₂d-), present śīyate, is an instance of weak suppletion, i.e. that the apparently etymologically isolated present allomorph śīya- in fact is from the root *k̑eh₂d- via a Proto-Indo-European *i̯e/o-present *k̑h₂h₁i̯e/o- <*k̑h₂d-i̯e/o- (with the well-established, but sporadic, change of *d> *h₁). Finally, it is argued that the evidence for a root *k̑ei̯(H)- ‘to fall’ may be disregarded, with OIr. ciid ‘cries’ and MW ciawr ‘falls’ deriving from the present allomorph *k̑h₂h₁i̯e/o- also seen in Vedic and with Brittonic *kui̯ð- ‘to fall’ deriving from a newly created Proto-Celtic *i̯e/o-present *kād-i̯e/o- based on the non-present allomorph *k̑eh₂d-, rather than from a d-present *kei̯-de/o-.

## 1. Ritualized mourning[^1]

> At the ritual announcement of the death, which is made by a man, the women of the tribe keen and wail and throw themselves to the ground.
>
> <!-- page: 178 -->
>
> At times they will strike themselves with sharp objects. It is expected that the men of the community will restrain the women from seriously injuring themselves. The men step forward and pull the weapons from the women’s hands and throw them out of reach. The women usually respond to this by ceasing their attempts at self-injury. […] The men of the tribe will also keen at times, particularly if the dead person was a close relative; but the more common emotional expression of the men is venting anger by dancing. (Golden 2009: 85)

The above quote describes the typical behaviour following the announcement of the death of a tribal member among the Yolngu, an Australian indigenous people. The behaviour may seem strange to Western readers but it is cross-culturally far from unusual. A casual trawl through descriptions of mourning rituals and behaviour in different cultures in different periods[^2] reveals a number of recurring elements relating to the immediate, short-term expression of grief, typically carried out at the announcement of the death or at the funeral. The reactions may be grouped into the following main categories:

• weeping and wailing

• falling to the ground

• throwing or rubbing dirt/dust/ashes on the head and/or body

• tearing of clothes

• beating the head and/or chest

• pulling or tearing out one’s hair

• lacerating the skin.

As in the example from the Yolngu above, the specific reactions often fall among gender lines. In other cultures, reactions may be tied to social standing; for example, servants and slaves are expected to react in specific ways to the death of their master. At times, mourning may even become institutionalized with the development of professional mourners, people who are compensated for performing ritualized acts of mourning. However, gender differences, societal stratification and the professionalization of mourning, while interesting in their own right, will not be treated in this chapter.

## 2. Mourning among the speakers of Proto-Indo-European

Considering how widespread the behaviours listed in Section 1 are, as reactions to the death of a relative or prominent member of society, it is probable that some or all of these behaviours were present among the speakers of Proto-Indo-European.[^3] But how would one go from observing that this behaviour would be probable to actually reconstructing how speakers of Proto-Indo-European behaved under such circumstances?

One way would be to look at attestations of relevant behaviour in the early texts in the various early Indo-European branches (cf. Sergent 2023: 238; West 2007: 498–499). Achilles’ reaction to the death of Patroklos provides an illustrative example:

> Iliad, XVIII, 22–31:
>
> ὣς φάτο, τὸν δ᾽ ἄχεος νεφέλη ἐκάλυψε µέλαινα:
>
> ἀµφοτέρῃσι δὲ χερσὶν ἑλὼν κόνιν αἰθαλόεσσαν
>
> χεύατο κὰκ κεφαλῆς, χαρίεν δ᾽ ᾔσχυνε πρόσωπον:
>
> νεκταρέῳ δὲ χιτῶνι µέλαιν᾽ ἀµφίζανε τέφρη.
>
> αὐτὸς δ᾽ ἐν κονίῃσι µέγας µεγαλωστὶ τανυσθεὶς
>
> κεῖτο, φίλῃσι δὲ χερσὶ κόµην ᾔσχυνε δαΐζων.
>
> δµῳαὶ δ᾽ ἃς Ἀχιλεὺς ληΐσσατο Πάτροκλός τε
>
> θυµὸν ἀκηχέµεναι µεγάλ᾽ ἴαχον, ἐκ δὲ θύραζε
>
> ἔδραµον ἀµφ᾽ Ἀχιλῆα δαΐφρονα, χερσὶ δὲ πᾶσαι
>
> στήθεα πεπλήγοντο, λύθεν δ᾽ ὑπὸ γυῖα ἑκάστης.
>
> So he spoke: a black cloud of grief closed over Achilles.
>
> With both hands he gathered up the dark grimy dust,
>
> scattered it over his head, befouled his handsome features
>
> and on his fragrant tunic the black ash settled.
>
> with his own hands he tore and defiled his hair.
>
> There stretched in the dust, great in his greatness he lay;
>
> Those maidservants won as spoils by Achilles and Patroklos
>
> shrieked aloud in their heartfelt anguish, ran out of doors,
>
> stood around warlike Achilles, all with their hands
>
> beat on their breasts, the limbs of all were weakened.
>
> (Translation from Green 2015: 339)

<!-- page: 180 -->

As can be seen, this passage displays many of the reactions listed in Section 1: descent to the ground, covering oneself in dirt, tearing of hair, wailing and beating the chest. Very similar behaviours can be observed in the Roman tradition:

> [I]n literary sources describing the mourning of the common people or of a crowd, both men and women were shown to recite laments and express similar gestures of sadness: pulling the hair, scratching the cheeks till blood was flowing, beating the head and (bare) chest, falling on the earth, putting the ashes of the extinguished hearth on the head were all ritual duties (iusta) of mourners towards the dead […]. (Erker 2022: 44–45)

However, even the oldest Indo-European texts are quite removed in time from the probable Proto-Indo-European period and there is no guarantee that the behaviours described in the texts, even if attested in different branches, necessarily date back to a shared proto-stage (unless they are highly specific and unusual in nature).

Another way to approach the question would be to look for depictions of mourning behaviour in the archaeological record. While this could potentially be a useful approach (and may be applied, e.g., to the Mycenaean period, as Iakovidis 1966 and Kramer-Hajos 2015 do), I am not aware of any such material from a context that could plausibly be associated the Proto-Indo-European period.

Finally, we may try to use the comparative method of linguistic reconstruction to demonstrate the status of the concept as belonging to a shared pre-stage. This is the approach attempted in the present chapter. Specifically, I will use a subset of reconstructed vocabulary displaying what may be described as metonymic extension, that is, where a reconstructable root is found with a primary, “basic” meaning and one or more secondary ones arguably derived from the primary one. The secondary usage may imply a specific kind of behaviour, in the present chapter argued to be specifically related to mourning.

## 3. Proto-Indo-European *pleh₂k-/*pleh₂g-

A probable instance of metonymic extension is provided by the reflexes of the verbal root commonly reconstructed as *pleh₂k-/*pleh₂g- (LIV²: 484–485). The basic meaning of this appears to have been something like ‘to beat, to hit, to slap’. This is directly continued in Greek πλήσσω ‘I strike’, Latin plangō ‘I strike, hit’, plāga ‘a wound’ and Baltic, exemplified by Lith. plàkti ‘to beat’. However, next to this we also find Latin

<!-- page: 181 -->

plangō ‘I beat the head or the chest as a sign of mourning; bewail, weep’[^4] (often with a part of the body, e.g. pectus, faciem, as the object). The later Slavic reflexes of PSlav. *plakati only have the meaning ‘to cry, to weep, to lament’ and the Germanic reflex *flōkan- turns up with the meaning ‘to clap, to strike’ (as a sign of joy or grief) in Old English flócan, the Gothic meaning is something like ‘to bewail, to mourn’ (Goth. faíflokun ‘they mourned’), whereas we find a deverbal fluoh ‘a curse’ in Old High German (cf. Modern German fluchen ‘to curse’). Based on these attestations we may assume that the specific root *pleh₂k-/*pleh₂g-, which is just one out of several roots reconstructable with a meaning ‘to hit, to strike’, was used not only about ‘hitting something’ but also specifically about ‘hitting oneself’ as an expression of emotional distress (typically, but maybe not exclusively, sorrow). Consequently, this root may be plausibly reconstructed for the common ancestor with both a primary, basic meaning and a more specialized meaning relating to a specific behaviour.

## 4. PIE 1. *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’ and 2. *k̑eh₂d- ‘sorrow; hatred’

This takes us to another possible example of what may be a metonymic extension of a root. In light of the frequent act of ‘falling to the ground’ as a physical manifestation of grief (cf. Section 1 above), I will propose that the PIE root 2. *k̑eh₂d- ‘sorrow; hatred’ developed by metonymic extension from 1. *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’, just like *pleh₂k-/*pleh₂g-, can be reconstructed with a secondary meaning ‘to express grief (by hitting oneself)’ directly developed from the primary meaning ‘to slap, to hit’.[^5] We shall first review the linguistic material.

### 4.1. PIE 1. *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall (down)’

The reconstruction of Proto-Indo-European verbal root 1. *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall (down)’ is primarily based on the following evidence:[^6]

> <!-- page: 182 -->
>
> Lat. cadō, cadere ‘to fall’ <pres. *k̑h₂d-e/o-
>
> Ved. śad-[^7] in śaśāda ‘has fallen’ <perf. *k̑e-k̑oh₂d-, śatsyati <desid. *k̑eh₂d-si̯e/o-
>
> Gr. (Hom.) κεκάδοντο ‘they retreated’ (i.e. ‘fell back’) <aor. *(k̑e-)k̑h₂d-e/o-[^8]

The reconstruction given here, *k̑eh₂d-, is far from universally accepted, however. It follows Lubotsky (1981: 134), who argues for the loss of laryngeals in roots ending in *VHD- when followed by an additional consonant. Following this, a desiderative *k̑eh₂d-si̯e/o- would yield the attested śatsyati regularly.

If this development of *-VHD- is not accepted, it is possible that an original alternation between full- and long-grade ā (in *k̑Vh₂d-) and zero-grade i/ī (in *k̑h₂d-) was regularized to a more normal long-grade ā and a full- and zero-grade a (back-formed from e.g. the 3sg. Perf. śaśāda). In general, expected root ablaut between -āC- and -ī/iC- is poorly preserved in Indic and there may be a drift towards a more typical full-grade -aC-.[^9] Likewise, the root vocalism of the Latin perfect cecidī ‘fell’, from *k̑e-k̑oh₂d-e, would have to be analogical to the short vowel in the present tense cadō <*k̑h₂d-[^10] and the plural forms of the perfect, *k̑e-k̑h₂d-.

One could alternatively reconstruct the root as *k̑h₂ed-, as LIV² (318 fn. 1) tentatively proposes, provided that *k̑h₂- is not an illicit Proto-Indo-European initial cluster and that it is not expected to give Gr. *kʰ-. Finally, one could reconstruct the root as *k̑ad-, as LIV² (318) and Bendahman (1993: 72–74) do. While this would remove the need for invoking analogy or controversial sound laws, it would come with the cost of having to reconstruct a verbal root with a-vocalism. Going forward, I will, for the sake of simplicity, refer to the root as *k̑eh₂d-, while not necessarily ruling out the alternative reconstructions *k̑ad- and *k̑h₂ed-.

### 4.2. PIE 2. *k̑eh₂d- ‘mourning, sorrow’

The phonological reconstruction of 2. *k̑eh₂d- ‘mourning, sorrow’ is less disputed, with the modern consensus favouring the presence of a

<!-- page: 183 -->

post-vocalic laryngeal. The attested reflexes are consequently derived from either a full-grade *k̑eh₂d- or a zero-grade *k̑h₂d-. Reflexes of this root are mainly attested in nominal formations (IEW: 517; EIEC: 259; Mallory & Adams 2006: 343–344; Stüber 2002: 114–115):

> *k̑eh₂d-es-> Gr. neut. κῆδος, Dor. κᾶδος ‘anxiety, grief, sorrow; funeral rites’
>
> *k̑h₂d-es-> PGerm. neut. s-stem *hat-az/iz- ‘hatred’ (e.g. Goth. hatis)
>
> *k̑eh₂d-V-[^11]> PCelt. *kādV-> PBrit. *kɔð (MW kawð ‘anger, wrath’; MBret. cuez ‘regret, anguish’)
>
> *k̑h₂d-ti-/*k̑h₂d-s-i-> PCelt. *kassi- ‘hatred’ (OIr. cais ‘hatred; love’, MW cas, MBret. cas ‘hatred’)
>
> *k̑(e)h₂d-o-/-i-[^12]> Osc. Gsg. cadeis ‘enmity’
>
> *k̑eh₂d-ro-> Avest. sādra- ‘grief’

A sole verbal formation is found, in Gr. κήδοµαι ‘I am concerned for, I care for; I mourn’. It is unclear if this may be projected back to Proto-Indo-European (as LIV²: 319 does). The problem presented by the semantic range of the reflexes, from ‘sorrow’ and ‘regret’ to ‘anger’ and ‘hatred’, while substantial, is hardly insurmountable. The semantic range was explained by Porzig (1942: 293; see also Stüber 2002: 115) as being due to the word originating in a very specific context, namely the violent death of a relative:

> The semantic diversification of the word family in Greek indicates that the term originally referred to an emotion arising in connection with blood relatives. More specifically, Homeric usage shows that κῆδος first of all denotes the emotional response to the violent death of a kinsman. This usage provides the semantic bridge to the meanings attested in Germanic and Celtic, suggesting that the original sense was a psychological state triggered by the killing of a blood relative, one in which grief, hatred and the obligation to avenge were still undifferentiated.[^13]

<!-- page: 184 -->

While this explanation is not strictly necessary, given the semantic instability of words designating emotions, it fits remarkably well with the idea that *k̑eh₂d- ‘sorrow, hatred’ developed as a secondary meaning from *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’, given that falling to the ground is one of the behaviours observed cross-culturally exactly in the situation outlined by Porzig, i.e. the death of a relative (although not necessarily a violent death).

Traditionally, the nominal formations in *k̑eh₂d- have been assumed to lack a verbal root, with the possible exception of Gr. κήδοµαι ‘I am concerned for, I care for; I mourn’. However, a verbal root is readily available if we allow for an etymological connection with 1. *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’. A connection between *k̑eh₂d- ‘sorrow’ with the root meaning ‘to fall’ is possible if the latter is reconstructed as *k̑eh₂d- (either following Lubotsky 1981 or by assuming analogical regularization of the ablaut). Other scenarios are also possible. The two roots could be connected if we allow for the operation of Schwebeablaut between *k̑h₂ed- (*k̑h₂d-) ‘to fall’ and *k̑eh₂d- (*k̑h₂d-) ‘sorrow; hatred’. It may even be possible to connect the two without positing a laryngeal in the root. There is some evidence for the existence of an acrostatic alternation between full grade and lengthened grade in the root of neuter s-stems (Stüber 2002: 204–207). Whether this ablaut is due to an inherent feature of the root (as a Narten root) or tied to the specific derivational type is not important at the moment. Positing nominal ablaut *k̑ād-(e)s-/ *k̑ad-(e)s- would allow an effortless connection to the verbal root reconstructed by LIV² (318) as *k̑ad- ‘to fall’.

Be that as it may, we may conclude that an etymological connection between 1. ‘to fall’ and 2. ‘sorrow; hatred’ is a distinct possibility and, if accepted, may be understood literally, rather than figuratively, as referring to cross-culturally common behaviours associated with the death of a relative or an important person in society.

## 5. PIE 1. *k̑eh₂d- and *k̑ei̯(H)- ‘to fall’

The attested reflexes of 1. *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’ present additional complications that may be relevant to the discussion. In Vedic the verbal root is part of a suppletive paradigm[^14] in which the present tense is formed by an apparently unrelated root śī-[^15] (PIE *k̑ei̯- in LIV²: 321; *k̑ei̯H- also

<!-- page: 185 -->

possible) and the rest of the verbal paradigm and its derivatives are formed by śad- (PIE *k̑ad- in LIV²: 318).

|  | PIE *k̑ei̯(H)- | PIE *k̑eh₂d- (or *k̑h₂ed-, *k̑ad-) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pres. | śīyate[^16] | – |
| Perf. | – | śaśāda |
| Fut. | – | śatsyati |
| Vb. adj. | – | śanná- |

One may notice the vague similarity between the two roots forming the suppletive paradigm, śī- and śad-. This naturally does not have to mean anything, since verbal suppletion does not require a specific phonological explanation. However, it might be worth considering whether this particular instance of suppletion is actually “weak”, that is, that it has come about by a sound change distorting an originally non-suppletive paradigm.[^17] The natural assumption in this case would be that the “elsewhere” root *k̑eh₂d- is the original form and that *k̑ei̯(H)-, which is restricted to the present tense, came about through a sound change. It is important to note that, apart from the Celtic forms discussed below in Section 5.2, Ved. śī- does not have securely established cognates in other Indo-European branches.[^18]

Descriptively, Ved. śīyate appears to continue a medial *i̯e/o-present with zero grade in the root. If the original form of the root was indeed *k̑eh₂d-, we would expect the present stem to have been PIE *k̑h₂d-i̯e/o-.[^19] I will now tentatively suggest that this *k̑h₂d-i̯e/o- (or *k̑d-(h₁)i̯e/o-) may actually be behind the attested Pres. śīyate. The mechanism that would allow this connection would be the development of *d> *h₁, a change that has been identified in a number of Proto-Indo-European formations. A particularly striking example of the sound change is

<!-- page: 186 -->

provided by the heteroclitic noun *u̯ed-r/n- ‘water’, where the change of *d> *h₁ gave rise to a weak suppletive paradigm, NAsg. *u̯e/oh₁-r̥ (<*u̯e/od-r̥), Oblsg. *ud-(e)n, faithfully reflected in Ved. NAsg. vār (3x /vá.ar/), Oblsg. ud(a)n- (Lubotsky 2013, building on de Vaan 2008: 644). The exact conditioning for *d> *h₁ is difficult to pinpoint, however, so for now it can only be described as a sporadic change.[^20]

If we apply the change *d> *h₁ to our hypothesized input form *k̑h₂d-i̯e-,[^21] we would expect it to yield PIE *k̑h₂h₁-i̯e-. It is unclear what would happen to this *k̑h₂h₁-i̯e/o-, since it is quite difficult to find precise parallels for the environment #THHi̯V-. However, if the Indic (and Iranian) present passive suffix -ya- is derived from PIE *-h₁-i̯e/o, as is often assumed,[^22] forms such as Ved. pass. dhīyate ‘is placed, is put’ <“*dʰh₁-h₁i̯é/ó-” (from the root *dʰeh₁- ‘to place, to put’) would provide a precise phonological parallel. We could then conclude that the regular outcome of #THh₁i̯V- was indeed Ved. Cīi̯V- and that śīyate was the regular outcome of *k̑h₂h₁-i̯é/ó-. However, one can hardly exclude the possibility of the passives having analogical vocalism, an explanation that is not available for the synchronically isolated śīyate. Alternatively, one could assume a Sievers type vocalization *k̑h₂h₁-ii̯e- after the heavy initial cluster, followed by laryngeal metathesis to *k̑h₂ih₁-i̯e- to give the attested śīyate.

In spite of this uncertainty, I will very tentatively suggest that the verbal root *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’ should be reconstructed as follows:

|  | Pre-PIE | PIE | Ved. |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Pres. | *k̑h₂d-i̯é-tor | > *k̑h₂h₁-i̯é-tor | > śīyate |
| Perf. | – | *k̑e-k̑óh₂d-e | > śaśāda |
| Desid. | – | *k̑éh₂d-si̯e-ti | > śatsyati |

<!-- page: 187 -->

In this way, we can explain the synchronic suppletive paradigm as having arisen from an older, regular paradigm of the type represented by the root *mer- ‘to disappear; to die’ (LIV²: 439), i.e. Pres. *mr̥-i̯é-tor, Aor. *mér-t, Perf. *me-mór-e. It should be noted that the Latin present stem seen in cadō, cadere will then have to be secondarily created, with *k̑h₂d- (or its reflex *kad-) introduced from zero-grade forms, such as the weak forms of the aorist and perfect and the past passive participle.

### 5.1. PCelt. *kii̯e/o- ‘to fall’ and ‘to weep’

If the Vedic suppletive Pres. śīyate ‘falls’ really is the result of a sound change from Pre-PIE *k̑h₂d-i̯e/o- (or *k̑d-(h₁)i̯e/o-), we would ideally want to find both the primary meaning ‘to fall’ and the secondary meaning ‘to mourn’ to be reflected in the present tense allomorph *k̑h₂h₁-i̯e/o-, just like we do in the elsewhere allomorph *k̑eh₂d-, as argued above in Section 4. It is possible that Celtic provides evidence for this. Schumacher (KPV: 404–410) has suggested that a cognate of Ved. śīyate is found in Celtic, in the verb *kii̯e/o- ‘to fall; to weep’.

If the reconstruction for Ved. śīyate proposed in this chapter, PIE *k̑h₂h₁-i̯e/o-, is accepted, we ought to be able to derive PCelt. *kii̯e/o- from this as well. For this to be the case, we could operate with a regular(?) loss of the laryngeals and subsequent development initial Ti̯V- to Tii̯V- in Celtic (cf. PIE *g̑ʰi̯em-> *gii̯emo- ‘winter’> MW gayaf, MBret. gouaff, HVann. gouian). Alternatively, we could assume a Sievers-like vocalization *k̑h₂h₁ii̯e/o- after the heavy initial cluster.[^23] In either case, it is likely that the laryngeals would ultimately be lost without a trace in Pre-Proto-Celtic, leading to the reconstructable Proto-Celtic *kii̯e/o-.

In Brittonic this *kii̯e/o- is reflected in a rare and apparently isolated Middle Welsh present ciawr ‘falls’ (secondarily inflected as an ā-stem), discussed in detail by Schumacher (KPV: 408–409). As such, the Welsh form only serves to ensure that the connection to śīyate is correct, by providing a perfect semantic match with Ved. śīyate ‘falls’. The meaning of the much-better-attested Irish reflex is more interesting. Here we find a complete verb ciid ‘weeps, cries’.[^24] Schumacher (KPV: 406) argues that the meaning is due to a semantic shift within Celtic or in Pre-Proto-Celtic, which is indeed possible. However, if the scenario

<!-- page: 188 -->

proposed above, namely (1) that PIE *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’ and ‘to express grief’ were one and the same root and (2) that the root *k̑ei̯(H)- is simply the allomorph of this root in the present tense, we could equally well assume that the meaning ‘to weep’ attested in Old Irish ciid simply goes back to Proto-Indo-European and that both meanings, ‘to fall down’ and ‘to express grief’, were still present at the common pre-stage of Goidelic and Brittonic. It should, however, be noted that in the current scenario the complete verbal paradigm of Irish ciid would have to be due to a secondary development, possibly very old,[^25] with new non-present forms being created from the present stem *kii̯e/o- according to the productive patterns.[^26]

### 5.2. PCelt. *kād- ‘to fall’?

As was argued above in Section 5, the very existence of a Proto-Indo-European root *k̑ei̯(H)- ‘to fall’ may be doubted. If Ved. śīyate and PCelt. *kii̯e/o- are instead derived from *k̑eh₂d- (or *k̑h₂ed- or *k̑ad-), we are left with PBrit *kui̯ð- ‘to fall’ (W cwyddo, MCorn. cothe, Bret. kouezhañ) as the only evidence for the existence of the Proto-Indo-European verbal root *k̑ei̯(H)- ‘to fall’.[^27] Usually PBrit. *kui̯ð- ‘to fall’ is reconstructed as PCelt. *kei̯d- (Schrijver 1995: 224), in turn derived from the root *k̑ei̯(H)- ‘to fall’ by means of a *d(ʰ)e/o-present (thus LIV²: 321; KPV: 404–406). A PCelt. *kei̯de/o- would indeed regularly produce the attested Brittonic forms. However, given that the evidence for an independent root *k̑ei̯(H)- ‘to fall’ is very limited, we may wonder if the Proto-Celtic reconstruction is accurate. Furthermore, the function of a Celtic d-present *kei̯de/o- alongside the already reconstructed, synonymous Proto-Celtic present *kii̯e/o- remains obscure. Indeed, the very existence of d-presents in Celtic is doubtful. Schumacher (KPV: 404–405, 569–570, 627–628) lists three instances of d-presents: PCelt.

<!-- page: 189 -->

*kei̯de/o- ‘to fall’ (PIE *k̑ei̯(H)-) discussed here, *tāde/o-[^28] ‘to melt, to thaw’ (PIE *teh₂(i̯)-) and *sende/o-[^29] ‘to pour, to skim’ (PIE *sem(H)-).

Another explanation of PBrit. *kui̯ð- ‘to fall’ may be possible. If an inherited verb with a present *kii̯e/o- and a non-present *kād-[^30] were split into two independent verbs, we might expect a new present stem to be created for the non-present *kād-, just like new non-present stems were argued to have been created for *kii̯e/o- in Goidelic. For primary verbs, the most likely productive present-forming formations will probably have been PCelt. *-e/o- and *-i̯e/o-, that is, *kād-e/o- or *kād-i̯e/o-.[^31] The latter of these could conceivably be what is behind

<!-- page: 190 -->

PBrit. *kui̯ð. We know that *-di̯-[^32] was metathesized to *-i̯ð- fairly late[^33] in the history of the Brittonic languages, as in PCelt. *mad-i̯e/o- ‘breaks’> PBrit. *mai̯ð- (Schrijver 1995: 317–319; KPV: 465). Accordingly, a *kād-i̯e/o- is expected to yield *kɔ̄i̯ð- before the operation of the new quantity system in Brittonic.

The development of the rare Brittonic diphthong *ɔ̄i̯ (possibly rather *ọ̄i̯[^34]) is not entirely clear. The only two relatively certain examples are in absolute word-final position, *mɔ̄i̯ ‘greater, more’ <PCelt. *māi̯ūs and *ɔ̄i̯ ‘egg’ <PCelt. *āu̯i̯o-(?). In Welsh these have wy (W mwy and wy), completely compatible with a derivation of W cwyddo ‘fall’ from PBrit. *kɔ̄i̯ð- <*kād-i̯e/o-. The Cornish and Breton outcomes of *ɔ̄i̯ are more complicated and indicate that the diphthong had not yet merged with *ui̯ <PCelt. *ei̯ in Proto-Brittonic. Schrijver (2007: 312–313) assumes that *ɔ̄i̯ was raised to *ʉi̯ in Proto-Brittonic. This was then retracted to *ui̯ in Welsh and eventually also to this in Cornish (but apparently it did not merge completely with *ui̯ <*ē <*ei̯). In Breton we find /yi/ in MBret. muy ‘more’ and (v)uy ‘egg’. However, these examples may only be relevant for absolute final position (and in mono­syllables). It is fully possible that *ɔ̄i̯ developed differently in closed, non-final (i.e. unstressed) syllables (which would comprise most inflected verbal forms). Therefore, it would not be surprising if *kɔ̄i̯ð- (i.e. possibly *kọ̄i̯ð-) yielded *kui̯ð- when the vowel was shortened in pretonic closed syllables before the following i̯ (for the early shortening of *ɔ̄ in pretonic closed syllables, see Schrijver 1995: 213–215, 251–254). It is therefore at least possible that W cwyddo, MCorn. cothe, Bret. kouezhañ ‘to fall’ may be derived from *kād-i̯e/o-.

<!-- page: 191 -->

## 6. Semantic parallels

Before accepting a connection between the two roots and, as a consequence, a semantic development from ‘to fall down’ to ‘to fall down as part of mourning behaviour’ to ‘sorrow; hatred’ we should preferably be able to point to examples where a similar semantic change took place.

A partial parallel is provided by the Akkadian verb napalsuḫu, which, apart from the primary meaning ‘to fall, to throw oneself down’, is also clearly used specifically for ‘to fall down in mourning’ (Gruber 1980: 465). In the Near Eastern context it is quite clear that this type of ‘falling to the ground’ is to be understood literally rather than figuratively, as it is often accompanied by covering oneself in dust and rolling around (cf. Olyan 2004: 29–31).

Taken at face value, OHG trūrēn ‘to mourn; to be sad’ (whence German trauern and Trauer ‘sorrow; mourning’) provides a more complete parallel. It is the stative derivative of the strong verb found in Goth. driusan, OEng. dréosan, OSax. driosan ‘to fall down’ and would as such be expected to mean ‘to be falling down’. The Old English adjective dréorig ‘sorrowful, sad’, ModEng. dreary <PGerm. *dreu̯zaga- ‘falling(?)’ appears to show the same semantic shift.[^35] Accordingly, some of the reflexes of PGerm. *dreu̯s- ‘to fall down’ appear to show a development similar to the one assumed here for *k̑eh₂d-. However, the matter is complicated by the existence of OEng. drūsian ‘to sink, to become slow, to droop’, which might open up the possibility that the development was via ‘to be lethargic’ and ‘to be depressed’ ending with ‘to mourn’.

## 7. Conclusion

In the preceding I have argued that *k̑eh₂d- ‘sorrow; hate’ (alternatively *k̑ād-/*k̑ad-) should be seen as a derivative of the verbal root *k̑eh₂d- ‘to fall’ (alternatively *k̑ad-). The semantic motivation is provided by the observation that ‘falling to the ground’ is a cross-culturally common reaction to the death of a relative. Furthermore, it was argued that the Vedic suppletive present śīyate ‘falls’ is in fact not suppletive in origin but the result of the sporadic change of *d> *h₁, allowing for a derivation from *k̑h₂h₁-i̯é/ó- <*k̑h₂d-i̯é/ó- (alternatively *k̑d-(h₁)i̯é/ó-).

<!-- page: 192 -->

Finally, the Brittonic words for ‘to fall’, W cwyddo, Bret. kouezhañ, were tentatively derived from a newly formed present *kād-i̯e/o- based on the non-present stem *kād-.
