# Chapter 5. The Heteroclite Noun for Well, Source


<!-- page: 115 -->

Chapter 5.

THE HETEROCLITE NOUN FOR ‘WELL, SOURCE’

§5.1

THE GERMANIC MATERIAL FOR THE WORD FOR ‘WELL,

SOURCE’

§5.1.1

GOTHIC

The hapax NOM.SG brunna [m, n-st.] translates Gr. πηγή ‘fountain, spring, well’ in Mk 5:29 (sa brunna bloþis izos ‘the fountain of her blood’ for ἡ πηγὴ τοῦ αἵματος αὐτῆς) in the Gothic bible. The passage in the Greek Vorlage uses the word πηγή to metaphorically describe the flow, flux, or stream of blood. Elsewhere in the bible the word πηγή has literal meaning, but these passages have regretfully not survived in Gothic. The Gothic form brunna may then translate ‘flow, flux’, i.e. the intended metaphorical meaning, but it is seems far more likely that the antecedent concept (or ‘source domain’ in modern terminology) of the metaphor itself was translated. The abundant cognates in the other Germanic dialects support the meaning ‘source, well, spring’, e.g. brunnr ‘source, well, spring, streaming water’, OHG brunno ‘source, well; streaming water’. Interestingly, Crimian Gothic has <brunna> glossing fons ‘fountain, spring’, securing the hapax Gothic form. The meaning of Lat. fons ‘fountain, spring’ and Gr. πηγή ‘fountain, spring, well’ indicates that the Gothic form meant ‘fountain, spring’. It remains uncertain whether brunna meant ‘source, well’ as well, although this seems likely. In all, Gothic brunna and Crimean Gothic brunna provide evidence for an East- Germanic masculine n-stem.

§5.1.2

OLD NORSE AND THE NORDIC DIALECTS

ON has the well-attested form brunnr ‘source, well, spring, streaming water’ [m, o-st.], with variant bruðr (cf. mannr ~ maðr ‘man’), cf. OSw. (SÖDE) brunder, brondir, bronder, brynnen, ODan. Brynd, etc. ‘source, well, spring’. The modern languages continue reflexes of ON brunnr without phonological or semantic difficulties, cf. Mod. Icel. brunnur


<!-- page: 116 -->

‘well, spring, source’, Danish brønd ‘source, well’, Swedish dialect (RIET) brunn, brónn ‘well, spring, source’, Älvd. (STEE) brunn [m.], etc. The ON word occurs in prose as well as in poetry, e.g. Hávamál 1113 DAT.SG urðarbrunni ‘well of Urðr’, corresponding to ǫl sp 198 DAT.SG urðar brunni ‘well of Urðr’, and ǫl sp 2810 DAT.SG Mímis brunni ‘well of Mímir’. It is curious, however, to find an ON masculine o-stem where all other Germanic dialects have a masculine n-stem. It is possible that the word was absorbed into the o-stems by analogy to the formally very similar unnr ‘wave’ [m, o-st.]. The associations of brunnr ‘well, spring, source’ as well as unnr ‘wave’ to (moving) water brings the words close semantically as well. From a Germanic point of view, the form does not pose any difficulties and can be connected to the common Germanic stem *brunn-. The prehistory of this stem is discussed in detail in §5.2.2, p.115121. In conclusion, then, ON has a masculine n-stem form brunnr ‘well, spring, source’, reflected in the later dialects as well.

§5.1.4

OLD DUTCH AND THE LATER FRANCONIAN DIALECTS

ODu. has the form brunno ‘well, source, stream’ [m, n-st.], cf. DAT.PL <brunnon> (in
<herren fan brunnon Israhelis> ‘from the sources [=originating] of Israel’, W.Ps. (ms. I)

ODu. thus points to brunno ‘well, source, stream’ [m, n-st.] with a metathesized
variant burne* (of which the gender is hard to ascertain) typically occurring in place names.

§5.1.6

OLD ENGLISH AND THE BRITISH DIALECTS

OE has the metathesized variants burne [f, n-st.], burna [m, n-st.] ‘a stream, river; bubbling water’, also occurring in place names e.g. B nstōw. The form brunna [m, n-st.] can be deduced from place names, e.g. Denisaesbrunna. §5.1.7

OLD FRISIAN AND THE FRISIAN DIALECTS

OFri. has burna ‘source, well’ [m, n-st.], which is the metathesized variant occurring predominantly in OE as well in most of the WG dialects. Mod.Fri. boarne continues OFri. burna directly. EFri. has (OUTZ) Brunn ‘source, well’ does not show metathesis and may be a loan from Danish or LG. The word is in modern languages typically replaced by other words meaning (or associated with) ‘source, spring, fountain, well’. For instance, Johannes Cadovius Müller (1691, KÖNI here) has soohde for ‘source, well’ (WFri. (WFT) saed) as well as putte for ‘draw well’ (cf. WFri. (WFT) pet, put).

§5.2.1.1

ANCIENT GREEK AND ARMENIAN

Greek continues a living heteroclite φρέᾱρ/φρέᾱτος [n, r/n-st.] ‘well, source, spring;
tank, cistern’, NOM.PL *φρέᾱτα (spelled φρείατα), contracted to φρητός (Egypt), etc. These
forms go back to older *φρέϝᾱρ < *φρηϝaρ (i.e. PGr. *ph ē  a ) by quantitative metathesis.
The proto-form *ph ē  a  can be derived from *bhréh₁ [unclear] continuing the strong PD1 stem (cf.
Beekes, 2010:1590). The Sanskrit material indicates a PD1 stem (cf. §5.2.1.2 below), but
since Greek has given up the root-gradation, the PD1 gradation type cannot be ascertained on
the basis of the Greek evidence alone. The Greek forms find an exact cognate in Arm. ałb w ,
ałbewr [r-st.] ‘source, well’, GEN/DAT.SG aɫber, ABL.SG y-aɫber-ē (cf. EDAIL:32f.). The word
is ubiquitous in the Armenian dialects, cf. Moks axpör, but also with aspiration häxpür, Nor
Bayazet haxpur, Ozim ha p‘ [unclear] , etc. EDAIL:32f. explains the initial aspiration from the
prepositional phrases such as ABL.SG y-aɫber-ē. The classical form Arm. ałb w , ałbewr
‘source, well’ derives from *bhréh₁ [unclear] , just like the Greek forms.

Greek provides evidence of a living heteroclite to an ossified strong stem, which points to a virtual stem *bhréh₁ [unclear] n-. This reconstruction is supported by the non-heteroclite ossified strong stem reflexes of *bhréh₁ [unclear] in Armenian.
