§7.1

SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION

In this thesis I have discussed the remnants of heteroclite r/n-stems in Germanic. Germanic continues a number of interesting reflexes of heteroclite nouns that sometimes offer a unique perspective on the Indo-European material. What is of particular interest is the partially parallel development of four heteroclite words: *péh₂ [unclear] /*ph₂ [unclear] n- ‘fire’, *séh₂ l /*sh₂ [unclear] n- ‘sun’, *bhréh₁ [unclear] /*bh h₁ [unclear] n- ‘well, source’, and *sk [unclear] h₁- /*skuh₁-én- ‘shelter, shed’. This development in itself suggests that the forms constituted a class unto themselves in Germanic. This class probably disintegrated not long before the break off of Gothic. Gothic is exceptional among the Germanic languages for continuing two living heteroclites: the word fon/fun-in- ‘fire’ and the word for ‘sun’ sauïl/sunn-. Especially the word for ‘sun’ sauïl/sunn- is unique and the only reflex among the Indo-European languages that truly continues a heteroclite paradigm.

In short, the development of the four heteroclites can be summarized in the following schema:

The parallel development from stage I to II shows the reinterpretation of heteroclite weak
stems as HD2 n-stems. In WG , however, the paradigm ‘fire’ was leveled to *f [unclear] r-. Both the
forms *f [unclear] r- and +skuen- generalized a raised vowel from the dative, so that the resulatant
forms *f  ir- and *skuin- could not be interpreted as n-stems. In Stage II, the collective weak
stem was inserted to satisfy Germanic morpheme structure constraints, because the structure
*Cnu-en- was not allowed. The new structure *Cnun-en- developed an early n-stem with
geminate from *brunn-en- and *sunn-(e)n- from the HD2 paradigm. As said, the parallel
development suggests that the forms constituted a class in later Germanic.
A hypothetical feature of this derivation is that the development relies on the law of
laryngeal loss, where *H > ∅ in the sequence *#CH  é-. It is interesting to find that the
sequence *#CH  é- had two distinct outcomes in Germanic, namely:

1. *#CH  é- > *#C  é- and was subsequently reinterpreted as *#C  -é-and remodeled as

*#C  C-é- in accordance to the Germanic minimal root constraints;
2. *#CH  é- > *#C  é- > *#C  i- (or *#CH  í- > *#C  í-) by raising before a high vowel

and this structure was retained.

For the first development the following relatively secure forms can be adduced:

1. PG *fun-en- < weak stem *ph₂ [unclear] n- (cf. ON funi, Go. fun-in-)
2. PG *sunn- < *sh₂ [unclear] n- (cf. ON sunna, Go. sunna, etc.)
3. PG *brunn- < *bh  h₂ [unclear] n- (cf. Go. brunna, etc.)

For the second development, the following relatively secure forms can be deduced (assuming
raising of *e > *i in Germanic):

1. PG *fuir < weak stem *ph₂ [unclear] n- (with leveling of the strong suffix *-r, cf. OHG

<uugir>, etc.)
2. PG *suin- < weak stem *sh₂ [unclear] n- (cf. ON syn or sýn)
3. PG *guim- < HD weak stem *  hh₂  -ém- (cf. OHG guimo, giumo ‘palate’)
4. (PG *skuin- < *skuh₁-én-, which did not have the environment *#CH  é-, but

which only retained the structure *#C é- with a raised vowel as did the other heteroclites in Germanic)

I conclude this thesis by providing an overview of relevant PIE gradation types and laws of
vowel lengthening, a broad formulation of the law of laryngeal loss, and a list of formulations
of relevant Germanic sound laws ordered in a relative chronology.
