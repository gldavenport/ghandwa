§2.1

THE HETEROCLITE INFLECTION

§2.1.1

FORMAL ANALYSIS OF HETEROCLITE INFLECTION

The typical shape of a PIE noun consiststed minimally of a root, R, plus an ending, E
(so called root nouns), but nouns mostly consisted of a root plus one or more suffixes, Sn,
(where n = 1 or more) and an ending. If we put this in a schema following Bammesberger
(1990) we get:

R + Sn + E

A peculiar phenomenon that occurs in some archaic neuter nouns is that the strong stem (forming the nominative and accusative case forms) employed a different suffix from the weak stem (forming the remaining oblique case forms). This type of inflection is called ‘heteroclite’. The heteroclite strong ending was *-∅, i.e. it was absent. The heteroclite formation can be schematized as follows (where Si ...):

N/A.SG
R + S1

OBL.SG  R + S2 + Eweak

The word for ‘heart’ *k [unclear] /*k [unclear] -  -ós, described on p.27 above, is thus a heteroclite noun
showing a strong stem with a ‘zero-suffix’ and a weak stem with the suffix *-  -. Formally,
then, the suffix S1 = ∅ and S2 = *-  -. However, there are different kinds of heteroclisy, using
different kinds of suffixes. I discuss the main types in the next paragraph.

§2.1.3

A NOTE ON THE GRADATION OF HETEROCLITE INFLECTION

The heteroclite inflection is found clearly functioning in Greek, Latin, and Indo-
Iranian, but in these languages the inflection was not as productive (if at all) as it was in
Hittite. The living inflection of Hittite is therefore of particular interest. Hittite may be able to
tell us something important about Indo-European gradation patterns of heteroclites. There are
principally two types of heteroclite r/n-stems in Hittite: PD ones and static ones (cf. HIL:131).
The PD paradigms, i.e. either PD1 or PD2, are discussed in §1.1.2, p.15, and the static ones
simply have R(é) in all forms and zero-grade everywhere else. The types can be illustrated
with the following examples: the PD word pa [unclear] ‘fire’ (for the etymology see §3.2.1.1, p.73)
and the static form mē [unclear] ‘period, time’ (cf. HIL:656f. to the root *m [unclear] h₂-).

§2.2

A SURVEY OF GERMANIC REFLEXES OF INHERITED

HETEROCLITES

In this section I provide a list and analysis of Germanic nouns that derive from old heteroclite r/n-stems. All nouns that are discussed here have lost the heteroclite inflection, but they often show traces of an earlier inflection. The reflexes that I discuss are the words for ‘day’ (§2.2.1), ‘water’ (§2.2.2), ‘udder’ (§2.2.3), ‘liver’ (§2.2.4), ‘excrement’ (§2.2.5), ‘spring’ (§2.2.6), ‘feather’ (§2.2.7), and ‘shelter, shed’ (§2.2.8).

§2.2.1

THE WORD FOR ‘DAY’

The proto-form PG *dagaz [m., o-st.] ‘period from sunrise until sunset’ can be deduced from a set of cognates from all Germanic languages, e.g. Go. dags ‘ἡμέρα’, ON dagr, OSw. dagher, OHG tag, tac, OS dag, OFri. ī, dei, OE dæg, etc. all meaning ‘period from sunrise until sunset, day’. The word for ‘day’ has recently been connected by Kloekhorst (fthc.b, see particularly p.2, note 8) to a root *dh [unclear] h- ‘to repeat itself (over and over again); a cycle’ that occurs in Lith. ã nas ‘many, often’, a nìn s ‘repeatedly’, a nùmas ‘frequency, multiplicitly’. This connection is very convincing and far more appealing than the received reconstruction to the root *dhegwh- ‘to burn’ (e.g. Orel, 2003:66). This connection is semantically not very fitting, for the sun may be said to burn, but not the day. In contrast, the semantic development from ‘to repeat itself (over and over again); cycle’ to the perpetually recurring ‘period from sunrise until sunset’ is very straightforward and attractive. Besides the form PG *dagaz, some forms occur with a long root vowel: Go. -dogs, ON œ [unclear] and œ n, and OE ō o -. First of all, the semantic connection between ‘shed, shelter’ and ‘rain shower’ is entirely unconvincing. This connection is especially objectionable since *skū - ‘rain shower’ can, despite De Vaan’s objections, easily be connected to the root *(s)k eh₁ -. De Vaan (1999:40f.) thinks that a connection between a root meaning ‘north’ to ‘shower’ is not likely. However, there is no good reason for the root *(s)k eh₁ - to have meant simply ‘north’. Instead, the root may very likely have meant ‘(harsh) northwestern wind’. Only Lith. š a [f.] meant simply ‘north’, whereas all other cognates clearly have connotations to harsh, cold wind, rain or storm. This recurring sense must have been original. Furthermore, De Vaan objects that the western wind is far more likely to bring bad weather than the northern wind and that therefore the root, allegedly meaning ‘north’, cannot be related. However, this objection dissolves when we reconstruct an original meaning ‘northwestern wind’, as found in Lat. caurus ‘northwestern wind’. The meaning ‘(harsh) northwestern wind’ developed in the Balto-Slavic to ‘(harsh) northern wind’ (and only secondarily to ‘north’) and in Germanic into the ‘(harsh) western wind’ that naturally brings bad weather, and then to ‘rain shower’. The original meaning of the root *(s)keh₁u- must therefore have been ‘(harsh) northwestern wind’. The root can *(s)keh₁u- semantically at least be easily connected to the Germanic form.

Secondly, the form *skū - ‘shed, shelter’ must be formally strictly separated from the word *skū - ‘rain shower’ as a homonym. De Vaan’s assumption of secondary ablaut of long *ū replacing the expected zero-grade *skur- (a phenomenon that is very poorly understood in itself) in *skū - ‘rain shower’ is not very appealing. Furthermore, the meaning of *skū - ‘rain shower’ can semantically hardly be connected to the root *sker- ‘to cut, crack’, which requires an enormous leap. The word *skū - ‘rain shower’ must therefore be connected to *(s)keh₁u- ‘(harsh) northwestern wind’.
