Introduction The linguistic status of the Indo-European languages of the Iberian Peninsula is a complex issue. Only in the west, at least three different IndoEuropean layers have been identified so far, commonly known as Celtic, Lusitanian and ‘Alteuropäisch’. Several scholars believe, however, there has been only one Indo-European layer in Hispania, specifically a Celtic one. In many works, e.g., LRP, I have claimed Lusitanian is not only non-Celtic, but much closer to Italic, in that the terms of comparison of the Lusitanian lexicon are often found in the Italic languages. Unfortunately, many forms are ambiguous, either because they can be accorded different etymologies, or because the likeliest etymology may be Celtic, albeit compatible with other possible dialectal ascriptions. In my experience, new findings and new interpretations in the last ten years now permit refining this assertion from a geographical point of view.

Although in terms of ethnicity matters were very confused all over the continent before the rise of romantic nationalism, and in spite of linguistic differences often being blurred by people travelling and often dying far from their homes, whereby PNs can be found virtually anywhere, I am increasingly inclined to believe some natural or political borders can be matched with dialectal borders. The Callaecia Lucensis, northern Asturia and the area of the Cantabri, besides the south-western Baeturia Celtica, appear to be mostly Celticspeaking. The Vettones and southern Astures are very mixed, but western Lusitania and the Callaecia Bracarensis, which more or less coincide with today’s Portugal, have yielded a great deal of evidence that is either incompatible with Celtic (at least as usually reconstructed so far) or simply reveals traits that are neither Celtic nor definitely non-Celtic, but are unknown anywhere else in the Keltiké. All this suggests that Lusitanians had penetrated the west of Hispania and possibly remained in a vast cul-de-sac under the pressure of the Celts, partially making their way to the south, but probably partly traversing the

Two Lusitanian personal names: PAVGENDA, TVRENDI The feminine PN PAVGENDA is indigenous, to judge from its distribution and context. Not many etymologies may account for it: the only reasonable cognate is Lat. paucus “little”. The phonetic side is not a problem: /p/ is preserved because the name is not Celtic, and /k/ is regularly voiced between vowels. The suffix -end- indicates this is a verbal form: It looks like a present participle with voiced result of the dental -t-, but the vowel -e- poses some questions. If the stem is a primary adjective *pau̯ ko-, identical to Lat. paucus “small” > “few in number” from IE *peH2u-ko(attested only in Latin with this suffix!), the suffix -nt- requires the whole form to be considered as the present participle of a stative deadjectival formation. This type is characterized by a suffix *-eH1-, which is often further enlarged by the common thematic suffix -i̯e/o-, especially in Italic and Celtic. PAVGENDA goes back to *pau̯ k-ē-(i̯o)-nt- “being small”. Clear instances of this formation are the Latin verbs seneō “I am old”, albeō “I am white”, and, for onomastics, the

antonymic PN Magnentius, and some PlNs of good omen like Florentia. Of course PAVGENDA is a derogatory name2.

The relationship of PAVGENDA and another PN in -end- TVRENDI (gen. sing., Castelo Branco, AE 1984, 478) to the Celtib. PN Turenta (K.1.3) is not completely clear. We have to allow for the possibility that the cluster -nd- is not original, but due to a tendency of -nn- towards dissimilation, like APLONDVS (Madrid, Badajoz) for APLONVS; CAESARDIA (León) beside CAESARRIA (Burgos, due to secondary gemination and resyllabification in *r.i̯- > -r.ri̯-), and in the Romance languages Sp. izquierda “left” (< Basque ezkerra), ardilla “squirrel”, OSp. libeldo (< libellum), Sp. celda (< cella), bula/bulda (< bulla), and in Aragonese marrano/mardano “lamb”, barro/bardo “mud”3. Occasionally, the dissimilation may be due to the belief, on the part of the scribe, that the segment -rr-, -nn- in foreign mouths is a corruption of the ‘correct’ -rd-, and -nd-. If this is right, perhaps TVRENDI and Turenta stand for Turenno-, in turn attested in western Hispania. Nevertheless, there is a chance these forms are simply, like Lat. Magnentius, the antonyms of PAVGENDA. An adjective *tūro- with the meaning “strong” from IE *tuH-ro- “swelling, inflating” is well known in Celtic and specifically in HC., where we find superlative forms like TVRAESAMVS (Vitoria), TVROLVS (western Hispania), PlNs like Turobriga (Cáceres), beside the Gaulish EN Turones. This adjective is widespread, as shown by OI. tura-, etc. (IEW: 1083), and especially frequent in onomastics. It is indirectly attested in Latin in the deadjectival verb ob-tūrāre “to block, obstruct”. Consequently, the western forms could be Lusitanian and not Celtic. The phonetic changes involved are far from specific. The features favouring a Lusitanian ascription are, on the one hand, the semantic match of PAVGENDA and TVRENDI and, on the other, the formational match with the Latin names in -entius, -a. Semantic paradigms and the appurtenance of compounded personal names to Celtic and non-Celtic dialects: The CORO- compounds and related forms

A number of compounded PNs in coro- are attested in the southern Callaecia Bracarensis and in Lusitania, not far from the places where the Lusitanian inscriptions were found. Only one, COROTVRETIS, was borne by a man from the Callaecia Lucensis. These PNs have several things in common: most importantly, the first member is *koro-. I have contended (B. M. Prósper: 2004) that this is the Indo-European word for “army” as attested in Iranian (OPers. kāra-), Baltic (Lith. kãras) and in Greek glosses (for Hsch. kovroς, cf. E. Kaczyńska 2007). Paradoxically, it is probably post-Anatolian (unless Hitt. mān “when, just as” is related) and virtually absent from Indo-Iranian, and it reflects a language stage with prepositions, not postpositions, as is deemed consistent with a SOV language like Indo-European. When it comes to its actual distribution, one wonders why *me- has been lost everywhere except in compounds. The meaning of *me- and that of its compounds, on the other hand, does not have to coincide fully. *me- may have had a purely locative meaning like “on” or “at”, not “until”, as often implied, and it seems to have frequently governed locative, not directive forms, which explains *me-t-i, *me-t-u, *me-bdi, *me-ĝhsr-i, probably *me-dhi in *medhi̯o- “middle”, and perhaps even the Greek introductory particle mevn and the Tocharian forms like mant “thus”. NPhryg. me in the formula me zemelwı ke dewı ke “among men and gods” may be the apocopated result of *met(a). The second term of *me-tḤ2 in Gk. metav may be the collective form of the anaphoric pronoun *so/to, which would mean that *me had originally a directional as well as a locative sense. However, note that this is the safest example of *me-tḤ2 that we have, so it could be formed on other adverbs in -α, for instance on katav from *ḱṃ-tḤ2, and then only the locatival variants *me-t-i, *me-t-u would be original. Anyway, one may well doubt whether this may be termed a preposition at all; it might be the oldest non-Anatolian remains of the scheme analyzed by F. Starke, 1977 as a “partitive apposition”, according to which the first element would be a noun-based adverbial and the second a noun that restricts its general meaning, e.g., Hitt. anda parna (directional) “to the inside, to the house = into the house”. Indeed, the alleged preposition *me was not proclitic, since it triggered the zero grade in the governed noun or pronoun, but this possibility looks far-fetched as long as *me resists internal analysis. V. Sadovski (2000) has studied the patterns of prepositional compounds in Sanskrit and has come to interesting conclusions concerning the differences between the hypostatic and the e[nqeoς subtypes. Syntactically, this latter type is an exocentric compound whose first term is an adverb, not a preposition: “having a god inside”. The hypostatic subtype is characterized by suffixation and bears the accent in the governed noun or the suffix, and this latter case may entail zero grade of the noun, whereas the e[nqeoς subtype mostly needs no suffix and, crucially, it is the first term, namely, the adverb, that is accented. If the noun is capable of Ablaut, its vocalism may be reduced as a consequence. Interestingly, Sadovski translates Gk. provcnu, OI. prajñu as an e[nqeoς “dessen Knie nach vorne gestreckt sind”. For him, the suffixless hypostatic class as a whole may have come into being through a reanalysis of the e[nqeoς type. This may be so with some of the cases reviewed above, for instance *pér-uti “(in) the year (that is) before”, which would provide the adverbial basis for an exocentric e[nqeoς compound *pér-uti̯-o- that would acquire a relational, not possessive meaning “one being in, or related to, the year before”.

The me- subtype is exocentric in so far as it receives thematic inflection, as in *medhi̯o- and *mebd(i)i̯o-, and no sense can be extracted from it if analyzed as an e[nqeoς set, unless we are ready to explore the remote possibility that *meis an adnominal or compound variant of the first person singular pronoun nom. *H1egh- // acc.-gen.-dat. *me- (the full adnominal variant is usually reconstructed as *(e)-me-ne as in Hitt. ammel, Av. mana, etc.).14 In that case, the compounded adverbials *me-bd-i, *me-ĝhsr-i, *me-sĝhu- would originally mean “at my foot”, “at my hand”, “within my power”, that is, “close” (which is the actual meaning of Arm. merǰ). This would at least account for the fact that the origin and structure of compositional *me is otherwise a mystery, completely alien to Anatolian and never preserved as a preverb15. On the more traditional view that all the me- forms are prepositional governing compounds proper, the construction of the adjective underlying the PN Mebdius can be described in two alternative ways: a) it is a hypostatical compound “one who is on foot”, based on a prepositional phrase by addition of the thematic vowel or the thematic suffix -i̯oin the oldest cases (Gk. katacqovnioς “dwelling under earth”, Lat. subiugius “being under the yoke”, OI. ántaspatha- “found on the way”), but some languages attest to the suffixless and comparatively rare variant, as OI. abhi-dyu“that moves towards the sky”.

b) perhaps less probably, its base *mebdi, no longer a preposition but an adverb, could no longer be analyzed and consequently became understood as the locative of an inexistent athematic stem *mebd- (cf. IE *Honer-i “in a dream” → *Honeri̯-o- > Gk. o[neiroς “vision”). *mebd(i)i̯o- would simply be a delocatival derivative16.

From a semantic point of view, *mebd(i)i̯o- was probably a way of designating a warrior who stands on foot in front of the enemy. The Callaecian form thus conveys more or less the same meaning as Gk. pezovς < *pedi̯os “(one who is) on foot”, itself probably based on a locative *pedi- (cf. A. J. Nussbaum, 1986: 286).
