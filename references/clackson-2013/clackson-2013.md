# Subgrouping in the Sabellian Branch of Indo-European

James Clackson  
University of Cambridge

## Abstract

The Sabellian branch of the Italic languages of Indo-European comprises Oscan, Umbrian, South Picene and a number of smaller varieties. This paper analyses the current model for subgrouping these languages and gives detailed consideration to a number of phonological and morphological isoglosses. The publication of a new corpus of the Sabellian inscriptions allows a better understanding of the relative chronology for language change in the Sabellian branch, and this leads to a new perspective on the interrelations between the Sabellian languages.

## 1. INTRODUCTION

The last fifty years have seen dramatic advances in our knowledge about the Sabellian branch of the Indo-European language family.[^1] In 1973 three new sixth-century inscriptions were discovered at Penna Sant’Andrea, in the Italian province of Abruzzo, employing a script and language which are now known as South Picene. Marinetti’s (1995) publication of these and twenty other previously known South Picene inscriptions, which could now be securely read and interpreted, gave scholars access to the earliest stages of the languages of what is now called the Sabellian group. There have been other important finds in the last fifty years, for example, the sixth-/fifth-century legal text from Tortora in Calabria in a hitherto unattested Sabellian variety (Lazzarini & Poccetti 2001), and numerous other shorter texts. There have been two major editions of the Sabellian texts this century (Rix 2002; Crawford et al. 2011). The new material has led to numerous articles and books dedicated to the Sabellian languages, including major studies of the phonology, morphology and lexicon.[^2] However, despite these discoveries, reconstructing the detailed family tree of the Italic languages has remained controversial. Although scholars agree that it is possible to identify a Sabellian branch of the Italic subgroup of Indo-European, comprising South-Picene, Oscan, Umbrian and the ‘minor dialects’ of central Italy (see further below for these languages), there is still uncertainty about the internal relationships within Sabellian (see for example, Coleman 1986; Meiser 1987a; 1996; Adiego Lajara 1992; 1993; Rix 2003; 2009; Wallace 1984; 2007). In this paper I aim to re-examine the question of the interrelationships within the Sabellian branch, taking advantage of the most recent edition of the texts (Crawford et al. 2011) which gives more information on the dating and archaeological context of the texts themselves and which, for most inscriptions, provides a photograph or drawing to allow the user to check the reading. However, it will be in order to make some general methodological remarks about linguistic subgrouping as a preface to this exercise.

## 2. LINGUISTIC SUBGROUPING

In the past decade, linguistic subgrouping has become a debated topic of linguistic research, principally owing to the importation of computer modelling algorithms and multidimensional scaling software from the biological sciences. Although data-based comparison of languages was by no means unheard of before the twenty-first century (see Kroeber & Chrétien 1937 for a notable early example of quantitative comparison), it was only with the ground-breaking work of Ringe et al. 2002, that a suitably sophisticated computer model, combined with the input of accurate data, was able to meet with general approval from the Indo-European linguistic community.[^3] Others have used different datasets,[^4] or different modelling programmes in attempts to refine the cladistics of the Indo-European tree.[^5] However, although the results of computational methods of cladistics are impressive, it is worth emphasising that the underlying methodology is not new. As Ringe et al. stress, their computational approach ‘is not intended to replace already existing methods, but to supplement them’ (2002: 66, emphasis in the original). The existing methods are founded on the premise that if two or more languages or dialects have undergone the same shared linguistic innovations, not found in other members of the family, then this can be an indication that they once constituted a linguistic unity, and hence that they should be treated as a separate subgroup of the family. Ringe et al. (2002: 66–69) point out, as others have done,[^6] that there are inherent dangers in the search for shared innovations. Apparent innovatory sound changes may represent independent but parallel processes; morphological and lexical innovations, which generally have less chance to be completely parallel, can sometimes be difficult to spot as innovations, rather than shared inheritances, or selections from a set of variables in the parent language. Sound changes are often the easiest innovations to identify, but changes such as palatalisation, monophthongisation or lenition are crosslinguistically common and thus are not necessarily diagnostic for subgrouping. The assessment of more unusual sound changes may also present challenges for the researcher. Consider, for example, the development of voiced aspirated consonants within the Indo-European language family: these sounds are rare cross-linguistically, and undergo a range of different developments within the Indo-European languages, and the significance accorded to a shared specific development of voiced aspirated consonants in any given case is partly a matter of the researcher’s judgement.[^7] In order to refine the existing model, Ringe et al. (2002: 70) note that ‘in any area of linguistic structure in which categorical distinctions are made BACKMUTATION is either impossible or vanishingly rare’. By ‘backmutation’ they mean the reversal of a change that has taken place, and this statement thus encompasses phenomena such as the irreversibility of phoneme merger, or the fact that once a morphological formant is replaced or lost, the same formant is not usually recreated. By this consideration, it is possible to detect which changes appear to be shared, but are in fact not shared, by seeing if the languages involved have already diverged on some other feature. In practice, Ringe et al. searched for the best tree for Indo-European by amassing a large set of characters representing phonological and morphological changes and lexical choices made by the daughter languages,[^8] and comparing how this data sat within all the possibly constructed trees of the family. Although Ringe et al. (2002) included Oscan and Umbrian, as well as Latin, in their work on Indo-European, the nature of the Italic languages unfortunately does not lend itself to a computational cladistic treatment, since in many of the languages the evidence is so scanty that the data required are too often missing. However, the question of subgrouping within the Italic branch does have an advantage over the grouping of the whole Indo-European family, in that the nature of the evidence allows the researcher a better chance of settling the question of whether one change took place before or after another, either through absolute chronology, that is the dating of individual texts through archaeological or epigraphic evidence; or through elucidating the relative chronology of changes. Ringe et al. (2002) do not explicitly mention relative or absolute chronology, but their methodology is, in effect, a method of picking out the best possible relative chronology among a mass of different developments. In the case of the Sabellian languages, since the changes involved are largely well known, have mostly been closely studied, and the evidence for them is attested in inscriptions which can usually be dated to within a fairly narrow time-range on archaeological grounds, we have a better chance of elucidating relative and absolute chronologies for changes than we do for many other areas of the Indo-European family.[^9] Recent years have also seen the proposal of a different model for Indo-European subgrouping, which aims to get away from the traditional wave and tree models. In a paper published in 1999 Andrew Garrett sketched out some of the challenges to the traditional model of a branching Indo-European tree that had been posed by improved knowledge particularly of the Greek and Celtic branches of Indo-European.[^10] The decipherment of Linear B in 1952 gave scholars access to a variety of Greek, Mycenaean Greek, from at least 500 years earlier than any previously attested Greek inscription. Mycenaean Greek already shows the development of features that are diagnostic in the classification of later Greek dialects, such as the development of *ti to si, yet all post-Mycenaean Greek dialects have also undergone shared innovations. Indeed, many of the innovations which were thought to be diagnostic for the Greek branch of Indo-European by Meillet (1913) turn out to be demonstrably post-Mycenean (Garrett 2006: 140). Similarly, for Celtic, the loss of *p, which had been held to be diagnostic for the subgroup, may still be in progress in the earliest Lepontic inscriptions (Garrett 1999: 150). Garrett is not the first to notice that shared innovations may take place over a dialect continuum, but he goes further than others have done in the conclusions which he draws. Emphasising how many varieties of Indo-European have been lost or are unknown, particularly in the earliest stages of the spread of the language family, Garrett proposes that the subgroups constructed for Indo-European may reflect a series of later convergences between related, but already differentiated dialects. The varieties which we reconstruct as forming a unity in the first millennium BC may have stood in a different relationship a thousand years earlier. To take an example from Greek again (Garrett 2006: 142), the Greek branch comprises both varieties which have an active verbal ending for 1PL -mes (in West Greek) and those which have -men (East Greek). This is not a diagnostic feature for Greek, but it is possible that this reflects a dialectal split at a much earlier stage, since *-mes is close to the ending reconstructed for Latin (*-mos), and *-men to that found in Anatolian languages (*-wen). At an earlier stage in pre-history the *-mes / *-men divide could have been significant for a very different grouping of Indo-European subgroups. Garrett concluded that this meant that Indo-Europeanists and historical linguists in general should be more wary of constructing subgroups, and more open to the idea that apparent subgroups may have arisen through convergence of closely related varieties. Few Indo-Europeanists have been willing to accept Garrett’s arguments for scepticism about subgrouping,[^11] and most have continued to operate with a branching-tree model, with shared innovations as the diagnostic for the construction of a subgroup (for example Weiss 2009: 465–476). The objections to Garrett’s proposals are founded on a reluctance to give weight to the ‘unknown unknowns’, that is the unrecorded Indo-European varieties which gave way to the languages which we know about, and which may have formed a bridge between what we now think of as different Indo-European subgroups.[^12] These varieties doubtless existed, but we do not know how they would have changed our picture of Proto-Indo-European (PIE) as a whole, or indeed what they would have been like; subgroups should be constructed on the basis of the evidence of what we have. Moreover, so far there has been no conclusive demonstration of an Indo-European subgroup that has actually arisen through later convergence. This is an entirely pragmatic approach, but it seems to me important that one should not lose sight of Garrett’s arguments about the possibility of linguistic convergence. If subgroups are constructed on the basis of what are in fact later convergences, then a false positive has been created, and this may have detrimental effects not only on the study of the languages themselves, but also on the conclusions drawn from linguistic data by associated disciplines, such as archaeology. In the discussion of the subgroups that follow, I attempt wherever possible to find isoglosses between the Sabellian languages that represent common innovations, and assess these on the basis of the family-tree model. I take as the working hypothesis the subgroups for the Sabellian branch of Italic that have most recently been proposed, and then examine the shared innovations in terms of absolute and relative chronology in order to see whether any of the shared innovations are in fact the result of convergence. In general in what follows I work down from the larger linguistic unit to the smaller, giving the isoglosses in turn.

## 3. THE SUBGROUPING OF SABELLIAN

The Sabellian branch of Italic comprises Oscan, Umbrian and South Picene as well as a number of other minor dialects or languages. In the influential edition of texts made by Rix in 2002 these languages are divided into three separate branches.[^13] A northern group, comprising Umbrian, a language well attested in the celebrated Iguvine tables, together with the scanty linguistic remains of the peoples known to the Romans as the Volsci, Aequiculi, Sabini, and Marsi.[^14] The Iguvine tables date from the third to the first century BC, but there are five inscriptions from the seventh and sixth centuries BC, found in the Tiber valley or as far west as Tolfa, in what is normally taken to be Etruscan territory at this date, which Rix terms ‘paläoumbrisch’ (ST. Um 2 = Imag. Ital. Forum Novum 2, ST Um 3 = Imag. Ital. Forum Novum 1, ST Um 4 = Imag. Ital. Caere 1, ST Um 40 = Imag. Ital. Sabini 1 and ST Um 41 = Imag. Ital. Capena 1), and one text from fifth-century Satricum which is termed ‘paläovolskisch’ (ST VM 1 = Imag. Ital. Satricum 1). A second group is labelled the South Picene group, and comprises both the texts from central Italy written in versions of the South Picene script, and twenty texts from southern parts of the peninsula, which Rix labels as ‘präsamnitisch’,[^15] among which is included the stele from Tortora in what is now Calabria (ST Ps 20 = Imag. Ital. Blanda 1, henceforward referred to as ‘the Tortora stele’). Two of the shortest South Picene texts, ST Sp BO 1 = Imag. Ital. Interpromium B and ST Sp BA 1 = Imag. Ital. Interpromium A, both written on helmets, are dated by Rix to the third century (see however now Crawford et al. 2011: 12–13 for arguments for a date around 400 BC); otherwise all of the inscriptions put in this group date to earlier than 400 BC. Rix’s final group of Sabellian languages is called the Oscan group, and comprises texts written in the distinctive Oscan alphabet from central and southern Italy together with the three varieties already characterised by Conway (1897: 233-4) as ‘North Oscan’[^16]: Paelignian (known from over fifty, mostly short, inscriptions), Marrucinian and Vestinian. Nearly all of the inscriptions in the Oscan group date from after 400 BC, although a couple of texts labelled ‘paleohernikisch’ are included in Rix (2002), ST He 1 = Imag. Ital. Anagnia 1 and ST He 2 = Imag. Ital. Anagnia 13.[^17] Rix spells out the reasons behind this subgrouping model most clearly in Rix (2003), assigning putative dates to the prehistoric language splits.[^18] I shall consider the linguistic isoglosses in favour of this model proposed both by Meiser (1996) and Rix (2003) in more detail below, but first it will be helpful to set out the general background. Rix explains the diversification of the Sabellian languages over Italy as a result of a series of small migrations and colonialisations, explicitly linking Roman reports of a practice known as the ‘Sacred Spring’, uer sacrum (Rix 2003: 163–164, 168–170; Rix 2009: 249, 262–263).[^19] The Sacred Spring, as reported by Festus (p. 379 in Lindsay 1913) and others, was the custom that all the men and animals born to a community in one year were declared sacred to a god and either sacrificed (the animals) or sent out to colonialise a new area of land (the men).[^20] By Rix’s model ‘Proto-Sabellians’ first stopped in Umbria, from where there were migrations southwards, through successive Sacred Springs. In this way, Rix hypothesises, the language family split into the three groups given above: Umbrian, South Picene and Oscan. The South Picene speakers separated early from the other groups and migrated southward, first to the central Apennine region and then in the eighth and seventh centuries (already with a knowledge of writing), they moved further south, hence explaining similarities both of language and (apparently) script between South Picene and the ‘Pre-Samnite’ inscriptions (Rix 2003: 168).[^21] Both the South Picene-speaking communities in central Italy, and the southern speakers of Pre-Samnite were subsequently replaced by Oscan speakers, whose southward migration is associated with a series of military victories in the fifth and fourth centuries BC, as reported by Greek and Roman historians. However, occasionally Oscan inscriptions in the south, or the ‘North Oscan’ varieties of central Italy betray traces of the ‘South Picene’ style substrate, such as in their name formulae (Rix 2003: 159 n. 31; see below on this supposed onomastic evidence).

### 3.1. Sabellian isoglosses

Before we look more closely at the isoglosses that have been given for the three different Sabellian language groups, it will be helpful to list features that all Sabellian languages share. Note that some of these features are neither innovations with respect to Indo-European, nor necessarily distinctive from other Indo-European languages, but they do mostly serve to differentiate the Sabellian languages as a group from other Indo-European languages of Italy (Latin, Faliscan, Venetic, Lepontic, Cisalpine Gaulish and Messapic, and probably also Sicel). Of the isoglosses given below, three are phonological,[^22] and five morphological. The list below differs in several respects from that given by Rix (2003: 156–157), who does not give any phonological isoglosses and who also includes isoglosses relating to the verb (the formation of the future and perfect), which cannot be shown conclusively from all three of his branches of Sabellian.[^23]

#### 3.1.1. (S 1) Development of inherited ‘voiced aspirates’

*dʰ and *bʰ > f in medial syllable, as in South Picene mefiín ‘middle’ < *medʰyo-, rufra ‘red’ < *rudʰro-,[^24] Oscan mefiaí ‘middle’ Umbrian rufru ‘red’ (Adiego Lajara 1992: 12; Weiss 2009: 467 n. 11);[^25]

#### 3.1.2. (S 2) Development of inherited labiovelars to labials

*kw > p in South Picene suaipis, Oscan suaepis, Umbrian svepis all ‘if anyone’ < *swai kwis (Adiego Lajara 1992: 12; Wallace 2007: 5; Weiss 2009: 467 n. 11);

#### 3.1.3. (S 3) Development of final *-n(t)s > -f

South Picene nerf ‘men’ (ACC.PL), Umbrian nerf ‘men’ (accusative plural) both < *nerṃs via *nerens, Umbrian zeřef ‘sitting’ < *sedēnt-s, Oscan staef ‘standing’ < *stā-yent-s (Weiss 2009: 467 n. 11);[^26]

#### 3.1.4. (S 4) Syncope of *o, *e or *i before *s in the final syllable of polysyllabic words

South Picene meitims < *meitimos, possibly ‘gift’, cf. ACC.SG meitimúm; Oscan húrz < *gʰortos ‘enclosure’; Umbrian meřs ‘law’ < *medos (Adiego Lajara 1992: 12, Wallace 2007: 4). Oscan and Umbrian also show abundant evidence for syncope in medial syllables, but in Umbrian medial syncope appears to have followed the development of intervocalic *d to a fricative consonant represented with ř in the native alphabet, rs in the Latin alphabet (Meiser 1986: 131), as the imperative teřtu/dirstu ‘let him give’ < *didetōd.[^27] The evidence in South Picene and other early dialects is inconclusive: a possible example of failure of medial syncope in South Picene is opesa[ ]úom (ST Sp RI 1 = Imag. Ital. Cures 1) if this does derive from the verbal stem *opesā- ‘to work, to make’ which gives *opsa- in Oscan (e.g. the gerundive úpsannúm) and osa- in Umbrian (e.g. imperative osatu);[^28]

#### 3.1.5. (S 5) Accusative singular in *-om for consonant-stem nouns

Oscan bratōm (in Greek alphabet) ‘thanks’,[^29] Umbrian uhturu ‘name of a magistrate’, and South Picene aúdaqum (read aúdaqúm by Rix, ST Sp AP 1) (Wallace 2007: 4). Unfortunately, aúdaqum is the only possible example of a consonant stem accusative in South Picene, and its status as such relies on the comparison to Latin audax (Untermann 2000: 135). However, Latin audax itself derives from a stem auid- (de Vaan 2008a: 60–61), and contains a Latin adjective suffix -ido- which most probably derives from PIE *-idʰo- (Nussbaum 1999); in South Picene, medial *-dʰ- develops to -f-, and hence the connection of aúdaqum and audax is uncertain;

#### 3.1.6. (S 6) Genitive singular in *-eis for o-stem nouns

South Picene kaúeis ‘of Gaius’, Oscan sakarakleís ‘of the temple’; Umbrian katles ‘of the puppy’ (with monophthongisation of *ei to e) (Rix 2003: 156; Adiego Lajara 1992: 12; Wallace 2007: 4; Weiss 2009: 467 n. 11);

#### 3.1.7. (S 7) Nominative plural of o-stem nominal and pronominal declensions

Nominative plural of o-stem nominal and pronominal declensions in *-ōs, of feminine ā-stem nominal and pronominal declensions in *-ās: South Picene safinús ‘Sabines’ (o-stem noun) estas ‘these’ (ā-stem pronoun); Oscan núvlanús ‘Nolani’ < *-ānōs, pús ‘who’ < *kwōs, aasas ‘altars’ < *āsās, pas ‘who’ < *kwās; Umbrian ikuvinus ‘Iguvines’, pure ‘who’ < *kwōs +i, iuengar ‘heifers’ (the last two forms showing Umbrian rhotacism);[^30]

#### 3.1.8. (S 8) Personal pronouns

These also appear to have innovated a new accusative case form of the structure in *Cēom,[^31] with initial consonant *m- (first person), *t- (second person) or *s- (third person): South Picene tíom ‘you’; Oscan siom ‘himself’; Umbrian tiom ‘you’; and ‘Palaeoumbrian’ míom ‘me’ (Wallace 2007: 4);

#### 3.1.9. (S 9) Formation of a present infinitive through a suffix *-om

South Picene opesa[ ]úom ‘to work’;[^32] Oscan ezum ‘to be’; Umbrian erom ‘to be’ (Rix 2003: 156).

It is more difficult to add to this list of features a number of exclusive lexical isoglosses which are found within the Sabellian languages alone, since the nature of the inscriptions means that we only have a small number of words attested across South Picene, Oscan and Umbrian. Adiego Lajara (1992: 12) sees the South Picene lexemes nír ‘man, chief’, toúta ‘community’ and okreí ‘citadel’ to be indicative of the Sabellian nature of South Picene; however, none of these is a lexical isogloss exclusive to Sabellian, since they all also occur in other branches of Indo-European (see their respective entries in Untermann 2000).

### 3.2. Features of the South Picene and Umbrian groups

Following the Meiser-Rix model, the need to separate out three distinct groups within the Sabellian languages is underscored by a series of linguistic isoglosses. The South Picene group is separated from the Umbrian group by the smallest number of isoglosses. For this reason some scholars, for example Marinetti (1985: 41–42) and Meiser in earlier work (1987a; see also 2003: 29) have been reluctant to make a hard and fast separation between the two groups. The features uniting these two groups (following Meiser 1996: 187–190 and Rix 2003: 160–161; 2009) comprise five phonological isoglosses, one lexical and one onomastic isogloss. As we shall see, however, these isoglosses are not always spread evenly through all the varieties belonging to each group, and we shall examine these changes in more detail than the earlier isoglosses.

#### 3.2.1. (SP/U 1) Change of initial *l > w

This change occurs in South Picene vepetín ‘in the stone (tomb)’ < *lepet- and Umbrian vapere ‘on the stone’ < *laped- (Rix 2009: 260).[^33] However, the change does not seem to have occurred in the Pre-Samnite texts (as shown for example by the personal name Luvcies (in ST Ps 13 = Imag. Ital. Nola 13) where Umbrian has Vuvçis, both from *leukio-). The minor varieties associated with the Umbrian group do not show any clear evidence for this change.[^34] Indeed, if Rix is right (1992: 38) to read iúkúh:ko and interpret ‘in the grove’ < Proto-Sabellian *loukōd- kom in the ‘Palaeovolscian’ text from Satricum, ST VM 1 (now read and interpreted differently at Imag. Ital. Satricum 1), this text shows a different development of initial *l. The same change of *l > *y- appears also to have taken place in the South Picene text ST Sp CH 1 = Imag. Ital. Anxanum 1, which writes iepeten, apparently the same word as vepetín (compare the phrase iepeten esmen on ST Sp CH 1 = Imag. Ital. Anxanum 1 with esmín … vepetín (ST Sp MC 1 = Imag. Ital. Urbs Salvia 1), esmín uepetín (ST Sp MC 2 = Imag. Ital. Urbs Salvia 2) and esmen vepeten (ST Sp TE 2 = Imag. Ital. Interamnia Praetuttiorum 4)).

#### 3.2.2. (SP/U 2) Monophthongisation of the diphthongs *ei and *ou (including *ou from earlier *eu) (Rix 2003: 160; 2009: 258–259)

The monophthongisation of *ei to a vowel written e/e is universal in Umbrian, but in South Picene and Pre-Samnite takes place only in the final syllable of the word, and there only sporadically. In absolute final position in South Picene, original *-ei is continued by -eí in possibly three texts (ST Sp TE 7 = Imag. Ital. Interamnia Praetuttiorum 3 and the fragmentary ST Sp AQ 2 = Imag. Ital. Aufinum 1 and ST Sp RI 1 = Imag. Ital. Cures 1), -eíh in one (ST Sp AP 2 = Imag. Ital. Asculum Picenum 2), and -eh in two others (ST Sp CH 2 = Imag. Ital. Interpromium 1 and ST Sp AQ 1 = Imag. Ital. Superaequum 1, assuming, with Adiego Lajara 1992: 77 that in the latter text k]aúieh ‘to Gaius’ is derived from *-iōi via *-iei). The marker of the genitive singular of o-stem names is written -eis in ST Sp AQ 1 = Imag. Ital. Superaequum 1 in the phrase k]aúieh: kaúieis: puqloh ‘to Gaius son of Gaius’ (note the apparent monophthongisation in k]aúieh), but elsewhere -es.[^35] Pre-Samnite texts show an even messier picture: the Tortora stele, written in the Greek alphabet, (ST Ps 20 = Imag. Ital. Blanda 1) has one word ending -ei (esei), and one possible GEN.SG in -es (amates), although the fragmentary context and lack of any clear etymology means that the interpretation of this word is completely uncertain. The other Pre-Samnite texts have only one example of an original final *-ei, venilei in ST Ps 3 = Imag. Ital. Capua 35,[^36] but fifteen other texts have likely examples of o-stem or consonant stem genitive singulars (all in name formulae).[^37] These texts are written in either the Etruscan alphabet, the Greek alphabet or the so-called ‘alphabet of Nocera,’ limited to the south of the Bay of Naples, but there is no correlation between choice of alphabet, or geographical find-spot and genitive ending. Five of the texts show a genitive ending -eis (one in the Noceran alphabet, the others in the Etruscan alphabet), and the other ten -es. There are also a handful of examples of genitive singulars in

-es in Oscan texts from southern Italy, explained by Rix (1996: 247; 2003: 163) as evidence of a Pre-Samnite substrate.[^38] The monophthongisation of ou is similarly complex. In Umbrian, the outcome of original *ou is consistently written u in the native alphabet (which lacks a letter o), or o in the Latin alphabet in the Iguvine tables, but an early Umbrian text (ST Um 3 = Imag. Ital. Forum Novum 1) has doukioí (of obscure meaning, taken as the dative of a name at Rix 2002: 135); the name Vuvçis (cited above < *leukio-, probably via a stage *loukio-) is twice so written in the Iguvine tables, but the derived family name is written Vuçiia at IIb 26. In South Picene, the diphthong in the word for ‘community’ is twice written oú (toútaih and toúta) but once with ú (tútas). In the scanty remains of the southern varieties of the South Picene group the evidence is also difficult. The only relevant attested forms are: Luvcies on ST Ps 13 = Imag. Ital. Nola 6, written in the Etruscan script which has no letter <o>; toutikem (read toutikes by Rix 2002: 71) on ST Ps 1 = Imag. Ital. Nerulum 1, written in the Greek alphabet; and towtid (or, as read at Imag. Ital. Blanda 1, towtid) on the Tortora stele, also written in Greek letters but this time using the Greek digamma to represent the second element of the diphthong. Luvcies contains the same original name element as found in Vuvçis and Vuçiia, and toutikem and towtid derive from the word for ‘community’, spelt as toúta or tútas in South Picene.

#### 3.2.3. (SP/U 3) Lenition of word-final *-d (Meiser 1996: 188; Rix 2009b: 259–260)

Final *-d developed to a consonant denoted with the sign h, as in the South Picene verb adstaíúh ‘they set up’ < *-ond, and is completely lost without trace in Umbrian and, as far as we can tell, from the minor varieties in the Umbrian group. The earliest Umbrian text to show this change is the sixth-century ‘Palaeoumbrian’ text from Tolfa, ST Um 4 = Imag. Ital. Caere 1, which has face with loss of original final *-d.[^39] However, the change appears not to have taken place in the Pre-Samnite inscriptions: ST Ps 3 = Imag. Ital. Capua 35, written in the Etruscan alphabet which represents both /t/ and /d/ with the letter <t>, has tetet ‘he gave’ < Proto-Sabellian *deded, and the Tortora stela, written in the Achaean Greek alphabet, has several word-forms with final -d, including fufwod ‘they were’, also showing the reflex of an original 3PL ending < *-ond.

#### 3.2.4. (SP/U 4) Palatalisation of *k before a front vowel (Meiser 1996: 189–90)

Only one South Picene text (ST Sp TE 5 = Imag. Ital. Interamnia Praetuttiorum 5) shows what might be the effect of palatalisation before front vowels.[^40] This text uses a unique letter, transcribed σ by Rix (2002) and Crawford et al. (2011). (but ś in Marinetti 1985: 53), in two words σidom and eσelsít, neither of which has a clear link with any other Sabellian word form.[^41] Only one other South Picene text has comparable sequences of velar followed by front vowel (ST Sp CH 1 = Imag. Ital. Anxanum 1, with iorkes, upeke and iokipedu) and this does not show palatalisation. In the Umbrian group palatalisation is the norm, although not found in the earliest texts (note face on (ST Um 4 = Imag. Ital. Caere 1 discussed above). The Pre-Samnite texts show no indication of palatalisation, although Meiser does detect it in names borrowed into Etruscan, which surface in Etruscan texts from Campania, such as luvzies (borrowed from the *leukio-/*loukio- name discussed above) in ET Cm 2.53.

#### 3.2.5. (SP/U 5) Development of *ū

A final phonological isogloss may be the change of *ū to ī (Meiser 1987a: 106; Rix 1992a:240). However, the evidence here is again complicated. The standard view is that there were two separate changes affecting inherited *ū in the history of the Sabellian languages. Firstly, a rule (sometimes called the pius rule, or Thurneysen’s Law) has been proposed for all the Italic languages by which *ū changes to *ī before a following *y, on the basis of words such as *pūyo- ‘dutiful’ > *pīyo- leading to Latin pius, Oscan piíhiúí (DAT.SG), and pihom on (ST VM 2 = Imag. Ital. Velitrae 1 (Weiss 2009: 142).[^42] This change is sometimes seen to have taken place in Celtic as well (Weiss 2009: 468), but the Celtic evidence has been explained differently by Zair (2009). Secondly, in Umbrian there are good examples in support of a more general fronting of all inherited long *ū to ī: pir ‘fire’ (NOM.SG) < *pūr, sim ‘pig’ (ACC.SG) < *sūm and mani ‘hand’ (ABL.SG) < *manūd.[^43] In Oscan this second, general change of *ū > ī appears not to have taken place in wordinitial syllable to judge from the word fruktatiuf ‘use, enjoyment’, which derives from an Indo-European root *bʰrūg- (Untermann 2000: 299). However, two original u-stem nouns in Oscan provide evidence for a change of *-ūd > -īd in the ablative singular: castrid < *-ūd ‘property (?)’, and manim ‘hand’, which is accusative, but can be explained by presuming that speakers shifted the word to the i-stem declension on the analogy of an (unattested) ablative *manid < *manūd.[^44] Consequently many scholars have argued for an Oscan sound change with *ū > ī, in word-final syllables (von Planta 1897: 158–159; Buck 1928: 41; Martzloff 2006a: 111–112).[^45] The fate of inherited *ū in South Picene is murky. Meiser (1987a: 106) and Rix (1992: 240) proposed that it regularly developed to ī, in the same way as in Umbrian, in order to give an etymology for the obscure term fítias which they related to the root *bʰū- ‘be, become’ (following Marinetti 1985: 123–124). However, Rix himself later rejected this etymology, and it has not found widespread favour (Untermann 2000: 289; Martzloff 2006a: 113). Other scholars looking at South Picene have seen evidence for the maintenance of original *ū in all positions, even before *-y-, deriving the South Picene word puíh (ST Sp AP 2 = Imag. Ital. Asculum Picenum 2) from *pūyo-, and interpreting its meaning as ‘piously’, which is appropriate in context (Adiego Lajara 1992: 90; Martzloff 2006a). If this interpretation is correct, then it may be simpler to posit a single change of *ū to ī across the Sabellian languages at a later date than the attestation of the South Picene inscriptions.[^46] The only counter-examples to this change are the Oscan words fruktatiuf ‘use’ and futír ‘daughter’ (where the long vowel, guaranteed by the spelling of the dative fuutrei, arises through compensatory lengthening following the loss of h in the medial cluster *-ht- < *-kt-). Seidl (1994: 350) mooted the possibility that in fruktatiuf the rounded vowel was preserved owing to the initial labial consonant, but this seems an ad hoc solution. Unfortunately, the evidence for the development of *ū rests on a small number of forms, and the picture is further complicated by issues of script. The change of *ū to ī most likely involved an intermediate stage of a front rounded vowel [yː], and it is perfectly possible that this was represented in some scripts by a <u> sign, disguising the fact that any change had taken place. In the light of these uncertainties the significance of this isogloss for grouping the Sabellian languages is minimal.

#### 3.2.6. (SP/U 6) Demonstrative pronoun with a stem esto-/ā- ‘this’ (Rix 2009: 260)[^47]

The pronoun *esto-/ā- is found in Umbrian, South Picene, and in one Pre-Samnite text from Capua, (ST Ps 3 = Imag. Ital. Capua 35, the longest Pre-Samnite text yet found apart from the Tortora stele (cited in full in section 3.2.7 below). However, although the archaeological provenance is secure, the stamnos on which the text was inscribed comes from Vulci, and hence it is possible that the inscription commemorates an incomer to Capua, and is not necessarily representative of the language of Capua in the fifth century (see the discussion at Imag. Ital. Capua 35). The use of the pronoun *esto- is not found in any language of the Oscan group. The pronominal system of Sabellian has recently been discussed by Penney (2002) and Dupraz (2007, 2012). Although they do not agree on the details, they are agreed that the stem *esto-/ā- is somehow connected with the Latin pronoun iste, and hence is an archaism within the Sabellian languages (although, in Indo-European terms, an innovation).[^48] The agreement between South Picene and Umbrian is in this case consequently not diagnostic for their relationship.

#### 3.2.7. (SP/U 7) Onomastic formulae

The onomastic links between the languages of the South Picene group and the Umbrian group have been stressed by Rix (2009: 261), and although onomastics is clearly not a purely linguistic feature, it is worth devoting some space here to the issue, particularly since the bulk of our evidence for the Pre-Samnite variety (and much of the diagnostic evidence for the minor languages in the Umbrian group) comprises name formulae. The onomastic system of the Sabellian languages shares a number of common features with the naming systems of other languages of central Italy (Latin, Faliscan and Etruscan). Names in all these languages generally combine an individual name (usually termed praenomen) and a family name (termed the nomen by the Romans, but often in modern works identified as the gens name or the gentilicium);[^49] a further element of the name formula, which is not initially obligatory but becomes increasingly common from the fourth century BC onwards is an indication of the father’s praenomen (often referred to as the filiation formula). In most of the languages of Italy, including Latin, Etruscan and Oscan, filiation is indicated through a case form of the father’s name, either with or without a following word for ‘son.’ However, names in Umbrian appear to have been constructed differently, to judge from a single attestation of a tripartite name formula, not written using abbreviations for the praenomina: vuvçis titis teteies (Iguvine tables Ib 45)[^50] ‘Lucius Teteius, son of Titus.’ There are two distinctive elements in this name formula: firstly, the form titis shows that the filiation was expressed not through the genitive case of the father’s name, but by a derived adjective (originally in *-ios).[^51] Secondly, the indication of filiation is written before the gentilicium, whereas in Latin, Faliscan and Oscan the order is the other way round, with filiation after the gentilicium.[^52] In the minor varieties of the Umbrian group, there is good evidence for the placement of the filiation before the gens name as well, although here the first two names are only found in abbreviated form, for example ST VM 3 = Imag. Ital. Antinum 1: pa. ui. pacuies ‘Pacius Pacuius, son of Vibius’. A statue base from Supinum, in the territory of the Marsi, (ST VM 4 = Imag. Ital. Marruvium 2), does use a derived adjective in *-io- to indicate the father, rather than the genitive, in the collocation i]ouies. pucle[s ‘to the Dioscuri’ (a calque of Greek Diòs Ko^uroi ‘boys of Zeus’, i.e. the demi-gods Castor and Pollux), and this might suggest that the patronymic adjective was also used in name formulae in Marsian. However, the Dioscuri are named in the same way in Paelignian (iouiois. puclois in (ST Pg 5 = Imag. Ital. Sulmo 2), and it is possible that the use of this suffix is an archaism that was felt appropriate to religious language. The languages of the South Picene group provide some similarities to the Umbrian pattern of names. Names in South Picene inscriptions are frequently difficult to interpret correctly, partly owing to the carefully crafted nature of the longer texts, often with hyperbaton or discontinuous phrases. There are two inscriptions which contain only a name formula: ]s / taluis / petrúnis (ST AP 4 = Imag. Ital. Falerio 2, see Stuart-Smith 2000 for this reading, although the text at Imag. Ital. is doubtful about the final -s of the first word) and noúínis: peteronis: efidans (ST AP 5 = Imag. Ital. Falerio 3, following Imag. Ital. for the reading). It is possible to take peteronis in the latter text as a patronymic adjective, since the praenomen petroh (with stem *petrōn-) is also attested in South Picene. However efidans is usually taken as an ethnic adjective (Meiser 1987b: 117), meaning that this is a formula without a nomen, which would be unusual. Alternatively, if peteronis is a gens name, it might match with unis in the former text, in which case taluis might be a patronymic adjective (with all but petr the final –s of the praenomen lost). Unfortunately, there is no known praenomen from which taluis (or taruis, the alternative reading), can be derived. One other South Picene text has a clear filiation formula using the genitive of the father’s name: (ST AQ 1 = Imag. Ital. Superaequum 1 k]aúieh kaúieis puqloh ‘Gaius son of Gaius’; but this is not followed by a nomen or any other element of a name formula, so it too is difficult to interpret. South Picene thus provides no unambiguous match to the Umbrian naming convention, but one Pre-Samnite text (which may in fact reflect the language of Vulci, rather than Capua where the text was found, see the discussion at (SP/U 6) above) does have the clearest parallel to the Umbrian formula of any other inscription found in Italy, and is worth citing the text here in full:

ST Ps 3 = Imag. Ital. Capua 35, vinuvs veneliis peracis estam tetet veniliei viniciiu ‘Vinuks son of Venelis Peracis gave this to Venilis son of Vinuks.’[^53] This fifth-century text, written in the Etruscan alphabet, appears to feature a father’s gift to his son (or possibly a son’s gift to his father); the filiation formula is expressed in both names through a patronymic adjective (veneliis and viniciiu), and occurs before the gens name peracis in the first name (note that the second name does not feature the gentilicium, since this is understood from the earlier name). This text is significant since it is the only text to feature the pronoun estam in the south of Italy as well (and shows monophthongisation of the inherited dative ending *ōi in viniciiu, but preservation of the final diphthong –ei in veniliei). The only other Pre-Samnite text with a tripartite name is a vase inscription from Vico Equense, dated to the century before the Capua dedication, ST Ps 5 = Imag. Ital. Surrentum 2 iefies|| esum: p[.] les: adaries ‘I belong to Iefiis son of P-ls Adariis.’ Although the presence of the verb esum ‘I am’ interrupts this name formula, the father’s name here precedes the gens name and is inflected in the genitive case. Rix (1996: 256–258, 2003: 160–161) claimed that the order of praenomen – filiation – nomen, was also preserved in some Oscan texts written in Greek letters from Lucania and Bruttium, reflecting the survival of a Pre-Samnite substrate, for example: ST Lu 19 = Imag. Ital. Lucania 1 trebis s festies ‘Trebis Festius son of S.’; ST Lu 40 = Imag. Ital. Cosilinum 2 alaponis pakwēis opies ‘Alaponis Opiis son of Pakus.’ However, this has now been convincingly disproved by La Regina (2002), through a re-reading of many of the inscriptions concerned. In consequence the first of the two texts should be read tribis {s} festies, with dittography of the final s of trebis, and the second in fact contains two names … sk]alaponis pakwēis / […] opies ‘Scalaponis son of Pakus, (and) Opiis.’ The onomastic evidence is probably best left out of any discussion of the linguistic affiliations of the Sabellian family. Not only is the evidence of the earliest inscriptions inconclusive, but the spread of the conventions of name formulae across the languages of Italy seems to have been relatively recent in the history of the languages (see Rix 1972 for a reconstruction of the origin and spread of the distinctively Italian onomastic formula involving a gens name). The parallel naming conventions found in Etruscan and Faliscan also show considerable variation across time and space both in the order of the elements praenomen, nomen and filiation formula (in Etruscan, see Rix 1995), and in the choice between a genitive of the father’s name and the use of a patronymic adjective (in Faliscan, see Bakkum 2009: 216–235). Variation in name formulae, particularly in the earliest periods, need not therefore necessarily correlate with dialectal or linguistic grouping.

### 3.3. Features of the South Picene group alone

These overlapping isoglosses between the Umbrian and South Picene groups should be set alongside features which are unique to each group (and also considered in light of features which each group shares with Oscan, to be discussed in sections 3.5 and 3.7). The following list of isoglosses gives proposed unique South Picene features (preceded with SP) and is followed in the next section by isoglosses uniting the Umbrian group.

#### 3.3.1. (SP 1) The second person plural ending

The South Picene 2PL ending –tas occurs only in the verb form videtas (ST Sp TE 2 = Imag. Ital. Interamnia Praetuttiorum 4), normally interpreted as PRES.IND ‘you (PL) see’ or imperative ‘see!’[^54]

Umbrian has a 2PL ending for the imperative –tu or –to, which plausibly derives from a PIE 2DU ending *-tā (Meiser 1992: 291–292, 2003: 45). Paelignian, the only language of the Oscan group to give evidence for the form of the 2PL,[^55] has an imperative eite ‘go’ with the inherited imperative ending *-te, although this could also have been borrowed from Latin (see the doubts of Meiser 2003: 45 n. 27). If the South Picene form is present indicative, not imperative, it is possible that both South Picene and Umbrian have generalised the dual for the plural and oppose an imperative *-tā to indicative *-tās, much as Latin shows a 2PL imperative -te besides the indicative *-tes. There is no evidence for this form in Pre-Samnite.

#### 3.3.2. (SP 2) Generalisation of the third person mediopassive endings *–tor (SG) and *-ntor (PL) to primary verbs (Rix 2003: 160)

Rix follows Meiser (1992) in the view that the Sabellian languages innovated new third person mediopassive endings *-(n)tir for primary tenses, by analogy to the active endings *-(n)ti, and restricted *-(n)tor to secondary formations (i.e. past tenses and subjunctives).[^56] By this theory, the Proto-Sabellian system is maintained in Umbrian, which has –ter or –te (< *-tir, via *-ter) for present indicative mediopassives, but –ntur or –tu (< *-(n)tor) for present subjunctive mediopassives, whereas in Oscan the secondary ending (*-(n)tor) has been replaced (see further section 3.5.5). There is only one clear example of a mediopassive verb in the South Picene corpus (and none in Pre-Samnite), the form qolofítúr, which is generally taken to mean ‘he is praised’ or ‘he is exalted’ although the exact details of its formation are unclear (see Untermann 2000: 408, Vine 2006, and Rix 2009: 256 for discussion of the origin of the verb). If this verb is indeed a present, South Picene will show a separate treatment of the mediopassive endings to the other two branches.

#### 3.3.3. (SP 3) Third person plural endings

The 3PL of the perfect has the form -úh in South Picene and -od in fufwod ‘they were’ on the Tortora stele. Since final -h in South Picene is known to derive from a final *-d (see isogloss SP/U 3) it seems straightforward to link these two endings (Rix 2003: 167). However, before the discovery of the Tortora stele, the South Picene ending was explained differently by Meiser (1987a: 120–121) who set up an ingenious four-part analogy to explain the South Picene 3PL ending. For Meiser, speakers of South Picene opposed a primary third singular ending *-t with 3PL *-nt. The secondary ending for the third singular had also developed (through the lenition of final *d) to *-h. For the secondary ending of the 3PL, analogy to the primary endings led them to create a new ending *-onh, which simplified to just -úh through the loss of the nasal before a spirant, a change which is commonly attested cross-linguistically (and also takes place in other Sabellian languages, and in some varieties of Latin). Since this change would lead to coalescence of third singular and plural secondary ending, the third singular is replaced in South Picene by the primary ending, leading to forms with final t as opsút ‘he made’. One might ask why Meiser resorted to this analogy in the first place, since it would seem equally natural to have a sound change from *-ond > *onh, with ensuing loss of the nasal. However, Meiser was originally uncomfortable with this since he had assumed (following Buck 1928: 80–81, 84) that there was a pan-Sabellian sound change of *-nd- > *n(n) (Meiser 1986: 94, 102). However, the new evidence of the Tortora stele, where -od in fufwod may simply be a writing for spoken -ond,[^57] makes this less likely as a Sabellian sound change; there is no other clear example of the treatment of original *-nd in Sabellian.[^58] In the Sabellian languages, as in Latin, the inherited PIE perfect and aorist merged to form a new tense, also called the perfect. The Pre-Samnite form fufwod appears to agree with (non-Sabellian) Faliscan in generalising *-ond, which derived from an Indo-European ending of the aorist paradigm *-ont, also to the perfect, since reduplicated fufwod, is without doubt in origin a perfect form (see Bakkum 2009: 181 for the Faliscan ending). The South Picene ending -úh is not used on any form that directly derives from an original perfect tense, and both the attested forms are associated with South Picene preterite formations which do not have any clear analogies in any other Sabellian language: adstaíúh ‘they set up’ and p]raistaíúh (probably also meaning ‘they set up’ but in a fragmentary context). We revisit the question of the 3PL ending of the perfect in the Sabellian languages in more detail in section 3.7.

#### 3.3.4. (SP 4) The o-perfect

The final exclusive South Picene feature is also morphological, and is closely connected with the question of the 3PL of the perfect. South Picene inscriptions (but no Pre-Samnite text so far found) show examples of past tense verbs in persons other than the 3PL with the vowel ú preceding the ending, for example, opsút ‘he made’ (with a variant opsúq written when followed by a word beginning with q-). All the other Sabellian languages show *e in the 3SG of the perfect (Oscan upsed ‘he made’, and compare Oscan deded ‘he gave’, Umbrian dede, Pre-Samnite tetet, as well as the perfect third singular fifiked, with meaning something like ‘he fashioned’, on the Tortora stele). The verbs opsút, adstaíúh and p]raistaíúh have given rise to the term ‘the ú-perfect’ which has been seen as a specific innovation of South Picene, and explained by Rix (1993: 337–339) through the generalisation of the *o variant of the thematic vowel from the 1PL and 3PL endings throughout the paradigm (a difficulty with this theory is that usually in South Picene ú represents the outcome of inherited *ō, see section 3.5.2). Beckwith (2007) noted that the forms fufwod on the Tortora stele might have the same origin as the South Picene ú-perfect, but the single attested 3SG form on the inscription, fifiked, shows that the generalisation of a back vowel throughout the perfect paradigm has not taken place in the language of the Tortora stele.

#### 3.3.5. (SP 5) A shared letter-form?

The scanty remains of the Pre-Samnite texts mean that there is only one feature which is exclusive to South Picene and Pre-Samnite, the 3PL ending in *-o(n)d. To these Rix (2003: 160, 168; 2009: 254) adds an extra-linguistic feature: the shape of the letter form used to represent /f/ on the Tortora stele (which otherwise uses the Achaean Greek alphabet), and perhaps also used in the alphabet of Nucera, which he compares to the sign used in South Picene for the letter represented in transcriptions as <v>. Rix is of the view that the speakers of Pre-Samnite languages migrated southwards after the invention of the alphabet. In the far south of Italy, the composers of the inscription now known as the Tortora stele adopted the alphabet of the neighbouring Greek colonists, but they maintained one earlier letter sign to represent a sound for which Greek had no equivalent letter, the consonant /f/. Although Rix (2003: 168) states that ‘Einen besseren Beweis für die Zugehörigkeit der Präsamniten zu den südpikenischen Sabineren kann man sich nicht wünschen’ (‘One cannot wish for a better proof that the Pre-Samnites belong with the South Picene Sabines’), not everyone has been so convinced (Triantafillis 2009: 485–486; Crawford et al. 2011: 19), and it is best to discount this alphabetic feature from the discussion of linguistic subgrouping.

### 3.4. Features of the Umbrian group alone

The exclusive developments of the Umbrian group are few in number. Umbrian itself has undergone a large number of specific sound changes not shared in other Sabellian languages (as detailed in Meiser 1986), and owing to the preservation of so much Umbrian in the Iguvine tables, we know a great deal more about Umbrian than most of the other Sabellian languages. However, the varieties usually grouped with Umbrian have left only very scanty remains. Rix (2002: 66–67) gives only nine texts under the rubric ‘Volsci, Marsi, Aequiculi and Sabini’. All except the ‘Palaeovolscian’ text from Satricum are written in the Latin alphabet, and at least one of these inscriptions shows clear Latin influence. The most extensive text is the so-called Tabula Veliterna (ST VM 2 = Imag. Ital. Velitrae 1 henceforth referred to as the Tabula Veliterna), a sacred law inscribed on bronze, and usually associated with the Volscian town of Velitrae (modern Velletri). Crawford has now shown (at Imag. Ital. Velitrae 1) that the link with Velitrae is probably mistaken, and that there are reasons for thinking the text is not the product of a Volscian-speaking community, since it records two meddices as the officiating magistrates, whereas the Volscians characteristically had three aediles as their magistrates. This text has been the subject of much speculation and analysis, not least because of the difficulty of ascribing it clearly to any branch of the Sabellian language family (see in particular the discussions of Poultney 1951 and Rix 1992a). The features which are shared between Umbrian and the Tabula Veliterna, as given by Rix (1992a: 240, largely following Poultney 1951) are as follows, and may be taken as the basis for consideration of the Umbrian group as a whole:

#### 3.4.1. (U 1) Loss of final -t

The 3SG future perfect atahus on the Tabula Veliterna, whose morphosyntax is clear although the meaning is disputed (see Untermann 2000: 131–132), shows the same loss of final -t as in some Umbrian forms such as fus, 3SG of the future perfect or future of ‘be’.[^59] However, the Umbrian forms co-exist alongside more common, and numerous, cases where the final -t is preserved: in the Iguvine tables written in the Latin alphabet the third singular of the future perfect or future is spelt with final -t twenty-three times against nine times without (including fust ‘he will have been’ eight times and fus eight times).[^60]

#### 3.4.2. (U 2) Development of *d

Rix also sees in the third-person imperative arpatitu on the Tabula Veliterna a parallel to the Umbrian fricativisation of intervocalic *d, represented by ř in the native alphabet and -rs- in the Latin alphabet. This rests on the interpretation of the first part of arpatitu as the outcome of the preverb *ad before a vowel, generalised to preconsonantal position; it is also possible, however that this is a separate change of *d before a labial, as also happens in Umbrian, compare ařfertur ‘priest’, ařpeltu and ařputrati, and in some early Latin forms, such as arfuise for adfuisse in the early Latin inscription Senatusconsultum de Bacchanalibus (CIL I² 581). In other inscriptions from the minor dialects linked to Umbrian, intervocalic d remains unchanged: nouesede in ST VM 5 = Imag. Ital. Marruvium 1, cisiedio in ST VM 6 = Imag. Ital. Supinum 1.

#### 3.4.3. (U 3) Loss of yod after dentals

Rix notes the loss (or the omission in writing) of the palatal glide after dentals in Umbrian and the Tabula Veliterna: thus se, plausibly taken as the 3SG of the optative ‘to be’ < *siēd may have undergone the same change as Umbrian si (also written sir and sei) ‘may you be’ < *siēs, and si ‘may he be’< *siēd. Again, there is little evidence to suggest that this change also affected the other minor languages, and an apparent counter-example to the change is provided by the name cisiedio in ST VM 6 = Imag. Ital. Supinum 1.[^61]

#### 3.4.4. (U 4) Accusative plural in -f

The single morphological feature which unites Umbrian and the language of the Tabula Veliterna (and for which evidence is lacking in other Volscian, Marsian and Sabine inscriptions) involves a nominal case form. The word asif, of uncertain meaning, occurs in the text next to the accusative bim ‘ox’, and is generally taken as ACC.PL,[^62] showing the same ending as in Umbrian accusative plurals of all classes: -f/-f. In Oscan, accusative plurals of all noun classes generally end -ss or -s, probably the result of an analogical extension of an ending *-fs which is discussed in detail in section 3.7. In South Picene, the ACC.PL ending -as is found in ā-stems, but consonant-stem accusative plurals are formed with -f (reflecting the regular development of final *-ns, the Italic outcome of PIE *-ms); the South Picene distribution of ACC.PL morphs appears to reflect the original Sabellian pattern.[^63] The language of the Tabula Veliterna thus appears to tally more closely to Umbrian than Oscan. However, in the absence of examples from South Picene it is not clear what the original ACC.PL in Sabellian of i-stems would be; if asif is originally an i-stem noun (and that is not certain), and i-stem nouns in Sabellian followed the class of consonant-stem nouns rather than ā-stems, then the ending is a shared inheritance with Umbrian, and not an innovation. These features are on their own insufficient to establish Umbrian and the minor varieties as a dialect group, but several of the isoglosses given in section 3.2 may be diagnostic for the Umbrian group alone. Thus monophthongisation of the diphthong *ei (SP/U 2) is well established in nearly all the texts from the minor languages.[^64] There is no clear diagnostic evidence for the monophthongisation of *ou other than toticu < *toutiko- on the Tabula Veliterna, but the minor languages do show monophthongisation of *ai > *e, as in Umbrian.[^65] Two other features are found only on the Tabula Veliterna: the loss of final *d, as in se < *siēd (compare SP/U 3 above); and the palatalisation of *k before a front vowel (SP/U 4), which is seen in the word façia ‘let him make’ < *fakiād, where ç transcribes a backward-facing C and may represent a palatalised consonant. Finally, the texts in this group which exhibit name formulae, except for ST VM 4 = Imag. Ital. Marruvium 2, which shows heavy influence from Latin, share the word order praenomen, filiation, gentilicium (SP/U 7).

### 3.5. The Oscan group

As we have already seen, several scholars have seen Umbrian and South Picene (and by extension the Umbrian dialect group and the south Picene dialect group) more closely related than either language to Oscan. Oscan is typically characterised as the most conservative language in terms of phonology, since it generally preserves original diphthongs and word-final consonants. Of the languages grouped with Oscan, we are again faced with a similar problem to the minor dialects of the Umbrian group: the textual remains are not at all extensive, and the interpretation of the two most extensive complete texts, the Marrucinian Rapino Bronze (ST MV 1 = Imag. Ital. Teate Marrucinorum 1), and the Paelignian epitaph of a priestess from Corfinium (ST Pg 9 = Imag. Ital. Corfinium 6, often referred to as the Herentas epitaph) are bedeviled by uncertainties and conjecture (see the bibliographies ad locc. at Imag. Ital.). Moreover, there is good evidence for two different registers of language in the Paelignian corpus, with the Herentas inscription exhibiting alternative forms to those in other Paelignian texts: for example, the NOM.SG of nouns in the ā-declension ends in -u in the Herentas inscription, but in -a in other Paelignian texts; an apparent ABL.SG aetatu ‘life’ in the Herentas inscription can be compared to aetate, apparently also ABL.SG meaning ‘life’ in another Paelignian text, ST Pg 10 = Imag. Ital. Corfinium 11. Scholars have been divided as to which of these varieties represents ‘genuine’ Paelignian, and how best to account for the variation. Lazzeroni (first 1965, but most forcefully 1991: 181) saw the Herentas epitaph as an example of anti-Roman sentiment, deliberately choosing variants that were not native to Paelignian if they differed to the equivalent Latin terms. Others, for example Poccetti (1981: 269; 2007) see the features as genuine Paelignian archaisms; furthermore Meiser (1987a) viewed the apparent Latinisms to be in fact evidence for a ‘South Picene’ substrate. As far as possible in the following discussion, I leave open the question of which variety represents the more archaic phase of Paelignian. Oscan is in general the most conservative branch of Sabellian in terms of sound changes, and there is only one possible phonological isogloss which may be shared between all the varieties of the Oscan group, and which did not take place in other varieties of Sabellian:

#### 3.5.1. (O 1) Anaptyxis

In Oscan an anaptyctic vowel breaks up clusters of r or l followed by consonant (henceforward RT clusters), as for example herekleis ‘of Hercules’ < *herklo-, a word originally borrowed (probably via Etruscan) from the Greek name *hērakl- and showing the effect of syncope of the medial vowel,[^66] followed by anaptyxis. Anaptyxis of this type is found in some Paelignian texts (e.g. herecl-) although the form hercl- is more common in Paelignian, and anaptyxis is also present in a Vestinian text (ST MV 5 = Imag. Ital. Incerulae 4). Many Oscan texts also show anaptyxis in clusters of stop followed by r or l (henceforward TR clusters) following a light syllable, as patereí (in the same text as fuutreí where the double writing shows the initial vowel was long, cf. also maatreís). In the Oscan examples, the anaptyctic vowel in RT clusters copies the vowel of the preceding syllable, in TR clusters the vowel of the following syllable. Two South Picene texts also show anaptyctic vowels breaking up TR and RT clusters, but in these cases neither the patterning of the vowel quality nor the restriction of TR anaptyxis to environments only after light syllables is observed: peteronis (from *petrōn-) in ST AP 5 = Imag. Ital. Falerio 3, with the vowel of the preceding syllable; matereı́h < *mātrei, patereı́h < *patrei and qupı́rı́h in ST Sp AP 2 = Imag. Ital. Asculum Picenum 2, with TR anaptyxis after long and short initial syllables, and arı́tı́h in the same text (if correctly derived from *artīd, see Adiego Lajara 1992: 93, Untermann 2000: 118 for doubts), with RT anaptyxis and the vowel of the following syllable. A couple of other important phonological changes appear to have taken place in Oscan and in Umbrian, but are questionable for the minor varieties lying between them. Although they are not strictly speaking isoglosses uniting the whole Oscan group, it is convenient to discuss them here.

#### 3.5.2. (O 2) Vowel mergers

I consider first changes to the inherited vowels, which led to a new system with three contrasting front vowels, both short and long (at least in word-initial syllables). As is well known, this development rests on a merger of quantity between inherited short *i and long *ē, both of which were represented in the Oscan alphabet with a newly adapted vowel sign ı́ (occasionally written double ı́ı́ to express the long vowel in word-initial syllable), for example pı́s ‘who’ < *kwis, lı́gatuı́s ‘legates’ (DAT.PL) < *lēg-. Inherited long *ī (and *ī which derived in some circumstances from *ū, see SP/U 3 above) is paired with a new short *i, which arises from various changes, including the new anaptyctic vowel (see section 3.5.1) and in the outcome of word-final *-ios (note that the *o is lost in this sequence already in Proto-Sabellian, see section 3.1.4). In the Oscan alphabet this vowel is represented as i, for example lúvkis ‘Lucius’ < *leukios, its long equivalent is written iı́, for example biı́tam ‘life’ < *gʷītām. Inherited short *e is paired with a new long vowel, which results from processes of compensatory lengthening, and these vowels are written e, the long version ee, for example kúmbened ‘it was agreed’ < *kom-gʷened, teerúm ‘earth’ < *tersom. Oscan also shows merger of inherited long *ō and short *u, with both vowels normally written as u, the accented ú representing the outcome of inherited *o. Hence for example the writing dunúm ‘gift’ < *dōnom.[^67] After the invention of the new vowel sign ı́, these vowel changes are most clearly seen in texts written in the Oscan alphabet, but the effects of the same merger are also apparent in Oscan texts written in other scripts: for example licitud ‘let it be allowed’ in Oscan written in the Latin alphabet, and leikeit ‘it is allowed’ in Oscan written in the Greek alphabet, both deriving from *likē-.[^68] Among the minor dialects of the Oscan group the same change appears to have taken place: in Marrucinian, for example lixs ‘law’ (NOM.SG) < *lēg-, but in Paelignian inherited *ē is normally represented by the letter e: fesn(am) ‘shrine’ < *dhēsnā- (Oscan in native alphabet fı́ı́snú), upsaseter ‘it should be made’ < *op(e)sāsēter (or perhaps *ōp(e)sēter to explain the initial u, see Wallace 1984: 88; Rix 1993: 335). The representation of inherited short *i in Paelignian is normally with i, but occasionally with e (see further Jiménez Zamudio 1986: 122). Paelignian appears therefore not to have undergone the merger of the front vowels, but it may have undergone merger of the inherited back vowels *ō and *u, as seen in upsaseter cited above. It is usually assumed that Umbrian has undergone the same set of vowel mergers as Oscan. Even though the two writing systems which are used for the Iguvine tables are not wholly consistent in their use of vowel signs, the evidence points towards a similar merger of inherited *i and *ē,[^69] and probably also *ō and short *u, although here the picture is obscured by a rule lowering u before nasals (Meiser 1986: 51–52, 120–122). However, the problematic Tabula Veliterna has enough examples of the treatment of original short *i, always written with i (pis ‘someone’ < *kwis, toticu ‘public’ < *toutiko-, medix ‘magistrate’ < *med(es)-dik-, sistiatiens ‘they set up’ < *sist-) and original long *ē, twice written with e (sepu ‘knowing’ < *sēpwo-, se ‘he may be’ < *siēd) to show that in the language of this text the two vowels are kept apart (Rix 1992a: 42).[^70] In South Picene also neither the merger of *i with *ē nor of *u with *ō has yet taken place; note for example videtas (ST Sp TE 2 = Imag. Ital. Interamnia Praetuttiorum 4) < *widē-, and perhaps kduú (ST Sp CH 1 = Imag. Ital. Anxanum 11) < *kluēo. However, there is a tendency to write the reflex of inherited long *ō in South Picene with a separate letter, <ú>, to that employed for the reflex of short *o (<o>), and sometimes the outcome of long *ē is written with a separate letter <ı́> (as nı́r ‘man’ (NOM.SG) < *nēr, compare nerf (ACC.PL) < *ner-). These writings may represent the fact that the inherited long vowels were raised, and thus indicate the first stage of the later vowel mergers (see Adiego Lajara 1992: 27–64 for the fullest discussion of the representation of vowels in South Picene texts).

#### 3.5.3. (O 3) Original *ā in absolute final position

In Oscan the reflex of original *ā in absolute final position merges with the outcome of short *o, and is written ú in the Oscan alphabet, -o in the Latin alphabet, and -o or -ō in the Greek alphabet (segúnú, segonō ‘statutes’ < *segnā, molto (Latin alphabet) ‘fine’ < *moltā). In South Picene, final *ā is maintained (qora, rufra and probably toúta). The rounding of *ā is also found in the Paelignian Herentas text (e.g. prismu < *prismā), but the presence of a number of female names and titles in Paelignian with nominative singulars ending in -a indicates either that the change did not take place in all registers of Paelignian, or that some Paelignian registers reintroduced the original vowel under Latin influence (see Jiménez Zamudio 1986: 120). The rounding of *ā also takes place in Umbrian, for example mutu ‘fine’ < *moltā. However, the earliest portions of the Umbrian Iguvine tables, and some Umbrian inscriptions, also show spellings with final a rather than u, which suggests that the final stage of this change is only in progress during the history of the Umbrian texts.[^71]

#### 3.5.4. (O 4) Medio-passive imperative and infinitive endings

The first possible example of an exclusive shared morphological innovations in the Oscan group is the extension of the r-ending of the medio-passive indicative to the medio-passive imperative (hence Oscan censamur ‘let him be assessed’, beside Umbrian persnimu ‘let him pray’) and medio-passive infinitive (Oscan sakrafı́r ‘to be sacrificed’, Umbrian pihaf(e)i ‘to be purified’).[^72] Unfortunately, the evidence comprises only forms in Oscan and Umbrian, and there are no relevant examples from any language in the South Picene group or any of the minor dialects. The same extension of the r-ending to the infinitive may have taken place to give the Latin passive infinitive in -ier.[^73] Meiser attempted to derive the Latin infinitives in -ier (and the longer form -rier as in amarier ‘to be loved’) from the same origin as the Sabellian infinitives (2003: 57–58) and consequently proposed that the extension of -r to the passive infinitive took place within Proto-Italic (2003: 57 n. 76). However, as was already shown by Rix (1976: 328), in the section of the Iguvine tables where the infinitive pihaf(e)i is written four times and cehefi once, final -r is correctly written in 120 times, and only missed off once. The most likely explanation is that the Umbrian infinitive never had final -r, and the extension of final -r is an exclusively Oscan feature.

#### 3.5.5. (O 5) Primary and secondary mediopassive endings

We have already discussed the possibility that Proto-Sabellian had a distinction between primary *-(n)tir and secondary *-(n)tor for the ending of the third person in the mediopassive of verbal conjugations. As discussed in section 3.3.2, South Picene may have generalised *-(n)tor to serve for both primary and secondary verbs. The languages of the Oscan group seem to have made the alternative choice, and extended the primary morpheme to secondary endings, including the subjunctive. Although the number of examples is small, the inclusion of a Paelignian example, upsaseter ‘might be built’ (ST Pg 2 = Imag. Ital. Superaequum 4), imperfect subjunctive, is significant since it shows that the ‘North Oscan’ dialects underwent the same change.[^74]

#### 3.5.6. (O 6) Perfect stem marker *-tt-

Another morphological feature for which there is good evidence among the linguistic remains of central Italy is the innovation of the perfect stem marker *-tt-, used to form perfects from derived verbs with a present stem formed in *ā, of the type prúfatted ‘he approved’ < *probʰā-tt-ed, whereas Umbrian uses the morpheme -nç-/-nś for derived verbs.[^75] The Oscan marker is well attested in Paelignian (coisatens ‘they oversaw’ < *koisā-tt-), and probably also in Marrucinian (amatens, meaning uncertain, see Untermann 2000: 83–84). However, it also seems to be found in the problematic Tabula Veliterna, already referred to several times above, since the form sistiatiens is most plausibly translated ‘they established’ and interpreted as *sistā-tt-ens, an ‘Oscan’ -tt- perfect, with the stem *sistā- is itself abstracted from an inherited present paradigm with ablaut and reduplication *si-steh₂-/*si-sth₂-.[^76] It is worth noting here also that one feature which Meiser (1996: 207) cited as a particular Oscan feature should probably be discounted. Meiser thought that in Oscan inherited pronominal forms with medial *-sm- were replaced, contrasting forms such as the Oscan locative eizeic/eı́seı́/eseı́ with South Picene locative esmı́n/esmen (with cliticised postposition *-en), and probable South Picene dative esmı́k (in a fragmentary context), Umbrian esme(i) (probably locative) and Umbrian dative esmik (see Dupraz 2012: 43–57 for a survey of the forms). However, the two most recent discussions of the pronominal system of the Sabellian languages are united in taking these forms to belong to a different paradigm to the anaphoric pronoun continued in Oscan eizeic/eı́seı́ (Penney 2002: 141; Dupraz 2007: 261–265; 2012: 57–59), and associating them in the same paradigm with the stem *esto-, which, as we saw above at section 3.2.6, is not attested in Oscan.

### 3.6. Subgrouping of Sabellian: the picture so far

After this lengthy consideration of the different isoglosses separating out the three groups of the Sabellian languages (according to the Meiser-Rix model), it is worth taking stock of their significance for subgrouping. Firstly, let us consider the South Picene group. Assessing the link between the earliest attested languages of central and southern Italy with South Picene is difficult owing to the paucity of Pre-Samnite texts, and their brevity (only two texts in this group have more than three words), or else the difficulty of their interpretation (as is the case for the longest text, the Tortora stele). One single text, ST Ps 3 = Imag. Ital. Capua 35, a funerary urn from Capua (cited in its entirety at section 3.2.7), could be seen as diagnostic for the Pre-Samnite group, since it shows both the pronoun *esto-, and the distinctively Umbrian pattern of filiation through a patronymic adjective placed before the gens name. However, these two features are not diagnostic for subgrouping, since the first may be an archaism (as discussed at section 3.2.6) and name-formulae are not reliable indicators of linguistic filiation. Moreover, the text concerned is written on an object made at Vulci, nearly 300 km north-east of Capua (see Imag. Ital. ad loc.), and so its geographical find-spot may not be a true indication of the location of the spoken variety represented on the inscription. This text should therefore probably be taken out of consideration for subgrouping. The Pre-Samnite texts also show more evidence for monophthongisation, especially of the GEN.SG ending *-eis > *-es.[^77] In itself monophthongisation of this sort is a very common sound change, which may well take place independently. In the case of the Pre-Samnite group, this change is not consistently observed in the texts, and it is possible, perhaps particularly for those written in the Etruscan script, that the writing of GEN.SG -es reflects Etruscan usage, where Besitzinschriften (i.e. short texts indicating the owner of an object) are often written with an Etruscan name ending in -es.[^78] Since monophthongisation still seems to be in progress in South Picene, it is difficult to see how this change can be used to group all these varieties together. The only remaining aspect which links South Picene and Pre-Samnite is the use of a 3PL marker in the preterite derived from Proto-Italic *-ond. However, we saw in section 3.3 that this ending in Pre-Samnite collocated with a perfect-tense 3SG -ed, but in South Picene with 3SG -út; South Picene appears to have innovated a new perfect marker -ú-, but Pre-Samnite has transferred inherited personal endings of the thematic aorist onto inherited perfect stems (as also happened in Faliscan). In summary for the South Picene group, the links between the South Picene inscriptions and the early texts from Tortora and the area around the Bay of Naples seem to be too few in number to give firm support to the reconstruction of a subgroup of Sabellian. Indeed, it is far from clear that the language of the Tortora stele is to be linked with the Campanian texts, or that the Campanian texts are definitely all to be linked together against Oscan. If one compares Besitzinschriften from the Bay of Naples from before and after 400 BC it is difficult to see any clear linguistic dividing line between texts: Rix’s division of the texts into Pre-Samnite and Oscan is based solely on presumed date of attestation, rather than linguistic features. In both the early and the later texts both the GEN.SG -es and -eis are found.[^79] The 1SG of the verb ‘to be’ is written esum or sum in the early texts, sum or súm in later texts (with the form sim a peculiarity to Saticula; see Mancini 1997). The reason for separating out these earlier texts from the later ones is given explicitly by Rix (2003: 163; see also 2002: 5–6): ancient accounts of the movement of Samnites southward leading to the capture of Greek and Etruscan cities in Campania in the second half of the fifth century BC. However, we must be careful about the use we make of the scanty (and sometimes conflicting) ancient Greek and Roman histories of other peoples around the Mediterranean. As was shown already by Bickerman (1952) such histories are often more concerned to fit other ancient peoples into a Greek or Roman world-view rather than an accurate historical reflection of what took place.[^80] Ancient ethnographers generally explained the presence of different population or linguistic groups in the same place through migration or invasion hypotheses,[^81] and did not have adequate categories to describe areas, such as Campania in the fifth century BC, which were inhabited by several different populations. Some scholars have seen a close link between the South Picene and the Umbrian group.[^82] However, few of the relevant features listed above can be shown both to be innovations and to have taken place in the prehistory of both branches. The best candidates for shared innovation are the extension of the 2DU endings to the plural, and the lenition of word-final *-d, since this is the only sound change which is unambiguously found in all the South Picene texts (although not in Pre-Samnite). However, several other changes which appear to be in progress or which affect some, but not all, of the South Picene texts are shared with Umbrian. The Umbrian group itself is not established beyond reasonable doubt, with the only distinctive isogloss the non-linguistic feature of the order and formation of personal names. The longest non-Umbrian text in this group, the ‘Volscian’ sacred law known as the Tabula Veliterna, shows some shared features with Umbrian, but also appears to have participated in the innovation of the characteristic *-tt- perfect of the Oscan group; indeed, the analysis in section 3.5 suggested that the *-tt- perfect is itself the best attested isogloss uniting all the varieties allocated to the Oscan group.

### 3.7. Shared changes affecting all Sabellian languages attested after c. 400 BC

In a few cases, all the languages of the Sabellian group appear to have undergone a shared change that is not found in the South Picene or Pre-Samnite inscriptions. These could be seen as isoglosses linking both the Oscan and the Umbrian groups following an early split of South Picene from the Sabellian family (thus Adiego Lajara 1990; 1992: 64). But since the tokens of these changes are nearly all attested at a later stage than the relevant forms in the South Picene group, they could result from common changes in the peninsula which took place after around 400 BC. We have already discussed a number of examples of shared phonological changes that occur in all Sabellian languages and post-date the earliest texts. First, we have observed that medial syncope occurs in all the later Sabellian languages, although in the South Picene inscriptions there is a possible example of an unsyncopated sequence in the stem opesa- ‘work’ which is attested in syncopated form *opsa- in other Sabellian languages (see section 3.1.4).[^83] Two other changes which seem to have taken place across most of the Sabellian languages are the characteristic vowel mergers between *i and *ē, *u and *ō, probably concomitant with a loss of distinctive length opposition except in initial syllables (see section 3.5.2), and the development of final long *ā to a rounded back vowel (see section 3.5.3); if we are right to suggest that the fronting of *ū to ī is also a change which takes place after the attestation of South Picene (see section 3.2.5), this could add a third phonological change to the list. A final phonological feature which has not yet been mentioned is the lenition of stop before *t, well known as a Sabellian development from examples such as Oscan saahtúm, Umbrian sahta beside Latin sanctus, with lenition of *kt > *ht, and Oscan scriftas, Umbrian screhto beside Latin scriptus with lenition of *pt. The development is usually taken as a Proto-Sabellian feature (see for example Weiss 2009: 467 n. 11), and is known to precede the process of medial syncope in Oscan and Umbrian, since clusters that arise after the syncope survive in Oscan and have a different development in Umbrian: for example Proto-Sabellian *faketōd ‘let him/her do’ becomes factud in Oscan but feitu in Umbrian. The earliest evidence for this change in Umbrian is the mid-sixth-century text ST Um 4 = Imag. Ital. Caere 1, from Tolfa, where the name setums is usually taken as equivalent to Latin Septimus. However, in South Picene and the Pre-Samnite text from Tortora there are no examples of the change having taken place,[^84] and some apparent counter-examples: South Picene deiktam has been taken as the past participle of the Indo-European root *deik′- (Untermann 2000: 159), although we would expect this to show a zero grade (*dik′to-); South Picene molk[t]ah (if this reading is correct) has been linked to Oscan molto, Umbrian muta ‘fine’, and may be connected to Latin mulcare (Untermann 2000: 482; for an alternative explanation for Latin mulcare see de Vaan 2008a: 393). The two attestations of a cluster *kt from Tortora are unfortunately both in a fragmentary context and have no clear etymology: fri{-}qto[d] and takiosqtod (the transcription q represents the Greek letter qoppa). Moreover, since this text is likely to represent an archaic law, these forms are probably best interpreted as imperatives in *-tōd, (thus Martzloff 2007:182–183; Nishimura 2008: 165–166; 2012: 388 who note that -sqtod could derive from *-sk′e-tōd, i.e., the *-tōd imperative attached to a verb with the PIE present forming suffix *sk′).[^85] In the absence of any better evidence from South Picene or other early inscriptions the hypothesis that the lenition of *kt occurred after the Proto-Sabellian period must remain inconclusive.[^86] There are two morphological features that are found in all the later Sabellian languages but not in South Picene, and these are worth investigating in a little more detail, particularly since the first has led to the assumption of a dialectal unity between Oscan and Umbrian as marked against South Picene (Adiego Lajara 1990). In Oscan, Umbrian and the minor varieties the secondary ending of the active 3PL is –ns (always written -ns in Oscan, in Umbrian the n is sometimes dropped). This is attested in all past-tense indicative and all subjunctive verb forms. Relevant examples include: the perfects Oscan fufens ‘they were’, upsens ‘they made’, Vestinian osins ‘they made’, Paelignian coisatens ‘they took care of’, Umbrian eitipens ‘they decided’ and ‘Volscian’ sistiatiens ‘they established’; and the subjunctives Oscan pútı́ans ‘that they are able’ (present subjunctive), trı́barakattı́ns ‘that they build’ (perfect subjunctive), patensı́ns ‘that they might show’ (imperfect subjunctive), Umbrian dirsans ‘that they give’ (present subjunctive), sis /sins ‘that they might be’ (present subjunctive). The standard explanation of this personal ending (for example Buck 1928: 81; Meiser 1986: 102; Martzloff 2006b: 65 n. 8) is that original *-nd (from PIE *-nt) underwent a sound change to *-n in Proto-Sabellian, which was then remarked by the addition of *-s.[^87] As we saw in section 3.3, the South Picene ending -úh and Pre-Samnite -od argue against a Proto-Sabellian change of *-nd > *-n, and indeed the only other evidence in support of it is the derivation of the Sabellian gerundive ending (found in formations such as Oscan úpsannam ‘to be made’) from original *-ndo-, parallel to the Latin gerundive ending -ndus. However, as Weiss (2009: 173) has pointed out, the Italic gerundive does not have to be explained from *-ndo-, since the Latin sequence *-nd- can have other origins.[^88] Whatever the origin of the ending, -ns, it must have been created very late in Proto-Sabellian or perhaps even after the common period, since it post-dates the sound-change of *-ns > *-f, which itself took place after the creation of a new nominative *-ōns in n-stems, hence Oscan úı́ttiuf ‘use’ < *oitiōns (see Rix 1986: 583 and Meiser 1986: 98–100 for discussion of the chronology). Adiego Lajara (1990) was the first scholar to see the full implications of the divergence of the 3PL ending in South Picene from Oscan and Umbrian. He argued that the 3PL endings represented separate developments from Proto-Sabellian, and that this was the result of a common innovation made by the ancestor of Oscan and Umbrian (and the other minor varieties) which had not taken place in ‘Ursüdpikenisch’. In the languages other than South Picene, either final *-nd had been simplified to *-n by sound change, and then a morphological innovation had extended this to *-ns, or there had been a sound change of final *-nd directly to *-ns;[^89] in South Picene *-nd developed first to *-d, and subsequently lenited to give final -h. Adiego Lajara was writing before the discovery of the Tortora stele, but the 3PL ending of this text clearly indicates that this language cannot be in his presumed Oscan and Umbrian branch of Sabellian. Adiego Lajara’s view of the importance of this morphological isogloss for the subgrouping of Sabellian has been followed by subsequent scholars (Wallace 2007: 5; Rix 2009).[^90] The Sabellian endings of the 3PL of the perfect are consequently of considerable importance for the question of subgrouping, and it is worth placing these endings in context. In the Sabellian languages, as in Latin, the inherited Indo-European verb system was remodelled, and the original perfect and aorist coalesced as a single past tense, normally referred to as the perfect.[^91] According to Meiser (2003: 84) the beginnings of this merger were already underway in Proto-Italic, although it was not completed before the split between the two branches of Italic, Latino-Faliscan and Sabellian; Latin and the Sabellian languages went their own ways in the reordering of inherited morphology into a single preterite. It seems likely that even within the Sabellian branch the process of building a single preterite tense out of inherited morphological material took place separately in different varieties.[^92] Thus Oscan and Umbrian made separate innovations for the creation of perfects to denominative and secondary verbs with Oscan forming a *-tt- perfect (discussed at section 3.5.6 above) but Umbrian a perfect in -nç-/-nś (for which see Willi 2010). Different Sabellian varieties sometimes chose different stems from the same verbal root for the preterite: compare the perfects from the stem *fak- ‘make, do’ where Oscan has a reduplicated perfect fefacid (perfect subjunctive), but Umbrian face (perfect indicative) and fakust (future perfect) could be derived from an original root aorist.[^93] In Latin, as is well known, the personal endings of the new perfect tense partly continue PIE perfect endings, and partly inherited aorist endings, with allomorphy between the 3PL endings -ēre (ultimately from the PIE perfect), -ērunt and -erunt (with -unt from the aorist) retained into the Classical period (Weiss 2009: 394). We have far less information on the personal endings of perfect in Sabellian, but there is some indication that they too incorporated both perfect and aorist endings in the newly created tense, judging from the

Paelignian 2PL form lexe, which appears to derive from a PIE perfect ending.[^94] From a comparison with the Latin developments, we might expect that the Sabellian personal endings of the new perfect would also have exhibited some allomorphy in the early period of the development of the individual varieties, and the shared innovation of a new ending -ns to the exclusion of all other endings at a stage when the perfect tense was not yet fully crystallised is perhaps surprising. As an alternative hypothesis to Adiego Lajara’s theory that Oscan and Umbrian jointly innovated a new 3PL ending at the very earliest stage in their history, I therefore suggest that it is worth considering a scenario whereby the ending -ns either represents a common choice from a set of allomorphs, or spread through language contact across the Sabellian language family at a later period, when the new verbal category of the perfect was better established. The second specific change which could be argued to show a shared Oscan and Umbrian development, as opposed to South Picene, is the spread of a single morpheme across all the accusative plural of all nominal stems (see also the discussion of the accusative plural in section 3.4.4). It is worth setting out the background to this innovation. In PIE the *eh₂-declension, the ancestor of the Italic ā-stems, appears to have already formed an accusative plural without a nasal, as continued, for example, in Gothic gibos and other languages (see Weiss 2009: 236 for further details). The PIE ending (after loss of laryngeals) *-ās is directly continued in South Picene ACC.PL qoras ‘monument’ (case and number guaranteed by context and parallel forms qora, NOM.SG, koram, ACC.SG). Other inherited nominal declensions, however, had a nasal in the ACC.PL, for example *-ons in the o-declension (Gothic dagans), *-ins in the i-declension (Gothic gastins) and *-ṃs in the consonant-stem declension (Gothic broþruns).[^95] In Proto-Sabellian a sequence of nasal + s underwent a change to *f, and this is the ending found for the ACC.PL of consonant stems in South Picene, as nerf ‘men’ < *nerṃs.[^96] In Umbrian, the ending -f is preserved across all declension classes, hence nerf ‘men’, vitluf ‘male calves’, and also extended to the ā-stems vitlaf ‘female calves’. In Oscan the ending -ss or -s occurs in all declensions: feı́húss ‘walls’ (o-stem), vı́ass ‘streets’ (ā-stem), aginss, urins, usurs (all of uncertain meaning, but likely consonant stems). Rix (1986, reprised at 2009: 255) explained the Oscan forms in -ss through a series of analogical changes. Firstly in Oscan the *-s of the ACC.PL ā-stem was extended to the other declensions, giving *-fs (a sequence in Oscan which is known elsewhere to have simplified early to -ss, compare DAT/ABL.PL luisarifs on ST Cp 8 = Imag. Ital. Capua 15, elsewhere ending in -ss). Subsequently to this, the ending *-fs (or even the simplified -ss) was spread by analogy back to the ā-stems. Rix’s explanation has generally been adopted (e.g. Tikkanen 2011: 32–33), despite the difficulty that it involves the ACC.PL of the ā-stems being first the model for the spread of -s, and then subsequently itself replaced. Rix’s account can be called into question by two pieces of evidence from opposite ends of the geographical range of the Oscan group. Firstly, the ACC.PL of the feminine anaphoric pronoun in Marrucinian is iafc (Dupraz 2012: 198-9), which is the only ACC.PL form attested in any of the Marrucinian, Paelignian or Vestinian inscriptions. Marrucinian iafc must continue exactly the same form as the Umbrian pronominal form eaf/eaf with an additional particle -c.

The Marrucinian form is often taken to represent the influence of Umbrian on ‘North Oscan’, sharing the same spread of *-f to the ā-stems as found in Umbrian (thus Dupraz 2012: 198 n. 206). However, the same form, also used anaphorically, now turns up in an Oscan legal text written in Greek letters found in Roccagloriosa, ST Lu 62 = Imag. Ital. Buxentum 1: iafk.[^97] These two forms taken together suggest that Oscan, at least in some pronouns, did share the analogical spread of the accusative plural ending -f to the ā-stems.[^98] It is noteworthy that the anaphoric pronoun seems to have escaped the generalisation of the nominal ending -ss which took place in adnominal forms (note the Oscan ACC.FEM.PL ekass of the demonstrative pronoun, used adnominally for example at ekass vı́ass in ST Po 1 = Imag. Ital. Pompei 13). These Oscan forms suggest that the analogical interplay of ACC.PL markers was more complex than envisaged by Rix. Is the overlap between Umbrian eaf/eaf and the Oscan forms significant for their subgrouping against South Picene? South Picene has one possible example of the ACC.FEM.PL of an ā-stem, estas in ST Sp AP 3 = Imag. Ital. Falerio 1, although in an unclear context (Dupraz 2012: 38). However, this form is placed alongside a noun in the same case, fı́tias, and it remains possible that a South Picene anaphoric pronoun would show the same extension of -f from masculine stems as found in Oscan and Umbrian, so the case must remain unproven. The paucity of relevant evidence in Oscan (and indeed in South Picene) makes it unlikely that we will ever be able to elucidate the history of the forms exactly.

## 4. THE SABELLIAN LANGUAGE FAMILY

The subgrouping of the Sabellian language family is undoubtedly complex. The survey above has shown that the supposition of three major subgroups, Oscan, Umbrian and South Picene, as proposed by Rix (2002; 2009), is problematic. Firstly, many of the supposed isoglosses underlying these groups can be shown to be illusory or of late occurrence. For example, the connection between South Picene and the early inscriptions of Southern Italy has been shown to be questionable. The link between Umbrian and the scanty remains of Marsian and Volscian also rests on shaky ground, and the members of the so-called Oscan group share no exclusive isoglosses between themselves. Secondly, the subgrouping proposed privileges the languages for which we have the greatest amount of surviving textual material: Oscan, Umbrian and South Picene. We have seen that there are also short inscriptional texts which appear to represent distinct varieties which otherwise have been lost, such as the Tabula Veliterna, or the text found at Capua, ST Ps 3 = Imag. Ital. Capua 35, which shows some ‘Umbroid’ features (monophthongisation of original *-ōi to -u, use of the pronoun estam) but other archaisms usually more characteristic of Oscan (preservation of final *-ei, and final stop preserved from *-ed). It is convenient to assign these texts to groups that feature better attested languages, but to do so blurs the true picture of variation. Writing before the majority of South Picene texts had been published, Wallace attempted to sort out the subgrouping of the minor Sabellian languages, that is the inscriptions found in the area between the Umbrian and Oscan (Wallace 1984). Wallace’s conclusions are worth repeating at length here (Wallace 1984: 142): ‘it seems impossible to recognise a clearcut dialectal division. Rather, it appears that we must recognise, at least in the pre-historic period, a dialect continuum for the Oscan-Umbrian languages. Oscan may appear at one end of the continuum and Umbrian at the other. In between the two we must recognise truly intermediate dialects’. Nothing published since 1984 argues against this picture of the Sabellian languages in the period after the fifth century, and Wallace’s model of a dialect continuum is strengthened by the examples of phonological change which we can observe taking place across already divergent Sabellian languages. The shared workings of medial syncope, for example, is widely recognised to be a later development, and has often been related to the supposition of an initial-stress accent in Sabellian, which is also observable in the Latin and Etruscan languages at a similar date, leading to medial syncope and various vowel-reduction effects.[^99] We have also seen that there may be some evidence to support the spread of a change of inherited long ū to a front vowel ī in the period after the fifth century (SP/U 5 above), although this remains more questionable. A more striking set of changes, which has affected many (although not all) of the later languages, is the change in the inherited vowel system, with a merger of inherited *i and *ē, and of inherited *ō and *u, and rounding of inherited *ā in final position (discussed in sections 3.5.2 and 3.5.3). Given these shared phonological changes across divergent varieties of Sabellian, is it appropriate to view the history of the Sabellian subgroup in Italy as reflecting a process of convergence, in the terms used by Garrett (1999, 2006)? New discoveries have recently made known some of the early varieties of Sabellian, which have revealed the language family to be more diverse than was previously suspected. In the same way that Garrett tried to show how features that Meillet assigned to ‘Proto-Greek’ could be demonstrated to be post-Mycenaean (Garrett 2006), several of the items that Buck (1928: 7–13) saw as diagnostic for membership of the Oscan-Umbrian (= Sabellian) language family can now be seen as later shared developments: syncope of medial vowels; change of final *-ā; and possibly the change of *kt > ht; not to mention the major restructuring of the vowel system which affected Oscan and Umbrian. These features are all phonological, but the example of the 3PL ending in -ns, which Buck took to be a common Sabellian feature, can now be seen to be limited to the varieties which are attested after 400 BC. We have seen in the last section that the agreement of Oscan and Umbrian on the 3PL perfect ending -ns was used by Adiego Lajara (1990) to argue for a model of the Sabellian family with an early split between an Oscan-Umbrian group and a South Picene branch (with later contact between the speakers of South Picene and Umbrian responsible for some of their shared features).[^100] However, this morphological feature is currently the only isogloss which can be confidently shown to unite the Oscan and Umbrian groups against South Picene and the language of the Tortora Stele. This may of course reflect the paucity of our knowledge of South Picene, for which we do not have comparable examples of other formations known to be innovations in Oscan and Umbrian, such as the newly created future perfect tense.[^101] However, the absence of other evidence in support of a separate Oscan-Umbrian branch of Sabellian does allow us to question whether the ending -ns, whatever its origin, spread across Sabellian dialects through morphological borrowing, or as the shared choice of one of a set of options, some time after the fifth century.[^102] The gaps in our knowledge mean that assessing the inner relations of the Sabellian subgroup of Indo-European will remain a topic for a debate. However, I hope to have shown that the Sabellian languages do provide an interesting test case for models of subgrouping, and incidentally testify to the importance of studying languages of scant attestation. Although it is always possible to lament the paucity of our material for the Sabellian languages, we are in this respect fortunate in that we have a large range of material from a fairly long time-period, without discernible interference through established written literary traditions, or through the overlay of standardised varieties (at least until the very latest stages of attestation).
