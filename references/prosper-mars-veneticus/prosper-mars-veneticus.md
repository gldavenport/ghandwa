## Abstract

this work aims to clarify the etymology of the Pan-Italic divine name Mars, which is attested not only, as usually believed, in Latin and sabellic, but also in the Venetic inscription of Monte Manicola. the divine name Mars is held to reflect an Indo-European verbal noun *mr̥ h2-ti- ‘crushing’, with a well-paralleled semantic evolution. In turn, this etymology gives rise to a broader reappraisal of the “palma-rule”, by which an Indo-European sequence *CR̥ H.C- is said to give Proto-Italic *CaRa.C- under the stress which is apparently coincident with the Greek outcome παλάμη. As will be shown, however, all the extant examples are rather suggestive of an output *CaR.C- with laryngeal loss for this rule, which falls into line with the more general evolution *CR̥ C.C- > *CaR(C).C-.

Nevertheless, a more plausible picture offers itself if we simply identify martsko with the PN Marcus and trace both forms to *martiko-. to mention a possible parallel, the EN Iapodes is mentioned in the Iguvine tablets as Iapuzkum numem. Iapuzkum has been taken from *i̯apod-isko- in order to justify the letter <z>, which in all likelihood is rendering an affricate phoneme. Under a more economical hypothesis that brings it into line with many other ENs, however, I would start from *i̯apod-iko- > -dko- (by syncope) > -tko- (by regressive assimilation of voicing) > -tsko- (by insertion of a sibilant that blocks full regressive assimilation and loss of the coronal segment, as in Venetic). In sum, the Italic family inherited a stem *mart- ‘war god’. the fact that it is now attested in all the subgroups of Italic leaves no room for doubt about the date of this structure. yet, -t-stems are infrequent, and this one is unparalleled. Alternatively, we could consider the possibility that this is a trivial -ti-stem belonging to the semantic sphere of ‘prowess’ or ‘warfare’. therefore, I trace Mars back to an action noun *mr̥ h2-ti- ‘crushing’, typically evolving into an agentive ‘crusher’. the Italic DN is attested mostly in the nominative or dative, which is non-diagnostic as to the inflectional type. In addition, most of the attested forms are common adjectival derivatives in -(i)i̯o-, -īno-, -iko-, none of which gives us any clue. the PN Mamertus betrays its oscan filiation in that it presupposes a resyllabified Vrt.t(i)i̯o-, which eventually underwent glide absorption: cf. mamerttiais (capua), vs. μαμερτοι, μαμερττοι (Potenza). by contrast, the derivative martiālis (instead of the expected †martālis) definitely points to a -ti-stem5. the root form mar- is difficult to explain by any account.
