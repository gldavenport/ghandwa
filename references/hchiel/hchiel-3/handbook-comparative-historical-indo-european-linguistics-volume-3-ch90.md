## 0. Introductory remarks

The history of Baltic syntax is difficult to reconstruct because of the late beginnings of its written tradition. Baltic texts from the 16<sup>th</sup>−18<sup>th</sup> centuries are mostly translations; the Old Prussian and Old Latvian translations, made by Germans with a rudimentary or at best imperfect command of the language, are of such poor quality that, in the case of Old Prussian, their value for syntax is nil, and for Old Latvian, their evidence must be used with caution and confronted with the modern language. For these reasons, Old Prussian will not be dealt with in this chapter. Old Lithuanian translations, though often slavish as well, are more reliable, but many genuinely Lithuanian constructions are not reflected in them because of the character of the mostly religious texts and the influence of the source texts.

## 1. Word classes

1.1. The main word classes in Baltic are verb, noun, adjective, preposition, and adverb, each with clear syntactic properties of its own.

1.2. In Lithuanian, neuter forms of adjectives are not used adnominally as a result of the loss of neuter nouns; they are used as default agreement forms in clauses without nominative subject or with inanimate pronouns as subjects, e.g. <i>te n’est tey pikt akyse tawo</i> ChB Gen. 21.12 ‘let it not be evil in thine eyes’ (<i>pikt</i> < <i>pikta</i>, neuter form of <i>piktas</i> ‘evil’); but they may also express the main predicate in the clause, e. g. <i>jog pikt buwo su jeys</i> ChB Ex. 5.19 ‘that they were in evil case’, lit. ‘that it was bad with them’. They may further shift to semi-verbal status, as in Lith. <i>baisu man</i> ‘I am afraid’, <i>baisu</i> being neuter form of <i>baisus</i> ‘terrifying’. The class of quasi-verbal predicators also comprises nominal forms, as in <i>(man) gėda</i> ‘(I feel) ashamed’ (<i>gėda</i>) ‘shame’, and words of unclear categorial status such as <i>(man) gaila</i> ‘(I feel) sorry’.

1.3. There are no articles, though slavish translation from the German leads to the use of demonstrative pronouns in the function of definite articles in Old Latvian and Old Prussian texts; Latvian residually retains this in Biblical expressions like <i>tas Kungs</i> ‘the Lord’. On the expression of definiteness by definite adjectives arising from enclitic anaphoric pronouns, cf. 6.4.2.

1.4. Derived (relational) adjectives exist but are relatively infrequent; instead, modifier (non-determiner) genitives are used in striking contrast to neighboring Slavic, cf. OLith. <i>aukso jostomis</i> ‘with golden girdles’ ChB Apc 15.6 (though <i>auksinė juosta</i> ‘golden girdle’, with an adjective, also exists), OLatv. <i>ar selta Johstahm</i> ‘id.’ GlB Apc. 15.6; Lith. <i>lietuvių kalba</i> ‘Lithuanian language’, lit. ‘language of the Lithuanians’.

1.5. Numerals and quantifiers in general are a heterogeneous class oscillating between noun-like and adjective-like behaviour; higher numerals are more noun-like and (even when having no substantival endings) tend to govern the genitive of the noun, e.g. Lith. <i>dešimt vaikų</i> ‘ten children:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span>’, whereas lower numerals are adjective-like in agreeing with the noun, e.g. Lith. <i>penki vaikai</i> ‘five:<span class="small-caps">NOM</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span>.<span class="small-caps">MASC</span> children:<span class="small-caps">NOM</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span>’; morphosyntactic interdependence is observed in phrases like Latv. <i>desmitām reižu</i> ‘tens of times’ as against <i>desmitiem logu</i> ‘tens of windows’, where the numeral, being itself in the dative-instrumental plural to express approximative number, agrees with the noun in gender while itself assigning genitive case to the noun.

1.6. Through univerbation, Latvian has developed a class of <i>genitivi tantum</i>, i.e. compound words having only a genitive, such as <i>divzaru</i> (<i>dakšas</i>) ‘two-pronged (fork)’, by univerbation of the genitival modifier <i>divu zaru</i> ‘with (of) two prongs’.

1.7. Whereas Lithuanian uses mainly typical prepositions and postpositions, Latvian makes ample use of relational nouns and adverbs. Relational nouns are nouns with a spatial meaning, placed after the genitive of the noun they refer to, e.g. <i>durvju priekšā</i> ‘door:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span> front:<span class="small-caps">LOC</span>’ ‘in front of the door’. This genitive may, however, be replaced with a dative of external possession whose position with regard to the relational noun is free, e.g. <i>durvīm priekšā, priekšā durvīm, durvīm … priekšā</i>, etc. ‘in front of the door (often with an idea of obstruction)’. Relational adverbs govern nouns in the dative but have no fixed position with regard to them and are not necessarily contiguous to them, e.g. <i>pāri ielai, ielai pāri</i> and <i>ielai … pāri</i> ‘across the street’. These relational adverbs may also be used as verbal particles, as in <i>lidot pāri</i> ‘fly over’, <i>palikt pāri</i> ‘be left over’ etc.

1.8. Preverbs have retained traces of their previous autonomy in Lithuanian and Latvian: enclitics obeying Wackernagel’s Law could be inserted between them and the verbal stem, e.g. <i>Parmidok</i> [<i>par</i>-<i>mi</i>-<i>dok</i>, from <i>par</i>-<i>duoti</i> ‘sell’] <i>ßą dien pirmgimdistę tawo</i> ChB Gen 25.31 ‘Sell me this day thy birthright’. In modern Lithuanian (and Latvian dialects), only the reflexive -<i>si</i>- can be used in this fashion. As these units continue to behave like clitics even though the prefixes have actually lost their autonomy, the notion of “Wackernagel affixes” has been introduced to describe this phenomenon (Nevis and Joseph 1993).

## 2. Nominal morphosyntax

2.1. Baltic has a nominative-type alignment system, using the nominative for intransitive subjects and transitive agents. Nominative and accusative function as structural cases whereas the remaining cases (dative, instrumental, locatives) are used for oblique objects and adverbials. The following features of case marking should be noted.

2.2. Subject and direct object marking show variation enabling a quantitative characterization; forms of marking alternating with the nominative or accusative are a) the genitive of indefinite quantity, less aptly called partitive genitive: OLith. <i>Ir dawe Jokubas Ezawuy <b>donos</b>, ir liszu <b>wirało</b></i> ‘then Jacob gave Esau <b>bread:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span></b> and <b>pottage:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span></b> of lentils’ ChB Gen 25.34; OLatv. <i>teem buhs <b>schehlastibas</b> dabbuht</i> ‘they shall obtain <b>mercy:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span></b>’ GlB Mt 5.7; the same occurs in intransitive subject position with existential verbs, e.g. <i>ira ir <b>kitu</b> be skayćiaus <b>smułku dayktu</b></i> SzPS 129,11 ‘there are countless (lit. ‘without number’) <b>other small things:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span></b>’; Latv. <i>ja tev ir <b>vaļas</b></i> ‘if you have <b>time:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span></b>’; b) the marking of distributive meaning with the preposition Lith. <i>po</i>, Latv. <i>pa</i>, e.g., OLith. <i>koznas eme pa graßy</i> ChB Mt 20.9 ‘they received every man a penny’; <i>noris koznam iß ju tektu po truputi</i> ChB Jn 6.7 ‘that every one of them may take a little’; c) the marking of approximate number by putting the numeral in the dative-instrumental plural governing a genitive noun, e.g. Latv. <i>rodas <b>desmitiem māju</b></i> ‘tens of houses appear’, <i>uzcēla desmitiem māju</i> ‘built tens of houses’ (this construction involves the reanalysis of an adverbial instrumental as a quantifier within the noun phrase, e.g. <i>lasa simtiem grāmatas</i> ‘reads book by the hundred’ → <i>lasa simtiem grāmatu</i> ‘reads hundreds of books’).

2.3. The object of negated verbs is usually in the genitive. This genitive of negation has become dissociated from the partitive genitive (mentioned in 2.2), of which it was originally a subtype. In Lithuanian, the accusative has completely been ousted with negated verbs, e.g. OLith. <b><i>sułaußtos nęndres</i></b> <i>nedałauzs</i> ‘<b>a bruised reed:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span></b> shall he not break’ ChB 12.20; in OLith. the accusative is, however, partly retained, especially when the object precedes the verb: <b><i>tus ßadzius</i></b> <i>nepapeiksit</i> ‘you will not rebuke <b>these words:<span class="small-caps">ACC</span></b>’ MC 4, 30. The subjects of negated intransitive verbs and passives are also occasionally in the genitive, e.g. <i>jemus <b>to</b> nedota</i> ‘to them <b>it:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span></b> is not given’ ChB Mt 13.11. In Latvian, the accusative has ousted the genitive, but this process was not completed until the second half of the 20<sup>th</sup> century. In Common Baltic, genitive and accusative probably co-occurred, their use being driven by syntactic and semantic factors (as in modern Russian). The subsequent generalization of the genitive in Lithuanian and the accusative in Latvian was probably driven by Polish and German influence respectively.

2.4. The genitive, traditionally used for the object with the supine (<i>Isziaio seieias setu <b>sekłos sawa</b></i> ‘A sower went out to sow his seed’ MP 9739), has been transferred also to infinitives of purpose with verbs of motion: <i>notejau ißryßt bet ißpildit <b>jo</b></i> ‘I am not come to destroy but to fulfill <b>it:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span></b> [sc. the Law]’ ChB Mt 5.17, and is used adverbially to express purpose with a motion verb without an infinitive as well, e.g. <b><i>wáisto</i></b> <i>tawę́sp atâimi</i> DC 159₄–₅ ‘I come to thee <b>for remedy:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span></b>’. These features are fully alive in Lithuanian but obsolete in Latvian.

2.5. Baltic makes widespread use of oblique marking for predicate nouns. The predicative instrumental is frequent and is traditionally said to be opposed to the nominative as conveying the notion of a temporary state, e.g. <i>buvo sodininku</i> ‘was a gardener, (temporarily) did a gardener’s job’ as against <i>buvo sodininkas</i> ‘was a gardener’. This distinction seems to be a broader areal feature also encompassing neighboring non-IE languages (cf. Stassen 2001). In Lithuanian, however, the semantic difference has become neutralized, the instrumental being used as a default agreement case in instances where there is neither a nominative subject nor another readily accessible agreement controller. So, e.g., we have agreement with a main clause nominative subject in <i>gatawas esmi … but <b>surißtas</b></i> ‘I am ready … to be <b>bound:<span class="small-caps">NOM</span></b>’ ChB Acts 21.13, but the instrumental in <i>liepe ios <b>linksmais</b> būti</i> ‘bade them be <b>cheerful:<span class="small-caps">INSTR</span></b>’ BP I 83.15. This spread of the instrumental as a default agreement case is probably due to Slavic influence. Latvian, which has lost the instrumental completely, has replaced the predicative instrumental with prepositional phrases containing the preposition <i>par</i>, e. g. <i>Tas Akmins irr <b>par Stuhŗa</b></i>-<i>Akmini tappis</i> GlB Mt 21.42 ‘the stone … is become <b>the head of the corner</b>’. This expression, however, has not acquired the function of a default agreement case but is opposed to the nominative as a marked predicative case, used for nouns, secondary predicates and with verbs denoting change of state.

2.6. The dative of external possession is used in both Baltic languages; whereas in Lithuanian, as in most IE languages, it is mostly restricted to animates and conveys a nuance of affectedness (<i>ta <b>szalczui</b> sutrins galwa</i> ‘she shall bruise <b>the serpent’s:<span class="small-caps">DAT</span></b> head’ BrP II 7.17–18), in Latvian all restrictions have been lifted; thus, virtually any adnominal possessive genitive (also inanimate) can be replaced with a dative, a device usually applied in order to extract the possessor from its fixed position before the noun, e.g. <i>tas Zirwis irr jau <b>teem Kohkeem</b> pee Śakni peelikts</i> GlB Mt 3.10 ‘the axe is laid unto the root of the trees [lit. <b>the trees:<span class="small-caps">DAT</span></b> unto the root]’.

2.7. Nominal agreement (in gender, number, case) is quite regular within the noun phrase but across noun phrases there are numerous instances of neuter agreement even if an agreement controller is present. The inherited neuter singular forms of adjectives and participles (not used adnominally) are often used as default agreement forms in predicative position, e.g. Lith. <i>buvo šalta</i>:<span class="small-caps">NEUTR</span> ‘it was cold’, but also <i>jau ir bulvės kasama</i> ‘the potatoes:<span class="small-caps">NOM</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span> are already being dug:<span class="small-caps">NEUTR</span>’, <i>pradalgiai suvelta</i> ‘the swaths:<span class="small-caps">NOM</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span> are tangled:<span class="small-caps">NEUTR</span>’. This fact should perhaps be viewed in conjunction with the lack of verbal agreement leading to the loss of 3pl. finite verb forms (4.1).

## 3. Adpositional phrases

3.1. Baltic has both prepositions and postpositions; at several stages of its development, areal links with Finnic seem to have given rise to the creation of postpositional phrases. The first wave was common Baltic, and has given rise to a quaternary system of locative cases: the inessive has PIE *-<i>en</i> postposed to the inherited PIE locative which it eventually replaced; the illative has *-<i>nā</i> postposed to the accusative, the adessive has -<i>pi</i> post-posed to the locative and the allative has -<i>pi</i> postposed to the genitive. The process comprised several stages, as the adessive postposition -<i>pi</i> is sometimes added to the new inessive rather than to the original locative, cf. OLith. <i>žmonėsemp</i> < *<i>žmonėsu</i> + -<i>en</i> + -<i>pi</i>. By the time of the oldest Lithuanian texts, these postpositional phrases had already become cases, as suggested by the fact that the original postposition is repeated in every component of the noun phrase, cf. <i>Storasteyp Sergiußiep Powilep zmogumpi ißmintingampi</i> ChB Acts 13.7 ‘with the deputy:<span class="small-caps">ADESS</span> of the country, Sergius:<span class="small-caps">ADESS</span> Paulus:<span class="small-caps">ADESS</span>, a prudent:<span class="small-caps">ADESS</span> man:<span class="small-caps">ADESS</span>’; still, in the case of the adessive (which consists of inessive + -<i>pi</i>), the affix -<i>pi</i> need not be repeated in every component, cf. <i>źmonese bediewiůsemp˙</i> ‘among godless:<span class="small-caps">ADESS</span> people:<span class="small-caps">INESS</span>’ DP 4452.

3.2. Prepositions can govern all oblique cases. In the modern languages, the locative (inessive) can no longer be governed by prepositions, but it is attested with prepositions in Old Lithuanian and it could evidently also be governed by the postposition -<i>pi</i>, yielding the adessives mentioned in 3.1. In Lithuanian, prepositions can govern prepositional phrases, e.g., <i>iš po stalo</i> ‘from under the table’, while in Latvian relational nouns must be used in such instances: <i>no galda <b>apakšas</b></i> ‘from (the place) <b>under</b> the table’.

3.3. In Latvian, the phonetic coalescence of the instrumental with the accusative in the singular and the dative in the plural has given rise to a unique pattern of adpositional government: all prepositions, regardless of the case they govern in the singular, govern the dative (originally instrumental) in the plural, e.g. <i>ap māju</i> ‘around the house:<span class="small-caps">ACC</span>’: <i>ap mājām</i> ‘around the houses:<span class="small-caps">DAT</span>’. This was a consequence of prepositions governing the instrumental being reanalyzed as governing the accusative in the singular and the dative in the plural, a pattern then carried over to prepositions governing the accusative and finally also to prepositions governing the genitive (some dialects have not reached this final state and retain the gen. pl. with prepositions). This pattern has not extended to postpositions, which govern the genitive regardless of number, e.g. <i>drauga</i> / <i>draugu dēļ</i> ‘for the sake of one’s friends(s):<span class="small-caps">GEN</span>’.

## 4. Verbal morphosyntax

4.1. Baltic is characterized by lack of number agreement in the 3<sup>rd</sup> person: the original 3<sup>rd</sup> person singular forms of finite verbs have ousted plural and dual forms. Though morphological explanations for the loss of a distinct 3<sup>rd</sup> pers. pl. ending have been advanced, a syntactic explanation (generalization of the τὰ ζῷα τρέχει rule) seems more plausible especially in view of a similar lack of nominal agreement in predicative position in Baltic (cf. 2.7). The former 3<sup>rd</sup> pers. pl. form seems to be preserved in the nom. pl. masc. form of active present participles (Lith. <i>dirbą</i>). The identity of present 3pl. and plural present participle forms has a striking parallel in Finnic, cf. Est. <i>elavad</i> ‘live:<span class="small-caps">PRS</span>.3<span class="small-caps">PL</span>’ and ‘living:<span class="small-caps">NOM</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span>’ (Cowgill 1970), but the exact nature of the parallelism is still unclear, as it is hard to distinguish cause from result. Modern Baltic retains the non-differentiation of finite 3<sup>rd</sup> person verb forms but in compound tense forms the participle agrees in number with the subject, e.g. Lith. <i>jos yra mačiusios</i>, ‘they:<span class="small-caps">FEM</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span> have seen:<span class="small-caps">FEM</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span>’.

## 5. Periphrastic formations

5.1. A system of periphrastic forms expressing anteriority is based on active past participles combined with the auxiliary ‘be’, cf. <i>koki tulima warga <b>esmi regeyes</b></i> ‘what great misery <b>have I seen</b>’ VE 1,14; <i>Manna Stunda wehl ne <b>irr nahkuśi</b></i> ‘mine hour <b>is</b> not yet <b>come</b>’ GlB Jn 2,4. The formation is structurally parallel to the Slavic formations of the type <i>esmь vidělъ</i>, though the participles used are different; the structural correspondence to Balto-Finnic formations (Finnish <i>olen mennyt</i> ‘I have gone’) is also striking. A nascent system of progressive tenses composed of the auxiliary ‘be’ and the present active participle is observed in Old Lithuanian, e.g. <i>kur Jônas <b>bů kriksztiiąs</b></i> ‘where John <b>was baptizing</b>’ DP 21₃₄–₃₅, but it never fully developed; in modern Lith., its only relic is the “preterite of interrupted action”, e.g. <i>buvo beišeinąs</i> ‘was about to go out’ (referring to an event that was not completed).

5.2. Probably connected (in its oldest shape) with this periphrastic tense system was the Lithuanian-Latvian irrealis, variously called subjunctive, optative, or conditional (whether it existed in Old Prussian as well cannot be ascertained). Probably the oldest form, vestigially preserved in Old Lithuanian, consisted of a preterite of the auxiliary <i>būti</i> ‘be’ and a past active participle, as in <i>jei</i>-<i>b negimęs</i> ‘if he hadn’t been born’ (Mažvydas), with the auxiliary enclitically attached to the conjunction (as in Russian <i>čto</i>-<i>by</i>, Polish <i>gdy</i>-<i>by</i> etc.). This formation has a parallel in OCS <i>bi dělalъ</i>. This oldest attested formation was subsequently replaced with a new form based on the supine in PIE *-<i>tum</i> and the auxiliary ‘be’; this formation was explained by Stang (1966) as having originated in purpose clauses with subjects coreferential with those of the main clause; the construction was, in these conditions, contaminated with that containing the supine, which in itself expressed purpose, e. g. *<i>išėjo jei</i>-<i>bi sėjęs</i> X <i>išėjo sėtų</i> ‘went out to sow’ would have yielded *<i>išėjo jeibi sėtų</i>, with subsequent finitization of the purpose clause and rise of a new 3<sup>rd</sup> person form -<i>bi sėtų</i>. For details, cf. Holvoet (2003).

5.3. An exclusively Latvian periphrastic formation is the debitive, which expresses necessity. In its present form, it consists of an uninflected form containing the marker <i>jā</i>-prefixed to the 3<sup>rd</sup> person indicative present, and a form of the auxiliary ‘be’. In the case of ‘be’, the form is <i>jābūt</i>, which suggests that the form was originally based on the infinitive. As established by Prellwitz (1904), the debitive arose from an infinitival relative purpose close, the element <i>jā</i> being a reflex of the PIE relative pronoun *<i>ye</i>/<i>o</i>-. In Old Latvian, the original meaning is partly retained, cf. <i>newa jums kas <b>ja ehd</b>?</i> GlB Jn 21.5) ‘have ye [anything] <b>which to eat</b>’. In such constructions, several case forms of the relative pronoun must originally have been used, but the shape generalized in modal meaning was the genitive <i>jā</i>- (a genitive of negation), which suggests that the exact source construction was something like *<i>nav jāsaka</i> ‘there is nothing to say’ → ‘there is no need to say’, with subsequent introduction of an affirmative form <i>jāsaka</i> ‘one must say’. For further details, cf. Holvoet (1998).

5.4. Combinations of the copula ‘be’ with passive participles yield a periphrastic passive in Lithuanian; the oldest variety has past passive participles (continuing the PIE verbal adjective in *-<i>to</i>-): <i>kas neintykies <b>bus papeyktas</b></i> ChB Mk 16.16 ‘he that believeth not <b>shall be damned</b>’; in part of the Lith. dialects, an imperfective passive based on the present passive participle in -<i>mo</i>- has arisen: <i>Sunus zmogaus <b>izdodamas est</b></i> ChB Mk 14.41 ‘the Son of man <b>is (being) betrayed</b>’. In Latvian, combinations of the copula with the passive participle in *-<i>to</i>- are used as a resultative passive, whereas the dynamic passive (<i>Vorgangspassiv</i>) requires a copula with the meaning ‘become’ (<i>tikt, tapt</i>) rather than ‘be’, which seems to reflect German influence: <i>tas Wihns tohp isgahsts</i> GlB Mk 2.22 ‘the wine is (lit. ‘becomes’) spilled’. Latvian has not shared in the introduction of a passive based on the participle in -<i>mo</i>-; the corresponding constructions have the modal meanings of possibility or necessity, e.g. <i>labība <b>ir pļaujama</b></i> ‘the corn <b>must/can be reaped</b>’.

5.5. Baltic frequently uses syntactic reduplication in the verb phrase to convey a categorical claim or demand. The finite verb form can be echoed by a participle, as in Latv. <i>nāc nākdams</i> ‘be sure to come’; cf. OLatv. GlB Gen. 2.17 <i>mirdams mirsi</i> ‘thou shalt surely die (lit. ‘dying shalt thou die’)’ (echoing Hebr. <i>môt tāmût</i>, Lat. <i>morte morieris</i>). Lithuanian uses special deverbal adverbs in -<i>te</i> for this purpose: <i>prašyte prašė</i> ‘urgently begged’. Reduplication involving infinitives alongside finite verb forms is used to bring out topic-comment structure, e.g., <i>žadėti žadėjo</i> ‘promise (s)he did’.

## 6. Word order

6.1. Sentential word order is subject to considerable variation: it is now predominantly SVO, but Ambrazas (most recently 2006: 66−76) assumes SOV to have been widespread in Common Baltic on the basis of Lithuanian folk texts, proverbs, etc. This would be in keeping with other instances of dependent-head order such as genitive order and the tendency towards noun-adposition order manifesting itself in Common Baltic.

6.2. Unlike sentential word order, word order in the noun phrase is subject to strict rules. Adjectives always precede nouns, except for special stylistic purposes. The same holds for demonstrative and possessive pronouns. The adnominal genitive is preposed in Lith and Latv, though the OLith. and OLatv. translations often slavishly follow the word order of the German or Polish originals, e.g. <i>wisoki krumą <b>łauko</b></i> ‘every plant <b>of the field</b>’ ChB Gen 2.5 alongside <i>padare Diewas <b>łauko</b> źwery</i> ‘God made the beast <b>of the earth</b>’ ibid. 1.25; OLatv. <b><i>tahs Paśaules</i></b> <i>Gaiśchums</i> ‘the light <b>of the world</b>’ Mt 5.14, but also <i>ta Kahriba <b>śchihs Dsihwośchanas</b></i> Mt 13.22 ‘the care <b>of this world</b>’. Actual usage was probably close to what it is now, i.e., the genitive was probably anteposed in most cases.

Modern Lithuanian deviates from the anteposition rule if the genitive is accompanied by a heavy modifier such as a relative clause, e.g. <i>žmonių įpročiai</i> ‘people’s habits’ but <i>įpročiai žmonių, kuriuos pažįstu</i> ‘the habits of people I know’. Latvian, on the other hand, sticks to the rule with exceptionless regularity, which is perhaps to be connected with the Finnic substratum, as Finnic regularly has anteposition; if word order has to be changed, the possessive dative (<i>Pertinenzdativ</i>) is used, e.g. <i>norauj pavedienu <b>pasakai</b>, kuras turpinājumu karalis vēlas dzirdēt</i> ‘she breaks off the thread <b>of the tale</b> [lit. to the tale:<span class="small-caps">DAT</span>], whose continuation the King wishes to hear’ (with the genitive, only <i>pasakas pavediens</i> is possible).

The partitive genitive is the only adnominal genitive to be regularly postposed, which evidently reflects the shift of the head word to the status of quantifier, e.g. <i>sudelu <b>ßałto</b> tykt <b>wandenia</b></i> ChB Mt 10.42, <i>ar weenu Biķķeri <b>śalta Uhdens</b></i> GlB Mt 10.42 ‘(with) a cup <b>of cold water</b> only’.

6.3. Adposition order. Inherited PIE prepositions retain their prepositional status, e.g. Lith. <i>į namą</i> ‘into the house’. A marked predilection for postpositional phrases can be noted at several stages of the history of Baltic: first, in common Baltic (cf. 3.1), and later on in Latvian; this is consistent with the dependent-head order in the noun phrase. Lithuanian, where the Finnic adstratum influence was ousted by that of Slavic, has in most instances abandoned the postpositional pattern; Lith. <i>dėl</i>, which, like Latvian <i>dēļ</i>, seems to be a borrowing from Old Russian <i>dělja</i>, appears as a postposition in the fossilized <i>todėl</i> ‘therefore’, but has now become a preposition (<i>dėl ligos</i> ‘because of illness’), whereas Latvian <i>dēļ</i> is still a postposition. Reversal of the “dependent genitive-head noun” order is also symptomatic of the development of a relational noun into an adposition, as in Lith. <b><i>gale</i></b> <i>stalo</i> ‘<b>at the end</b> of the table’, <b><i>vidury</i></b> <i>kiemo</i> ‘<b>in the middle</b> of the courtyard’ (alongside <i>stalo gale, kiemo vidury</i>). In Latvian, this is exceptional (<i>vidū jūras</i> ‘in the middle of the sea’ is attested in the dialects): here prepositions encounter the competition of postpositional expressions provided by relational nouns (cf. 1.7); these function as species of postpositions, e.g., <i>priekšā</i> ‘in front of’, locative of <i>priekša</i> ‘front’, in <i>lai juhśo Gaiśchiba śpihd <b>Ļauscho preekścha</b></i> GlB Mt 5.16 ‘let your light shine <b>before men</b>’; this can be regarded as an instance of Finnic influence.

### 6.4. Loss of clitics

6.4.1. Both Lithuanian and Latvian have lost the enclitic forms of personal and reflexive pronouns; they are vestigially attested in Old Lithuanian, unattested in Latvian: OLith. <i>reykia mi eÿt</i> ‘I must go’ ChB Lk 14.18 as against modern Lith. <i>man reikia eit</i>, where <i>man</i> is orthotonic. Only the enclitic reflexive pronoun, also originally a sentential clitic (<i>wisesi tur ischpaßinti</i> MT 2a, 5−6 ‘all should confess’, where -<i>si</i> is attached to the sentence-initial subject; MLith. <i>visi turi išsipažinti</i>), through entering into a closer relationship with the verb, eventually became attached to it, either word-finally as in <i>rengiuo</i>-<i>si</i> ‘I get dressed’ or (now only in part of the Lithuanian and Latvian dialects) inserted between preverb and verbal stem as in Lith. <i>ap</i>-<i>si</i>-<i>rengti</i> ‘get dressed’ alongside <i>ap</i>-<i>rengti</i> ‘dress’. This probably passed through the stage of a phrasal clitic, as shown by instances like <i>delsi rangianćiu zmoniu</i> ‘because of the thronging people’ SE 178.15, with <i>si</i> in Wackernagel position after the preposition <i>dėl</i> (modern Lithuanian would have <i>dėl be</i>-<i>si</i>-<i>rangiančių žmonių</i>, with the continuative particle <i>be</i>- prefixed to the verb form in order to enable insertion of the reflexive marker). This process is connected with a functional shift: the original (suffixal or infixal) reflexive pronoun has become an inflectional (middle voice) marker, whereas reflexive function has been taken over by the orthotonic reflexive pronoun as in <i>mato save</i> ‘sees himself/herself’. In general, the loss of pronominal clitics is parallelled by a similar development in East Slavic.

6.4.2. Another clitic that has shifted to morphology, probably through the intermediate stage of a phrasal clitic, is the anaphoric-relative pronoun *<i>ye</i>/<i>o</i>-, which has acquired the function of a definiteness marker in combination with adjectives (<i>geras</i>-<i>is žmogus</i> ‘the good man’ as against <i>geras žmogus</i> ‘a good man’) but is still residually attested in Old Lithuanian as a phrasal clitic functioning in phrases of different types as a marker of their functioning as definite descriptions, e.g. <i>widurįięio nuraminimo</i> ‘of inner appeasement’ (<i>viduryje</i> ‘inside:<span class="small-caps">LOC</span>’ + <i>jo</i> ‘<span class="small-caps">REL</span>:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span>’ <i>nuraminimo</i> ‘appeasement:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span>’); <i>Tewas jusu danguis</i> ‘your heavenly Father’ (<i>dangų</i> ‘heaven:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span>’ + <i>jis</i> ‘<span class="small-caps">REL</span>:<span class="small-caps">NOM</span>’).

6.4.3. Non-pronominal clitics are well attested in Old Lithuanian and Latvian, cf. the OLith. interrogative -<i>gu</i>, as in <i>walgiey</i>-<i>gu iß ano medzia</i> ChB Gen. 3.11 ‘hast thou eaten of the tree’ or the preposition -<i>drinag</i> ‘because of’, as in <i>to</i>-<i>drinag mêßło smírdincżio</i> ‘because of this stinking filth’ DP 15.43. They have completely disappeared in the modern languages.

## 7. Sentential syntax

### 7.1. Impersonal sentences

Two types will be distinguished here: sentences with zero subjects and sentences based on verbs with oblique marking for all noun phrases.

7.1.1. Null subjects with active verb forms in indefinite but referential function (as English <i>they</i>, German <i>man</i> etc.) occur in Lithuanian and Latvian, cf. OLith. <i>Sunu zmogaus <b>izdoda</b> ing rąkas grießniku</i> ‘the Son of man <b>is betrayed</b> (lit. ‘[they] betray the Son of man’) into the hands of sinners’ ChB Mt 26.45; owing to the homonymy of 3<sup>rd</sup> pers. sg. and pl. forms this construction is completely undifferentiated in number; Latvian has, however, introduced a number differentiation that becomes manifest when the verb form contains an agreeing participle; the pl. variety has an indefinite specific subject, e.g. <i>mani meklējuši</i> ‘they (reportedly) looked for me’, whereas the sg. variety (with the participle and predicate noun in the nom. masc. sg.) implies an indefinite non-specific subject, e.g. <i>ja nav ēdis</i> ‘if one has not eaten’. The latter construction seems to have arisen under Finnic influence (cf. Holvoet 1995).

7.1.2. Many verbs, especially describing mental and physical states, have oblique (usually dative) marking for the experiencer and consequently cannot combine with nominative subjects, e.g. <i>gayli <b>mi tos mines</b></i> ChB Mt 15.32 ‘<b>I (<span class="small-caps">DAT</span>)</b> have compassion <b>on the multitude (<span class="small-caps">GEN</span>)</b>’; <i>śwehtigi irr tee</i> … <b><i>kam</i></b> <i>ślahpst pehz tahs Taiśnibas</i> GlB Mt 5.6 ‘Blessed are they <b>which</b> [lit. ‘whom:<span class="small-caps">DAT</span>’] do […] thirst after righteousness’; cf. also modern Lith. <i>man skauda galvą</i> ‘my head aches’ (<i>man</i> ‘1<span class="small-caps">SG</span>.<span class="small-caps">DAT</span>’ <i>skauda</i> ‘ache:<span class="small-caps">PRS</span>.3’ <i>galvą</i> ‘head:<b>ACC</b>’).

### 7.2. Evidential marking

Participles are often used instead of finite verb forms as the main predicative form of the sentence with an evidential meaning: Lith. <i>čia žmonės gyvenę</i> [žmonės:people:<span class="small-caps">NOM</span> live:<span class="small-caps">PART</span>.<span class="small-caps">ACT</span>.<span class="small-caps">PRET</span>.<span class="small-caps">NOM</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span>.<span class="small-caps">MASC</span>] ‘people have reportedly / evidently been living here’. They are also often used in complement clauses after <i>verba dicendi</i>, e.g. OLatv. <i>Ko śakka tie Ļaudis</i> / <i>ka es tha Zilweka Dehls <b>eśśohts</b></i>? ‘Whom do men say that I the Son of man <b>am:<span class="small-caps">PART</span>.<span class="small-caps">PRES</span>.<span class="small-caps">NOM</span>.<span class="small-caps">SG</span>.<span class="small-caps">MASC</span></b>?’ GlB Mt 16.13. Though the origin of this feature is disputed, the most likely explanation is that it originated from the analytic perfect through deletion of the auxiliary ‘be’, e.g. Lith. <i>jis gyvenęs</i> ‘he reportedly/evidently lived’ from <i>jis yra gyvenęs</i> ‘he has lived’; the construction, originally restricted to past participles, subsequently spread to other participles (an explanation advanced by Litvinov 1989). Lithuanian and Latvian evidentials show diverging developments: whereas the Lithuanian evidential shows a meaning cluster consisting of inferential, mirative, and quotative, the Latvian evidential is exclusively a hearsay marker. Typological parallels suggest that the Lithuanian state of affairs is older and that Latvian narrowed the meaning of the evidential.

In Lithuanian, evidential constructions also contain passive participles, and they are then formally similar to passives; such evidential pseudo-passives are derived mostly from intransitive verbs, e.g. <i>čia vagies būta</i> [thief:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span>.<span class="small-caps">SG</span> be:<span class="small-caps">PART</span>.<span class="small-caps">PRET</span>.<span class="small-caps">PASS</span>.<span class="small-caps">NEUTR</span>] ‘a thief has been at work here’; such constructions may contain several passive participles, i.e. both the auxiliary ‘be’ and the main verb may be in the participial form, e.g. <i>mano būta užmigta</i> [me:<span class="small-caps">GEN</span> be:<span class="small-caps">PART</span>.<span class="small-caps">PRET</span>.<span class="small-caps">PASS</span>.<span class="small-caps">NEUTR</span> fall-asleep:<span class="small-caps">PART</span>.<span class="small-caps">PRET</span>.<span class="small-caps">PASS</span>.<span class="small-caps">NEUTR</span>)] ‘I must have dozed off’. The fact that these constructions violate many typological generalizations about passives (e.g., they may be derived from non-accusative verbs) suggests that they must be set apart from the passive proper.

### 7.3. The possessive construction and modal constructions evolved from it

For Common Baltic, a possessive construction of the type <i>mihi est</i> can probably be reconstructed (cf. Vykypěl 2000); it is still the only possessive construction in Latvian, whereas Lithuanian has it only residually (e.g. in expressing age: <b><i>Zmoguy</i></b> <i>nes <b>anamuy buwo</b> daugiaus neyg kiaturios deßymtis metu</i> ‘<b>that man was</b> more than forty years old’ ChB Acts 7.23). Old Prussian and Lithuanian have introduced the verb OPr. <i>turritwei</i>, Lith. <i>turėti</i>, originally ‘hold’ for ‘having’, perhaps under the influence of Slavic <i>iměti</i>. The original use of a Common Baltic possessive construction <i>mihi est</i> is attested indirectly by the existence of modal constructions which it underlies. Expanded with an infinitive, it yields a necessitive construction, as in OLith. <b><i>buwa taw</i></b> <i>důti mana pinnigus Maininikamus</i> ‘<b>Thou oughtest</b> … to have put my money to the exchangers’ BrB Mt 25.27, OLatv. <i>kur Kristum <b>bij dsimt</b></i> ‘where Christ <b>should be born</b>’ GlB Mt 2.4. The construction is now obsolescent in both Lithuanian and Latvian; in Lithuanian, it has been superseded by <i>turėti</i> ‘have’ and ‘must’. In Latvian, it has been replaced with the debitive, also based on the possessive construction <i>mihi est</i> (cf. above).

### 7.4. Nominative objects

Nominative objects originated in the necessitive ‘be’ + <span class="small-caps">DAT</span>. + <span class="small-caps">INF</span>. mentioned in 7.3. The infinitive was originally an adverbial infinitive of purpose, e.g. [<i>man yra javai</i>] <i>pjauti</i> ‘I have corn to reap’ yielded <i>man yra</i> [PRO <i>javai pjauti</i>] ‘I have to reap corn’. After having lost its subject status, the nominative was replaced with the accusative in part of the Lith. dialects (<i>man javus pjauti</i>), but Eastern High Lithuanian retained it (<i>man javai pjauti</i>). In the dialects retaining the nominative, this pattern was extended to embedded clauses with impersonal (not only modal) verbs. The nominative object has been discussed in the context of the Finnic construction with nominative object; though it can be explained as an indigenous development, the areal context could certainly have contributed to the retention of the nominative.

### 7.5. Rise of the passive construction

Passives may be personal (<i>ziankłas <b>nebus</b> ieÿ <b>dotas</b></i> ChB Mt 12.39 ‘<b>there shall no</b> sign <b>be given</b> to it’) or impersonal (<i>tuskiankit ó <b>bus</b> jums <b>atadarijta</b></i> ‘knock, and <b>it shall be opened</b> unto you’ ChB Mt 7.7). The agent phrase in the passive construction originated as a possessive genitive originally functioning as a modifier to the noun phrase: in Lith. <i>tėvo pastatyti namai</i> ‘the house built by father’, we must postulate an original constituent structure [<i>tėvo</i> [<i>pastatyti namai</i>]] ‘father’s built house’. The possessive character of the genitive is evident from the use of otherwise specifically adnominal genitive forms in the case of certain pronouns in Lithuanian: <i>mano pastatyti namai</i> ‘the house built by me’ (with the adnominal genitive as in <i>mano namai</i> ‘my house’ as opposed to the adverbal genitive in <i>bijo manęs</i> ‘fears me’).

Subsequently a reanalysis must have taken place that drew the originally possessive genitive into the adjectival (participial) phrase, with a concomitant shift from the original possessive meaning to agentive meaning: Latv. [[<i>tēva celta</i>] <i>māja</i>] ‘the house built by father’. The shift of the participial phrase to the position of predicate nominal gives rise to an agentive construction, i.e. a sentence pattern used to identify the agent of an accomplished action: <i>māja ir</i> [<i>tēva celta</i>] ‘the house is built by father’ (‘father-built’). To this day, the Latvian agentive genitive may not be extracted from the participial phrase to become directly dependent on the verb, so that Latvian has no real agented passive, but only an agentive construction (until the 19<sup>th</sup> century, an agented passive with an agent phrase introduced by <i>no</i> ‘from’ was used under German influence, but it was ousted from the standard language in the 20<sup>th</sup> century). This extraction has occurred in Lithuanian, where the genitive can now occupy any position and is used not only in the stative (resultative) passive, but also in the dynamic passive, as in <b><i>Tėvo</i></b> <i>buvo pastatytas namas</i> ‘<b>By father</b> a house was built.’ This construction has been extended to intransitive verbs, yielding the passive-like evidential mentioned in 7.2.

### 7.6. Double dative construction

In Lithuanian, both subject and object of an adverbial or adnominal infinitive of purpose (which is itself originally the dative of a verbal noun) are in the dative; this construction is comparable to the Sanskrit construction <i>áhaye hántavaí</i> RV V.31.4 ‘for destroying the dragon’, e.g. Lith. <i>tinklas <b>žuvims</b> gaudyti</i> ‘a net to catch <b>fish:<span class="small-caps">DAT</span></b>’, OLith. <i>Lawonus kitus ing wandeni mete <b>Szuwims</b> walgijti</i> Mž 427₁₂–₁₃ ‘threw the corpses into the water <b>for the fish:<span class="small-caps">DAT</span></b> to eat’. Ambrazas has convincingly argued (against the attraction theory, which assumes the object case to have been attracted by the dative infinitive) that two co-ordinated datives of purpose are involved. In Latvian, the double dative construction is attested only with the dative in subject function: OLatv. <i>Kur pirkam mehs Maises <b>ścheem</b> ehst?</i> ‘Whence shall we buy bread, that <b>these</b> may eat [i.e. for them to eat]?’ GlB Jn 6.5, and even this variety has been largely eliminated though insertion of a relative pronoun, yielding an infinitival relative clause, e.g. <i>galotne <b>kur putnam</b> uzmesties</i> ‘a tree-top <b>for a bird</b> to alight on’ instead of <i>galotne <b>putnam</b> uzmesties</i>.

### 7.7. Rise of embedded infinitival clauses

Infinitival clauses arise as a result of reanalysis of constructions with an infinitive of purpose. A dative complement belonging to the main clause is interpreted as an embedded clause subject, which is reflected in word order and manifest from the semantic interpretation of the sentence. Cf. OLith. <i>neger žmogui būti vienam</i> ‘It is not good that man should be alone (<i>non est bonum esse hominem solum</i>)’, where the dative is originally a main clause complement (‘to be alone is not good for man’) and is subsequently reanalyzed as an infinitival clause subject: <i>neger</i> [<i>žmogui būti vienam</i>] ‘for man to be alone is not good’. The functional equivalent of the Greek and Latin <i>accusativus cum infinitivo</i> is thus, in principle, the <i>dativus cum infinitivo</i>, but the scope of this construction is quite restricted; usually the syntactic status of the dative in constructions with infinitival clauses is ambiguous between main clause complement and subject of the infinitive. Whether or not the subject of the infinitive is explicitly expressed, all nominals expected to agree with it were originally in the dative, and this has been retained in Latvian: OLatv. <i>kas aiskawe <b>manni kristitam</b> tapt</i> ‘what doth hinder <b>me (<span class="small-caps">ACC</span>)</b> to be <b>baptized (<span class="small-caps">PART</span>. <span class="small-caps">PRET</span>. <span class="small-caps">PASS</span>. <span class="small-caps">DAT</span>. <span class="small-caps">SG</span>. <span class="small-caps">MASC</span>)</b>?’ ChB Acts 8.36; MLatv. <i>lūgsim <b>viņu</b> būt mūsu <b>viesim</b></i> ‘let us ask <b>him (<span class="small-caps">ACC</span>)</b> to be our <b>guest (<span class="small-caps">DAT</span>)</b>’. Lithuanian has abandoned this agreement pattern, retaining the datives only when the implicit subject of the infinitive is coreferential with a main clause complement in the dative, e.g. <i>Diewas prisake <b>mums Mielaschirdingiems</b> buti</i> BrP II 253,4 ‘God bade <b>us (<span class="small-caps">DAT</span>)</b> be <b>merciful (<span class="small-caps">DAT</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span>.<span class="small-caps">MASC</span>)</b>’<b>;</b> in other cases the instrumental was substituted for the original dative, e.g. <i>liepe <b>ios linksmais</b> buti</i> ‘bade <b>them (<span class="small-caps">ACC</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span>.<span class="small-caps">MASC</span>)</b> be <b>cheerful (<span class="small-caps">INSTR</span>.<span class="small-caps">PL</span>.<span class="small-caps">MASC</span>)</b>’.

### 7.8. Participial clauses

Lithuanian and Latvian have an abundance of participial constructions, part of them absolute. The absolute construction has a subject in the dative, if it is overtly expressed, and the participle is also in the dative, sometimes truncated: OLith. <i>Ir iszejo Izaokas mełstis łaukań, <b>besiartynańt wakaruy</b></i> ‘And Isaac went out to meditate in the field <b>at eventide</b> (lit. ‘at the approach of the evening’)’ ChB Gen 24,63; OLatv. <b><i>wiņņam śehjot</i></b><i>, kritte zitta Zeļļmalî</i> GlB Mt 13.4 ‘And <b>when he sowed</b>, some seeds fell by the wayside’; the indeclinable forms are also used without overt subjects, as in MLith. <i>auštant</i> ‘at daybreak’.

The <i>accusativus cum participio</i>, though most frequent with verbs of perception, extends to cognition verbs and verbs of saying (corresponding to the AcI of other languages; the Baltic <i>dativus cum infinitivo</i> is not used with such verbs): OLith. <i>ko <b>mane sant</b> sakote</i> ‘whom say ye that <b>I am</b>’ ChB Mt 16.15 (with a truncated present active participle), OLatv. <i>Saduzzeeŗi kas śakka Augścham <b>zelśchanu</b> ne <b>eśśam</b></i> GlB Mt 22.23 ‘the Sadducees, who say that <b>there is</b> no <b>resurrection</b>’ (with a truncated accusative of the present passive participle).

Participles may also replace the finite verb in relative clauses and certain (interrogative) complement clauses if the subjects are coreferential, e.g. OLith. <i>ne żinóio ką <b>bîłąs</b></i> ‘he did not know what <b>he said</b> [lit. ‘what saying’]’ DP 594.50, OLatv. <i>tas Wehjśch puhśch kur <b>gribbedams</b></i> ‘the wind bloweth where <b>it listeth</b> [lit. where wishing]’ GlB Jn 3.8.

### 7.9. Finite complement and adverbial clauses

In Lithuanian, complementation types are distinguished by mood and sometimes also by specialized complementizers: in OLith. we have <i>jog</i> + indicative for truth-valued complements, e.g., <i>zynojau <b>jog esi</b> zmogus kietas</i> ‘I knew <b>that thou art</b> an hard man’ ChB Mt 25.24, and <i>kad</i> (<i>idant</i>) + subjunctive for state-of-affairs complements: <i>praße <b>kad ißeytu</b> iß ju rubezu</i> ‘they besought him <b>that he would depart</b> out of their coasts’ ChB Mt 8.34. In MLith, only mood distinguishes truth-valued from state-of-affairs complements, the same complementizer <i>kad</i> being used for both. <i>Kad</i> also introduces final, conditional, temporal, and other adverbial clauses, though alternative subordinators are also available.

Latvian has changed this system by introducing the hortative adverb <i>lai</i> (older form <i>laid</i>, the imperative of <i>laist</i> ‘let’, used in hortative constructions of the type <i>lai nāk</i> ‘let him/her/them come’) as a deontic complementizer (as opposed to <i>ka</i>, which is used mainly in epistemic complements): OLatv. <i>śacki teem Bährneem Jsrael <b>Lai tee eet</b></i> ‘tell the children of Israel <b>that they should go</b>’ (where <i>lai tie iet</i> originally meant ‘let them go’); here the choice of the mood form is no longer used to mark the type of complement clause but may be used to mark a greater or lesser degree of likelihood of the event occurring. On the other hand, <i>lai</i> has also become a conjunction in final, consecutive, concessive, and other meanings (also with indicative or subjunctive according to the degree of reality), e.g. <i>lai viņš nāk</i> / <i>nāktu</i> ‘in order that he should come:<span class="small-caps">IND</span>/<span class="small-caps">SUBJ</span>’.
