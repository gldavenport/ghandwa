## 1. Noun formation

In the Baltic languages, there are relatively few compound words (Skardžius 1943: 393 ff.; Urbutis 1965: 252, 437 f.). Particularly old is Li. <i>viešpatìs</i> ‘lord’ (cf. OInd. <i>viś</i><i>pátiḥ</i> ‘chief of a settlement or tribe’). The absolute majority of derived nouns and adjectives in Baltic are suffixal and ending derivatives. Various derivational affixes have been studied for a long time (cf. particularly Leskien 1891; Endzelīns 1943, 1948, 1951; Skardžius 1943; Otrębski 1965). Vincas Urbutis (1965, 1978) began to apply modern methods to the study of noun formation and, relying on these, exhaustively described the system of noun formation in contemporary Lithuanian, distinguishing thereby the various semantic derivational categories. A derivational category is a class of derivatives which have a common derivational meaning and special derivational affixes. Recent research shows that Baltic distinguishes three old categories of nouns derived from verbs [1.1−1.3] (Ambrazas 1993) and five old categories derived from nouns [1.4−1.8] (Ambrazas 2000a).

### 1.1. Nomina actionis

Originally in the Baltic languages (just as in PIE; cf. Benveniste 1948), in this derivational category, derivatives with *-<i>ti</i>- and *-<i>tu</i>- predominated (cf. Li. <i>būtìs</i> and Sl. *<i>bytĭ</i>, OInd. <i>bhūtís</i> ‘existence, being’; Li. <i>lietùs</i>, Latv. <i>liêtus</i> ‘rain’). Forms of the infinitive and supine developed from these. Later in the Baltic languages, derivational endings became more widespread, particularly with *-<i>o</i>- (cf. Li. <i>miẽgas</i>, Latv. <i>mìegs</i> ‘sleep’) and *-<i>ā</i> (cf. Li. <i>snaudà</i>, Latv. <i>snaũda</i> ‘somnolence’).

During the independent development of Lithuanian, derivatives with the suffix -<i>imas</i>/ -<i>ymas</i> (< *-<i>ī</i>-<i>mo</i>-), e.g. <i>piešìmas</i> ‘drawing’, and in Latvian derivatives with the suffix -<i>šana</i> (< -<i>sjo</i>-<i>nā</i>), e.g. <i>bûšana</i> ‘existence’, which came from another adjective suffix *-<i>no</i>-/-<i>nā</i>, were created. Close to the latter are derivatives in -<i>s</i>-<i>na</i>, which are productive in Old Prussian, e.g. <i>billīsna</i> ‘sayings’ (see further Bammesberger 1973: 87 ff.; Schmalstieg 1974: 64 ff.; Parenti 1998). Derivatives of similar origin in Lithuanian with -<i>s</i>-<i>e</i>-<i>na</i> are productive only in the Lower Lithuanian dialect (Urbutis 1965: 295), spoken in an area where at one time the Curonians lived; cf. Low. Li. <i>eĩsena</i> ‘going, walking’.

### 1.2. Nomina agentis

In the Baltic languages, derivatives of adjectival character with the suffixes *-<i>tā</i>-<i>jo</i>-, *-<i>ē</i>-<i>jo</i>-, e.g. Li. <i>artójas</i>, Latv. <i>arājs</i> (< *<i>artājs</i>), OP <i>artoys</i> ‘plowman’; Li. <i>siuvė́jas</i>, Latv. <i>šuvējs</i> ‘tailor’ play the most important role in this derivational category. Here they have completely ousted the names of actors with the old suffixes *-<i>tel</i>-/-<i>ter</i>-/-<i>tor</i>-, cf. Sl. *<i>datel’ĭ</i> and OInd. <i>dā´tar</i>-<i>, dātár</i>-, Gk. <i>dṓtōr, dōtḗr</i> beside Li. <i>davė́jas</i> ‘giver, donor’ (Sɫawski 1976: 50).

### 1.3. Nomina instrumenti

The basis of this derivational category consists of derivatives with *-<i>tlo</i>- which have cognates in many related languages; cf. Li. <i>árklas</i>, Latv. <i>arˆkls</i>, Gk. <i>árotron</i>, etc. ‘plough’. However, in the Baltic languages, they began to use suffixes derived from the <i>nomen actionis</i> suffix *-<i>tu</i>- very widely to form the names of tools or instruments. These include the suffixes *-<i>tuv</i>-/*-<i>tov</i>-/*-<i>tev</i>-; cf. Li. <i>sėtùvas, sėtuvė˜</i> and Latv. <i>sȩ̄tuvs, sȩ̄tuve, sȩ̄tuva, sȩ̄tuvis, sȩ̄tava, sȩ̄tave, sȩ̄teve</i> ‘bast basket’; <i>káištuvė</i>/<i>kaištùvė</i> ‘scraper, shaving knife’, <i>kaištùvas</i> ‘scraper, knife for scraping hides or skin’, and OP <i>coestue</i> ‘brush’, etc.

### 1.4. Nomina qualitatis

At one time, the <i>nomina qualitatis</i> were formed essentially with the same derivational suffixes as the <i>nomina actionis</i> (Ambrazas 1994). These categories were better distinguished in the Baltic languages when derivatives with the suffix *-<i>ībā</i> became more widespread; cf. Li. dial. <i>lýgyba</i>, Latv. <i>līdzība</i> ‘equality’.

### 1.5. Nomina collectiva

In OP, there is a whole group of collective derivatives with the old derivational ending in *-<i>ā</i>; cf. <i>slayo</i> ‘sled’ (cf. Li. <i>šlãjos</i> ‘sled’): <i>slayan</i> ‘sled runner’ (for more about these see Mažiulis 1981; Degtjarev 1994). In Lithuanian and Latvian, these are very rare; cf. Li. <i>álksna</i>/<i>alksnà</i> ‘alder grove’, Latv. <i>álksna</i> ‘swampy place’. Nevertheless, the derivational ending *-<i>ā</i> most likely serves as the basis for the collective plural ending -<i>ai</i> (cf. Li. dial. <i>liepaĩ</i> ‘lime grove’, <i>siuvėjaĩ</i> ‘tailor’s family’; see Stundžia 1981, 1992). The rather old affix *-<i>ij</i>-<i>ā</i> appears in Li. <i>brolijà</i> ‘brothers and sisters’ and Sl. <i>bratrĭja</i> ‘brothers’, Attic Gk. <i>phratría</i> vs. Homeric <i>phrḗtrē</i> ‘clan, tribe’, and there are also some newer suffixes (Ambrazas 1992a, 2004a: 50−51).

The Balts also use collective nouns with the adjective suffix *-<i>ī</i>-<i>no</i>- (cf. Li. <i>šeimýna</i>, OP <i>seimīns</i> ‘domestic servants’ as well as Li. <i>beržýnas</i> and Sl. *<i>berzina</i> ‘birch grove’) (Sɫawski 1974: 121, 123), which is also encountered partially in the Italic languages and perhaps also in German and Albanian (Jokl 1963: 133−134; Butler 1971: 27−28) and Thracian (Duridanov 1969: 57).

### 1.6. Nomina feminina

Particularly old are the <i>nomina feminina</i> with the derivational ending *-<i>ā</i> (which earlier had been characteristic of <i>nomina collectiva</i>); cf. OLi. <i>ašva</i>, OInd. <i>áśvā</i>, Av. <i>aspā</i>, Lat. <i>equa</i> ‘mare’. In the Baltic languages, these were ousted by derivatives with *-<i>(j)ē</i>; cf. Li. <i>draugà</i> ‘company, circle, society’ → <i>draũgė</i> (for more see Ambrazas 2000a: 71 ff., 79 ff.). Differently from Indo-Iranian (but similarly to the Italic and Celtic languages), in the Baltic languages, the corresponding <i>nomina feminina</i> with *-<i>ī</i> /-<i>(i)jā</i>- are rare (for more see Ambrazas 2000a: 74 ff., 2004a: 67 ff.).

### 1.7. Diminutives

In the Baltic languages (as in other related languages; cf. Jurafsky 1996: 565 ff. and lit.), the suffix *-<i>ko</i>- played an important role in the creation of diminutives. Old Prussian nominal derivatives with the suffix *-<i>iko</i>- were productive (e.g., <i>malnijkix</i> ‘small child’). Diminutives are formed in Lithuanian with the suffix *-<i>u</i>-<i>ko</i>- (e.g. <i>berniùkas</i> ‘boy, lad’), in East High Lithuanian dialects also with *-<i>ā</i>-<i>ko</i>- (e.g. <i>berniõkas</i> ‘boy, lad’) and in some Latvian dialects with *-<i>ē</i>-<i>ko</i>- (e.g. <i>sunȩ̄ks</i> ‘small dog’).

It is true that in the course of time Lithuanian diminutives with -<i>elis</i>- became common (e.g. <i>vaikẽlis</i> ‘small child’), and Latvian derivatives with -<i>iņš</i> (-<i>iņa</i>) and -<i>ītis</i> (-<i>e</i>) appeared. The latter two suffixes are connected with a special kind of complementary distribution; cf. <i>brālītis</i> ‘little brother’, <i>saulīte</i> ‘little sun’, but <i>dēliņš</i> ‘little son’ (for more see Rūķe-Draviņa 1959: 22 ff., 168 ff.)

### 1.8. Nomina attributiva

Diminutives with *-<i>ko</i>- and nominalizing adjectives with *-<i>(i)</i>-<i>jo</i>-, *-<i>no</i>-, *-<i>en</i>-, *-<i>ro</i>-, *-<i>uo</i>-, *-<i>isko</i>-, and *-<i>mo</i>- furnish the basis for this derivational category (Ambrazas 2000a: 113 ff., 2000b). In the Baltic languages, the <i>nomina attributiva</i> compound suffix with *-<i>in</i>-<i>īko</i>-/*-<i>in</i>-<i>eiko</i>- is especially productive (cf. Li. <i>laukinỹkas</i> ‘farmer’, OP <i>laukinikis</i> ‘landholder’, Latv. <i>laũcinieks</i> ‘farmer’), perhaps in the distant past borrowed from Proto-Slavic (cf. Ambrazas 2000a: 118 ff., 2004a: 57).

### 1.9. Noun declension

The inherited case system of Baltic is comparable to that of Slavic, retaining seven of the eight cases reconstructed for PIE − all but the ablative, which has merged with the genitive, as in Greek, except that the syncretic form is the inherited ablative rather than the genitive. This system is manifest in East Baltic, but the limited corpus of Old Prussian renders its case system uncertain. East Baltic languages have lost the neuter gender in nouns, although Lithuanian retains it in predicate adjectives in clauses without nominal subjects (cf. Holvoet, this handbook, 1.2) as well as in substantive use: <i>gẽra</i> ‘the good’. Generally, neuter nouns are transferred to masculine gender, most famously in the word for ‘100’, <i>šim˜tas</i>. Old Prussian does retain a few neuter forms, notably <i>assaran</i> ‘lake’ (< PIE *-<i>om</i>); cf. OCS <i>jezero</i> (which, however, has the old pronominal ending *-<i>od</i>). The dual is retained only in Lithuanian, and even there it is in retreat, having been lost in many dialects. Baltic descendants of the PIE *-<i>(j)o</i>- (= Baltic *-<i>[j]a</i>-) and *-<i>(j)ā</i>-stem declensions are still productive, whereas the Baltic descendants of the PIE *-<i>i</i>-, *-<i>u</i>-, and especially the consonant stem declensions are not. Nouns of these unproductive categories are gradually shifting to the productive categories. In Lithuanian, old adjectives of the Baltic *-<i>u</i>-stem declension and old nouns of the Baltic *-<i>ju</i>-stem declension are passing into the *-<i>ja</i>-stem declension, whereas new words are appearing in the Baltic *-<i>u</i>-stem and *-<i>ju</i>-stem declensions. One encounters also perhaps a few remnants of the *-<i>ī</i>- and *-<i>ū</i>-stem declensions in specific forms of other stems.

The -<i>ja</i>-stem endings are the same as the above but may have the Sg N Li. <i>(bról)</i>-<i>is</i>, Latv. <i>(brāl)</i>-<i>is</i>, ASg Li. <i>(bról)</i>-<i>į</i>, Latv. <i>(brāl)</i>-<i>i</i> ‘brother’. Thus the Li. Il derives from the addition of the postposition *-<i>nā</i> to the original A ending: Sg *-<i>an</i>+<i>nā</i> > -<i>añ</i> (Rosinas 2005: 164), Pl *-<i>ṍs</i> + *-<i>nā</i> > -<i>úosna</i>; the OLi. Al derives from the addition of the same postposition to the G ending: Sg -<i>óp(i)</i>, Pl -<i>ump(i)</i>. Similar forms were created for other stems. But in the modern language it is only the illative which can be said to be a living case, functioning as a directive.

## 2. Adjective formation

In the Baltic languages adjective formation has been investigated less than the formation of nouns. Still, it seems that here it makes sense to distinguish three old, but closely related derivational categories (for more see Ambrazas 2005a, 2007).

### 2.1. Adjectives of action or result

In the Baltic languages, there are old verbal Adjs with the suffixes *-<i>lo</i>-, *-<i>no</i>-, *-<i>ro</i>-, *-<i>uo</i>-, *-<i>u</i>-, *-<i>o</i>-. Of these, the last two show an interesting relationship: in general, -<i>u</i>-stem adjectives in the Baltic languages partially replace old oxytone *-<i>o</i>-stem adjectives (the type seen in Gk. <i>phorós</i> ‘carrying’ [cf. Hamp 1984, 1994]); and in Lithuanian -<i>u</i>-stems adjectives ousted *-<i>o</i>-stems from use (Skardžius 1943: 33 ff.; Zinkevičius 1981: 20 ff.; Vanags 1989, 1990).

### 2.2. Attributive adjectives

Adjectives inherited from late PIE with the suffix *-<i>(i)jo</i>- have almost disappeared in the Baltic languages, often becoming nouns, e.g. Li. <i>vasãris</i> ‘summer, spring’, Latv. <i>galējs, galījs</i> ‘ultimate extreme, last’ (for more see Skardžius 1998b: 433 ff.; Ambrazas 2005b: 131 ff.). The nominalization of this type of derivative (e.g. the aforementioned <i>nomina agentis</i> with *-<i>tā</i>-<i>jo</i>-, *-<i>ē</i>-<i>jo</i>-) created the conditions for the spread of the <i>(i)jo</i>-stems in the Baltic languages (cf. Ambrazas 1992b).

On the other hand the derivational suffixes *-<i>i</i>-<i>no</i>-/-<i>in</i>-<i>jo</i>- (also *-<i>īn</i>-<i>go</i>-, which developed from <i>-īno</i>- on Baltic soil; see Skardžius 1998a: 100 ff.; Ambrazas 2003; Kaukienė 2004: 90 ff.), *-<i>is</i>-<i>ko</i>-, *-<i>ā</i>-<i>to-</i>, *-<i>ō</i>-<i>to</i>-, *-<i>ē</i>-<i>to</i>-, *-<i>ī</i>-<i>to</i>- are widely used to create nominal Adjs.

### 2.3. Diminutives

Adjective diminutives with the suffix *-<i>ā</i>-<i>ko</i>- are very productive in Lithuanian. In Latvian, the comparative degree of adjectives has developed from them; cf. Li. <i>mažókas</i> ‘rather small’ and Latv. <i>mazâks</i> ‘smaller’. Lithuanian diminutives of archaic formation with -<i>int</i>-<i>elis(</i>-<i>e)</i> have developed from ancient nouns with *-<i>nt</i>- (for more see Ambrazas 2004b).

2.4. Originally the adjective declension did not differ from the noun declension. The *-<i>o</i>-stem (= Baltic *-<i>a</i>-stem) pairs with the IE *-<i>ā</i>-stem declension (= Baltic *-<i>ā</i>-stem) to furnish M and F adjectives, respectively; thus, Sg N M Li. <i>gẽr</i>-<i>as</i> ‘good’, F <i>ger</i>-<i>à</i>. Baltic *-<i>ja</i>-stem adjectives with the Sg N M -<i>is</i> pair with F *-<i>ē</i>, thus Sg N M Li. <i>dìdel</i><i>is</i> ‘big’, F <i>dìdel</i>-<i>ė</i>. The Lithuanian and Latvian M adjective declension has adopted some endings of the demonstrative pronouns; thus Sg D Li. <i>ger</i>-<i>ám</i>, L <i>ger</i>-<i>amè</i>, PlN <i>ger</i>-<i>ì</i> D <i>ger</i>-<i>íem(us)</i>, Du D <i>ger</i>-<i>íem</i>, I <i>ger</i>-<i>iẽm</i> (Endzelīns 1971: 167). Baltic *-<i>u</i>-stem adjectives with the NSg -<i>us</i> pair with *-<i>ī</i>-/-<i>jā</i>-stems to furnish M and F adjectives, respectively; thus Li. Sg N M <i>plat</i>-<i>ùs</i>, G <i>plat</i>-<i>aũs</i>, ‘wide’, etc., N F <i>plat</i>-<i>ì</i>, G <i>plačiõs</i>, etc. The definite adjective is formed in principle (with various phonetic adjustments) by the addition of the corresponding case and number form of the 3<sup>rd</sup> person pronoun to the inflected adjective, thus Li. Sg N M <i>mažàs</i>-<i>is</i> ‘the small’ < -<i>as</i> + <i>jìs</i> ‘he’, G <i>mãžo</i>-<i>jo</i> < -<i>o</i> + <i>jõ</i>, etc. Latv. Sg N M <i>mazaĩs</i> < -<i>a</i> + -<i>ìs</i>, G <i>mazã</i>, etc.

## 3. Numerals

The cardinal numerals are usually declined. Sg N M/F pairs Li. <i>víenas</i> / <i>vienà</i>, Latv. <i>viens</i> / <i>viena</i>, OP <i>ains</i> / <i>aina</i> ‘one’, etc. are all declined like -<i>a</i>-/-<i>ā</i>-stem adjectives. The Du M/F NA Li. <i>dù</i> / <i>dvì</i> ‘two’ share the same G <i>dvejų˜</i>, D <i>dvíem</i>, and I <i>dviẽm</i>, but the Li. L M is <i>dviejuosè</i> and the F is <i>dviejosè</i>. Latv. <i>divi</i> ‘two’ is uncharacterized as to gender and case and the only occurring Old Prussian form is <i>dwai</i>. The other numerals from ‘3’−‘10’ are: ‘3’ Li. <i>trỹs</i>, Latv. <i>trîs</i> (-<i>i</i>-stem); ‘4’ Li. <i>keturì</i>, Latv. <i>četri</i> (with <i>č</i> from the influence of Ru. <i>četyre</i>); ‘5’ Li. <i>penkì</i>, Latv. <i>pièci</i>; ‘6’ Li. <i>šešì</i>, Latv. <i>seši</i>; ‘7’ Li. <i>septynì</i>, Latv. <i>septiņi</i>; ‘8’ Li. <i>aštuonì</i>, Latv. <i>astuôņi</i>; ‘9’ Lith. <i>devynì</i>, Latv. <i>deviņi</i>; ‘10’ Li. <i>dešimtìs, dẽšimt</i> (indecl.), Latv. <i>desmit</i>, dial. <i>desimt</i>. The numerals ‘4’−‘9’ have corresponding F’s in -<i>ios</i>. ‘10’− ‘20’ are all built with the suffix -<i>lika</i> (cf. Goth. <i>ainlif</i> ‘11’, <i>twalif</i> ‘12’) to a base which represents, in the case of ‘13’−‘19’, an original Neut. Pl (as is -<i>lika</i> itself) in -<i>ió</i>- (<i>keturiuólika</i> ‘14’, etc.). Similarly neuter is <i>trý</i>- (<i>trýlika</i> ‘13’). But <i>vienúolika</i> ‘11’ and <i>dvýlika</i> ‘12’ must be analogical. All these forms are inflected as *-<i>ā</i>-stems, but with accusative in -<i>a</i>, reflecting the original neuter status of -<i>lika</i>. The collocation would originally have meant ‘X left over (after 10)’. In Latvian, the corresponding forms <i>viênpadsmit, divpadsmit, trîspadsmit</i>, etc. mean literally ‘X after 10’. The decads are formed either by prefixing the unit digit to the indeclinable -<i>dešimt</i> (<i>dvìdešimt</i> ‘20’, <i>trìsdešimt</i> ‘30’, etc.) or by syntagms consisting of two congruent words (<i>trỹs dẽšimtys, kẽturios dẽšimtys</i> ‘three, four tens’, etc.). Latvian utilizes a compound with <i>desmit</i> (<i>četrdesmit</i> ‘40’, <i>pìecdesmit</i> ‘50’, etc.). Li. <i>šim˜tas</i>, Latv. <i>sìmts</i> ‘hundred’ is an -<i>a</i>-stem noun, whereas Li. <i>tū´kstantis</i> ‘thousand’ and Latv. <i>tũkstuotis</i> ‘thousand’ are both -<i>ja</i>-stems (although the former was earlier declined like a F -<i>i</i>-stem). Old Prussian attests only the APl <i>tūsimtons</i>.

### 3.1. Ordinal numerals

These are ordinary -<i>a</i>-/-<i>ā</i>- adjectives: Sg M N Li. <i>pìrmas</i>, OP <i>pirmas</i> ‘first’ (however, Latv. <i>pìrmais</i> is a definite adjective, and Old Prussian as well shows the definite declension outside the Sg M N, e.g. Sg A <i>pirmannien</i>); Li. <i>añtras</i>, Latv. <i>ùotrs</i> ‘second’ (most likely also OP <i>antars</i>; cf. ASg <i>āntran</i>); Li. <i>trẽčias</i>, Latv. <i>treš(ai)s</i> ‘third’; <i>ketvir˜</i>-<i>t</i>-<i>as</i>, ‘fourth’ (this and subsequent ordinals, with the original exception of ‘seventh’ [Li. arch. <i>sẽkmas</i>] and ‘eighth’ [Lith. arch. <i>ãšmas</i>], with <i>t</i>-suffix, including the teens in -<i>liktas</i> and the decads in -<i>dešimtas</i>, both with the same first member as the cardinal). Latvian uses the cardinal + -<i>padsmitais</i> for the teens and + -<i>desmitais</i> for the decads.

## 4. Pronouns

4.1. The pronominal system of Baltic includes demonstrative pronouns with both proximal and neutral reference as well as, archaically, a distal deictic demonstrative. As elsewhere in IE, pronominal inflection differs in part from that of nouns, especially in the Sg D L and in the dual. As noted in 2.4, these differences have been adopted as well in adjectival inflection. As a proximal deictic, Baltic utilizes a heteroclitic stem in *<i>ši</i>-/ *<i>šja</i>-, the first of these variants appearing in the Sg N A and the second elsewhere: Sg M N Li. <i>šìs</i>, Latv. <i>šis</i>, OP <i>schis</i> ‘this’ (generalization of the stem *<i>šja</i>- in the latter two languages); G Li. <i>šiõ</i>, Latv. <i>šà</i>; D Li. <i>šiãm(ui)</i>, Latv. <i>šam, šim</i>, OP <i>schism</i>; A Li. <i>šĩ˛</i>, Latv. <i>šùo</i>, OP <i>sch(i)an, schien</i>; I Li. <i>šiuõ</i>, earlier <i>šiúo</i>, Latv. <i>šùo</i> (= A) or, with instrumental intonation, <i>šuõ</i>; L Li. <i>ši(a)mè</i>, Latv. <i>šamī, šimī</i>; PlN Li., Latv. <i>šiẽ</i>, OP <i>schai</i>, G Li. <i>šių˜</i>, Latv. <i>šùo</i> (both nominal forms), OP <i>schiēison</i>; D Li. <i>šíems</i>, older <i>šíemus</i>, Latv. <i>šiẽm(s)</i>, A Li. <i>šiuõs</i>, Latv. <i>šuõs</i>, OP <i>schans, schins</i>; I Lith. <i>šiaĩs</i>, Latv. dial. <i>šiẽs</i> with vocalism of the D; L Li. <i>šiuosè</i>, Latv. <i>šuõs</i>. The Lithuanian dual of pronouns is rather fuller than that of nouns, adding a G and L to the usual NA, D, I via both internal inflection as well as suffixation of the relevant forms of the numeral ‘2’: NA <i>šiẽdu, šiuõdu</i>, G <i>šių˜dviejų˜</i>, D <i>šíemdviem</i>, I <i>šiẽmdviem</i> (or, with generalization of the stem form of the NA, <i>šiẽdviem</i> in both D and I), L <i>šiuõdviese</i>. To these were paired a F in *-<i>ī</i>/-<i>jā</i>-, e.g. Li. <i>šì</i>, G <i>šiõs</i>.

In addition, Baltic shows the form Li. <i>tàs</i>/<i>tà</i>, Latv. <i>tas</i>/<i>tã</i>, OP <i>stas</i>/<i>sta</i> (but more frequently F <i>stai</i>)/Neut. <i>sta</i> (< *-<i>at</i>, PIE *-<i>od</i>, and contrast <i>asseran</i> ‘lake’ < PIE *-<i>om</i>, the old nominal neuter ending), SgG M <i>stesse(i)</i> in the neutral deictic value ‘that’; and in many Lithuanian dialects, a third degree of (distal) deixis is distinguished by the form <i>anàs</i>/<i>anà</i> ‘yon’. Moreover, Old Prussian shows an enclitic anaphoric pronoun *<i>di</i>-, *<i>dja</i>-which finds its closest match in Iranian.

4.2. The Lithuanian interrogative, relative pronoun N <i>kàs</i> ‘who, what’ is declined: G <i>kõ</i> (with possessive meaning <i>kienõ</i> ‘whose’), D <i>kám</i>, A <i>ką˜</i>, I <i>kuõ</i>, L <i>kamè</i>, Latv. N <i>kàs</i>, G <i>kà</i>, D <i>kam</i>, A <i>kùo</i>, I <i>kùo</i>. The Latvian L does not exist, but the Adverb <i>kur</i> ‘where’ or the L <i>kurā</i> of <i>kurš</i> ‘which, who’ may be used in this value. Old Prussian has Sg N <i>kas</i>, D <i>kasmu</i>, PlN <i>quai</i> = /kai/, A <i>kans</i>. Li. Sg N M <i>kurìs</i>, F <i>kurì</i> ‘which, who’ < <i>kur</i> ‘where’ + <i>jìs</i> ‘he’, <i>jì</i> ‘she’ is declined like the corresponding pronouns. The Latvian cognate NSg <i>kuŗš</i> ‘which, who’ is declined like a regular soft stem adjective.

## 6. Verb formation

In the Baltic languages, the formation of verbs, which is intertwined with inflectional and syntactic characteristics, has not yet been systematically investigated. From the synchronic point of view, there are only the first attempts to classify the Lithuanian verbs into certain semantic groups or derivational categories (cf. Jakaitienė 1994; J. Pakerys 2005). Still, historical investigations lead one to suspect that a large number of these may be of nominal origin; cf. Li -<i>inti</i>, -<i>auti</i>, -<i>uoti</i>, -<i>yti</i>, -<i>oti</i> (Fraenkel 1938; Stang 1942; Endzelīns 1951: 803 ff.; Schmid 1963; Otkupščikov 1967: 78 ff.; Georgiev 1960, 1982; Karaliūnas 1980; Zinkevičius 1981: 91 f. and literature).

### 6.1. Verb inflection

The apparent etymologically 3<sup>rd</sup> person singular form of the Baltic verb functions with Sg, Pl, and Du subjects. Baltic retained, however, the PIE difference between athematic and thematic verbal endings. The etymological present tense archaic athematic endings of OLi. <i>bū´ti</i> ‘to be’ are: (3 person) <i>es</i>-<i>ti</i>, Sg 1 <i>es</i>-<i>mi</i>, 2 <i>esi</i> < *es-si, Pl 1 <i>es</i>-<i>me</i>, 2 <i>es</i>-<i>te</i> (Zinkevičius 1981: 99). The Latv. 1 Sg <i>ęs</i>-<i>mu</i> ‘I am’ (like Li. dial. <i>esmù</i> and probably OP <i>asmu</i>, etc.) shows a contamination of the etymological ending *<i>-mi</i> with the thematic 1 Sg ending -<i>u</i> (Endzelīns 1951: 704). OP shows the 3 person <i>ast, æst, est</i>, <i>asti</i>-<i>ts, hest, asth, asch</i> (this last surely a misprint), 1 Sg <i>asmai, asmau, asmu</i>, 2 <i>essei, assai, asse, æsse</i>, Pl 1 <i>asmai</i>, 2 <i>astai, estei, asti</i> (Trautmann 1910: 304; Smoczyński 2005: 17−28). In the contemporary Baltic languages, the Pres tense verb is completely thematic, e.g., 1 Sg Li. <i>es</i>-<i>ù</i>, etc., Latv. <i>es</i>-<i>u</i> ‘I am’, etc. The athematic endings differ from the thematic endings only in the 1 singular and the 3<sup>rd</sup> person. The thematic 1 Sg ending Li., Latv. -<i>uo</i> retained in the reflexive -<i>uo</i>-<i>s(i)</i>-, < PIE *-<i>ō</i> is ordinarily shortened to -<i>u</i>. The Li. 2 Sg ending -<i>i</i> (in Latvian reconstructed as *-<i>i</i>) may derive from thematic *-<i>ei</i> > *-<i>ie</i> > -<i>i</i> or athematic *-<i>i</i>, in which case the diphthong observed in the reflexive -<i>ie</i>-<i>s(i)</i> is analogical to the diphthong in the 1 Sg, i.e., -<i>u</i>: -<i>uo</i>-<i>s(i)</i>:: -<i>i</i>: -<i>ies(i)</i>, (Schmalstieg 2000:47). The 1 Pl ending Li. -<i>me</i>, Latv -<i>m</i> < *-<i>me</i>, the 2 Pl ending Li. -<i>te</i>, Latv. -<i>t</i> < *-<i>te</i>, the Li., 1 Du ending -<i>va</i> < *-<i>va</i>, and the Li. 2 Du ending -<i>ta</i> < *-<i>ta</i>. The long vowels or diphthongs encountered in the various reflexive forms, e.g., Li. 1 Pl -<i>mės</i>, Latv. -<i>mies</i>, etc. are all probably analogical (Schmalstieg 2000: 49). The thematic 3 person ending Li. -<i>a</i> (< PIE *-<i>o</i>) shows only the thematic vowel, with no trace of the *-<i>t(i)</i> familiar from other IE languages. In the thematic paradigm the -<i>a</i> has been generalized to all persons (except 1, 2 Sg); cf., e.g. Sg 1 Li. <i>ved</i>-<i>ù</i>, Latv. <i>vęd</i>-<i>u</i> ‘I lead’, 2 Li. <i>ved</i>-<i>ì</i>, Latv. <i>vęd</i>, 3 person Li. <i>vẽd</i>-<i>a</i>, Latv <i>vęd</i>; Pl 1 Li. <i>vẽd</i>-<i>ame</i>, Latv. <i>vęd</i>-<i>am</i>, 2 Li. <i>vẽd</i>-<i>ate</i>, Latv. <i>vęd</i>-<i>at</i>; Li. Du 1 <i>vẽd</i>-<i>ava</i>, 2 <i>vẽd</i>-<i>ata</i> (Schmalstieg 2000: 45−46, 137). (It is a general principle of the indicative forms of the Lithuanian verb, outside of the future, that the Pl and Du can be formed by the addition of 1<sup>st</sup> Pl -<i>me</i>, 2<sup>nd</sup> Pl -<i>te</i>, 1<sup>st</sup> Du -<i>va</i>, 2<sup>nd</sup> Du -<i>ta</i> to the 3<sup>rd</sup> person.) A sample “half-thematic” conjugation (i.e. with 1 Sg -<i>u</i>, other present forms without evidence of the etymological thematic vowel) is Li. <i>žin</i>-<i>óti</i>, Latv. <i>zin</i>-<i>ât</i> ‘to know’: Sg 1 Li. <i>žin</i>-<i>aũ</i>, Latv. <i>zin</i>-<i>u</i> < *<i>-āu</i>, 2 Li <i>žin</i>-<i>aĩ</i>, Latv <i>zin</i>-<i>i</i> < *-<i>āi</i>, 3 person Li. <i>žìn</i>-<i>o</i>, Latv. <i>zin</i>-<i>a</i> < *-<i>ā</i>, etc. A sample “half-thematic” verb with *-<i>i</i>-stem present and *-<i>ē</i>-stem infinitive is Li. <i>gul</i>-<i>ė́ti</i>, Latv. dial. <i>gul</i>-<i>êt</i> ‘to lie’: Sg 1 Li. <i>gul</i>-<i>iù</i>, Latv. <i>guļ</i>-<i>u</i> < *-<i>ju</i>, 2 Li. <i>gul</i>-<i>ì</i>, Latv. <i>gul</i>-<i>i</i> < *-<i>ie</i> < *-<i>ei</i>(?), 3 person Li. <i>gùl</i>-<i>i</i>, Latv. <i>gul</i> < *-<i>i</i>, etc. (Endzelīns 1951: 792). The type may also be represented in OP <i>turrettwey</i> ‘to have’ = Li. <i>turė́ti</i>, see Schmalstieg (1974: 201−202), Smoczyński (2005: 372−381). The Baltic preterit is formed with either the suffix *-<i>ā</i>- or *-<i>ē</i>- plus the personal endings. An example of the *-<i>ā</i>-preterit: Li. <i>jùsti</i>, Latv. <i>just</i> ‘to feel’: Sg 1 Li. <i>jut</i>-<i>aũ</i>, Latv. <i>jut</i>-<i>u</i> < *-<i>āu</i>, 2 Li. <i>jut</i>-<i>aĩ</i>, Latv. <i>jut</i>-<i>i</i> < *-<i>āi</i>, 3 person Li. <i>jùt</i>-<i>o</i>, Latv. <i>jut</i>-<i>a</i> < *<i>-ā</i>, etc. An example of the *-<i>ē</i>-preterit: Li. <i>nèšti</i>, Latv. dial. <i>nest</i> ‘to carry’: Sg 1 Li. <i>neš</i>-<i>iaũ</i>, Latv. <i>neš</i>-<i>u</i> < *-<i>ēu</i>, 2 Li. <i>neš</i>-<i>eĩ</i>, Latv. <i>nes</i>-<i>i</i> < *-<i>ēi</i>, 3 person Li. <i>nẽš</i>-<i>ė</i>, Latv. <i>nes</i>-<i>e</i> < *-<i>ē</i> (Endzelīns 1971: 234; Schmalstieg 2000: 288). The future tense is formed by adding -<i>s</i>- to the infinitive stem which in turn becomes a stem for the future conjugation, thus Li. <i>tàpti</i>, Latv. <i>tapt</i> ‘to become’: Sg 1 Li. <i>tàp</i>-<i>siu</i>, Latv. <i>tapš</i>-<i>u</i> < *-<i>sju</i>, 2 Li. <i>tàp</i>-<i>si</i>, Latv. <i>tap</i>-<i>si</i> < *-<i>si</i>, 3 person Li. <i>tàp</i>-<i>s</i>, Latv. <i>tap</i>-<i>s</i> < *-<i>s</i>, Pl 1 Li. <i>tàp</i>-<i>sime</i>, Latv. <i>tapsim</i> < *-<i>sime</i>, etc. (Endzelīns 1971: 231). Athematic forms such as Li. dial. Pl 1 <i>eis</i>-<i>me</i> ‘we shall go’, 2 <i>eis</i>-<i>te</i> are probably more original than standard <i>eĩs</i>-<i>i</i>-<i>me, eĩs</i>-<i>i</i>-<i>te</i>. There are various explanations for the connecting vowel -<i>i</i>-, see Kazlauskas (1968: 366−367) and Schmalstieg (2000: 262−276). Lithuanian has a frequentative past tense formed with the suffix -<i>dav</i>-, e.g. Sg 1 <i>tàp</i>-<i>dav</i>-<i>au</i>, 2 <i>-dav</i>-<i>ai</i>, 3 -<i>dav</i>-<i>o</i> ‘I/you/(s)he used to become’, etc. In Latvian, a special debitive mood is formed by prefixing the element <i>jā</i>- to the 3<sup>rd</sup> person verb form, e.g., <i>jā</i>-<i>mãca</i> ‘must teach’. The Old Prussian morpheme -<i>ai</i>- < PIE optative *-<i>oi</i>- expresses the imperative; cf., e.g., 2 Pl <i>id</i>-<i>ai</i>-<i>ti</i> ‘eat!’. The old optative in -<i>ai</i> may also be attested in such Lithuanian dialect imperatives as <i>rãš</i>-<i>ai</i> ‘write’; see Zinkevičius (1966: 370−371); Schmalstieg (2000: 240−241). Similarly for the Li. 3 person optative (“permissive”) <i>te</i>-<i>dirb</i>-<i>iẽ</i> (< *-<i>oi</i>-) ‘may he/she work’, the same PIE optative morpheme may be posited. The Li. dialect imperative 2 Sg <i>duõ</i> ‘give!’ may reflect an old root imperative (Zinkevičius 1981: 131). The usual contemporary Lithuanian imperative is formed by adding the morpheme -<i>k</i>- to the infinitive stem, e. g. 2 Sg <i>dúo</i>-<i>k(i)</i>, 2 Pl <i>dúo</i>-<i>k</i>-<i>ite</i> ‘give!’, 1 Pl <i>dúo</i>-<i>k</i>-<i>ime</i> ‘let us give’ (or perhaps to the old etymological root imperative, here with acute intonation according to the infinitive stem, see Zinkevičius 1981: 130). The Latvian imperative is usually identical with the 2<sup>nd</sup> person of the indicative. With the exception of the 1 Sg, the Lithuanian conditional (“subjunctive”) was apparently originally formed by the addition of personal endings (etymologically from the 3 preterit <i>bit[i]</i>) to the etymological supine -<i>tų</i> (<-<i>tum</i>). At an early time, there was a paradigm Sg 1 <i>rašyčia</i> (-<i>io</i>, -<i>iau</i>) ‘I would write’, 2 <i>rašytumbei</i>, -<i>i</i>, 3 person <i>rašytų</i>, Pl 1 <i>rašytumbime</i>, 2 <i>rašytumbite</i>, etc. The origin of the 1 Sg ending -<i>čia</i> < *-<i>tjā</i> is a mystery, but probably on the basis of other forms of the conjugation the 1 Sg <i>rašytumbiau</i> was formed. With the simplification of -<i>mb</i>- to -<i>m</i>- such forms as 1 Sg <i>rašytumiau</i>, 2 <i>rašytumei</i>, Pl 1 <i>rašytumime</i>, 2 <i>rašytumite</i> came into existence. On the basis of the 1 and 2 Sg, the 1 and 2 Pl and dual adopted the <i>ē</i>-stem conjugation giving Pl 1 <i>rašytumėme</i>, 2 <i>rašytumėte</i>, etc., now usually reduced to Pl 1 <i>rašytumė</i>, 2 <i>rašytutė</i>, etc. (Schmalstieg 2000: 216−232; Zinkevičius 1981: 122−128). Today, the most widely distributed paradigm of this heavily-refashioned category is built to the infinitive stem with endings Sg 1 -<i>čiau</i>, 2 -<i>tum</i>, 3 pers. -<i>tų</i>, Pl 1 -<i>tume</i>, 2 -<i>tute</i>, etc.

The present active participle is formed by the addition of the formant -<i>nt</i>- to the present stem of verbs in *-<i>(j)a</i>, *-<i>(j)ā</i> giving a stem -<i>ant</i>- to which the adjective endings may be added (verbs with a present stem in *-<i>i</i> have -<i>int</i>-). The NSgM may be illustrated by Li. <i>ved</i>-<i>ą˜</i>-<i>s</i>, Latv. dial. <i>vęd</i>-<i>u</i>-<i>s</i> ‘leading’, OP <i>sīd</i>-<i>an</i>-<i>s</i> ‘sitting’ < *-<i>ant</i>-<i>s</i>, the NSgF by Li. <i>vẽd</i>-<i>ant</i>-<i>i</i>, Latv. dial. <i>vęd</i>-<i>uot</i>-<i>i</i> ‘leading’. An alternative *-<i>(j)a</i>-stem NSgM is represented in Li. <i>vẽd</i>-<i>ant</i>-<i>is</i>. The Li. NPlM participles <i>ved</i>-<i>ą˜</i> < *-<i>an</i> and <i>vẽd</i>-<i>ant</i>-<i>ys</i> are both encountered. Otherwise the declension parallels that of the *-<i>ja</i>-/*-<i>jā</i>-stem adjective. The future active participle is formed on the Fut stem *-<i>sj</i>-; cf. Li. <i>bū´</i>-<i>sią</i>-<i>s</i> ‘future, which will be’, Latv. <i>bû</i>-<i>šu</i>-<i>s</i> < *-<i>sj</i>-<i>ant</i>-<i>s</i> and it is declined like the pres. active participle. The past active participle NSgM Li. <i>lìk</i>-<i>ę</i>-<i>s</i> ‘having left’, Latv. <i>lic</i>-<i>is</i> ‘having put’, derives from *-<i>en</i>-<i>s</i>, NPlM Li. <i>lìk</i>-<i>ę</i> < *-<i>en</i>. All the other cases are derived with a formant -<i>us</i>-the declension of which parallels that of the *-<i>ja</i>-/*-<i>jā</i>-stem adjective, thus GSgM Li. <i>lìk</i>-<i>us</i>-<i>io</i>, Latv. <i>lik</i>-<i>uš</i>-<i>a</i>, NSgF Li. <i>lìk</i>-<i>us</i>-<i>i</i>, Latv. <i>lik</i>-<i>us</i>-<i>i</i>, GSgF Li. <i>lìk</i>-<i>us</i>-<i>ios</i>, Latv. <i>lik</i>-<i>uš</i>-<i>as</i>, etc. Note, however, OP NSgM <i>īd</i>-<i>uns</i> ‘having eaten’. The pres. passive participle is derived from the present stem by means of the EB suffix -<i>m</i>-, e.g. NSgM Li. <i>dãro</i>-<i>m</i>-<i>as</i>, Latv. <i>darã</i>-<i>m</i>-<i>s</i> ‘being done’. The commonly quoted OP present passive participle <i>poklausī</i>-<i>man</i>-<i>as</i> ‘being heard’ should probably be read as *<i>poklausīna</i>-<i>m</i>-<i>as</i>, see Smoczyński (2000: 166−170, 2005: 197−198). The future passive participle is formed by the addition of the suffix -<i>m</i>- to the future stem, e.g., Li. <i>bū´si</i>-<i>m</i>-<i>as</i> ‘future’, Latv. <i>būša</i>-<i>m</i>. A special Li. adverbial active participle in -<i>dam</i>-<i>as</i>, Latv. -<i>dam</i>-<i>s</i>, is formed on the present stem, e.g. Li. <i>eĩ</i>-<i>dam</i>-<i>as</i>, Latv. <i>ie</i>-<i>dam</i>-<i>s</i> ‘going’. The past passive participle is formed by the addition of -<i>t</i>- to the infinitive stem, e.g., Li. <i>dúo</i>-<i>t</i>-<i>as</i>, Latv. <i>duô</i>-<i>t</i>-<i>s</i>, OP <i>dā</i>-<i>t</i>-<i>s</i> ‘given’. All participles with the formants -<i>m</i>- or -<i>t</i>- are declined like regular *-<i>a</i>-/ *-<i>ā</i>-stem adjectives. Finally, the infinitive is in Lithuanian -<i>ti</i>, in Latvian -<i>t</i>, and in Old Prussian either -<i>t</i>, -<i>ton</i>, or -<i>twei</i>. All go back to old action nouns in *-<i>ti</i> or *-<i>tu</i>. Old Prussian -<i>ton</i> is the old supine in -<i>tum</i> utilized, as we have seen above, in the constitution of the Lithuanian subjunctive.
