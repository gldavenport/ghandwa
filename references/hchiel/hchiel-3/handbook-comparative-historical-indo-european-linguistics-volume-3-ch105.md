## 1. Introduction

Siculian (or Sicel) was the language spoken by the inhabitants of central and eastern Sicily, documented from the end of the 6<sup>th</sup> century to the 4<sup>th</sup> century BCE. According to historical sources (among others Hellanikos FGrH 4 F 79b 3; Philistos FGrH 556 F 46,4; Thuc. 6,2,4 f.; Diod. 5,2,1; 5,6,1−4), the Siculians (or Sicels) are believed to have entered the island either around the 13<sup>th</sup> century or in the middle of the 11<sup>th</sup> century BCE (or in two waves) (de Simone 1999: 500, 2006: 690), leaving their ancient settlements in Italy (stretching from Cisalpine Gaul to Etruria and Picenum and on to southern Italy) and thus driving the prior inhabitants, Sicanians and Elymians, to the west of Sicily.

The Siculian language is widely believed to be of Indo-European, Italic origin, perhaps even belonging more closely to the Latino-Faliscan, Sabellian, or Ausonian branches (cf. Pisani 1953: 5, 18; Zamboni 1978: 954, 956). It is attested in less than thirty inscriptions on stone, brick, ceramic, and metal. The scripts used are Greek as well as “Siculian”, a specific alphabet borrowed from West Greek, probably of the Euboic-Chalkidic type. The direction of writing is sinistroverse, dextroverse, boustrophedic (Centorbi), or spiral (Sciri Sottano). To this inscriptional corpus may be added around twenty-five glosses (judged the most reliable among the total of ca. 100 supposedly Siculian glosses) (Whatmough 1933: 449−474). Both types of documentation present great difficulties in interpretation.

With regard to the glosses, the fundamental problem is evaluating their linguistic status. Are the supposedly Siculian words really Siculian? Is their similarity to Latin, Sabellian, or Italic in general due to a genetic relationship or are they simply borrowings into or out of Siculian? Do they perhaps go back to a Mediterranean stratum whose traces are found in other neighboring languages also? Thus Hesychius’ βατάνια might not be genetically related to Lat. <i>patina</i> ‘dish’ and thus not prove a close linguistic relationship between Latin and Siculian. Rather, both words more probably are borrowings from a non-Indo-European Mediterranean language (*<i>bat</i>-/*<i>pat</i>-) correspondences of which might also be found in Minoan (<i>pa-ta-qe</i> ‘drinking vessel’) and even in Iranian (*<i>bātiaka</i>- ‘drinking vessel’ > Modern Persian <i>bād(i)yah</i>; borrowed into Greek as βατιάκη) (Campanile 1969: 310 f., Beekes 2010: 206).

Of the fewer than thirty inscriptions in total, only six appear to be at least in part intelligible and to be Siculian (i.e., most certainly neither Greek nor belonging to some other Italic or pre-Italic language). They are: a block of sandstone from Mendolito (end of the 6<sup>th</sup> century BCE, ca. 50 letters: ¹<i>iam</i> ˌ <i>akaram</i> ˌ <i>e</i>?<i>p</i>??<i>as</i> ˌ <i>kaag</i>?<i>es</i> ˌ <i>gẹpẹḍ</i> ²<i>te</i>?<i>to</i> ˌ <i>veregaies</i>? ˌ <i>eka</i> ˌ <i>doara</i>[<i>ịẹạḍ</i>]; cf. Morandi 1982: 166, Agostiniani 1992: 146, Manganaro 1998: 254−257), a guttus from Centorbi (first half of the 5<sup>th</sup> century BCE, ca. 100 letters: <i>nunus</i> ˌ <i>teṇti</i> ˌ <i>mím</i> ˌ <i>arustainam</i> ˌ <i>íemitom</i> ˌ <i>esti</i> ˌ <i>durom</i> ˌ <i>nanepos</i> ˌ <i>durom</i> ˌ <i>íemitom</i> ˌ <i>esti</i> ˌ <i>velíom</i> ˌ <i>ned</i> ˌ <i>emponitantom</i> ˌ <i>eredes</i> ˌ <i>vịino</i> ˌ <i>brtome</i>[; cf. Morandi 1982: 169), an amphora from Montagna di Marzo (end of the 6<sup>th</sup>/beginning of the 5<sup>th</sup> century BCE, 92 letters: ¹<i>tamuraabesakedqoiaves</i> ˌ <i>eurumakes</i> ˌ <i>agepipokedḷutimbe</i> ²<i>levopomanatesemaidarnakeibuṛeitaṃomịaetiurela</i>; cf. Manni Piraino 1978: 11−13, Prosdocimi 1978: 26, Agostiniani 1992: 152, Martzloff 2011), a kylix from Aidone (6<sup>th</sup> century BCE, 4 letters: <i>pibe</i>; cf. Lejeune 1990: 28, Watkins 1995: 40), a cup from Castiglione (ca. 600 BCE, 6 letters: <i>nendas</i>; cf. Agostiniani 1992: 149), and the above example from Sciri Sottano.

Here, the fundamental difficulty is that of understanding the content. This is caused mainly by the <i>scriptio continua</i>, making word boundaries merely guesswork. Regarding the stele from Sciri Sottano, this means that, supported by <i>nendas</i> on the cup of Castiglione, only the first six letters seem to be identifiable as a personal name (although even this assumption is contestable, cf. Agostiniani 1992: 141). All further words cannot be proven to really exist. Then there is the linguistic problem: e.g., although Aidone’s <i>pibe</i> almost certainly seems to invite someone to ‘drink’, its linguistic characteristics don’t allow for any closer identification with Latin, Sabellian, or even Italic. The morphological structure is clearly Indo-European *<i>pi</i>-<i>ph₃</i>-<i>e</i>, but it could be even Proto-Indo-Iranian, cf. Ved. <i>píba</i>, or Proto-Celtic, cf. OIr. <i>ib</i> (Watkins 1995: 40).

## 4. Grammatical characteristics

Due to the rather doubtful character of Siculian documentation, the presentation of its phonology must remain very basic: Corresponding to the vowel graphemes, one can assume five phonemes /a/, /e/, /i/, /o/, /u/, about the length of which nothing can be said, as well as the diphthong /ai/ (perhaps also /ei/, /oi/, /au/). In the group of nasals and liquids /m/, /n/, /r/, /l/ appear. Voice seems to have been distinctive, as seen from the respective plosive pairs /p/, /t/, /k/ and /b/, /d/, /g/. Perhaps /kʷ/ can be added here. The fricatives included /s/ and /v/ (and possibly /h/). Nothing is known about the Siculian system of accentuation.

From the scarce data it may be gleaned that the Siculian nominal system had a nom. sg. in -<i>s</i> (neuter in -<i>m</i>) (Castiglione, Centorbi, Mendolito, Sciri Sottano) − or are (some of) the <i>s</i>-forms rather gen. sg.? −, an acc. sg. in -<i>m</i> (Centorbi, Mendolito), and apparently a dat. pl. in -<i>pos</i> (Centorbi), as well as a thematic gen. sg. in -<i>oio</i> (Licodia Eubea, c. 600 BCE, 13 letters: ¹<i>adiomis</i> ²<i>raroio</i>, cf. Agostiniani 1992: 150). The only two sure verb forms seem to be 3<sup>rd</sup> sg. ind. prs. act. of ‘to be’: <i>esti</i> (Centorbi), again in its character too Indo-European to be of help in establishing any further linguistic relationship, and the 2<sup>nd</sup> sg. impv. prs. act. of ‘to drink’: <i>pibe</i> (Aidone).

Hardly anything reliable and non-commonplace can be said about Siculian syntax and lexicon.
