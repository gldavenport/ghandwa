## 1. Name, geographic localization, time period

The name “Messapic” traditionally designates the pre-Roman language of the 2<sup>nd</sup> Augustan region, <i>Apulia et Calabria</i>. But, according to the current state of our knowledge, this name (“Messapic language”) is better confined to the inscriptions of the actual Salento peninsula, i.e. south of a hypothetical line connecting Brindisi-Taranto: only in this region is the name of the Messapians (<i>Messāpii</i>, Mεσσάπιοι: the “genuine” Messapians; cf. de Simone 1984 on the <i>Daunia</i>) found in ancient sources (Krahe 1937: 20−27, 1955: 14−15; de Simone 1979; Lombardo 1991: 52−53; on the sources in general: Lombardo [ed.] 1992), and indigenous inscriptions (about 600 texts of differing lengths) are attested here since about the middle of the 6<sup>th</sup> century BCE (cf. now MLM I−II; Marchesini 1999). A relatively homogeneous linguistic community, which we may designate by the traditional name “Iapygian” (Nenci 1978), can be considered to have existed in this region at that time. The recent discovery of a Greek incised inscription on a “Laconic” vessel (dated to the 1<sup>st</sup> half of the 6<sup>th</sup> century BCE) from Tsakona near Sparta, which testifies to the cult of a “Messapic Zeus”, is especially remarkable (Lombardo 1991: 106−109; de Simone 1991a). But the question remains whether this cult had been taken over from actual Messapia (in this case probably through the medium of the Laconic city of Tarentum); the other possibility is that this “Messapic Zeus” was indigenous to Laconia in the first place (Mεσσαπέαι is the name of a place in Laconia: Krahe 1937: 21, 1955: 14−15; see also Valmin 1939; Stanco 1987).

## 2. Alphabet

The alphabet of the Messapic inscriptions has been derived from a “red” Greek model and altered expressly for the purpose of adaptation to the language. The precise source alphabet is likely to have been the Laconian-Tarentinian alphabet, with the letters <i>H</i> (consonantal, not [ē]) and <i>F</i>; the letter <i>X</i>/<i>+</i> of the “red” alphabets (= [ks]) is used to denote the sound [š] in Messapic, which stands in phonological opposition to [s]; the letter <i>z</i> normally has the phonetic value of a voiced counterpart to <i>s</i> (de Simone 1972: 185−186), but in some cases it might also denote a dental affricate, while the Greek letter Φ (=[pʰ]) was superfluous in Messapic and hence has not been borrowed. But the Greek letter Θ, which denotes a dental affricate and/or spirant in Messapic is regularly used (de Simone 1972, passim; de Simone 1983b; Lejeune 1991; for the phonetic value of Θ in Laconia and Tarentum, cf. especially de Simone 1972: 172). This sound is secondary in Messapic, where it arose partly from a palatalization of the original sound sequence <i>*ty</i> (de Simone 1972: 156−159); in more recent inscriptions, Θ is also used before ao- or o-, where it is most likely a replacement for the older letter <img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-005.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_05.png"> (see below).

The main feature of the Messapic alphabet is probably the absence of the letter <i>u</i>, as only <i>o</i> is used (de Simone 1972: 133−138; MLM I: 6−18; see below). Furthermore, the Messapic alphabet has some “special characters”. These include, first of all, the letter ᛉ (with its variants <img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-006.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_06.png"> and <img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-007.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_07.png">), which occurs almost exclusively in Archaic inscriptions (6<sup>th</sup>−5<sup>th</sup> century BCE; de Simone 1972: 177−180; MLM I: 6−10); it seems likely that <i>ᛉ</i> has been borrowed from a “red” Greek alphabet (with the original sound value [kʰ]) and has partly been functionally remodeled. The function of this sign in Messapic is not clear in all cases: but there are undoubtedly examples where <i>ᛉ</i> is used in combination with <i>-i-</i>in intervocalic position, such as in <b><i>Haiva</i></b><img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-008.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_08.png"><b><i>ias</i></b> (MLM II: 159, 12 Bal; 1<sup>st</sup> half of the 6<sup>th</sup>−1<sup>st</sup> half of the 5<sup>th</sup> century BCE); in later inscriptions, the same sound combination is denoted with the grapheme sequence <i>-hi-</i>, such as in <i>Haivahias</i> (<i>MLM</i> II: 159, 3 Car), which can only be the younger counterpart to <b><i>Haiva</i></b><img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-009.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_09.png"><b><i>ias</i></b>. In these examples, the sequence <i>-VᛉiV-</i> or <i>-VhiV-</i> must denote the phonetic realization of a <i>-y-</i> in intervocalic position (approximately <i>-VyyV-</i>). Probably only a simple graphic variant of <i>-VhiV-</i> is the sequence <i>-Vh(h)V-</i>, such as in <i>Kabahas</i> (<i>MLM</i> II, s. v.) and <i>Andirah(h)o</i> (dat.; <i>MLM</i> II, see below): <i>-VyyV-</i> > <i>-VhhV-</i>? A second Messapic “special character” is <img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-010.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_10.png">, which is likely to represent a graphemic innovation (modification of <i>ᛉ</i>?) (de Simone 1972: 172− 177; <i>MLM</i> I: 18). This letter occurs almost exclusively at the beginning of a word, a position in which it denotes a dental aspirate, affricate, or spirant which developed from <i>*t</i>. Cf., for instance, <img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-011.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_11.png"><i>aotor-</i> (<i>MLM</i> II: 341−342). In later inscriptions, <img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-012.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_12.png">- is often replaced by θ-: <i>θ(a)otor-</i>. It is important to note that <img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-013.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_13.png"> cannot represent the result of a palatalization of the group <i>*ty</i>, which is usually expressed by <i>-(t)θ</i>-; furthermore, a grapheme sequence *-<img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-014.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_14.png"><i>es</i> (< *-<i>tyos</i>) is not attested (de Simone 1972: 175−176). But the Laconian-Tarentinian alphabet is not the only signary used in writing Messapic: in the archaic period, other Greek influences can be identified (Marchesini 1999); it is certainly no coincidence that the verb meaning ‘to write, incise’ in Messapic is of Greek origin (see below). So far there are almost no Messapic abecedaria (the only attested example, from Vaste, is in bad condition and can only partly be interpreted: <i>MLM</i> II: 115, 2 Bas).

The epigraphic record of the two regions north of the line Brindisi-Taranto (i.e. Daunia and Peucetia) begins much later (not before the 4<sup>th</sup> century BCE), and involves only a modest number of texts. Moreover, these texts are not written in the actual Messapic alphabet but rather in a local variant of the Hellenistic alphabet (the so-called “Apulian alphabet”) with the two letters <i>o</i> and <i>u</i>, as well as <i>H</i> with the phonetic value [ē] (de Simone 1988a)<i></i>.

## 3. Documentation

Today, the Messapic inscriptions are easily accessible in <i>Monumenta Linguae Messapicae</i> (= <i>MLM</i>). This work offers not only an edition of all texts which were available at the time of its compilation, but also a complete list of the lemmata which can be identified in the Messapic inscriptions, i.e. a complete <i>Index Verborum</i>. This word list, which serves as a general orientation into Messapic, offers as well a linguistic commentary with bibliographical notes on the individual lemmata. An enclosed CD-Rom facilitates access to the sources and the individual words and will make it possible to continually supplement the book with new texts and lemmata. It also offers the opportunity to retroactively correct mistakes or inaccuracies. Hence, <i>MLM</i> is not just a “complete” book but a “dynamic” corpus, which can be continually extended and kept up to date. Another important innovation offered by <i>MLM</i> is an electronically generated chronological listing of Messapic inscriptions, which has been carried out using the program <i>BASP Seriate</i> (<i>The Bonn Archaeological Software Package</i>; cf. <i>MLM</i> I: 1 f.; for details and the theoretical background see Marchesini 2004): this enables scholars to systematically investigate the letter types (<i>types</i>) used in each <i>unit</i> (= inscription), i.e. the determination of the regular distributional affinities of the individual letter forms in the Messapic inscriptions. It can be observed that certain groupings of letter types always or predominantly occur together (are distributionally related), in contrast to others, so that the relevant groups of letters stand in complementary distribution to each other. By applying the program <i>BASP Seriate</i> it has become possible to identify certain phases in the development of the Messapic alphabet, which correspond in a principled way to the relative chronology of the individual texts (<i>units</i>). On the other hand, archaeologically datable inscriptions provide the basis for establishing an absolute chronology of the individual developmental stages of the Messapic alphabet so determined.

The Messapic inscriptions are mostly short and have for the most part been found in burial sites; this is why the Messapic corpus mostly consists of personal names, which have been investigated by Jürgen Untermann in a masterly monograph (Untermann 1964; de Simone 1972: 192−201; Untermann 1995; <i>Aletium</i>: de Simone 1983a; de Simone 2013, passim). The main result of Untermann’s work has been its demonstration that the Messapic masculine family names at the time of the texts were names of gentes, while the female names were partly still patronyms. Some examples (Untermann 1995; note especially: <i>biles</i>, <i>-ihi</i> ‘son’, <i>biliva</i> ‘daughter’): <i>Staboos Šonetθihi Dazimaihi beileihi</i> (‘of Stabuas Šonetius, son of Dazimas’); <i>Dazoimihi Balehi Daštas bilihi</i> (‘of Dazimas Bales, son of Dazet’); <i>Dazes Blatθeias Plastas</i> (‘Dazet Blatteius, of Plazet [son]’). A female name formula is <i>Lahona Θeotoridda … Θeotoridda Θaotoras Keošorrihi biliva (MLM</i> I, Cae 22): in this text <i>Lahona Θeotoridda</i> (as the donor of the dedication to <i>Aprodita</i>) carries a family name (<i>Θeotoridda</i>) derived from the father’s first name (<i>Θaotor</i>), but which differs from the <i>nomen gentile</i> of the father (<i>Keošorres</i>, <i>-ihi</i>) (Untermann 1964: 188; de Simone 1972: 199−200). Especially relevant for cultural history are the cases of “hieronymic” designations: under certain circumstances, the deceased could suppress the “true” and official name in their burial inscriptions and have themselves named as “priest/priestess” of Demeter or of Aphrodite (de Simone 1975, 1983c, 1988b, 1993a: mysteriosophic cults of Demeter-Aphrodite). The appellative used for this purpose in Messapian was <i>tabaras</i> (fem. <i>tabarā</i>) (see below), for instance in the formulas <i>tabarā Damatras</i> (‘priestess of Demeter’) or <i>tabarā Aproditia</i> (‘priestess of Aphrodite’); a male formula is <i>tabaras Mahharaos</i>, although here the exact function of the name <i>Mahharaos</i> (gen.) is disputed. This special Messapic way of “hieronymic” naming has cultural parallels in the Greek area, such as <i>θεοδούλη δημήtria</i> (Syracuse), but also in Italic: <i>Sacracrix Cerria</i> (‘priestess of Ceres’; cf. <i>MLM</i> II, s. v. <i>tabarā</i>; for Ceres: Untermann 2000: 386− 387; Widmer 2004: 32). It is remarkable that part of the Messapic <i>nomina gentilia</i> (such as <i>Blas(s)ius</i>, <i>Tutorius</i>, <i>Tutoria</i>, <i>Τουτώριος</i>, etc.) have direct continuations in Latin and Greek (cf. the <i>Indices</i> in Musca 1996, furthermore de Simone 1964: 27−28; Messapian names in Dyrrhachion and in Greece: de Simone 1993c: 38−39). In a few instances in Messapic, family cults (“<i>teonimi famigliari</i>”) are attested, as in the case of <i>Totor Dazinnes</i> (name of a deity + gentilic derivation), cf. cases in Latin such as <i>Dea Hostia</i>, <i>Lares Hostilii</i>, and in Etruscan <i>Uni Ursmnei</i>, <i>Selvans Sanχuna</i>, etc. (de Simone 1991c).

But there are also a few longer texts (from Basta, Brindisi, Monopoli), which have not been found in burial sites and hence have a different content and which do not consist only of personal names. Only a small part (as few as 22 texts) of the very important complex of inscriptions from the Grotta della Poesia near Lecce could be published in <i>MLM</i> (de Simone 1998a). The grotto contains a so far undetermined number of Messapic inscriptions in addition to Latin inscriptions and at least one Greek text. Scholars of Messapic keep eagerly awaiting the publication of this very important epigraphic complex, which could significantly expand our knowledge of Messapic. In the Grotta della Poesia lies buried, so to speak, a thesaurus of the Messapic language.

According to its current attestation, Messapic is a typical “fragmentary language” (a so-called “<i>lingua di frammentaria attestazione</i>/<i>Trümmersprache</i>”), the investigation and linguistic evaluation of which are subject to certain methodological limitations (for a basic presentation of the relevant complex of problems, cf. Vineis 1983). In particular, the “etymological method” has to be treated with special caution (for a paradigmatic negative Messapian example, see de Simone 1993b). But the status of “fragmentary languages” keeps evolving over time, and this must always be kept in mind.

## 4. Linguistic affinities

At the present time, realistically speaking, it is not possible to situate Messapic within the framework of the Indo-European language family (cf. the approaches by Milewski 1965; Orioles 1991; Radulescu 1994; and Matzinger 2005; de Simone 2013, passim). The question of whether Messapic is a dialect of “Illyrian” (on “Illyrian” see de Simone 1972: 127−131, 1985: 45−46, and this handbook), much less <i>the</i> Illyrian language, is in my view an issue belonging to the history of scholarship and is no longer current. The fact that Messapic is a “fragmentary language” does not necessarily mean that it can yield no etymologies and that we do not know anything about its phonology. Of primary interest is the fact that Messapic shows many and numerous derivations of the predominantly western Indo-European appellative <i>*teutā</i> ‘people, community’ as the basis for several personal names (<i>MLM</i> II: <i>Taotinahiaihi</i>, <i>Taotorres</i>, <i>Taoteθθes</i>; de Simone 1991b: 302) as well as in the function of a divinity name (<i>Taotor</i>/<i>Θautour</i><img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-015.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_15.png"><i>otori</i> [dat.]: <i>MLM</i> II, 172−173, 325, 344). From this point of view Messapic appears to be a constituent of the western Indo-European languages. A securely etymologized appellative in Messapic is <i>biles</i>, fem. <i>biliā</i> (<i>-io[v]a</i>; <i>MLM</i> II, s. v.) ‘son, daughter’, which is likely to be cognate with Latin <i>fīlius</i>,<i>-a</i> (: <i>*bʰū-lyo-</i>/<i>yā</i>; Untermann 2000: 271−272); another important appellative is the divinity name <i>Venas</i> <<i>*wenos-</i> (<i>MLM</i> II, s. v.; cognate with the place name <i>Venusia</i>), which is therefore an inherited word and the exact correspondent of Latin <i>Venus</i> and Oscan Ƒενζηι [dat.] (: Old Indic <i>vánas</i> ‘desire’; Untermann 2000: 837−838; Janda 2005: 11, 53; see also the newly attested Ƒε(ν)ζηι in Caulonia: Ampolo 2004); in syntactic connection with <i>Venas</i>, often <i>Zis</i> (= <i>Ζεύς</i>) < <i>*dyēs</i> is found (<i>MLM</i> II, s. v.): both divinity names appear together in the “invocation formulas” (“formule di invocazione”) <i>kla(o)hi Zis Venas</i> ‘listen, Zeus (and) Venus’ (de Simone 1988a, 1991b: 308−312); the divinity name <i>Venas</i> is also invoked in connection with <i>Θautour</i> (de Simone 1988a, 1991b: 308−312). Tarentine <i>Δίς</i> = <i>Zεύς</i> in Rhinton is probably borrowed from Messapic (de Simone 1972: 160); most likely also borrowed from Greek are the divinity names <i>Damatra</i> (with variants; <i>MLM</i> II: 98−100) < Δᾱμᾱ´τηρ and <i>Aprodita</i> (<i>MLM</i> II: 20−21) < Ἀφροδίτᾱ. For an overview of Greek borrowings in Messapic, cf. Giacomelli (1979). An appellative lacking an assured explanation is <i>Andirah(h)o</i> (dat.; with variants: <i>MLM</i> II, 14−15; de Simone 1991b: 305−307; Poetto 1997), which appears as a determining attribute of the divinity name <i>Taotor</i>. Part of the sacral vocabulary are the following lexemes: <i>Damai</i> (dat.) (<i>MLM</i> II, s. v.); <i>Id(d)i</i> (dat.) (<i>MLM</i> II, s. v.); <i>logeti-bas</i> (: Greek <i>Λάγεσις</i>, <i>Λάχεσι</i>ς; <i>MLM</i> II, s. v.; note -<i>bas</i> [dat.-abl.] < *-<i>bhos</i>); <img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-016.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_16.png"><i>ana</i> (<i>MLM</i> II, s. v); <img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-017.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_17.png"><i>aol(n)e</i>/<i>Taolne</i> (dat.; <i>MLM</i> II, s. v.). Further note the appellative <i>deiva</i>/<i>diva</i> (: <i>divan[ov]a</i>), which (in its various forms) is likely to be the Messapic continuation of PIE <i>*deiwós</i>. An appellative which has not yet been interpreted is <i>daranθoa</i>/<i>deranθoa</i> (<i>MLM</i> II, s. v.). The lexeme <i>vinaihi</i>/<i>vunaihi</i> (gen.; <i>MLM</i> II, s. v.) probably means ‘wine’ and hence is likely to be a “Mediterranean” loan. Lexemes which have not yet been interpreted are: <i>daos</i>/<i>daus</i>, <i>eiteui</i>, <i>vastei</i>/<i>vasti</i> (‘civitas’?), <i>veteui</i> (all <i>MLM</i>, s. v.).

## 5. Phonology

Messapic phonology is also no <i>terra incognita</i>, as some of its features can be stated with confidence (de Simone 1964, 1965, 1972 [passim]; outdated research report: de Simone 1962). The most important phonological feature is the treatment of PIE <i>*o</i>, which regularly appears as <i>a</i> (de Simone 1972: 133−144). A paradigmatic example is the form <i>tabaras</i>,<i>-ā</i> ‘priest/-ess’ (with the variant <i>-o[v]a</i>: <i>MLM</i> II: 317−320, 339 [<img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-018.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_18.png">-]) mentioned above. The best etymology for this form is <i>*to-bhoros</i>/<i>-ā</i> (: <i>*bher-</i>) ‘offerer’, the closest semasiological relative of which is Umbrian <i>ařfertur</i> (Untermann 2000: 48−49). Furthermore, the original phonological opposition <i>ō</i>/<i>o</i> can also be seen in Messapic, e.g. in the inflection of <i>n</i>-stems, which regularly show <i>-ō</i> (nom.), <i>-ōnas</i> (gen.; <i>ō</i>: <i>o</i> [> a]). Corresponding to this sound change is also the development of the diphthong <i>*ou</i> (< <i>*ou</i> and <i>*eu</i>) to <i>ao</i>, as in <i>Taot-</i><img src="images/handbook-comparative-historical-indo-european-linguistics-volume-3-fig-chapter04-019.png" alt="Inline image: 10_chapter04_fig_19.png">/<i>aotor</i> (de Simone 1972, 49−53). This newly formed <i>ao</i> further evolves in the history of the Messapic language to <i>ō</i> (cf. <i>Θotor</i>; for the multiple monophthongizations in Messapic see de Simone 1964, 1965, 1972: 149−156). The abandonment of the original phonological opposition <i>o</i>/<i>a</i> in Messapic is likely to be connected to the fact that during the reformation of the Greek standard alphabet the (superfluous) letter U was not taken over (de Simone 1972: 140−144). Hence, the Messapic language only has an <i>o</i>/<i>u</i> phoneme, and not a phonological opposition <i>u</i>: <i>o</i>. Further important phonological features of Messapic are multiple palatalizations, such as <i>Bla(t)θes</i> < <i>*Blatyos</i>, <i>Zis</i> <<i>*dyēs</i>, <i>Artorres</i> < <i>*Artōryos</i> etc.; the grapheme/graphemic sequence <i>(t)θ</i> in <i>Bla(t)θes</i> (: Latin <i>Blattius</i>/<i>Blassius</i>) seems to denote a dental affricate or spirant [(t)s] or [(t)š] (for details cf. de Simone 1972: 156−168; Orioles 1972; 1978; Gusmani 1976: 134−141). A new datum of importance for historical phonology is provided by the particle <i>anda</i> ‘and, as well’ (<i>MLM</i> II, s. v.), which most likely can be traced back to PIE <i>*n̥dó</i> and therefore provides evidence for the outcome of the syllabic nasal in Messapic. The same feature can be found in Italic, cf. for instance Oscan <i>fangvam</i> (<i>fancua</i>) ‘tongue’ < <i>*dhn̥g̑wā</i> (Untermann 2000: 264), <i>anter</i> ‘between, within’ < <i>*h₁n̥-tér</i> (Untermann: 2000: 108−109)<i></i>. But it would be premature on this basis alone to claim a close relation between Messapic and the Italic dialects. Similar in function to <i>anda</i> is the enclitic particle <i>-θi</i> (<i>-si</i> after <i>-s</i>) (de Simone 1972: 158−159; <i>MLM</i> II, s. v.).

The question of the development of the PIE system of occlusives in Messapic is at present still unclear. The only thing certain is that the series of voiced aspirates <i>*bh</i> and <i>*dh</i> are represented by the simple unaspirated voiced obstruents (de Simone 1972: 169− 170), cf. for instance <i>berain</i>, <i>beran</i>, <i>(ta-)baras</i>, <i>-ā</i> (: <i>*bher-</i>), <i>(hipa-)des</i> (: <i>*dheh₁-</i>) (see below; on the contrasting developments of these sounds in “Italic” cf. Stuart-Smith 2004); but no clear evidence is available for the treatments of the velar and palatal series. An example of the outcome of a labiovelar is provided by the personal names <i>Penkahe[e</i>?] and <i>Penkeos</i> (gen.; <i>MLM</i> II, 274), so long as the suggested etymology from <i>*penkʷe</i> can be maintained; but it must be admitted that as of this point the evidence for the treatment of labiovelars in Messapic is exceedingly slender. In contrast, the realization of PIE <i>*s</i>, which initially and in intervocalic position has become <i>h</i>, is rather clear (de Simone 1972: 181−182: <i>hipa-</i>; <i>klaohi</i>/<i>klohi</i>; <i>Venas</i>). Finally, it should be noted that chiefly in initial position the voiceless dental <i>t</i> represents a dental aspirate or affricate (> spirant?) under still unclear conditions (see above).

## 6. Morphology

In the realm of morphology, the attested examples and the relevant inflectional paradigms of the noun have been compiled and discussed by myself (de Simone 1978; see also Prosdocimi 1989, 1990). The question of how to reconstruct the predecessor of the genitive case of the Messapic <i>-a-</i> and <i>-ya-</i>stems is still heavily disputed, i.e. (in normal orthography) <i>-aihi</i> and <i>-hiaihi</i> (following a consonant <i>-[K]Kihi</i>). The author repeatedly has argued for the traditional opinion (for details see de Simone 1992, 2013; an agreeing opinion: Eska-Wallace 2001: 87−88; contra: Matzinger 2005, see de Simone 2013, passim), according to which the ending <i>-ihi</i> should be understood as <i>-ī</i>. According to the paradigmatic scheme <i>Dazimas</i> (nom.) ~ *<i>Dazimī</i> (gen.) (cf. Latin <i>lupus</i>, -<i>ī</i>; on the Celtic <i>-ī-</i>genitive cf. de Bernardo-Stempel 2003: 39, 45) ~ *<i>Daziman</i> (acc.), the paradigm would have been analogically leveled to <i>Dazimas</i> ~ <i>Dazimaī</i> ~ *<i>Daziman</i>. A structural parallel to this development is seen in the Messapic <i>-ya-</i>stems (following a consonant), which in some cases also show leveling: -<i>(K)Kes</i> (nom.) ~ <i>-(K)Keihi</i> (gen.) ~ -<i>(K)Ken</i> (acc.). Here <i>-(K)Keihi</i> (-<i>ehi</i>) is likely to have replaced an older attested form -<i>(K)Kihi</i> (cf. <i>Dazihi</i>: <i>Daz[z]es</i>). Examples of this phenomenon are <i>Balehi</i>, <i>beileihi</i>, <i>Kazareihi</i>/<i>Kezareihi</i>, <i>Klatθeihi</i>, <i>Korθeihi</i>, <i>Kraθeheihi</i>, <i>Otθeihi</i>, <i>Šoranneihi</i> [unpublished inscription]; furthermore, note <i>Bostahi</i>: <i>-a-hi</i>. A comparable case in Latin would be the genitive singular of <i>-a-</i>stems, as in the case of <i>Duelonai</i>, where <i>-ai</i> was originally disyllabic (<i>Balehi</i> [:<i>Bostahi</i>]: <i>Duelonai</i>). Most Messapic family names in <i>-e-</i> are constructed on the basis of <i>-e-</i>stems (< <i>*Kya-</i>) (such as <i>Dazehias</i>: <i>Daz(z)es</i>, etc.), which are surely a late development of Messapic itself. A contrary opinion is expressed by Orioles (1991: 165−167), Gusmani (2006) and Prosdocimi (1989, 1990, 2006). According to these authors, the Messapic ending <i>-aihi</i> is likely to go back to *<i>-oiso</i> or *<i>-oisyo</i>. The hypothesis of Gusmani-Prosdocimi basically is a partly modified reissue of the old opinion of Pisani, according to which <i>-ī</i> (: <i>lupī</i>) goes back to *-<i>osyo</i>. This hypothesis had already been unanimously rejected by Pisani’s contemporaries. In any event there is to this day not the slightest shred of evidence that the ending *-<i>oiso</i>/<i>*-oisyo</i> may have been present in an early stage of Messapic (de Simone 2013, passim). Even if Celtic and Venetic are taken to provide evidence for *-<i>oiso</i> (<*-<i>osyo</i>?: de Bernardo-Stempel 2003: 37, 45; for the morphological explanation that *-<i>osyo</i> has been refashioned as*-<i>oisyo</i> after the pronominal genitive plural *-<i>oisom</i> see Eska-Wallace 2001: 80), this does not mean that this must have been the case in Messapic as well, especially insofar as Messapic is not especially closely related to either Celtic or Venetic. Furthermore, there is no evidence in any Indo-European language for an attested ending *-<i>oisyo</i>. As if this were not bad enough, the hypothesis of Gusmani-Prosdocimi is also subject to substantial phonological difficulties in Messapic, which cannot be investigated more closely here.

Through the evidence of the Messapic inscriptions, we now know some verbal forms, including the following (all to be found in <i>MLM</i> II, s. v.): <i>apistaθi</i>: (de Simone 1991b: 312−315) 3<sup>rd</sup> person singular present (<i>verbum dedicationis</i>) from<i>*opi-steh₂-ti</i> (: <i>*steh₂-</i>); <i>berain</i>: 3<sup>rd</sup> person plural optative from <i>*bher-oi-nt</i> (: <i>*bher-</i>, cf. Gothic <i>bairain-a</i>); <i>beran</i>: 3<sup>rd</sup> person plural subjunctive from *<i>bher-ā-nt</i> (: <i>*bher-</i>; <i>ma beran</i> ‘ne ferant’); <i>dareti</i> (?); <i>hadive</i> ‘sat’: <i>*sod-i-v-e</i>, 3<sup>rd</sup> person singular perfect of the causative *<i>sodéye</i>/<i>o-</i>, cf. Gothic <i>satjan</i>. A remarkable feature is the -<i>v</i>- morpheme of the perfect tense, which also occurs in other texts (see below); <i>hipades</i> (= Lat. <i>dedicavit</i>) ‘(s)he/it dedicated’: 3<sup>rd</sup> person singular s-aorist from <i>*supo-dhē-s-t</i> (: <i>*dheh₁-</i>); <i>(in)kermaθi</i> (?); <i>klaohi</i> (<i>klohi</i>) ‘hear!’: 2<sup>nd</sup> person singular imperative from <i>*k̑leus-</i> (: <i>*k̑leu</i>-; Peters 2006); <i>kraapati</i> [?]; <i>(ni)ligaves</i> ‘set down’: stem <i>liga-v-e-</i>, 3<sup>rd</sup> person singular perfect (:<i>*leg̑h-</i>?; de Simone 1991b, 315); <i>no</i> (= Lat. <i>sum</i>) ‘I am’ (de Simone 1987; contra: Prosdocimi 1988); <i>pido</i> ‘handed over’ (?); <i>preve</i> (?); <i>stahan</i> (?); <i>stihati</i> (?). The verbal form which was formerly read <i>dupave</i> (‘made’?) is actually to be read as <i>aupave</i> (Poetto 2003). The initial syllable of this verb can be interpreted as <i>a-u(pave)</i> (with a preverb?) or <i>au-(pave)</i> (< <i>*ou-</i>). Finally, the verbal form <i>eipeigrave</i>/<i>ipigrave</i> (<i>MLM</i> II: 122, 189; <i>epig[ra]van</i>: 124) ‘wrote, incised’ is a “mixed form”, as the stem <i>ipigra-</i> surely has been borrowed from Greek (loanword: ἐπιγράφω; but the morphological ending <i>-ā-v-e</i> is clearly Messapic. It has to be noted that Messapic shows two other important cultural loans from Greek: <i>argorian</i> (<i>MLM</i> II, s. v.) <i><</i> ἀργύριον and <i>argora-</i> (<i>MLM</i> II, s. v.) < ἄργυρος (in the compound <i>argora-pandes</i> [‘coin officials’]).
