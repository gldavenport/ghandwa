## 1. Introduction

### 1.1. Preface

1.1.1. Our discussion of the Greek lexicon will focus on the stage of the language starting from the Mycenaean period and extending until the beginning of the *Koine*, an interval of about 1100 years. In what follows all references to Greek will refer to this period unless otherwise specified. For a general introduction to all aspects of the history of Greek until late antiquity see Christidis (2007). An investigation of the Greek lexicon must take into account chronological, dialectal, and stylistic levels. In this chapter I will select for discussion a few salient points.

### 1.2. Chronology

1.2.1. Proto-Indo-European: The Greek language descends from Proto-Indo-European through generations of speakers who had no means of recording their language. In the course of time, individual groups of speakers left their original language communities and found new areas to live in, bringing their language with them and subsequently adjusting it to new living conditions. Nowhere is this adaptation better seen than in the lexicon. The language of the groups of now scattered speakers had the same underlying grammar but subsequently developed independently in different places. A considerable part of the Greek lexicon dates back to the Proto-Indo-European period. See section 2 below.

1.2.2. Balkan period: For centuries the ancestors of the Greeks lived in the Balkan area. It is unclear whether the neighboring Proto-Greeks, Proto-Armenians, Proto-Phrygians and Proto-Albanians (who all split off from the Proto-Indo-European-speaking language community) were in the first instance speakers of a joint intermediate proto-language which broke up afterwards, or whether this was a sprachbund in which neighboring languages mutually influenced each other (cf. Hajnal 2003: 117−145).

1.2.2.1. Among the joint features shared by Greek and Armenian is the word for ‘god’ θεός < **dʰes-ó-* (< older **dʰh₁s-ó-*), cognate with Armenian *di-kʽ* ‘gods’ (plural) <**dʰēs-*(< older **dʰeh₁s-*). We have to assume that during the joint contact period both groups of speakers gave up using the word **deiwó-* (cf. Indo-Iranian **daivá-* and Proto-Italic **deiwo*- [Old Latin *deivo-* develops into both Classical Latin *deus* and *dīvus*, cf. Meier-Brügger 2002: 64, 89]) which was inherited from Proto-Indo-European times, and which was replaced by derivations from **dʰ*(*e*)*h₁s-* (cf. de Lamberterie 1997: 73). (On the further analysis of **dʰh₁s-ó*-, compared to **wets-ó-*, cf. Meier-Brügger 2006). The Armenian form is normally traced back to an athematic **dʰēs-es*, with the usual reduction of the ending -*es* and re-characterization by -*kʽ*. The reconstruction of a thematic plural **dʰēsṓs* seems possible, too. Cf. the discussion of the replacement of the endings -*es* and -*ōs* by -*kʽ* in de Lamberterie (1979: 322). For further information on Graeco-Armenian see Meier-Brügger (1992 I: 65) and de Lamberterie (1997).

1.2.2.2. Part of the joint Greek-Old Phrygian innovations are the terms *wánakt-* ‘ruler’ and *lāwāg(etā)-* ‘leader of a *lāwó-*’. Both are Mycenaean, but they are also part of the title known from the Old Phrygian inscription *Midai lavagtaei vanaktei* (6ᵗʰ century BCE) ‘to Midas, the *l*. and *v*.’. The common features can be understood more easily if the terms had been coined in a period when Greeks and Phrygians were in close contact with each other. A borrowing of the terms from Greek into Old Phrygian in the archaic to classical period can be ruled out as these terms had fallen out of use in Greece at that time. For further information on Graeco-Phrygian, see Meier-Brügger (1992 I: 65).

1.2.3. Proto-Greek: The immigration of the Proto-Greeks into the country which was later named “Greece” after them seems to have already taken place by the transition from Early Helladic II to Early Helladic III after 2500 BCE (cf. DNP 8: 578). After this period no further hiatus can be found in the settlement strata. The Proto-Greeks there came into contact with the Pre-Greek population that already lived in this area (cf. Strunk 2003: 85−98; Panagl 2003, 99−103; and Lindner 2003: 105−108). On loan words from this period, see 3.1. A long regional and local stability was followed by the first dialectal differentiations. In the south, a sibilant pronunciation transformed *-*ti*- into *-*si*-; in the north, conservative speakers retained **t* before **i*.

1.2.4. Mycenaean: The language of the clay tablets in the so-called Linear B script from the Mycenaean period between 1400 and 1200 BCE is Greek. According to these documents the *Akʰaiwoi* (using -*si*- instead of -*ti*-) are the leading class in the palaces of Knossos (KN), Khania (KH), Pylos (PY), Mycenae (MY), Tiryns (TI), and Thebes (TH). The lexicon is easily accessible in Aura Jorro 1985−1993. It is noteworthy that Semitic loan words are already well-established in this period, see 3.2.1.

1.2.5. Dark Ages: Beginning in the 11ᵗʰ century BCE, the first colonization wave from Greece leads to settlements on the Aegean islands and the coast of Asia Minor (cf. DNP 6: 646 f.). The -*ti*-language-speaking *Dṓroi* reshuffle the continental linguistic landscape. Achaia, Elis, Messenia, Laconia, the Argolid, and Crete become their home territories. The -*si-*speaking settlers, who originally also lived on the entire Peloponnese and also in Crete, withdraw to Attica and to the high plateau of Arcadia. Another group moves to Cyprus. The degree of continuity that exists between the end of the Mycenaean period and the beginning of the Classical period remains an intriguing question. The lexical evidence plays an important role in this; see the basics in Hajnal (1998). The research of Plath (1994) on the terminology of the chariot and its associated paraphernalia shows that there is a considerable hiatus between the Mycenaean specialized vocabulary and the Homeric evidence. On the other hand, terms for weapons are remarkably stable, cf. Mycenaean <*e-ke-a*> /*énkʰeha*/ neuter pl. ‘spears’ and Homeric *énkʰos* or *tókso*- ‘bow’ in both Mycenaean and Homeric (Plath 1994: 410).

1.2.6. Archaic Period: Greek, supported by the geographical structure of segregated landscapes, is subdivided into dialects. In about 730 BCE, the Greek alphabet is created after a Phoenician model. Colonization activities are strongly expanding (so-called “major Greek colonization”), which extends the Greek language area towards the Black Sea and Lower Italy with Magna Graecia (cf. DNP 6: 647−651).

1.2.7. Classical period around 450 BCE with Athens as the leading cultural center: The philosophical texts of Plato and Xenophon, the histories of Thucydides, the rhetorical texts of Demosthenes and Isocrates, and Attic tragedy and comedy form the basis of Classical Greek literature. Cultural change within the Archaic Greek political system also brings changes in the lexicon, an example of which is provided in 5.2.3.5.

1.2.8. Hellenistic period: The emergence of a supra-regional language (the so-called *Koine*) leads to the gradual extinction of local dialects. Each dialect has to be described on its own. The position of Greek in the Eastern Mediterranean area is dominant. The relations with all adjacent languages are also a source of many loanwords in the Greek lexicon (cf. Blanc and Christol [eds.] 1999 and 3.3 below).

### 1.3. Style, literature, inscriptions

1.3.1. Owing to the cultural rise of Athens, the Attic language developed into the literary standard dialect. Hence, school grammars and school dictionaries rightly take the Attic dialect and its canonized classics as their norm, see 1.2.7.

1.3.2. Special consideration must be given to the artificial epic language of Homer written in hexameters. The product of an oral tradition preserved by guilds of singers (*aoidoí*) and extending back to the 2ⁿᵈ millennium BCE, it combines Ionic and Aeolic linguistic elements, replete with a mix of metrically-driven forms, archaisms, and neologisms. The two great Homeric epics, the Iliad (15,693 verses) and the Odyssey (12,109 verses), mark the transition between the oral and the written tradition. On epic vocabulary see LfgrE.

1.3.3. While the Homeric epics often cultivate a modestly archaic (courtly) style, the style of Attic comedy reflects contemporary popular speech. A third, independent category of evidence is furnished by epichoric inscriptional sources. For an adequate overall assessment of Greek, both literature and inscriptions have to be considered.

### 1.4. P. Chantraine’s Etymological Dictionary as a reference work

1.4.1. The amount of data on the Ancient Greek lexicon is immense. I base the following especially on Chantraine (1999) to which I will often refer. This work has the advantage that it not only offers the etymology of each word but presents its particular history as well. As far as the sources allow, the history of a word or word family can be described on a diachronic level, beginning with the word’s formation and extending to its later history and its interaction with various semantic fields. The reasons for coining new words (i.e. the motivation for their etymologies) can no longer be perceived in many cases.

1.4.2. Besides Chantraine (1999), Frisk (1960−1972) and Beekes (2010) are essential. For Mycenaean, Aura Jorro (1985−1993) and Bartoněk (2003) are the most important reference works.

### 1.5. Outline of topics and organization of this chapter

1.5.1. The lexicon of every language is subject to continuous fluctuation. The external form of a word can change owing to sound changes; on the semantic level, meanings can be altered through narrowing or generalization. Words can become obsolete and be replaced by new words. The majority of the Greek lexicon consists of words of Proto-Indo-European origin, augmented by words which were derived or internally changed in the course of time as well as loanwords.

1.5.2. Section 2 will present inherited words, 3 will deal with loanwords, which at all times keep enriching the lexicon; 4 presents special problems of the Greek lexicon, and 5 discusses the most important Greek models of word formation (by suffixation or composition). Finally, 6 summarizes the literature quoted in Sections 1−5.

## 2. The inherited vocabulary

### 2.1. Preliminary remarks

2.1.1. A large part of the Greek lexicon can be shown to date back to Proto-Indo-European times (see above 1.2.1). The following examples only present a very small selection from an enormous number of possibilities. I prefer to thoroughly discuss a few lemmas rather than giving an overly general presentation. The two handbooks LIV (2001) and NIL (2008) offer excellent access to the basic problems. In both collections, Greek avatars form a substantial part of the whole.

2.1.2. For athematic nouns, I will quote the strong as well as the weak stem; for thematic nouns I will quote the stem form in -*o-*. Similarly, I will cite thematic verbs uniformly with -*e-* as thematic vowel.

### 2.2. Nouns

#### 2.2.1. Terms for family relationship

2.2.1.1. These terms are part of traditionally conservative vocabulary, and as a rule they can be directly traced back to Proto-Indo-European. But from time to time, striking gaps occur, where the speakers have completely renewed a single word. Cf. 2.2.1.5 on ‘sister’. Our presentation must be selective.

2.2.1.2. ‘father’: Voc.Sg. *páter* Nom.Sg. *patḗr* Acc.Sg. *patéra* / weak stem *patr-ˊ* (a secondary stem *patér*- also occurs). Attested from Mycenaean, Homer onward. The denotation of Zeus, god of the sky, as *patér-* is inherited, cf. the Homeric Vocative *Zeũ páter* (etc.) and Latin *Iuppiter* (< Proto- Italic **Dyew+pater*). For further on Ancient Greek ‘father’ see Chantraine (1999: 863−865). Derivation: < Proto-Greek **patér-* < Proto-Indo-European **ph₂tér-* (cf. NIL 2008: 554−562).

2.2.1.3. ‘mother’: Voc.Sg. *mẽter* Nom.Sg. *mḗtēr* Acc.Sg. *mētéra* / weak stem *mētr-ˊ* (a secondary stem *mētér-* also occurs). Attested as Ionian-Attic *mē-* < *mā-*; *mā-* is Mycenaean, Doric etc. The initial accent of the Nom. of the strong stem in Greek is unusual, cf. Vedic *mātár*- and Old English *mōder* < Proto-Germanic **mōđér-*; Proto-Indo-European ‘mother’ and ‘brother’ both have a long initial syllable; did ‘mother’ initially also have an acrostatic flexion like ‘brother’? Is the Greek initial accent of the Nom. Sg. an archaism? Has the Vedic and Proto-Germanic stress been secondarily shifted after the accentuation of ‘father’? Cf. Meier-Brügger (2002: 84). For further reading on Old Greek ‘mother’, see Chantraine (1999: 698 f.). Derivation: < Proto-Greek **mā́ter*- < PIE **méh₂ter-*or **mā́ter-* (cf. NIL 2008: 457−461).

2.2.1.4. ‘brother’: *pʰrḗter-* Ionian-Homeric, otherwise everywhere with inherited -*ā*-. In Homer only in derivation and composition; as a simplex first attested in Aeschylus, normally only as a plural, denoting members of a phratry. On the meaning cf. Homeric *apʰrḗtōr* ‘without phratry’ (denoting the loss of political-social status without the right of the otherwise guaranteed solidarity of the “phratry”, cf. M. Schmidt in LfgrE s.v.). Derivation: < Proto-Greek **pʰrā́ter-* < PIE **bʰréh₂ter*- or **bʰrā́ter-* (cf. NIL 2008: 38− 41). It seems that in Proto-Indo-European ‘brothers’ first denoted the male members of a core family consisting of several generations living in a household community; in Greek the term was extended to an association larger than the household community; but in most Old Indo-European languages, the term was restricted to the male members who were sons of the same father or the same mother, (cf. Risch 1981c: 650). At a very early point in Greek (Proto-Greek?) words were coined for ‘brother’ in the narrower sense (full and half brothers) based on inherited linguistic material. These include *kasígnētos* (Homer, poetic, Cyprian, Lesbian; *katígneitos* Thessalian < Proto-Greek **katígnēto-* ‘born together’) and *adelpʰeó-* (Homeric and dialectal, Attic *adelpʰó-*; < Proto-Greek **sm̥-gʷelbʰ-eyó-* ‘coming from the same womb’ or **sm̥-gʷelbʰó-* ‘having the same womb’), probably a specification in syntagmatic association with *pʰrā́ter*-, afterwards independent (cf. Chantraine 1999: 18f., 503, 1372, 1440).

2.2.1.5. ‘sister’: Derivatives of the Indo-European form **swésor-* can only indirectly be found in Ancient Greek in the lexicographers (for instance Hesychius), cf. Chantraine (1999: 355), especially *éor*- (cf. also NIL 2008: 680−683). The speakers seem to have early given up the term, replacing it by others. Homer uses *kasignḗtē*, while in the Classical period from Aeschylus on *adelphḗ* is in use. It is possible that in parallel to the plural of *pʰrā́ter*- the plural of **swésor-* originally had the general meaning ‘the female members of a core family living in a household’. The glosses *éor* = *thugátēr* ‘daughter’, *anepsiā́* ‘cousin’ and *éores* = *prosḗkontes, sungeneĩs* ‘relatives’ could point to this.

2.2.1.6. ‘son’: Mycenaean <*di-wo i-je-we*> /*Diwós hīyéwei*/ (PY) ‘to the son of Zeus’, from Archaic times *hūú-*, *huyyú-, huyyéw-*, *huyyó-* etc. < Proto-Greek **sūyú-*. The old Indo-European languages do not have a single word for ‘son’, but judging from Tocharian B *soy*, the form **sūyú-* seems to date back to PIE times, cf. Chantraine (1999: 1154); the Mycenaean form is also discussed by Hajnal (2006: 64−66).

2.2.1.7. ‘daughter’: Voc. Sg. *tʰúgater* Nom. Sg. *tʰugátēr* Acc. Sg. *tʰugatéra* and *tʰugátra*/ *tʰugatr-ˊ* and *tʰugatér-*. Attested in Mycenaean <*tu-ka-t-*> /*tʰugat*-/, Homer, and onward. The Greek accent on the second syllable in the Nom. Sg. is secondary. For further reading on Greek ‘daughter’, see Chantraine (1999: 444 f.). Derivation: < Proto-Greek **tʰugáter-* < Proto-Indo-European **dʰugh₂tér-* (cf. NIL 2008: 126−130).

2.2.1.8. ‘father-in-law’: Attested from Homer onward as *hekuró-*, cf. Chantraine (1999: 330). Derivation: < Proto-Greek **swekuró-* < Proto-Indo-European **swek̑uró-* (cf. NIL 2008: 672−675).

2.2.1.9. ‘daughter-in-law’: Homeric-Epic *nuó-* is replaced early in Greek by *númpʰā*-/ *númpʰē-* (originally ‘bride’), cf. Chantraine (1999: 760). Derivation: < Proto-Greek **snuhó-* = Proto-Indo-European **snusó-* (cf. NIL 2008: 625 f.).

#### 2.2.2. ‘horse’

According to the evidence of related Indo-European languages, a PIE form for ‘horse’ can be confidently reconstructed as **(H)ék̑wo*- (cf. NIL 2008: 230−233). The Mycenaean form is <*i-qo*>, the Archaic and Classical form from Homer on is /*hippo-*/. Both continue **(H)ék̑wo-*, but the Mycenaean vowel -*i-*, the double *-pp-* and the aspiration of the post-Mycenaean period are striking. As regards Mycenaean phonology, raising of -*e*- to -*i*- in labial contexts is normal. The Archaic and Classical -*pp*- most likely developed from Proto-Archaic *-*kʷkʷ*-. The speakers of the 2ⁿᵈ millennium BCE seem to have understood the consonant group *-*kw*- as a labiovelar -*kʷ*-, which underwent gemination to maintain the original prosodic structure of the etymon. But the aspiration has no clear motivation. The retention of the vowel -*i-* in post-Mycenaean Greek seems to indicate that /*hippo-*/ had spread throughout the Greek speaking area as a Mycenism (cf. Chantraine 1999: 467 f.). For an evaluation of this Mycenism (for Chantraine still “*inexpliqué*”) cf. Risch 1981b: 458.

#### 2.2.3. ‘ship’, ‘seafaring’, ‘sea’

2.2.3.1. The Proto-Indo-European word for a boat used on rivers and lakes was **néh₂- u-* (cf. Latin *nāvi-*; the -*i-*stem is secondary via a case form such as Acc. Sg. *nāv-em*); in Greek, it was *nau-*/*nāw-* (so already in Mycenaean <*na-u-do-mo*> /*naw-dómos*/ ‘ship’s carpenter’ and in personal names like <*na-u-si-ke-re*[we]> /*Nawsi-kléwes-*/ [on the details of the phonology and morphology see Meier-Brügger 2002: 215 f.]). The extension of this term to seaworthy ships is trivial.

2.2.3.2. As Latin *nāvigāre* < **nāv-ag-* ‘to go by ship, to sail’ shows, the root **plew-*‘swim, hover’ (cf. LIV 2001: 438 f. and PIE **pléwmon-* ‘lung’) in the meaning ‘sail the seas, sail, ship out’ is not common in PIE and thus points to the fact that the Greeks themselves adjusted the word to the new option of sailing the seas, cf. Old Greek *pléō* (Homer and later) ‘I sail (the seas), I ship out’ and *plóos* (Homer and later) ‘navigation, seafaring’. The formations *ploũtos* (Homer and later) ‘abundance’; *plúnō* (Homer and later) ‘I wash’ (cf. among others Homeric *neóplutos* ‘just washed’), which belong to the same word family, prove that Greek also knew the older meaning.

2.2.3.3. From Homer onward there is evidence for *pónto-* (if <*po-ti-jo*> is correctly interpreted as /*Pontiyos*/, then the word is already found in Mycenaean), a word of Proto-Indo-European origin. From the forms **pont-*(/**pn̥t-*) (also **ponti-*) are derived Armenian *hown* ‘ford’, Latin *pont-* ‘bridge’ and Old Church Slavonic *pǫtĭ* ‘path’. Evidence for **pént-oh₂-*/**pn̥t-h₂-és* is provided by Vedic *pánthā-* m. (Rigveda and later) ‘way, path, track’ with a weak stem *path-* (cf. also Young Avestan Acc. Sg. *paṇtąm*, Old Avestan Abl. Sg. *paθō*). The group goes back to the verbal root **pent-* ‘find (a way)’ < ‘get somewhere, meet’ (cf. LIV 2001: 424 f. and cf. Gothic *finþan* and Greek *pátos* [Homer and later] ‘path’). The meaning ‘sea’ is unique to Greek among the Indo-European languages (in Latin for instance *mare* < **mori-* was chosen in this meaning) and can be best understood if we assume that the Greeks changed the meaning when they reached their new homeland. In contrast, the origin of the parallel Greek term *tʰálassa* (Homer and later) is unclear.

### 2.3. Adjectives

2.3.1. Greek possesses many inherited adjectives, such as *néu̯o*- (Mycenaean, Homer onward) ‘new, young’ and *hēdú*- (Homer and later; < Proto-Greek **swādú-* < Proto-Indo-European **swéh₂du-*/**suh₂déw-*) ‘pleasant, sweet’. These often show peculiarities which in some instances turn out to be archaisms dating from PIE times. An example is the occasional avoidance of feminine forms together with the *o*-vocalism in *polú-* ‘much, many’. The latter seems to have been extracted from compounds, where it was a frequently occurring first member, and is thought to have its origins in an abstract of the PIE type **h₁ósu-*/**h₁ésu-* ‘existence’. For further details see Meier-Brügger (1992 II: 83−85) and (2002: 220−226, 293 f.) (here with the reconstruction of the PIE abstract **pólh₁u*-/**pélh₁u*- ‘abundance’, beside the internally derived adjective **pélh₁u*-/**pl̥h₁éw-*[the levelling of which produces Vedic *purú-*]).

### 2.4. Numerals

2.4.1. The numerals are comparatively faithful reflections of the Proto-Indo-European forms (cf. Meier-Brügger 2002: 236−240). Here I note only *hekatón* ‘100’. For a century the etymology of the initial *he-* of this form was unclear. According to the current consensus, *k̑*it is based on PIE **dk̑m̥tó-* with **ek*- arising via **h₁*from **dk̑*-. The aspiration is apparently the result of popular etymology, the speakers taking the unexplained **e-* as a form of *hen-* ‘one’ (cf. Meier-Brügger 2002: 238 f.).

### 2.5. Pronouns

2.5.1. The pronouns are mostly based on PIE forms (cf. Meier-Brügger 1992 II: 85− 93). But phonetic and formal developments in the individual dialects sometimes make the forms completely non-perspicuous. An example is the first person plural acc. ‘us’, where completely new paradigms are formed on the basis of the original oblique stem **n̥s-me-*, cf. *ámme* (Aeolic) and *hēmeĩs* (Attic-Ionic) < Proto-Greek **n̥s-mé* (*-es*).

### 2.6. Verbs

2.6.1. Most verbs are inherited, cf. athematic *es-* ‘exist, be’ (with dialectal forms such as *esmi, emmi, eimi*; there is evidence for individual forms of this paradigm since Mycenaean times, e.g. 3Pl. <*e-e-si*> /*eh-ensi*/; on Proto-Indo-European **h₁es* ‘be’ see LIV 201: 241 f.) and thematic *bhére-* ‘bear, carry, bring’ (evidence since Mycenaean times; on Proto-Indo-European **bʰer-* see LIV 2001: 76 f.). The same principle applies for the verb as for the noun, adjective, and pronoun: the Proto-Indo-European forms all show through, but the phonetic and formal developments in the individual dialects have also led to changes. Sometimes, formal and semantic archaisms can be observed. Each case has to be investigated separately.

## 3. Loan words

### 3.1. Loan words from Pre-Greek sources

3.1.1. The place names of Archaic Greece show a mixture of Greek and non-Greek forms. Among non-Greek names are *Kórintʰo-* (already in Mycenaean), *Tíruntʰ*-, *Erú-mantʰo-* (already in Mycenaean); *Mukḗnā-*, *Atʰā́nā*- (already in Mycenaean); *Halikarnāssó*-, *Knōssó-* (already in Mycenaean); *Tʰẽbai* (already in Mycenaean: *te-qa-de* = *Tʰēgʷans-de* ‘to Thebes’), *Krēt-* (already in Mycenaean) and names of mountains such as *Ólumpo-, Parnassó*- and *Díktā-*. The following place names can be explained through Greek: *Pleurṓn-* (already in Mycenaean, lit. ‘side of an area’, from *pleura* ‘rib, side’), *Maratʰṓn-* (lit. ‘[place] rich in fennel’, derived from *máratʰ(w)o-* ‘fennel’, for which evidence is already found in Mycenaean), *Selinówont-* (already in Mycenaean, lit. ‘[place] rich in celery’), *Hríyo-* (already in Mycenaean, lit. ‘peak’, < **sri-o-*), *Hél-es-*(already in Mycenaean, lit. ‘swamp’, < **sél-es-*), *Leũk-tr-o-* (already in Mycenaean, lit. ‘lookout’, from a verbal *léusse*- [< PIE. **leuk-ye-*]) and *Platayyái* (lit. ‘plain’, < PIE **pl̥th₂éy-ih₂-*) (on the problem of place names, see Risch 1981a: 145−157).

3.1.2. The coexistence of Greek and non-Greek place names is proof that the Greeks found central places such as *Atʰā́nā-* or *Kórintʰo-* to be already inhabited and named, but that smaller places could still be given names in their own language. The interpretation of the non-Greek or Pre-Greek place names is itself disputed (see above 1.2.3).

3.1.3. To the same stratum as the Pre-Greek place names belong culture words such as <*a-sa-mi-to*> /*asámintʰo-*/ ‘bath-tub’ (already in Mycenaean, then Homer, not used in Attic) and *kupárisso-* ‘cypress’ (as a derivative already in Mycenaean, afterwards from Homer onwards), cf. Chantraine (1999: 122, 600) and Beekes (2000 and 2010) (with whose judgments the reader is welcome to disagree).

### 3.2. Loan words from Semitic sources

3.2.1. From Mycenaean times, there is evidence for words for items such as metals, spices, and clothing borrowed from Semitic sources, cf. for instance <*ku-ru-so*> /*kʰrūsó-*/ ‘gold’ (Mycenaean [the same form is used as a noun as well as an adjective], afterwards from Homer on used as a noun), <*ku-mi-no*> /*kúmīno-*/ ‘caraway’ (Mycenaean, afterwards in use from Hippocrates on), <*sa-sa-ma*> /*sāsama*/ ‘sesame’ (Mycenaean, then Attic-Ionic as *sḗsamo-*), <*ki-to*> /*kʰitṓn-*/ ‘undergarment worn on the body’ (Mycenaean, then used from Homer on). Cf. for all of these Chantraine (1999 s.vv.) and Burkert (1984: 36 ff.) (“*Zum Lehnwörterproblem*”).

### 3.3. Loan words in Hellenistic times

3.3.1. The spread of Greek throughout the entire eastern Mediterranean area also brought an enrichment of the vocabulary from eastern sources (cf. Blanc and Christol [eds.] 1999). Here, too, every case has to be considered separately. See for instance Chantraine (1999) under the entries for *mnã* and *pélekus*. Another example is the migrating word *arrabṓn* ‘down payment’ (from Classical times onward) (cf. Chantraine 1999: 115) and the Jewish and New-Testament Greek terms *páskʰa* ‘Passover’ and *hrabbí* ‘Rabbi’ (originally a form of address ‘sir’, afterwards an honorary title for excellent teachers of the law).

## 4. Special problems presented by the Ancient Greek lexicon

4.1. Besides clearly analyzable words of Indo-European origin and well understood loan words, there remain large numbers of words which, despite decades of research, have to this day resisted etymological or morphological analysis. One example: The term for ‘king’ in the Mycenaean period is <*wa-na-k-*> /*wánakt-*/, but in Archaic and Classical times this word has been replaced by *basiléw-*. This term is attested already in Mycenaean as <*qa-si-re-u*> /*gʷasilew-*/, but seems in this dialect to have denoted a local clerk. For details see Chantraine (1999: 84 f., 166 f.). There is no agreement on the origin of either term (Chantraine assumes a borrowing in both cases): /*wánakt-*/ with a second member *-ak-t-* from *ágō*? (cf. Hajnal 1998: 60 ff.); /*gʷasilew-*/ *w*ith -*ew-s*uffix to a noun **gʷasi-lo-* ‘tangible assets’ (**gʷasi-lo*- itself can be a derivation in -*lo-* to **gʷasi-*, the -*si-*abstract of **gʷem-* or **gʷeh₂-*)? (cf. Peters 1993: 39072; Janda 2004: 84−94).

4.2. Further problems of morphological interpretation occur with nouns such as *atástʰa-lo-* ‘overconfident, carefree’ (poetic Homer onward, also in Herodotus) (Chantraine 1999: 132: “*origine inconnue*”) or *kʰróno-* ‘time’ (Homer onward) (Chantraine 1999: 1277 f.: “l’analyse *kʰr-óno-* ne fournit ni un suffixe, ni un radical acceptables” [The analysis *kʰr-óno-* provides neither an acceptable suffix nor an acceptable root]). These terms all look like genuine Greek words, but here the basic linguistic problems have not yet been solved. See also *tʰálassa* (formed with a genuine, inherited Greek suffix -*ih₂-*/ *-yéh₂-*) in 2.2.3.3.

## 5. Word formation

### 5.1. Preliminaries

5.1.1. The formation of new words never comes out of the blue. Normally, speakers analogically apply living morphological processes of word-formation in order to produce new items from existing material. One example: From Mycenaean onward nouns in -*ēw*-are very much alive, cf. *hier-ḗw-* (Mycenaean, Homer onward), a derivative of *hieró-*(adjective: ‘holy, belonging to a deity or to the cult of a deity’; substantive: N. Pl. ‘sacrifice’) meaning ‘one who deals with the *hierá*’ = ‘priest’. On this basis (according to what I will call Pattern A) new forms could be produced with the verbal stem suffixes *-ye-* or *-s-*, for instance verbal stems of the type *hiyeréws-* (aoristic, athematic, here *-ews-*with shortening of the long diphthong from **-ēw-s-*) or **hiyerēwye-* (present, thematic; actually **hiyeréyye-* with *-*eyye-* < *-*ewye-* < *-*ēwye-* should be expected but has been replaced by the more transparent *hiyeréwe-* influenced by the aorist stem *hiyeréws-*) meaning (in Homer) ‘sacrifice as a victim’. Furthermore (what I call Pattern B), it was possible to form the noun **hiyerēwiyo-* from *hiyer-ēw-* by means of the suffix *-iyo-*. Cf. the phonetically developed Homeric neuter *hiyerḗïo-* ‘victim, sacrificial animal’. While in Patterns A and B the derivation begins with the substantive in -*ēw-*, the speaker furthermore has the option (what I will call Pattern C) of directly forming a verbal -*ews*-/-*ewe-* from *hiyeró-*, without using -*ēw-* and also of adding the nominal suffix -*ēwiyo-* (from which is derived the younger -*ḗïo-* or -*éyyo-*), cf. Homeric *bouléwe-* ‘advise, decide’ and Classical *bouléiiā-* (e.g. in Aristophanes) ‘council membership’ besides *boulḗ-* (Homer onward; -*ḗ-* Attic-Ionic, otherwise older -*ā́-*) ‘council (in the meaning of assembly or advice)’; a nominal **boulew-* is found nowhere.

5.1.2. In addition to suffix-based word formation, a second pattern available for the creation of new words in Greek was compounding, a process of Proto-Indo-European origin (for further reading see 5.3). The literature on word formation is massive (cf. Risch 1974; Meier-Brügger 1992 II: 18 ff., 2002: 284 ff.)

### 5.2. Suffix formation

5.2.1. The number of Proto-Indo-European suffixes is very large (cf. Meier-Brügger 1992 II: 20−33, 2002: 286−295). From derivational chains with two or more members, speakers can isolate suffix conglomerates and thus extend the number of suffixes, compare -*ḗwiyo*- < -*ḗw- + -iyo*- above in 5.1.1 In the following, some suffixes are given as examples.

5.2.2. While suffixes such as *-iyo*- or *-ter*-/-*tor-* are alive from Mycenaean onwards, a suffix like *-i-kó-* (which consists of inherited material) attained currency only within the attested history of Ancient Greek, especially in the emerging Ionic scientific language (cf. Meier-Brügger 1992 II: 22).

5.2.3.1. One example repays closer study: Verbal abstracts in -*ti-* are inherited from PIE, where they showed ablaut between a strong stem **-tey-* and a weak stem **-ti*- and were normally built to the zero-grade of the root. Furthermore, this formation is often used in compounds (cf. Vine 2004). We may trace this type throughout the period covered in this chapter as follows:

5.2.3.2. Mycenaean (ca. 1200 BCE): A clear example is <*a-pu-do-si*> /*apú-dosi-*/ ‘remuneration, payment’, contrasting with <*o-pe-ro*> /*ópʰeles-*/ ‘debt’. Furthermore, there is indirect evidence for /**apú-dasi-*/ ‘distribution’ in the term <*a-pu-da-se-we*> /*apu-das-ēw*-/ ‘someone whose task is distributing’ and /**én-kʰusi-*/ ‘a pouring in’ in the vase term <*e-ku-se-we*> /*en-kʰus-ēw*-/ ‘vase for pouring in’.

5.2.3.3. Homeric: The Epic language knows about 50 abstracts in -*si-*. Compared to Classical times, the use is rather restricted. For instance no derivations from so-called secondary verbs of the type *kákōsi*- to *kakóō*, can be found as yet (cf. Risch 1974: 38−40).

5.2.3.4. From Homer to Classical times: Through the Ionic prose of the philosophers and scientific authors, the occurrence of abstract nouns in general increases enormously and abstracts in *-si-* become extraordinarily productive (cf. Holt 1940). The formations are often lexicalized and take on concrete meanings, cf. *bási-* ‘step’ > ‘base of a statue’; *árosi-* ‘ploughing; cultivated land’, *bóustasi-* ‘place where cattle stand overnight’, *próba-si-* ‘flock’, but partly they can be used syntactically as infinitive-like verbal nouns (cf. Thucyd. 1, 137, 4 *tḕn tõn gepʰurõn*... *diálusin* ‘for the demolition of the bridges’).

5.2.3.5. The history of abstracts in Greek: The forms in *-si-*, typical of Mycenaean and Attic-Ionic, ought to stand beside Doric and Aeolic forms in *-ti-* (for the characteristic South Greek sound change of *-ti-* to *-si-*, see 1.2.3). Although it is true that individual formations in *-ti-* can be found in the latter dialects (among others, epic-poetic *mḗti-*‘cleverness, plan’ and *pʰáti-* ‘rumor, report’, furthermore *ámpōti-* ‘ebb’ in Herodotus), they otherwise show only *-si-*forms. This phenomenon can be explained if we assume that the Attic-Ionic forms in *-si-* were so popular that even the Dorians and the Aeolians used them. The Ionic philosophers and scientific authors were responsible for this popularity. Furthermore, although the behavior of the root syllable in these forms dates back to the proto-language, the suffix ablaut has undergone refashioning according to the dialects, with Attic showing a lengthened-grade -*sēy-* in the Gen. (cf. e.g. *-seōs* < **-sēy-os*) and the replacement of **-sisi* by *-sesi* in the Loc. Pl., whereas in Ionic, there is simplification to a general *-si(-)* throughout the paradigm.

### 5.3. Composition

5.3.1. Limitations of space preclude a thorough discussion here of Greek compound formation. In this and the following sections I will provide only a single example of the most common types of compounds, together with some remarks, referring the reader to the sources mentioned in 5.1.2 for further reading. All real compounds in Greek are nouns (cf. Risch 1974: 181). The great number of compound verbs such as Homeric *ek-pʰére-* ‘carry out’ have to be treated separately (the so-called pre-verbs emerged from originally independent adverbs). Among nominal compounds, one occasionally observes contractions representing internal sandhi developments attendant upon the univerbation of two-word expressions (cf. Risch 1974: 213 f.). An example is *despótē-* ‘householder’ (lit. ‘lord of the house’) (perhaps already Mycenaean as <*do-po-ta*>, but not in Homer, because its cretic prosodic structure excluded it from the hexameter) < Proto-Greek **dens + pot-* < Proto-Indo-European **dem-s* (Gen. Sg. of **dom-* ‘house, farm, the extended family living in a farm’ *+* **pot-* ‘lord’, cf. Chantraine 1999: 266 f.). The average speaker was surely no longer able to understand the first member *des-*. This type of compound is traditionally termed determinative, because it indicates an internal dependency relationship, frequently, as here, Gen. + head noun.

5.3.2. The basic semantic structure of so-called possessive compounds (called *bahuvrīhi’s* according to Sanskrit terminology) is similar to nominal phrases, cf. Homeric *argu-ró-tokso-* ‘having a bow (*tókso-*) [made] of silver (*arguró-*)’ < *‘bow’ is ‘silver’. In the nominal phrase the owner is given in the dative case (‘to him the bow is silver’ = ‘he has a silver bow’), whereas in the possessive compound the nominal phrase adjectivally characterizes the owner (‘having a silver bow’). Possessive compounds date back to Proto-Indo-European times, but a multitude of independent Greek developments can be identified.

5.3.3. The basic semantic structure of so-called verb-noun compounds is equivalent to that of a verbal phrase. One part of the compound is a verbal derivative, the other part serves as a verbal complement (object, agent, adverbial, or adverb) (cf. Risch 1974: 189 ff.). Particularly common is the so-called verbal governance compound which specifies a verb together with its object. Because the compound is fundamentally adjectival, the verbal notion may usually be understood participially. An example is the Mycenaean personal name <*a-ke-ra-wo*> /*Agé-lāwo*-/ lit. ‘leading the folk’ and Homeric *kouro-tro-pʰó*- lit. ‘child-nourishing’, then substantively ‘wet nurse, helper who raises children’. Verb-noun compounds also date back to Proto-Indo-European, again with typically Greek developments.

5.3.4. Derived compounds are an independent group. They are actually adjectival compounding derivations from two-member expressions, particularly preposition + object. Cf. Homeric *einháliyo-* ‘existing in the sea’ (of sea animals and the island of Cyprus; *ein-* with metrical lengthening instead of *en-*) < *en* + *halí* (cf. Meier-Brügger 1992 II: 35, 2002: 297)
