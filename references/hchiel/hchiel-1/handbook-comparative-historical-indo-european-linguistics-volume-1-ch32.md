## 1. Time and place

Iranian languages are known from three linguistic-chronological periods commonly referred to as Old, Middle, and New or Modern Iranian.

Old Iranian constitutes the earliest manifestation of the Iranian branch of the Indo-Iranian subgroup of Indo-European and comprises Old and Young Avestan and Old Persian. The Avestan languages were presumably spoken in the late 2ⁿᵈ and early 1ˢᵗ millennia BCE in Central Asia and the area of modern Afghanistan and Sistan, which constitute the geographical horizons of the Young Avesta. Old Persian was the language of the Persian population settled in southern Iran (area of modern Fārs) and is attested in inscriptions of Achaemenid kings datable from 522/521 to ca. 350 BCE.

Middle Iranian languages are known from a large variety of sources from the third century CE and into the Muslim period (from the 7ᵗʰ century in the west to the 11ᵗʰ− 12ᵗʰ centuries in the east).

New Iranian languages are all from the Islamic period, but few of them have long literary traditions. New Persian is known from the 8ᵗʰ century, Gurani from the 14ᵗʰ, and Pashto from the 16ᵗʰ (Skjærvø 2006c).

## 2. Old Iranian dialects

The Iranians separated from their Indian relatives, perhaps about the beginning of the 2ⁿᵈ millennium BCE, and individual Iranian tribes developed as the population expanded west and east. In the 2ⁿᵈ and early 1ˢᵗ millennia BCE, Old Iranian languages were probably spoken from north of the Black Sea to modern Tajikistan, perhaps even modern Xinjiang. By around 1000 BCE, some tribes had migrated onto the Iranian Plateau, where the presence of Parsuwas and Mātai in northwestern Iran and, later, farther south, is recorded in the Assyrian dynastic annals from the 9ᵗʰ and 8ᵗʰ centuries (Waters 1999).

Several of these tribes are known from the Achaemenid inscriptions and the Greek historians, as well as from the Young Avesta. Over the entire northern stretch we find Scythians/Sakas; in the area of the modern Central Asian republics and Afghanistan, Choresmians, Sogdians, Bactrians, and others; and, on the Iranian Plateau, Parthians, Medes, and Persians. All of these are known from literary remains.

Iranian languages have traditionally been divided into two geographical groups, (South-)Western and (North-)Eastern, a division based on the Middle Persian and Parthian texts discovered at Turfan and confirmed by modern dialects (Tedesco 1921). With the discoveries of additional Middle Iranian languages and further study of the modern dialects, this classification has become less useful.

Another classification is based on the developments of the palatal affricates **ć* and **ȷ́* and the groups **ćᵘ̯* and **ȷ́ᵘ̯* (see Schmitt 1989: 27−28), which define three dialect areas:

−Southwestern Iranian, where **ć* and **ȷ́* merged with *θ* and *d* and **ćᵘ̯* and **ȷ́ᵘ̯* with *s* and *z*, comprises the “Perside” dialects in the area of ancient Pārsa, modern Fārs, including Old, Middle, and Modern Persian.

−Northeastern Iranian, where **ć* and **ȷ́* merged with *s* and *z* but **ćᵘ̯* and **ȷ́ᵘ̯* with *ś* and *ź*, include Khotanese and modern Wakhi.

−Central Iranian, where **ć* and **ȷ́* merged with *s* and *z* and **ćᵘ̯* and **ȷ́ᵘ̯* with *sp* and *zb*, comprises all the other languages and dialects.

Northwestern Iranian, represented by the Alanic dialects and modern Ossetic, shares the Central isoglosses, but is also characterized by the early developments of internal *ry* to *l* and, later, initial *p* to *f*.

Other dialect isoglosses can be established on the basis of morphology and lexicon.

## 3. Old Persian and Median

Old Persian is the only Old Iranian language documented by contemporary texts and the only one known from two later stages (Middle, and New Persian). It was written in a cuneiform script devised under Darius I to record his deeds in the manner of contemporary kings. The trilingual Bisotun inscription is the longest Old Persian text and also provided the key to deciphering the other Ancient Near Eastern cuneiform inscriptions. The remaining corpus consists of shorter inscriptions by Darius I and his son and successor Xerxes. Later inscriptions are few and bear witness to a post-Old Persian or proto-Middle Persian stage of Persian (Skjærvø 2002).

Median is known only from numerous non-Persian forms in Old Persian, e.g. *aspa* beside OP. *asa* ‘horse’. Old Persian and Median loan words are also found in Aramaic (Greenfield 1986), Elamite (cited rather than borrowed; Hinz 1975), Akkadian, Greek (Schmitt 2002), and elsewhere.

## 4. Avestan and the *Avesta*

The Avestan languages are known from the *Avesta*, the sacred book of the Zoroastrians, a collection of mostly ritual texts presumably orally composed in the 2ⁿᵈ and 1ˢᵗ millennia BCE. The texts were orally transmitted until written down in the late Sasanian period, ca. 600 CE in what Hoffmann called “the Sasanian archetype” (Skjærvø 1995, 2006; Kellens 1998; Hoffmann and Narten 1989), a concept criticized by Kellens (1998: 487) and by Cantera (2014: 75, 317, 326−328; see also Skjærvø 2016: 165b, 175b−176a, 176−177).

Old Avestan, linguistically close to Old Indic (Rigvedic), should probably be dated to the late 2ⁿᵈ millennium BCE, and Young Avestan, closer to Old Persian, to the early 1ˢᵗ millennium. Young Avestan shares some new morphological features with Old Persian, which may have developed while their speakers were still in Central Asia (Skjærvø 2003−2004: 33−34). Old Avestan and Young Avestan are apparently not simply different stages of the same language, as there are phonological and morphological isoglosses separating them, but the form of the language in the manuscripts may not be a good guide to the original, spoken, Old Avestan (Skjærvø 2003−2004: 29−32).

The most recent edition of the *Avesta* is Geldner’s (1886−1895), which contains the *Yasna*, *Vispered*, *Yašt*s, *Khorde Avesta*, and *Videvdad*. Westergaard’s (1852−1854) edition was more complete, but based on fewer manuscripts. The Avestan texts are the following (Kellens 1987, 1998):

−The *Yasna*, which originally accompanied the morning sacrifice (*yasna*; Skjærvø 2006b), also contains the *Old Avesta*: the five *Gāθā*s, metrical poems originally also probably recited at the morning (and/or new year) ritual, and the *Yasna haptaŋhāiti*, a praise hymn in archaic poetic form (Watkins 1995: 232−240).

−The *Videvdad* (Av. *dāta- vī.daēuua-* ‘the established law for keeping the bad gods away’, Pahlavi *Windēdad*), accompanies the *videvdad sade* purification ritual, an extended *yasna* recited from midnight until after sunrise, in which parts of the *Yasna* are replaced by the *Vispered* and the chapters of the *Videvdad* are inserted in and around the *Old Avesta* (Skjærvø 2006a).

−The *Yašt*s are twenty-one hymns to individual deities. Westergaard’s edition counts four additional “*yašt*s”: Yt. 21−22 = *Hādōxt nask* 1−2, Yt. 23 = *Āfrīn payghāmbar Zardušt*, and Yt. 24 = *Vištāsp yašt* (the last two are compilations of older and later material).

−The *Khorde Avesta* (little Avesta) is a miscellany of hymns (among them five *Niyāyišn*s ‘songs’, which are short versions of corresponding *Yašt*s; the *Sī-rōzag*s, invocations of the thirty deities in charge of the days of the month), and liturgical texts for daily use (e.g. the *Āfrīnagān*).

−Instructional texts include the *Hērbedestān* (religious training) and the *Nērangestān* (performance of rituals).

−The *Frahang ī oīm ēk* (the dictionary [beginning with Avestan] *oīm* [‘one’ = Pahlavi] *ēk*) contains Avestan words and their Pahlavi equivalents, evidently compiled from texts, some of them no longer extant.

−There are numerous Avestan “fragments” scattered throughout the Zoroastrian literature, most of them in the Pahlavi translations with commentaries on Avestan texts (Kellens 1987: 40).

## 5. The Avestan script

About 600 CE (Kellens 1998), a phonetic alphabet was invented in order to write down the known Avestan texts and to ensure their correct recitation. It is usually assumed that it was based on the Pahlavi script as known from the extant manuscripts, with the addition of forms taken from the Psalter script (Hoffmann 1987).

What the principles were that governed the phonetic analysis of the spoken text we do not know, but they were probably the same that applied to the learning of the text. Thus, the priests may simply have assigned a sign to each of the sounds they had been taught to utter during recitation, e.g.: ‘to say this word, say the sound X’ > ‘to write this word, write the sign X’.

Between the invention of the script and the earliest manuscripts (13ᵗʰ century), the original value of some letters had been forgotten; thus, the two nasal vowels *ą* and *ᶕ* and three sibilants *š*, *š́*, and *š ̣*were no longer correctly distinguished. The distribution of these letters varies from scribe to scribe.

Words are separated by a dot, and individual words and components of compounds are not distinguished. In Western academia, the dot is kept in compounds, but usually replaced by a space between words. The common practice of asterisking words reinterpreted as compounds (or compounds reinterpreted as individual words) is based on unfamiliarity with the script.

## 6. Avestan manuscripts

The extant manuscripts were written in Persia and India only in the 13ᵗʰ to 19ᵗʰ centuries and fall into two categories: with and without (*sade*) Pahlavi translation. The quantity and quality of these manuscripts vary greatly: The Videvdad is known from the Pahlavi Videvdad (PV) manuscript family, the two oldest members of which (K1, L4, L40) are both quite incomplete, and a Persian and an Indian *sade* family (PVS and IVS). By contrast, the *Yasna* is known from three Pahlavi Yasna (PY) families (Persian PY [PPY], Indian PY [IPY], Sanskrit Yasna [SY]) and three *sade* families (YS, PVS, IVS); The *Vispered* is found in Pahlavi and *sade* manuscripts (PVr, VrS, PVrS, IVrS). The *Yašt*s and *Niyāyišn*s are known from the *Yašt* manuscript family in two branches (represented in Geldner by F1 and J10), some of them also from the *Khorde Avesta* (XA) manuscripts. The latter are very numerous and cannot be stemmatized). The *Hērbedestān* and *Nērangestān* are known from two manuscripts, both copied from a single, incomplete, manuscript. The remaining texts are also known from a small number of manuscripts.

The oldest manuscripts are from the 13ᵗʰ−14ᵗʰ centuries (PVr ms. K7a: 1278?; IPY mss. J2 and K5: 1323; PV mss. K1: 1324, L4: 1323; IXA ms. Jm4: 1352), and, from the evidence of the manuscript colophons and mistakes common to all the manuscripts of a text, they all go back to single manuscripts for each part of the *Avesta* that were in existence around 1000 CE (Kellens 1998). It should be noted that the orthography of the oldest members of the IPY and PV (K5 and J2, K1 and L4), all four copied by the same scribe, is quite erratic.

Most manuscripts of the *Avesta* are much later, however, and, for some parts of the text, the manuscript tradition does not go back beyond the 16ᵗʰ−19ᵗʰ centuries (*Yašt* ms. F1: 1591, J10: 1832; PPY mss. Mf1: 1741, Pt4: 1780). This means that text criticism in the Classical sense can only restore the readings of manuscripts no older than 1000 CE, but mostly only much later readings (see Cantera 2014, chapter 2, on the problems involved in constructing stemmata of Avestan manuscripts).

The history of the *Avesta* before that time is poorly known, except for the fact that it was transmitted orally by people who spoke later stages and various dialects of Iranian and by more or less conscientious scribes, all of whom left their marks on the extant text (Skjærvø 2007a).

A desideratum is the study of individual manuscripts to determine the scope of scribal variations in paleography and orthography. Without it, no definitive descriptive orthography, hence also phonology and morphology, of Avestan can be written. Current and earlier descriptions are to a large extent based upon Geldner’s edition, which is the one most often referred to for grammatical purposes (his choices of “default” letters also do not correspond to what we know of the script today, e.g. *š ̣*rather than *š*). It is not a completely critical edition, however, since the relative importance of the manuscripts was not clear to him during the publication (see his *Prolegomena*). The edition can therefore not (as increasingly recognized) be used as the only basis for grammatical analysis. Bartholomae (1904) introduced several readings based on the better manuscripts, marking them with (⁺), a practice long imitated by Indo-Europeanists, although the rationale was mostly overlooked.

## 7. The oral Avestan text

The extant text of the *Avesta* is the result of 1,500 to 2,000 years of oral transmission. During this time, the texts had, presumably, been composed and recomposed in memory and performance before being committed to memory so as not to be changed (“crystallized”). This happened to both the Old Avestan and the Young Avestan corpora. Over the generations, the priests interfered with the text, intentionally and unintentionally, but, most importantly, the priests who performed the texts that were written down no longer knew the original meaning of the Avestan texts; rather, their understanding of the texts was that of the inherited tradition, which we know mainly from the Pahlavi texts redacted in the 9ᵗʰ−11ᵗʰ centuries. Thus, the oral background of the Avestan texts suffices to explain grammatical inconsistencies and (from our point of view) erroneous forms and uses (Skjærvø 2006a: 112−115).

The idea that Old and Young Avestan might have been used to compose new texts long after they died out, adducing the use of Latin in Europe, is untenable, as Latin was learned from written texts (Skjærvø 2003−2004: 17−19).

This situation also renders theories about dialect features in Young Avestan doubtful, such as those of Schindler (1982: developments of final **-aŋh* < IIr. **-ans*) and Hoffmann (Hoffmann and Forssman 1996: §§ 6.2, 63 cg: Arachosian dialect features), as well as the assumption of “Middle Avestan” texts (Pirart, Tremblay; references and summary of the evidence in Kellens 2011: 107−113).

On the other hand, much of the extant text is evidently correct, and, in spite of all its inconsistencies and contrary to the common opinion of pre-World War II European scholarship, must reflect a real linguistic system (Morgenstierne 1942).

## 8. Middle Iranian

Middle Iranian languages were spoken from western Iran to western Xinjiang. They include, from west to east (surveys in Schmitt [1989] and Windfuhr [2009]):

−Alanic, descended from western Scythian.

−Middle Persian (Pahlavi), known from 3ʳᵈ-century inscriptions, Manichean texts (from the 3ʳᵈ−4ᵗʰ centuries on), the “Psalter” (a fragment of the *Psalms of David*; 4ᵗʰ−5ᵗʰ cents.?, Skjærvø 1983: 178−179), official documents (7ᵗʰ cent.; Weber 2008: XIV− XV), and Zoroastrian texts (the earliest redacted in the 9ᵗʰ century).

−Parthian, known from 2ⁿᵈ to 3ʳᵈ-century inscriptions and Manichean texts (from the 4ᵗʰ century on).

−Bactrian, known from inscriptions (from 2ⁿᵈ cent.) and a large corpus of documents (3ʳᵈ−8ᵗʰ cents.; Sims-Williams 2000−2007).

−Choresmian, known from a small number of inscriptions and from manuscripts.

−Sogdian, known from a few inscriptions and a large corpus of texts: 4ᵗʰ-century letters, Manichean texts (4ᵗʰ century on?), official documents (ca. 700 CE), Buddhist texts mostly translated from Chinese, Christian texts (many still unpublished) mostly translated from Syriac.

−Khotanese, in three stages: Old Khotanese, known mainly from Buddhist texts mostly translated from Sanskrit; Middle Khotanese, known from Buddhist texts and official documents and letters (7ᵗʰ−9ᵗʰ centuries), and Late Khotanese known from Buddhist texts and letters (9ᵗʰ−10ᵗʰ centuries).

−Tumshuqese (Old and Middle), poorly known from a few Buddhist texts, letters, and official documents.

Several non-Iranian languages contain loan words from Middle Iranian, notably, Parthian and Middle Persian in Aramaic, Armenian, and Arabic (Shaked 1986; Bailey and Schmitt 1986; Tafażżolī 1986) and “proto-Sogdian” in Tocharian.

## 9. New Iranian

New Iranian languages and dialects can be classified in the following larger groups (surveys in Schmitt [1989] and Windfuhr [2009]):

−Ossetic, descended from Alanic, spoken in the Caucasus and with a large literature.

−Dimli-Caspian dialects spoken in pockets in Turkey and in north(west)ern Iran.

−Kurdish, in three main geographical variants (North, Central, South).

−The “Central Dialects,” spoken in (north)western Iran and in a large area to the south of the great salt desert.

−Lori and Fārs dialects, spoken in southwestern and southern Iran. Persian (Farsi) belongs here, but, as an official language, spread widely and developed into several dialects, among them Tajiki spoken in Tajikistan. The earliest texts are in Judeo-Persian (written in Hebrew script).

−Larestani, Bandari, Bashkardi, and other southeast-Iranian dialects.

−Balochi, spoken in eastern Iran, Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Central Asia.

−Pashto, spoken in several dialects in and around Afghanistan.

−“Pamir languages”, including Shughni and related dialects, Yidgha-Munji, Wakhi (related to Khotanese), etc.

−Yaghnobi, spoken in southern Tajikistan and related to Sogdian.

## 10. Texts and grammatical descriptions

General works: Abaev et al. (1981−1987); Schmitt (1989); Windfuhr (2009) and Skjærvø (2006c). Etymological dictionaries: Abaev (1958−1989); Steblin-Kamenskiĭ (1999); Morgenstierne (1974, 2003); Rastorgueva/Edel’man, (2000−); Ḥasandust (1383/2004−); Cheung (2007). Articles on individual languages/dialects, dialect groups, and grammatical features throughout the *Encyclopædia Iranica* (also at *http://www.iranicaonline.org/*).

The Old Persian corpus is well edited (Kent 1953; Schmitt 1991, 2000, 2009); grammars: Kent (1953; also syntax); Brandenstein/Mayrhofer (1964); Skjærvø (2007b, 2009); dictionary: Schmitt (2014).

Of the Avestan corpus there are only editions and translations of individual texts. Old Avesta: Kellens/Pirart (1988, 1990, 1991); Humbach (1991); Humbach and Faiss (2010; goes little beyond Humbach 1991), West (2010; a throwback to pre-1950 interpretations); *Gāθā*s only: Humbach (1959); Insler (1975); *Yasna Haptaŋhāiti*: Narten (1986); Hintze (2008). Young Avesta: several modern editions of individual *Yašt*s and other texts; desiderata include editions of the *Yasna* and *Videvdad*. Note also the *editio princeps* of the *Drōn yašt* liturgy (Karanjia 2010).

Bartholomae’s dictionary (1904) has not been replaced; for Old Avestan, individual editions must be consulted.

There are no up-to-date complete grammars; partial grammars: Hoffmann (1987); Hoffmann-Forssman (1996); Beekes (1988); Skjærvø (2007b). Complete syntax in Reichelt (1909); Skjærvø (2009); Old Avestan in West (2011). Most recent discussion of phonology and theoretical issues in de Vaan (2003). Comprehensive translations include Darmesteter (1892−1893; all known texts) and Wolf (1910; incomplete German translation based on Bartholomae 1904); select translations include those in Malandra (1983) and Skjærvø (2011).

The Middle Iranian texts have been well edited, including numerous up-to-date translations. Grammatical descriptions are fewer and often sketchy. The literature on modern languages and dialects is large (see the articles in Skjærvø 2006c and Windfuhr 2009).

Numerous Avestan manuscripts are now available in the Avestan Digital Archive at http://ada.usal.es/ (including numerous descendants of K1 and L4). For the Copenhagen collection of Avestan and Pahlavi manuscripts see http://www.kb.dk/manus/ortsam/2009/okt/orientalia/subject640/en/ and for the Munich collection (M51ab) see https://www.bsb-muenchen.de/ (search for Cod.Zend).
