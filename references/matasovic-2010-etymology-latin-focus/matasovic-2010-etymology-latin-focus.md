# The etymology of Latin _focus_ and the devoicing of final stops before *s in Proto-Indo-European

Ranko Matasović

Source: _Historische Sprachforschung / Historical Linguistics_ 123 (2010): 212-216. Published by Vandenhoeck & Ruprecht (GmbH & Co. KG). Stable URL: https://www.jstor.org/stable/41219150.

Latin _focus_ „hearth, fireplace“ represents an etymological _crux_.[^1] Despite many attempts to find the origin of this word, there is, at present, no commonly accepted etymology. The connection with Arm. _bocʿ_ „fire“ (Walde-Hoffmann's 1930-1950, I: 521, IEW 162) is untenable, since Arm. _-cʿ-_ points to the PIE cluster *-sk-. The Armenian word might or might not be related to Arm. _bosor_ „red“, but this connection would not help us understand the origin of Lat. _focus_. A PIE root of the shape DʰeT- would contradict the phonotactic rules of PIE root-formation. The connection of _focus_ with Lat. _fax_ „torch“, Lith. _žvãkė_ „candle“ is impossible because of the diverging vocalism. Hamp's (1992) attempt to explain _focus_ as a back-formation from _foculus_ „small stove“ is extremely difficult. Hamp assumes that _foculus_ is derived from *dʰgʷʰe-tlo-, from the root *dʰegʷʰ- „to burn“ (Lat. _foveo_ „to warm“), and that Proto-Italic *xʷetlo- developed in Latin first to *xʷotlo-, and only subsequently to *fotlo- > Lat. _foculu-_. However, as Michiel de Vaan (2008: 228) points out, the change of *we- to *wo- is datable to the fifth century BC, and the development of *xʷ to *f is almost certainly earlier (it is probably common Italic). After briefly reviewing the work of his predecessors, de Vaan follows Ernout and Meillet (1979: 243) in leaving the question of the etymology of _focus_ unresolved.

Derivationally, _focus_ is quite isolated in Latin; although it is attested since Plautus, it has only a handful of derivatives, including the mentioned _foculus_. This could point to borrowing from some unknown source, but to make such an assumption is nothing short of acknowledging ignorance. The other possibility is that _focus_ represents a derivative from some well-known PIE root, but that the shape of the root was changed beyond recognition.

I would like to propose a new solution to this old problem. I believe that Lat. _focus_ developed from the PIE root-noun *dʰōgʷʰ-s „flame, fire“ from the root *dʰegʷʰ- „to burn“ (Ved. _dáhati_, Lith. _degù_, _dègti_, OCS _žegǫ_, LIV 115f.). The root is attested in Latin in the derivative _febris_ „fever“ (< *dʰegʷʰri-) and in the causative _foveo_ „to warm“ (< *dʰogʷʰ-eye-).[^2] The root-final *-gʷʰ- would have been devoiced and delabialized before the nominative *-s, and the result was the cluster *-ks-, as in Lat. _vōx_ „voice“ < *wōkʷ-s. Another reflex of the same PIE root noun is probably preserved in PCelt. *degʷi- „flame, blaze“ (OIr. _daig_, cf. Matasović 2009: 93), with the transfer to the i-stems. If PCelt. *degʷi- generalized the stem of the oblique cases, we may reconstruct to PIE a static paradigm with the Nom. sg. *dʰōgʷʰ-s, Acc. sg. *dʰogʷʰ-m, Gen. sg. *dʰegʷʰ-s. This would have been parallel to, e.g., the inflection of the word for „night“, PIE Nom. sg. *nōkʷt-s (> Ved. _nák_), Acc. sg. *nokʷt-m (> Lat. _noct-em_), Gen. sg. *nekʷt-s (> Hitt. _nekuz_), cf. Schindler 1972. Of course, it is also possible that in PCelt. *degʷi- the e-grade was introduced under the analogical influence of the verb (PIE *dʰegʷʰ-o- > Skt. _dáhatí_).

The evolution of meaning from „flame, fire“ to „hearth“ might appear problematic, but these meanings are quite often related. It is true that the attested developments are usually in the reverse direction, from „hearth“ to „fire“, as in the history of Lat. _focus_ in the Romance languages, where the original Lat. _ignis_ was generally replaced by the reflexes of _focus_ in the meaning „fire“ (e.g. It. _fuoco_, Sp. _fuego_, Fr. _feu_). Similarly, Croatian _vȁtra_ (of unknown origin, but certainly connected with Alb. _vatër_, _votër_ „fireplace, hearth“) changed the meaning from „hearth“ to „fire“ in the contemporary language, replacing the inherited reflex of Proto-Slavic *ognь (Russ. _ogón'_, etc.). However, the extension of meaning from „fire“ to „fireplace, hearth“ is attested in Celtic; the older meaning of PCelt. *tepnet- is found in W _tân_ „fire“, while OIr. _teine_ (Gen. sg. _teined_) has both meanings, „fire“ and „fireplace, hearth“ (DIL 583). We may assume that the same change of meaning occurred in the history of _focus_.

The etymology of Lat. _focus_ presented here would certainly be more convincing if we could find parallel examples, in which the apparently irregular correspondences of voiceless stops in one language, with voiced stops in others, are due to devoicing before final *-s, and the subsequent generalization of the voiceless stop in the whole paradigm. And indeed, there are similar cases. Latin _hortus_ „garden“, Gr. _khórtos_ „enclosed place, feeding place“, OIr. _gort_ „field“ < *„enclosed field“ and W _garth_ „pen, enclosure“ (< PCelt. *gorto-, Matasović 2009: 164-5) all have the voiceless _-t-_ because these forms developed by thematicization of a root noun, PIE *gʰōrdʰ-s, Acc. *gʰordʰ-m, Gen. *gʰrdʰ-os „enclosure“. The reflexes of the aspirated root-final *dʰ are preserved in OCS _gradъ_ „town“, Lith. _gardas_ „pen“, Skt. _gṛhá-_ „house“ (with the zero-grade of the root from the oblique cases, cf. Matasović 2004: 99). Here too we may start from a PIE root-noun in which the voiced or aspirated root-final consonant became voiceless before the word-final *-s. The voiceless root-final consonant was subsequently generalized throughout the paradigm. To posit both *gʰor-to- and *gʰor-dʰo- in PIE, as de Vaan does (2008: 290-291), is possible in principle, but it is rather unmotivated, since both forms would have had exactly the same meaning („enclosure“) in PIE. Clearly a simpler explanation, deriving all the attested forms from a single PIE etymon is preferable. If *gʰor-to- is indeed a verbal adjective to the root *gʰer- „enclose“, the o-vocalism in Celtic and Greek is unexpected (Lat. _hortus_ may, of course, be from both *gʰrto- and *gʰorto-). Moreover, the very existence of a root *gʰer- „enclose“ is uncertain. LIV (157) and IEW posit *ĝʰer-, but the connection of the words for „enclosure“ with Skt. _hárati_ „takes, brings“ is doubtful, both formally (OCS _gradъ_ and Lith. _gardas_ point to the plain velar in PIE)[^3] and semantically (the evolution of meaning from „enclose“ to „take, bring“, or vice versa, is possible, but far from obvious).

Likewise, PCelt. *druko- „bad“ (OIr. _droch-_, MW _drwg-_, etc., cf. Matasović 2009: 105) represents a thematicization of the stem *druk-, which is the regular outcome of the PIE root-noun *dʰrōwgʰ-s „lie, deceit“ (cf. Skt. _drúhyate_ „deceives“, _drúh-_ „deceit“, OHG _gi-trog_ „deceit“, IEW 276) before word-final *-s (with *gʰs > *ks). The zero-grade of the root was generalized, apparently, from the oblique cases (Gen. sg. *dʰrugʰ-os, etc.).

Next, Lat. _falx_ (Gen. sg. _falcis_) „hook, scythe, sickle“ is most likely related to OIr. _delg_ „thorn, pin“, W _dala_ „sting“, OIc. _dálkr_ „pin, spine, dagger“, Lith. _dìlgė_ „nettle“, but both the vocalism (_-al-_ rather than *-ol-) and final _-c-_ (rather than *g) are unexplained (de Vaan 2008: 200). Even if the word is borrowed in Latin from some IE language of Italy, as de Vaan thinks, its root-final _-c-_ may be due to devoicing before final *-s in the Nom. sg. of the PIE root noun (*dʰōlg-s, Gen. *dʰlg-os).

Finally, PCelt. *mēko- „sack, bushel (of corn)“ (OIr. _míach_) can be plausibly connected to OPr. _moasis_ „barley“, Latv. _mìeži_ (pl.), Lith. _miẽžiai_ (pl.) „barley“, if one starts from PIE root-noun *mēygʰs [mēyks] from which the root form with *k could have been extended analogically (Matasović 2009: 162). However, in the absence of cognates from other languages,[^4] this last example is clearly less persuasive than the others.

It is worth mentioning that the same process of the generalization of devoiced root-final stops in root nouns can be observed in the history of Latin. Lat. _pāx_, _pācis_ „peace“ owes its root-final _-c-_ to the generalization of the root form which is expected before the final _-s_. The comparison with _pango_ „to fix“, Gr. _pegnymi_ „to attach“, Skt. _pajrá-_ „solid, firm“ shows that the PIE root was *peh₂ĝ-, and that the voiceless stop was generalized in Latin on the analogy with the Nom. sg., where *g was regularly devoiced before word-final *-s. The root form with _-c-_ was extended to all formations from this root not only in Latin (cf. also _pācātus_ „peaceful, settled“, _pacīscere_ „to arrange by negotiation, betroth“), but also in other Italic languages, cf. Umbrian _paca_ (postposition) „because of“, Paelignian _pacris_ „merciful, auspicious“.

To conclude, then, there are both formal and semantic parallels to the scenario proposed here for the development of Lat. _focus_ from the PIE root noun *dʰōgʷʰ-s „fire“. Latin either generalized the o-grade of the root from the stem of the Acc. sg. (*dʰogʷʰ-m), or introduced the o-grade analogically with the other thematic stems (cf. the same o-vocalism in _hortus_ above).

[^1]: I would like to thank Michiel de Vaan for useful comments on the earlier version of this paper.

[^2]: The etymological connection of _focus_ and _foveo_ is alluded to in Servius' commentary (_Commentary on the Aeneid_, 12.118): _quidquid ignem fovet, focus vocatur_. This also shows that _focus_ is not a sacrificial altar in Latin (_āra_), but rather a common fireplace, so that the semantic evolution from the noun meaning „fire“ is even more probable. On the semantic opposition between _āra_ and _focus_ see Nagy 1974: 88.

[^3]: Russ. _zoród_ „hay-stack, enclosure for a hay-stack“ and Lith. _žárdas_ „rack for drying flax“ are probably unrelated to OCS _gradъ_ ad Lith. _gardas_, because the accentuation of the two sets of forms disagree: the former words point to Balto-Slavic acute, and the latter to Balto-Slavic circumflex (Derksen 2008: 548). Hitt. _gurta-_ „citadel“ is also often connected with these words, but the vocalism (_-ur-_ rather than expected _-ar-_) is quite irregular.

[^4]: The comparison with Khotanese _massa_ „field“ in Mallory and Adams (eds.) 1997: 51 is extremely doubtful.
