# On the Origin of Greek μῆλον, Latin mālum, Albanian mollë and Hittite šam(a)lu- ‘apple’

Guus Kroonen  
Institute for Nordic Studies and Linguistics, University of Copenhagen

## Abstract

In the present paper, I discuss the much-debated origin of Greek `μῆλον`, Latin `mālum` and Albanian `mollë` ‘apple’. On the basis of the attestations, the proto-form of this apparently Mediterranean etymon is usually reconstructed as `*meh₂lo-`. There is another possible cognate, however, that I would like to adduce here, i.e. Hittite `šam(a)lu-` ‘apple’. With this new *comparandum*, the original reconstruction can, in compliance with the known sound changes in Hittite and the other languages, be corrected to `*smh₂l-`. This improved proto-form opens a new direction for the discussion of the etymology of this culturally significant word.

## Text

Cross-linguistically, names of fruits and crops are especially prone to borrowing. In many cases, the word has entered the language more or less simultaneously with the product entering the culture. When a word wanders through several languages and cultures, it can be especially tricky to establish its ultimate origin, because one or more intermediate languages may be absent from the linguistic record.

One such *Wanderwort* of unclear origin is the Greek word `μῆλον`. It carries the meaning ‘apple’ as well as a range of other tree fruits such as the crab apple, quince, apricot and citron. Only a few possible lexical *comparanda* are available to us, and so far only Lat. `mālum` and Albanian `mollë` have been accepted as being connected to the Greek word. The nature of the connection is uncertain, however.

Latin `mālum` is formally close to the Greek word, and similarly refers to both the apple and a variety of other fruits with a kernel (Lewis and Short 1879), i.e. crab apple, quince, apricot, peach, citron and even gourd. Together with `μῆλον`, it is technically possible to derive both from a quasi-Indo-European proto-form `*meh₂-lo-`. This reconstruction also holds for Albanian `mollë`, which simply means ‘apple’. In view of the Dorian attestation `μᾶλον` it cannot be excluded, however, that both the Latin and the Albanian word were borrowed from a West Greek dialect in the first millennium BC,[^1] or that the Albanian word was borrowed from Latin at an even later stage (Orel 2000: 31).

To be rejected is the older etymological link with Hitt. `maḫla-` in the meaning of ‘apple tree’ as proposed by Cuny (1930). Since it is not known whether the retention of `*-h₂l-` as `-ḫl-` is regular in Hittite, laryngeals usually disappearing before other consonants (Kloekhorst 2008: 539), this link raises a possible formal objection. More fatally, the meaning ‘apple tree’ has after Cuny’s publication turned out to be incorrect; the word is now generally glossed as ‘young grapevine’ (Puhvel 1997: 4-5), as a result of which the etymology has now lost much if not all of its initial appeal.

In conclusion, the geographic distribution of the quasi-Indo-European postulate `*meh₂-lo-` is quite restricted, i.e. limited to Greek in a worst-case scenario and to the central Mediterranean at best. This means that, though the reconstruction `*meh₂-lo-` theoretically allows us to treat the given forms as if they were cognates inherited from a single Indo-European source, the conspicuous lack of cognates in other branches, e.g. Indo-Iranian, Balto-Slavic and Germanic, makes such an Indo-European etymology unlikely.

There is one hitherto overlooked yet phonetically regular and semantically straightforward *comparandum* from Anatolian, however, that I would like to add here. This is Hittite `šam(a)lu-`, which is attested with the Sumerogram `GIŠḪAŠḪUR` ‘apple tree’ (Sumerian `geš` ‘tree; wood’ + `ḫašḫur` = Akkadian `ḫašḫūru-` ‘apple’). Though the inclusion of this word into our set of *comparanda* only slightly improves the otherwise limited distribution of the etymon, it does shed new light on both the proto-form and its origin.

As to the proto-form of the etymon, the inclusion of Hitt. `šam(a)lu-` suggests that this was `*smh₂l-` rather than `*meh₂lo-`. The required sound changes are in line with our knowledge of the historical developments in Hittite, Greek, Latin and probably also Albanian. First of all, the different onsets are explained by the fact that word-initial `s` remains before `m` in Anatolian, while it is regularly lost before resonants in both Greek and Latin, cf. PIE `*sm-ih₂ (*gʰes-l-ih₂)` > Gr. `μία` ‘one’, Lat. `mille` ‘one thousand’.[^2] Second, by reconstructing a zero-grade sequence `*smh₂l-`, the remainder of the word forms can be explained as resulting from different vocalizations of the laryngeal: The cluster `*CRHC-` regularly receive an anaptyctic vowel between the resonant and the laryngeal in both Greek and Latin, viz. `*CReHC-`, which subsequently became lengthened by the loss of the following laryngeal, cf. PIE `*tl̥h₂-tó-` > Gr. `τλητός`, Dor. `τλᾱτός`, Lat. `lātus` ‘borne’.[^3] In Hittite, however, the anaptyctic vowel regularly surfaced in front of the resonant, viz. `*CeRHC-`, thus triggering the vocalization of `*m` to `*am`. In conclusion, the central Mediterranean and the Anatolian forms can be unified in a proto-form `*smh₂l-`, the only remaining difference being that it surfaces as an *o*-stem in the former and as a *u*-stem in the latter.

As a curious twist to the etymology proposed here: Though Hitt. `šam(a)lu-` is generally left out of consideration as a possible cognate of the Greek and Latin words,[^4] it has in fact been equated with one of the other areal words for ‘apple’, i.e. the Northwest European `*h₂eb-ol-` (Ivanov 1978: 160-162; Gamkrelidze & Ivanov 1978: 160-162; Gamkrelidze & Ivanov 1984: 639; Zavaroni 2008).[^5] This equation requires at least two *ad hoc* assumptions, however: 1) the emergence of an *s mobile* in Hittite, and 2) an otherwise unknown and demonstrably incorrect change of `*-ml-` to `*-bl-` in the European languages. Since the sound correspondences between Gr. `μῆλον`, Lat. `mālum` and `šam(a)lu-` are fully regular, it seems preferable to drop the connection with `*h₂eb-ol-`. In fact, the only sound that `šam(a)lu-` and `*h₂eb-ol-` really have in common, as Kloekhorst subtly notes in his etymological dictionary (2008: 717), is the `l`.

On a cautious note, the fact that the reconstruction `*smh₂l-` works for the attested forms in Anatolian, Greek, Latin and Albanian does not necessarily prove that we are confronted with an Indo-European lexeme (cf. Markey, 1988, p. 59). Given the still fairly limited distribution of the word, the alternative scenario would be that both Greek and Anatolian adopted the word independently during their migration from the Pontic-Caspian Steppes into their historically attested areas of settlement. It is possible, for instance, that the suffixal variation between Greek `*smh₂lo-` and Anatolian `*smh₂lu-` is a symptom of such repeated borrowing. As Latin `mālum` and Albanian `mollë` may be loanwords from Greek, the safest way to account for the origin of the words is probably to assume that they were adopted by the future speakers of Hittite and Greek somewhere in the eastern Balkans or Anatolia.[^6]

At this point, we can only speculate about the deeper etymology of the word. There is one possible clue that probably should be mentioned here. Klimov in his *Etymological Dictionary of the Kartvelian Languages* reconstructs the Proto-Kartvelian word for ‘pear’ as `*msxal-` (p. 169). Though it remains to be seen what the value of this *comparandum* is, the reconstruction `*msxal-` is just one metathesis short of the form `*smh₂l-` as reconstructed in this paper, and since the word basically consists of one large cluster of consonants, it does not seem inconceivable that the onset was affected by such a metathesis at some stage. Since in both Greek and Latin the etymon was used to denote a wide variety of fleshy stone fruits, including the quince, the original meaning may have been equally broad, e.g. ‘stone fruit’.

Whether the formal similarity between `*smh₂l-` and `*msxal-` is purely accidental or actually reflects ancient linguistic contact, i.e. from Kartvelian to Anatolian and Greek, obviously remains to be seen, but the scenario is at least not unimaginable from the historical perspective. The wild ancestor of the domesticated apple, *Malus sylvestris*, is found throughout temperate Europe, and it has proven difficult to delimit the area of initial domestication. Interfertility between cultivated and wild varieties impairs the use of genetic evidence from living plants (Zohary & Hopf 2004: 174), and although it is known from Greek and Roman sources that the tree was in use in Classical times (White 1970: 248), the archaeological evidence is inconclusive as to where and when it was included in horticultural practice. Friedrich in his seminal work on Indo-European dendronyms nevertheless suggests the “highland region south of the Caucasus” as a likely center of early domestication (1970: 58). Though it is improbable that `*smh₂l-` referred to the domesticated apple only, the spread of the cultivated variety from the Caucasus to the Aegean certainly dovetails with a possible parallel trajectory of a Kartvelian word into Anatolian and Greek. In view of the sound changes that occurred in Greek `μῆλον` and Hittite `šam(a)lu-`, particularly the loss of the laryngeal, the adoption of this proto-form may have taken place as early as the late third millennium, and certainly no later than the beginning of the second millennium BC.

## Notes

[^1]: A similar borrowing scenario is extant in the adoption of Lat. `mēlō, -nis` ‘apple-shaped melon’, Italian `melo` ‘apple tree’ from Ionic-Attic or Koine Greek `μῆλον`. Note that It. `mela` ‘apple’ is derived from `melo` according to the well-known equation tree : fruit = masculine : feminine, cf. `pero` ‘pear tree’ : `pera` ‘pear’ (Tournier 1794, 76).

[^2]: For the Indo-European age of this syntagm, cf. Skt. `sahásra-`, Av. `hazaŋra-` ‘1000’ < PIE `*sṃ-gʰéslo-`.

[^3]: There is no need to reconstruct `*μᾰσλον` (Blažek 2004: 19) or `*melno-n` (Erdal 1993: 34) in order to explain the long vowel.

[^4]: Cf. Markey (1988, 59): “An Anatolian `šamalu-` certainly lacks analogues elsewhere in Indo-European [...]”. Blažek (2004: 19): “The Anatolian designation of ‘apple’ is without any safe cognates.”

[^5]: Blažek (2004: 23) compares this word to e.g. Arabic `ʔubullat` ‘fruits verts de l’arak, figues sèches comprimées en masse’ and `ʔabal-` “frais, vert, fourrage”. For criticism, see Piwowarczyk 2014.

[^6]: Archaeologically, the people who brought Greek to Greece are notoriously difficult to trace. It can therefore be surmised that they migrated to Greece by sea, possibly through the eastern Balkans or Anatolia (Anthony 2010: 368-369). Without any archaeological evidence such a migration will remain impossible to prove, but an Anatolian route is not contradicted, at any rate, by the close connection of Greek `*smh₂lo-` with Hittite `*smh₂lu-`.
