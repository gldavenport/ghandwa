## C. s-stems[^25]

### 1.

The neuter s-stems, which formed verbal abstracts in Proto-Indo-European, preserve their **-s-** unchanged in the nominative-accusative singular, e.g. **genus, generis** ‘nation’ < **\*ǵénh₁-os, \*ǵenh₁-es-es** from the verbal root **\*ǵenh₁-** ‘be born’ = Ion. Gk. **γένος, γένεος**.

#### a.

The type **-us, -eris** appears to continue the **o/e** ablaut of the suffix that characterizes this type in Proto-Indo-European, but technically the **e** is ambiguous since it could continue any short vowel.

**foedus, foederis** ‘treaty’ (Enn.+)[^26] < **fīdō** ‘I trust’.

**pondus, ponderis** ‘weight’ (Plaut.+) < **pendō** ‘I weigh’.

#### b.

The type **-us, -oris** (< **-os, -oris**) has analogically reintroduced an **o** from the nominative-accusative into the oblique stem to replace the phonologically regular **-er-** with **-or-**. A trace of **-er-** is found in the adverb **temperī** ‘at the right time’.[^27]

**corpus, corporis** ‘body’ (Plaut.+). Cf. the Vedic root noun **kṛ́p-** ‘with beautiful form’.

**tempus, temporis** ‘time’ (Plaut.+).

### 2. -nes-

The complex suffix **-nes-** results from the suffixation of thematic forms in **-no-** with the **s**-suffix. It appears that this suffix was particularly characteristic of words having to do with exchange and commerce already in Proto-Indo-European.[^28]

**mūnus, mūneris** ‘duty, task’ (Plaut.+). Cf. Lith. **mainas** ‘exchange’.

**pignus, pignoris/pigneris** ‘surety’ (Plaut.+).

### 3.

The masculine s-stems in **-ōs** formed verbal abstracts, especially to verbs of the second conjugation (e.g. **dolor** ‘pain’ ~ **doleō** ‘I am in pain’, **tepor** ‘warmth’ ~ **tepeō** ‘I am warm’, **timor** ‘fear’ ~ **timidus** ~ **timeō**).[^29] This type was originally an internal derivative of the neuter s-stem type. These regularly became **-ōs, -ōris**, e.g. **honōs, honōris** ‘honor’. In the post-Plautine period, the **r** was thoroughly generalized to the nominative and the long **ō** of the nominative was shortened before final **r**. These changes gave the Classical Latin paradigm **honor, honōris**. Sometimes the **s** is preserved in a derivative like **honestus** ‘honorable’, **arbustum** ‘a copse’, etc.[^30]

# II. Liquid and Nasal Stems

## A. r-stems[^32]

### 1.

Within the class of r-stems there are agent nouns (*nomina agentis*) in **-tor**. These agent nouns are matched by similar forms throughout Indo-European, e.g. Gk. **δώτωρ** = Ved. **dātar-**, Umb. **ařfertur** ‘priest’ (Ib 41, etc.) < **\*ad-fer-tor** lit. ‘bringer’.[^33]

**actor** ‘drover, performer’ (Plaut.+) < **agō** ‘I drive’.

**amātor** ‘lover’ (Plaut.+) < **amā-re** ‘love’.

**arātor** ‘plowman’ (ILLRP 454.14, 132 BCE+) < **arā-re** ‘plow’.

**auctor** ‘an authority’ (Plaut.+) < **augeō** ‘increase’, cf. Umb. **uhtur** (III 7) ‘chief magistrate’ < **\*auktor**.

**dēfēnsor** ‘defender’ (Ter.+) < **dēfendō** ‘I defend’.

**scriptor** ‘writer’ (Lucil.+) < **scrībō** ‘I write’.

**tōnsor** ‘barber’ (Plaut.+) < **tondeō** ‘I shear’.

**uictor** ‘victor’ (Plaut.+) < **uincō** ‘I conquer’.

#### a.

Remember that the feminine of these forms was made in Proto-Indo-European via the *devī* suffix **\*-ih₂**. In Latin this was extended by a suffix **-k-**: so **\*genetōr > genetri-x** (= Gk. **γενέτειρα**, Ved. **janitrī-**).

#### b.

Already in Old Latin there are some denominative forms like the following:

**gladiātor** ‘gladiator’ (Cat.+) < **gladius** ‘sword’.

**iānitor** ‘door-keeper’ (Plaut.+) < **iānus** ‘gateway’.

**salīnātor** ‘operator of a saltern’ (Cat.+) < **salīnae** ‘saltern’, after **fabricātor** ‘a maker’ (Cic.+) *vel sim.* < **fabricō** ‘I fashion’; **fabrica** ‘craft’.[^34]

## B. n-stems

n-stems come in a number of varieties.

### 1. Names of persons ending in -ō[^42]

#### a.

These often function as *cognomina* and are regularly derived from adjectives:

**Catō, Catōnis** ‘sharpy’ < **catus** ‘sharp’.

**Varrō, Varrōnis** < **uārus** ‘bow-legged’.

**mandūcō** ‘glutton’ (Pompon., Apul.) < **mandūcus** (name of masked character with champing jaws in Atellan farce) < ‘chewing’ < **mandere** ‘to chew’.

#### b.

These are probably derived from a Proto-Indo-European suffix in **\*-e/on-**, mainly to thematic adjectives. When this was added to a thematic stem the result was **\*-ōn-**. The suffix has individualizing force. Cf. Gk. **στραβός** ‘squinty-eyed’ > **Στράβων** ‘the squinty-eyed one’. This suffix is probably at the root of the Germanic weak inflection of adjectives: Goth. **blinda** ‘the blind’ < **\*-ōn-** vs. **blinds** ‘blind’.[^43]

### 2.

There is another suffix **-ōn-** in Latin that also served to form terms for individuals, but in this case from nominal bases.

#### a.

Notice the **n** was preserved in the nominative after an **e** but lost after an **o** in Latin.[^49]

### 5. The suffix -iō formed abstracts[^50]

#### a.

Residually it was used to make abstracts from adjectives and agentive nouns:

**perduelliō** (Val. Antias+) < **perduellis** ‘enemy’.

**dupliō** ‘doubling’ (Lex XII+) < **duplus** ‘double’.

**consortiō** ‘partnership’ (Cic.+) < **cōnsors** ‘partner’.

1. This type is comparable to the productive Germanic pattern of forming abstracts to thematic bases, e.g. **\*berhta-** ‘bright’ (Goth. **bairhts**) > **\*berhtīn-** (Goth. **bairhtei**).
2. The suffix may be reconstructed as **\*-ih₂-on-** and analyzed as an n-stem derivative of instrumental **\*-ih₂**: **\*dupl(h₁)o-** ‘double’ + **\*dupl(h₁)ih₂** ‘double’ -> **\*dupl(h₁)ih₂-on-** ‘doubling’ > **dupliō**.

#### b.

**-iō** also occurs in the formation of abstracts mainly from compound verbs:

**adagiō** ‘saying’ (V. Sor., Var.) < **aiō** ‘I speak’.

**occidiō** ‘slaughter’ (Cic.+) < **occīdō** ‘I kill’.

**condiciō** ‘covenant’ (Plaut.+) < **condicō** ‘I fix by agreement’.

**conspiciō** ‘(augural) observation’ < **cōnspiciō** ‘I see’.

**rebelliō** ‘revolt’ (Cic.+) < **rebellō** ‘I revolt’.

1. Many of these n-stem forms co-occur beside thematic nouns in **-io-/-ia**:

   **occidiō** (Cic.+) ~ **succidia** (Cat.+).

   **condiciō** (Plaut.+) ~ **indicium** (Plaut.+).

   **contāgiō** (Enn.+) ~ **contāgium** (Lucr.+).

2. It is plausible to derive the compound deverbal abstracts in **-iō** from the instrumental of these thematic neuters. The instrumental of compound deverbal nouns in **-io-** is well attested in Vedic as the so-called absolutive, e.g. **niṣadya** ‘sitting down’.

**actiō** ‘action’ (Rhet. Her.+) < **agō** ‘I do’.

**audītiō** ‘hearing’ (Cic.+) < **audiō** ‘I hear’.

**lēctiō** ‘selection, reading’ (Rhet. Her.+) < **legō** ‘I select, read’.

**mōtiō** ‘motion’ (Cic.+) < **moueō** ‘I move’.

**nātiō** ‘tribe’ (Plaut.+) < **nāscor, nātus** ‘I am born’.

#### b.

On the other hand, in Oscan and Umbrian the oblique stem in **-in-**, e.g. Umb. **natine** (IIa 21) ~ Lat. **nātiōne**, is difficult to derive from the same thematic instrumental source. Therefore it is likely that at least some **-tiō** nouns derive from the instrumental of a ti-stem verbal abstract.

### 7. Some complex n-stems[^52]

#### a. -ēdō

**-ēdō** is to be analyzed as an n-stem built to a stem in **-ēd-** as is directly attested in the first example mentioned below. Subsequently these forms came to be associated with s-stems and ē-verbs. Cf. Gk. **ἀλγηδών** ‘suffering’ < **ἀλγέω** ‘suffer’. These formations tend to indicate conditions of physical or mental discomfort.

**cuppēdō** ‘delicacy’ (Var.+) < **cuppēs, cuppēdis** ‘glutton’.

**dulcēdō** ‘sweetness’ (Rhet. Her.+) < **dulcēscō** ‘I become sweet’.

**torpēdō** ‘lethargy’ (Cat.+) < **torpeō** ‘I am numb’.[^53]

#### b. -idō

**cupīdō** ‘desire’ (Plaut.+) < **cupiō, cupere, cupīuī**.

**libīdō** ‘desire’ (Plaut.+) < **libet** ‘pleases’.

**cupīdō** and **libīdō** obviously form a semantic and formal set. The **i** before the suffix **-dō** is also seen in the perfect stem **cupi-**. The origin of this **i** may be a present stem **\*loupi-**, apparently reflected by Osc. **loufi[r]** ‘or’ (Lu 1.8), lit. ‘if one wants’.[^54]

#### c. -ūdō

**hirūdō** ‘leech’ (Plaut.+), etymology uncertain.

**testūdō** ‘tortoise; movable screen for siege engines’ (Lucil.+) < **testū** ‘earthen pot’.

#### d. -tūdō, -tūdinis

**-tūdō, -tūdinis** mainly makes deadjectival abstracts:[^56]

**altitūdō** ‘height’ (Cat.+) < **altus** ‘high’.

**clāritūdō** ‘fame’ (Cat.+) < **clārus** ‘famous’.

**cōnsuētūdō** ‘intimacy, custom’ (Plaut.+) < **cōnsuēscō** ‘I am accustomed’.

# III. i-stem Suffixes

## A. Primary i-stems[^61]

**anguis** ‘snake’ (Naev.+), Lith. **angìs** ‘snake’, Pol. **wąż**.

**auis** ‘bird’ (Plaut.+), Umb. **auif** ‘birds’ acc. pl. (VIb 47, etc.), originally a root noun, cf. Ved. **véḥ** < **\*h₂uoi-s**.

**mare** ‘sea’ (Liv. Andr.+), Goth. **mari-saiws** ‘lake’, OIr. **muir** ‘sea’ < **\*mori**. The acrostatic neuter i-stem **\*mor-i**, gen. sg. **\*mer-i-s** was remade to **\*mori**, gen. sg. **\*mreis**, Lindeman variant **\*mrreis > \*mareis**, from which the **a**-vocalism was generalized in Latin.

**ouis** ‘sheep’ (Cat.+), Umb. **uvem** acc. sg. (III 8, etc.), Gk. **(ϝ)ἴς**, Ved. **ávi-**, HLuv. **ha-wa/i-**.[^62]

## B.

There are a number of cases where i-stems appear to replace thematic or other stems.

### 1.

In some cases there seems to be no functional difference associated with the i-suffix:

**dēns**, gen. pl. **dentium** ‘tooth’ beside **dentum** (Var. L. 8.67). Cf. Ved. **dan**, gen. sg. **datáḥ**.

**mēnsis** ‘month’ (Plaut.+), gen. pl. usually **mēnsum**, cf. Umb. **menzne** ‘month’ (IIa 17) < **\*mens-en-**, Ved. **māḥ** ‘month’, OAv. **mā** < **\*meh₁ns-** < **\*meh₁-** ‘measure’ (Lat. **mē-tior**, etc.).

**nāuis** ‘ship’ (Liv. Andr.+) vs. Gk. **ναῦς**, Ved. **nau-**.

**nox**, gen. pl. **noctium** ‘night’ (Lex XII+) vs. Gk. **νύξ, νυκτός**.

These forms probably result in part from simple confusion of consonant- and i-stem inflection.

### 2.

On the other hand, some forms in **-i-** are nominalizations of o-stems.[^63] These nominalizations were apparently originally characterized by acrostatic ablaut.

This pattern has various ramifications in Latin and elsewhere.

#### a.

Some forms in **-ri-** are nominalizations of adjectives in **-ro-**:

**febris** ‘fever’ (Plaut.+) < **\*dʰegʷʰris** < **\*dʰegʷʰro-** (Gk. **τέφρα** ‘ashes’) < **\*dʰegʷʰ-** ‘burn’ (Ved. **dahati**, etc.).

**ocris** (Liv. Andr., Fest.) ‘rugged mountain’; Gk. **ἄκρις** ‘jagged point’ < **\*h₂ek-ro-** ‘sharp’ (Gk. **ἄκρος**) < **\*h₂ek-** ‘be sharp’.[^65]

#### b.

In Latin we occasionally find an i-stem adjective corresponding to an o-stem adjective elsewhere in Indo-European. It is possible in some of these cases that the i-stem form was originally a nominalization of the o-stem which was later readjectivalized.

**lēuis** ‘smooth’ (Cat.+) ~ Gk. **λεῖος**.

**tenuis** ‘thin’ (Plaut.+) ~ Gk. **τανα(ϝ)ός** ‘tall’.

#### c.

u-stem adjectives do not survive as a type in Latin. Instead they appear as i-stems:

**leuis** ‘light’ (Plaut.+) < **\*h₁legʷʰus**, cf. Ved. **raghú-**.

**suāuis** ‘sweet’ (Naev.+) < **\*swādus**, cf. Gk. **ἡδύς**, Ved. **svādú-**.

**tenuis** ‘thin’ (Cat.+) < **\*tenu-**, cf. Gk. **τανύ-**(...), Ved. **tanú-**.

**grauis** ‘heavy’ < **\*gʷr(e)h₂u-**, cf. Gk. **βαρύς**, Ved. **gurú-**.

**breuis** ‘short’ < **\*mregʷhu-**, cf. Gk. **βραχύς**, Av. **mərəzu-**.

**dulcis** ‘sweet’ (Plaut.+) < **\*dlukis** < **\*dlukuis**,[^66] cf. Gk. **γλυκύς** < **\*dlukus**, Myc. **de-re-u-ko** /dleukos/ ‘grape must’.

In at least some cases the Latin forms are substitute i-stems for thematic forms as in §2 above. Oscan appears to have preserved thematic inflection in the forms **bra...** (Cm 3) ‘grauis’ (?) acc. pl. and **uruvi** (Cm 1 B 30) ‘wide’ nom. sg. fem. The inferred o-stems of the type **\*tenuo-** are themselves external derivatives not of the u-stem adjectives seen in other languages, but of the nominal base from which the u-stem adjectives are themselves internal derivatives.[^67][^68]
