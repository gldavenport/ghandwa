# Icelandic leppa ‘fox’ < *h₂lop-n-íh₂-

## A New Example of Kluge’s Law in Germanic

## Abstract

Kluge’s Law, unlike Grimm’s and Verner’s Law, remains controversial because of the scarcity of clear cognates linking Germanic geminates with Indo-European nasal clusters. This article argues that Icelandic leppa ‘fox’ continues a formation *h₂lop-n-íh₂-, directly related to the Indo-European word for ‘fox’. As a previously unnoticed cognate, it enlarges the corpus of forms supporting nasal assimilation in Proto-Germanic, thereby strengthening the case for Kluge’s Law.

## Keywords

Kluge’s Law – geminates – Germanic – Indo-European – Icelandic

Unlike Grimm’s and Verner’s Law, Kluge’s Law remains contested in Germanic studies (cf. Fulk 2018, 114–117; Goblirsch 2018, 35–39). While it is accepted by various scholars, in different formulations (Lühr 1988; Rasmussen 1989a, 1989b; Kortlandt 1991; Kroonen 2011; Scheungraber 2014), it is rejected by others. One of the main criticisms of Kluge’s Law in Germanic is the scarcity of cognate sets in which a Proto-Germanic geminate corresponds to a cluster with a stressed nasal in another Indo-European branch (Trautmann 1906; Kuryłowicz 1957, 133 fn. Hopper 1989, 247; Ringe 2006, 115; 2017, 136–140). Thus, while Kluge’s Law

<!-- p. 296 -->

was formulated more than a century ago, the identification of nasal clusters underlying Proto-Germanic geminates remains an ongoing task.

In its original formulation, Kluge’s Law accounted for the rise of the PGm. voiceless geminates through the assimilation of PIE *n by voiced fricatives in stressed syllables (Kluge 1884). This process is situated between the various stages of Grimm’s and Verner’s Law, i.e. before the devoicing of the PIE mediae proper but after the rise of voiceless and voiced fricatives from the PIE tenues and mediae aspiratae (cf. table 1). PIE *n was not assimilated either by stops in unstressed syllables, or by *z and *ǥw, the latter likely because it had already shifted to a labial glide (written *w before and *u after vowels).

The Germanic n-stems exhibit an abundance of geminates, due to the very nature of their suffixation, and thus provide strong internal support for Kluge’s Law (Kluge 1884, 169; Lühr 1988, 191; Rasmussen 1989b, 252; Kroonen 2011). An additional, though less well-explored, environment is formed by feminine ih₂-stems derived from such n-stems. The Indo-European age of this environment is suggested by e.g. Skt. takṣṇī́-, Gr. τέκταινα ‘female carpenter’ < *tetḱ-n-ih₂-, which may represent an old derivation from Skt. tákṣan-, Gr. τέκτων ‘carpenter’ < *tetḱ-on-. The formation Skt. rā́jñī-, OIr. rígain f. ‘queen’, W rhiain f. ‘maiden, lady’ < *Hrēǵ-n-ih₂- ‘queen’, related to Skt. rā́j-, Lat. rēx, OIr. rí m. ‘king’ < *Hrēǵ-,[^3] is at least as old and has likewise been derived from the n-stem represented by Skt. rā́jan- < *Hrēǵ-on- (cf. Meyer 1875, 44–45; Kluge 1899, 21).[^4] Debrunner (Ai.Gr. II/2, 177), however, conversely assumes that rā́jan- is a backformation to rā́jñī-, which seems plausible given the isolation of the n-stem to Indic. In all likelihood, the n-ih₂-suffix had started functioning as an independent feminizing element already within Proto-Indo-European (Hirt 1895, 21–22; Kluge 1899, 21; Meillet 1931, 22), as demonstrated also by the coinage of

<!-- p. 299 -->

Skt. pátnī-,[^5] Gr. πότνια, Lith. vieš-patni f. ‘mistress’ < *pot-n-ih₂- to Skt. páti-, ToA pats, B pets, Gr. πόσις m. ‘husband’ < *pot-i-.[^6]

Within Germanic, a limited but significant number of animal names in *-n-ih₂- can be identified.[^7] In North Germanic, Elfd. ynn f. ‘female black grouse, grayhen’ presupposes an ON *yrn < *urznī-.[^8] It is evidently formed to ON orri m. ‘male black grouse, blackcock’ < *urzan-, cf. Gr. ἄρσην, Cret. ἔρσην, YAv. aršan- m. ‘male’ < PIE *(h₁e)rs-en- (Noreen 1882, 242 fn. 1; Hellquist 1918, 28). In West Germanic, an archaic moviertes Femininum is G Ricke f. ‘doe’ continuing PGm. *rikkī- (Paul 1880, 133–134 fn. 2; Kluge 1884, 179; Hirt 1895, 257–258). In view of its cognates OE rā(ha), OHG rēho m. ‘roe deer’ < *raiha(n)- < *(H)roiḱ-o(n)-, it arguably continues *Hriḱ-n-íh₂-, with a geminate resulting from Kluge’s Law. A further plausible, though uncertain, case is OE henn, OHG henna ‘hen’. This feminine is usually derived from *hanjō- (cf. Torp 1909, 69), assuming West Germanic gemination in the attested forms. In the absence of a Gothic or Old Norse cognate, however, it can alternatively be reconstructed as *hannī-, i.e. an ī-stem to the n-stem Go. hana, ON hani, OE hana, OHG hano < PGm. *hanan- ‘cock’ < *kh₂n-on-, cf. Lat. canere ‘to sing’, etc. (Kluge 1899, 22).[^9]

The feminizing ī-stems formed a closed category in Old Norse animal designations and were not synchronically productive. The most important vestiges are æðr ‘(female) eider’ < *awe/adī- (Sw. åd m. ‘male eider’ < *awe/ada-), gyltr ‘sow’ < *gultī- (Swi. golz m. ‘boar’ < *gulta-), merr ‘mare’ < *marhī- (marr m. ‘horse’ < *marha-), *neðr, Elfd. neðer f. ‘slow worm’ < *nadrī- (naðr m. ‘snake’ < *nadra-), reyðr ‘rorqual’ < *raudī-, *rær, gen. *reyjar, Nw. røy, Elfd. rä ‘female capercaillie’ < *rawī-, ylgr ‘female wolf’ < *wulgī- (ulfr ‘(male) wolf’ < *wulfa-) and ýrr ‘female aurochs’ < *ūrī- (úrr, úri ‘(male) aurochs’ < *ūra(n)-).[^10] The lack of productivity seems to confirm the antiquity of the aforementioned nī-stems.

<!-- p. 300 -->

In both North and West Germanic, there is a tendency to replace ī-stems with jō(n)-stems (Schmidt 1889, 69–93; Suolahti 1899, I, 7–8; Hellquist 1918, 44–45; Wilmanns 1899: II, 248). In West Germanic, this is evidenced e.g. by ON merr appearing as OHG mariha, OE m(i)ere ‘mare’, and possibly also by the aforementioned *hannī appearing as OHG henna. In Old Norse, doublets of the type gyltr ~ gylta ‘sow’ (see above) are found. One particularly informative doublet is ylgr ⁓ ylgja ‘she-wolf’ < *wulgī ~ *wulgjōn-. Outside West Norse, it is parallelled by OE wylf < *wulfī on the one hand and OSw. ylva and OHG wulpa, MHG wülpe < *wulbjō(n)- on the other. This complex variation is probably to be understood as the result of various levelings of an original paradigm *wulbī, gen. *wulgjōz < Pre-Gm. *wulpī́, gen. *wulkjā́s < *ulkʷ-íh₂-, *ulkʷ-iéh₂-s,[^11] cf. Skt. vr̥kī́-, Lith. vìlkė, etc., with additional shifts from ī- to jō(n)-stems.

A jō(n)-suffix that developed from ih₂-stems became productive already in Northwest Germanic. In West Germanic, the archaic Ricke < *rikkī- was displaced, for instance, by OE rǣge, OHG reia ‘female roe-deer’ < *raihjōn-, a more recent formation to OE rā(ha), OHG rēho m. ‘male roe-deer’ < *raiha(n)-. In Nordic, this type is characterised by the weak ending -( j)a rather than strong -r, even in the Old Norse period. For instance, Icel. hrefna f. ‘she-raven’ (ON Hrefna p.n.) < *hrabnjōn- appears to have been derived from hrafn m. ‘raven’ < *hrabna-, a thematicization of the more primary n-stem OHG rabo, MDu. rave < *hraban-. ON birna f. ‘she-bear’ < *bernjōn- may be another such formation, ultimately being derived from the n-stem *beran-, cf. OE bera, OHG bero m. ‘bear’. Finally, Icel. erna f. ‘female eagle, eagless’ < *arnjōn- can likewise be seen as an inner-Germanic formation from *aran-, cf. Go. ara ‘vulture’, ON ari, OHG aro m. ‘eagle’ < PIE *h₃ér-on-, cf. Hitt. ḫaran- ‘id.’. It cannot be excluded, however, that erna continues an inherited ī-stem *arnī- < *h₃ér-n-ih₂-, a possibility supported by the direct cognate Gr. ὄρνῑς f. ‘bird’.

A further feminine jōn-stem that may continue an archaic ī-stem, though it has not yet been identified as such, is Icel. leppa f. ‘fox’. It is attested in a 17th-century spell collected by Jón Árnarson and ascribed to Gísli í Melrakkadal (Davíðsson 1898–1903, 98). The spell aimed to ward off foxes, the first step of which is to address the animal by all its names. Accordingly, the word leppa appears in a list of heiti and taboo words (cf. Mohr 1933, 137): “vargur, vemma, svemma, sauðbítur, suga, blóðsuga, rebba,[^12] refur, refkeila, leppa, lánghala,

<!-- p. 301 -->

tæfa, týfa, týr,[^13] dýr, ætla, lágfætta, ófríð tóa, ótryggjandi meinúlfur manns”. Blöndal (1989, 557) derives the word from leppur m. ‘patch, piece of cloth’, but the above parallels support a scenario in which leppa is a moviertes Femininum, i.e. a jōn-stem continuing an older ī-stem *leppr < *lappī-.

From the Indo-European perspective, the latter option is semantically more transparent than the former. The possibility of an archaic formation *lappī- is supported by the presence of a likely related PIE word for ‘fox’. This word is primarily represented by what appears to be an athematic ḱ-stem (cf. Gdr. II/2, 237; Palmér et al. 2021), cf. Gr. ἀλώπηξ f. ‘fox’, Arm. ałowēs ‘fox’ < *h₂lōp-eḱ- and plausibly also Skt. lopāśá- m. ‘fox, jackal’, Khot. rrūvāsa ‘jackal’, P rōbāh ‘fox’, etc. < *Hraupāća- for expected **rāpāća- (but see de Vaan 2000). Latv. lapsa f. ‘fox’ < *h₂lop-ḱ-eh₂- may also be included here as a derived thematicization. Lith. lãpė f. ‘fox’, on the other hand, at least synchronically resembles a formation Proto-Baltic *lopiā < *h₂lop-ieh₂-, and the same base *h₂lop- is also present in Celtic forms deriving from *loɸerno-, cf. W llywarn, Bret. louarn m. ‘fox’ (Schrijver 1998).[^14] In light of Kluge’s Law, leppa can be added as an additional cognate, continuing *h₂lop-n-íh₂ (cf. table 4).

**Table 4. Kluge’s Law and the evolution of four Germanic nī-stems**

| Stage |  |  |  |  |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Protoform | *Hriḱ-n-íh₂ | *h₂lop-n-íh₂ | *kh₂n-n-ih₂ | *(H)rs-n-íh₂ |
| Grimm (frication) | *rihnī́ | *lofnī́ | *hannī | *ursnī́ |
| Verner (voicing) | *riǥnī́ | *loƀnī́ | *hannī | *urznī́ |

| Grimm (devoicing) | *ríkkī | *lóppī | *hannī | *úrznī |
| Proto-Germanic | *rikkī | *lappī | *hannī | *urznī |

A final question that remains to be answered here concerns the exact Pre-Germanic derivational base from which *lappī- was ultimately derived. On

<!-- p. 302 -->

the one hand, since the n-ih₂-suffix can be shown to have been an independent morphological element already in Proto-Indo-European, a possible derivational base would be Pre-Germanic *h₂lop-o-, i.e. the same root form present in Baltic and Celtic. However, the application of this n-ih₂-suffix appears to have been restricted to human designations, whereas the aforementioned Germanic animal names in *-n-ī- rather represent ih₂-stems created to primary n-stems. These observations suggest a second scenario, namely that Germanic once possessed a masculine n-stem *laban- < *h₂lop-én- that was at some point lost, but not before it gave rise to a moviertes Femininum *lappī- < *h₂lop-n-íh₂-.[^15] In either case, ON leppa provides new potential evidence in favor of Kluge’s Law and can be added to the corpus of forms supporting the emergence of the Proto-Germanic geminates by the assimilation of Proto-Indo-European nasals.
