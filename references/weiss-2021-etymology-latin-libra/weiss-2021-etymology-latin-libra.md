# The Etymology of Latin lībra

Michael Weiss  
Department of Linguistics, Cornell University  
SCS, Greek and Latin Linguistics Panel, January 5, 2021

<!-- source-page: 1 -->

## 1.

A common system of measurement terms was a feature of the Italic cultural koine that extended from Central Italy to Sicily.

### 1.1.

The origin of the names of measurement terms, most notably, the unit (Lat. lībra, Gk. λίτρα) and its duodecimal subdivision (Lat. uncia, Gk. ὀγκία) have been variously seen as Latin, Greek, or Sicel.

#### 1.1.2.

In fact, by paying careful attention to the dates of attestation and the relative and absolute chronology of sound changes, I have shown that uncia cannot be of Latin or Sicel origin, but instead is a specifically Siciliote Greek form derived from Gk. ὄγκος ‘mass, volume’.[^1]

## 2.

The unit lībra, on the other hand, must be of Italic origin because the correspondence -b- ~ -t-, as has long been seen, points to divergent reflexes of a PIE voiced aspirate *dʰ (Schulze 1895).

### 2.1.

The Lat. b is the regular reflex of *dʰ by the RUbL rule (Weiss 2020:84). In Sicel the voiced aspirates became voiced stops (Mendolito γεπεδ ‘had’ ~ Osc. 3s perf. subj. hipid), but voiced stops were subsequently devoiced before sonorant consonants. Hence *līdʰrā > *līdrā > *lītrā (Willi 2008:22).

### 2.2.

The Greek spelling λίτρα with ι resolves another uncertainty.[^2]

#### 2.2.1.

First, we need to establish the quantity of the ι. Scholars unanimously treat it as long, but in fact the evidence is not clear cut. It is of course difficult to determine the quantity of the vowel ι before two consonants. The best piece of evidence is the Hellenistic spelling of the word with ει. The earliest instance of this spelling that I have been able to find is from a Roman tax law from Ephesus dating to 75 BCE (Ephesos 4*5). This text consistently uses ει to spell ῑ both in words of Latin origin (Σατ]ορνείνωι ‘Saturnīnō’, εἰδῶν ‘īduum’) and in words of Greek origin (Βειθυνίας, Μειλήτῳ).[^3] In this inscription we find at l. 79 λείτρων and this spelling is quite common in later Greek documents. The metrical evidence is ambiguous but consistent with a long vowel. Below are the metrical instances of λίτρα and its derivatives up until the 1st cent. BCE. Unfortunately, none of the examples is conclusive.

<!-- source-page: 3 -->

Finally, we have a piece of contradictory evidence, which, paradoxically, supports a long scansion. Herodian (Περὶ ὀρθογραφίας, 3.2, p. 546, l. 2) reports that the word λίτρα should be spelled with an ι and in addition εὕρηται γὰρ ἐν συστολῇ παρὰ τοῖς Δωριεῦσιν “For it has been found in systole among the Dorians”. Herodian uses the word συστολή to refer to the shortening of a long vowel and so the inference is that Herodian considered a long vowel the norm for this word.[^5] Herodian uses the explanatory particle γάρ because he is implicitly arguing against spelling this word with ει, a spelling which was common in Herodian’s time, the 2nd century CE. That is, Herodian argues that the word should be spelled with iota because (γάρ) an iota was potentially either long or short (δίχρονος) and the first syllable of λίτρα was supposedly short for the Dorians thereby excluding a spelling λείτρα which could only represent a long vowel. I have no idea why Herodian thought the vowel was potentially short for the Dorians (presumably referring to the Doric-speaking Sicilians), but if there was such a pronunciation, it must have been secondary: the long ι of Greek matches perfectly with the long ī of Latin (cf. #et lībella aliqua si ex parti claudicat hilum (Lucr. 4.515) and the Romance reflexes (It. libbra, OFr. livre, etc.).

#### 2.2.2.

The monophthongal ι of λίτρα is attested on inscriptions from the 6th century BCE. That means that we must be dealing with an original long vowel not the result of the monophthongization of a diphthong. Greek, Latin, Oscan, and Sicel would not have monophthongized a diphthong at such an early date. The Greek, Latin, and Oscan facts are well known and established. The dialect inscriptions of Sicily have no evidence for the monophthongization of *ei̯. Latin did not monophthongize this sequence until the 2nd century BCE. Oscan kept this diphthong intact until the bitter end and Sicel also preserved the diphthong *ei̯. Cf. the dative singular forms darnakei burei from the Montagna di Marzo inscription.

## 3.

We may therefore confidently reconstruct a preform *līdʰrā. Such a form is amenable to two theoretical morphological analyses. It may either be from a root *(hₓ)lei̯hₓdʰ- with the suffix *-rā- (< *-reh₂), or from a root *(hₓ)lei̯hₓ- with the instrument suffix *-dʰrā (< *-dʰreh₂). The zero-grade root in the feminine instrument nouns is well attested (cf. MIr. clethar f. ‘support’ < *k̑litreh₂) and according to Olsen 1988:4 to be expected. There are no known roots of the first shape at all,[^6] but there are several candidates of the second shape.

### 3.1.

The root *leih₂- ‘disappear’ (LIV²:406, Gk. λίναμαι ‘I avoid’ (Hsch.), λιάζομαι ‘I incline’ etc.) seems semantically a non-starter, but it has been proposed by Simkin 2012:172 and before him by Peruzzi 1985. According to Simkin’s theory the descendants of *lei̯h₂- developed the meaning ‘incline’ and “This [i.e. *lih₂dʰreh₂] would make good sense as a name for the scales or one of their constituent parts, such as the ‘slider’ or sliding counterweight of the steelyard balance.” But *lih₂dʰreh₂ was originally the name of a weight and not a scale (see 5 below). This issue could be remedied by supposing that *lih₂dʰreh₂ named the weight as ‘an incliner’ or ‘the sinker’. Note,

<!-- source-page: 4 -->

however, that the counterweight was conceptualized by the Greeks as the ‘stayer’ (Gk. σταθμός/-όν ‘weight’, στατήρ ‘weight’). But a more serious issue is that on most accounts λίτρα is not a Greek word in origin, but it is only in Greek that the meaning ‘incline’ is found for reflexes of the root *lei̯h₂-. The meaning of the cognate forms points to ‘weaken’, ‘disappear’ (Goth. af-linnan ‘to go away’, OE linnan ‘to cease, depart’, Ved. -lī́yate ‘dissolve’, ‘become hidden’) whence the Greek reflex alone has developed via ‘turn aside’ to ‘incline’. The original meaning is still preserved in Gk. λῑμός ‘hunger’ and λοιμός ‘plague’. Peruzzi 1985:61–2 anticipates Simkin’s hypothesis connecting λίτρα with the family of λίαζομαι and answers our second objection by deriving the form λίτρα from Greek, but his phonological explanation of Lat. lībra as an early loanword from Greek via a form of coastal Oscan where *tr became *dr and a supposed Latin sound change of *dr to br is completely unworkable.[^7]

## 4.

Βut the root *leihₓ- ‘pour’ (LIV²:405) deserves further consideration. This root, perhaps not the most familiar, is actually fairly well attested, though not otherwise known in Italic.

### 4.1.

Baltic reflects a simple thematic present *lei̯hₓ-e- in Lith. dial. lejù (inf. líeti), Latv. leju, lêju (inf. liêt).[^8] Slavic has evidence for a lengthened-grade present stem *lēi̯hₓ-e- in OCS lějǫ, inf. lijati but also for a zero-grade present *lije- (Russ. l’ë-, etc.; inf. Russ. lit’, SCr. lȉti) perhaps built to the infinitive stem. The Baltic and Slavic forms could be united under a Narten present *lēi̯hₓti, 3s mid. *lei̯hₓe. (LIV²:403; ALEW² s.v.; LED 700), though this is not the only possibility.

### 4.2.

MW dillyd ‘to flow, pour, run’ < *dī-eks-lii̯e- (GPC s.v. dillyddaf).

### 4.3.

A well-established nominal derivative is *lei̯hₓ-tus ‘pouring’.

#### 4.3.1.

The Proto-Baltic ancestor of Lith. lietùs AP 3 and Latv. liêtus ‘rain’ must be an archaic formation since it does not match the zero-grade of the synchronic verb ‘to rain’ lýti and the suffix -tu is not productive in Baltic except in the formation of supines.

#### 4.3.2.

Slavic *litva (SCr. dial. lȉtva ‘strong rain lasting many days’, Cz. litva ‘libation to the gods’, Ru. litvá ‘strong rain’ might continue an *-eh₂ instantive derivative of the -tu-stem. The original meaning clearly must be ‘pouring’.

#### 4.3.3.

In Germanic, Gothic offers a potentially exact formal match to the Baltic forms in leiþu, a hapax accusative which means ‘fruit wine’.[^9] The rest of Germanic has a neuter o-stem (ON líð ‘ale’, OHG līt ‘fruit wine’, OE líþ ‘alcoholic drink’) which is most likely secondary (EWAHD s.v.). Cf. Goth. faírƕus ‘world’ m. u beside OHG ferah n. o, OE feorh n. o, ON fjǫr ‘life’ n. o.

#### 4.3.4.

Celtic too has a plausible cognate of the -tu-stem found in Germanic and Balto-Slavic.

<!-- source-page: 5 -->

A masculine -tu-stem *lītus is well attested in Old Irish líth, OBret. lit m., ModBret. lid, and probably in the Gaulish personal names Litu-genus and Litu-marus, but the meaning of these forms is divergent. In Irish the word means (1) ‘festival day’ (Wb. 27a24 nachib berar . . . inna llíthu et a ssapati gl. in parte diei festi; Ml. 47b2 arna lithu gl. propter . . . festa); (2) ‘rejoicing’ (Mon. Tall. 68 ba erdath ┐ ba lith mor . . . la C.C. ann . . . dona bráithribh “there was celebration and rejoicing with Cúchulainn then with the brothers”); (3) ‘luck, prosperity’ (BDD 54 ba hé mo líthsa co mbad hé docorad “it was my good luck that it was he who happened to be here”). In Breton the word means ‘celebration’ and the verb phrase ober lid means ‘to make a party, host generously’.

##### 4.3.4.1.

There are two phonological plausible etymologies for these words. Thurneysen 1900:20–22 proposed a proto-form *plētus (i.e. *pleh₁tus) ‘fullness’, which he compared to Gk. πληθύς ‘throng’, MHG vlætic ‘beautiful, fine’, MHG unvlât m. o ‘dirt’, and feminine personal names like Frankish Albofledis, and OHG Sigiflat. Zimmer 1888, on the other hand, proposed comparison with the Germanic and Balto-Slavic word we have just been considering.

##### 4.3.4.2.

Both comparisons are phonologically possible and both are semantically somewhat speculative. On Thurneysen’s theory the festival was named after the “‘Fülle’ alles Guten… die eine Festfeier zu bieten pflegt.” Zimmer doesn’t spell out the semantic pathway he envisages, but we may suggest ‘pouring’ developed to ‘libation to the gods’, as a related form *litva did in Slavic, whence ‘festival’. Cf. ON ǫl ‘ale’ and ‘banquet’, ‘festival’, OSw. ærvisöl ‘funeral feast’, Sw. barnsöl ‘baptism party’ and OIr. cuirm ‘beer’ and ‘entertainment’, e.g. Thes. ii 295.16 oc cormaim gaibtir dúana ‘gifts are taken at a feast’.[^10] The morphology of Thurneysen’s *pleh₁tus is possible, but it is actually not as well supported as Thurneysen believed. The MHG forms are not close semantically. The meaning of the element -fledis in female personal names is unknown, and the Greek form has an aspirated consonant. There is no independent evidence for *pleh₁tus.

##### 4.3.4.3.

But an interesting passage in the Life of St. Patrick (Trip. 54) decisively favors Zimmer’s etymology. The king’s druid Lucet Máel puts a drop of poison in St. Patrick’s goblet. Patrick recites an incantation which cause the liquid to solidify like ice. He turns the cup upside down, the poison falls out and then with another prayer he reliquifies the contents. The Irish version gives the words of his blessing.

(6)

> Is ed inso ro gab Patraic forsin cailech:  
> ‘Gaibiu anfis, ibu anfis, fria sia úathain ibiu lithu in Christo Ihesu. Amen.’ .  
> i. cia beith a fis ocund cenco fil, íbthar i n-anmum Ísu Críst  
> This is what Patrick said over the vessel:  
> ……………………………………………………….…. in Christ Jesus. Amen.  
> i.e. whether we know or do not know what it is, it will be drunk in Christ’s name.

The same charm is given in Pseudo-historical prologue to *Senchas Már* (BL Harley 432) in a somewhat different form: Iubu fis friibu fis ibu anfis frisbru uatha ibu lithu Christi Iesu.

The start of the incantation has been variously restored but the last part is transmitted in both version without significant variant:

(7)

```text
ibiu líthu    in Christo Iesu
ibu lithu     Christi Iesu
```

Here we have the 1st sg. of the verb ibid ‘drink’ with the acc. pl. of an o- or u-stem. It seems best to identify this form with the u-stem líth we have just been discussing, but in what sense? Carey 2019:25 translates ‘I drink good omens’, but I suggest that the most plausible interpretation is that this charm preserves the original sense of ‘pouring’, ‘libation’. Cf. the paraphrase íbthar i n-anmum Ísu Críst “it will be drunk in the name of Christ.” In Italic there is no certain derivative of this root.[^11]

## 5. Semantic history of *līdʰrā

### 5.1.

In Latin the word lībra has two basic meanings: (1) a pound in weight; (2) a pair of scales. Later meanings such as ‘level’ and ‘the constellation Libra’ are derived from ‘pair of scales’.[^12] The meaning ‘pound’ is attested in the XII tables. The meaning ‘pair of scales’ is not attested directly in Old Latin, but must be quite old since it is found in the evidently archaic legal formula of mancipatio: Varro L. 5. 163 veteribus in mancipiis scriptum “raudusculo libram ferito” “written in old acts of conveyance ‘strike the scales with an ingot’.” Cf. also Festus p. 322 L “rudusculo libram ferito”, asse tangitur libra. “‘strike the scale with an ingot’, i.e. the scale is touched with an as.”[^13] Nevertheless, the ‘pair of scales’ meaning is secondary because we often see words for ‘weight(s)’ develop into ‘scale’. Cf. the descendants of Lat. pēnsum ‘weight’, which have acquired the additional meaning ‘scale’ in Sardinian pezu and Spanish peso (REW 6394), or Lat. centēnārium ‘hundredweight’, which via Greek, Syriac, and Arabic, is the source of Turk. kantar ‘scale’, etc. The antiquity of ‘weight’ is confirmed by the fact that λίτρα is attested in the meaning ‘weight’, ‘coin’, ‘measure of volume’ but never ‘scale’.[^14] It also seems obvious that the meaning ‘weight’ is antecedent to ‘coin’. Cf. again Sp. peso.

<!-- source-page: 7 -->

### 5.2.

Coinage is actually a relatively recent innovation in Italy introduced by the Greeks starting in the 8th cent. BCE. To quote Crawford 1985:1 “The peoples of Italy themselves for the most part retained systems which involved the accumulation, and eventually no doubt the transfer by weight, of lumps of copper or various copper alloys.”[^15] There is no evidence for coinage at Rome before the 3rd century.[^16] If the word *līdʰrā was inherited from Proto-Italic, it could not be the name of a coin. Further, even in the more likely scenario that the word was borrowed from one language of ancient Italy to another, the loan must have happened at a time when the voiced aspirate could still be interpreted in the same way as it would have been in an inherited item and this must have been before the advent of the Greeks. The result is the same *līdʰrā could not have been the name of a coin. *līdʰrā must have been the name for a weight and most probably a weight of the sort that would have been used as a unit of exchange.

### 5.3.

We know that prior to the advent of the Greeks the people of Italy used rough unshaped ingots of metal as units of exchange (aes rude). I suggest that ingot is precisely the original meaning of *lī-dʰrā. The so-called instrument suffixes also make result nouns, e.g. Gk. δαιτρόν is a ‘portion’ not an ‘instrument for apportionment’. Latin castra is originally a ‘portion of land’, but Latin must have also known a true instrument noun from *k̑es- ‘cut’ which provided the base for the denominal verb castrāre ‘to castrate’. Thus *lī-dʰrā could have meant both ‘instrument for pouring’, i.e. ‘a pitcher’ and ‘a pour’ and a ‘pour’ of metal is precisely an ingot. ME ingot which derives from an unattested OE *ingot < *en ‘in’ + *gʰeu̯d- ‘pour’. The Russian for ‘ingot’ slítok is in fact a derivative of our very root *lei̯hₓ-. ’.[^17]

## Appendix: Uncertain and unlikely forms of *leihₓ- in Italic

1. The Italic river Līris (Strabo 5.238 Λεῖρις), modern Liri, rises in the territory of the Marsi and running through southern Latium and Campania joins with the Gari to become the modern Garigliano. But in antiquity the whole length was known as the Liris. The name Liris is apparently not very old because Pliny and Strabo both report the original name of the river was Clanis (Plin. HN. 3.59; Strabo 5.3.7). Now clanis is a name common to many Italian rivers (e.g. the Clanis of Etruria now the Chiana), and probably pre-Italic. From this we can infer that the naming of the river Liris was relatively recent, not having completely eliminated all trace of the earlier name, and coined by the historical inhabitants of the region, i.e. Italic speakers.[^18] A derivation from *lihₓri- ‘pouring’ would be semantically and formally plausible to which we can compare a potentially closely related *lihₓro- in Old Irish lír ‘diarrhea’ (BCrólige 25), but this is obviously uncertain.

<!-- source-page: 10 -->

2. It’s an old idea that lītus, lītoris ‘shore’ is a derivative of this root. Lītus, by a legal tradition going back to P. Aquilius Gallus is defined as the maximum extent of the waves (Cic. top. 3 solebat…Aquilius…quaerentibus…quid esset litus ita definire: quo fluctus eluderet. “Aquilius used to define litus as follows for those who asked what it was: where the tide plays.” )[^19] Lītus was distinguished by the Romans from rīpa ‘bank of a river’ and ōra more generally ‘edge’ or ‘border regions’.[^20] The word survives uniquely in Venetian lido. It is conceivable that that ‘pouring’ came to refer to the ‘flood tide’ and then to the land potentially covered by the flood tide, but I can find no parallel for such a development. Instead, Persson’s old connection (1893:272) with *lei̯t- ‘depart’, recently revived by de Vaan 2008:346, seems preferable. The Iranian, Tocharian, and Germanic reflexes of this verb (Aves. -iriθiieiti ‘dies’ and TB lita ‘went away’, litk- ‘avoid’, Goth. -leiþan, OHG līdan ‘go away’) show that the original meaning was not just ‘go’ but ‘go away.’ Thus a *lei̯tos should mean ‘a going away’ which came in Latin to be specialized in the sense of ‘low tide’ whence ‘beach’. For the semantic development cf. OIr. tráig which means both ‘ebb tide’ and ‘beach’ and is derived from a verb of motion *tregʰ- ‘run’ (Goth. þragjan ‘run’).[^21] This derivation is also morphological more straightforward than connection with the root *leihₓ- or the -tu-stem *leihₓtu-.

3. Lat. lībāre ‘to libate’ has been traditionally compared to Gk. λείβω ‘I pour’. The former is often considered a loanword from Greek, but the morphological productivity and root ablaut of the root līb- in Latin argue against this explanation. Beside lībāre ‘to pour a libation’ (Cat. +) we find lībum ‘(sacrificial) cake’ (Enn.+) and dēlibūtus ‘thickly smeared’, ‘steeped’, formed on the analogy of imbūtus ‘imbued’ with a short i pointing to a zero-grade.[^22] Cf. also the cognomen Lĭbō of the Scribonii, Marcii, and Poetelii. Risch 1981 has also made a good case against the idea that the Greek and Latin forms are independent reflexes of a PIE *lei̯b-, noting that the Latin verb does not apply exclusively to libations but also to solid offerings and lībum, which is most naturally taken as the derivational basis of the family, is a solid cake. The word remains without a convincing etymology in my opinion. The Greek forms, if ultimately cognate with the root *leihₓ-, would have to be parallel extensions of a root *lei̯- since the zero-grade forms show a short vowel (λιβάς ‘spring’, etc.)

In Statius’s Silvae (4.3.85) the river Vulturnus claims to excel the Liris with its puro gurgite ‘pure flow’. Finally, Silius Italicus 4.346–8, in the most detailed account, describes the Liris as qui fronte quieta (Thilo’s emendation for transmitted fonte quieta)/dissimulat cursum ac nullo mutabilis imbri/perstringit tacitas gemmanti gurgite ripas “a placid stream that conceals its flow, and, never affected by rain, brushes its silent banks with sparkling wave.” [Translation J. W. Duff, Loeb edition]. Of course, the poets no doubt exaggerated and the Modern Liri is no clearer than other rivers and in parts quite rapid, but it wasn’t and isn’t particularly muddy, like, for example, the Big Muddy River of Southern Illinois.
