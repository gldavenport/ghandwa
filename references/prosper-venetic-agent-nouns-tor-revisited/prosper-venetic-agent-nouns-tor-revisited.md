## Abstract

This work collects a sizable number of hitherto overlooked Venetic names which help clarify the structure of Venetic agent nouns in -tōrand underpins the idea that Venetic is an Italic language very close to Latin. A tentative interpretation of some morphological and syntactic aspects of the Venetic Tavola d’Este is additionally offered.

**Keywords: Italic languages, Sabellic, Venetic, Latin epigraphy, IndoEuropean linguistics**

### 1. INTRODUCTION.1 THE ITALIC AGENT NOUNS IN -TŌR

Latin, Venetic and Sabellic agent nouns in -tōr- are formed on the stem of the pp. (see Watmough 1997, 84). The Italic languages thus contradict the so-called ‘IE rule’, which posits an acrostatic inflectional type and predicts an e-grade of the root all along the paradigm, as in the classic example *déh3-tōr ‘giver’. Additionally, the lengthened grade of the suffix has spread from the nominative -tōr to the rest of the forms. Venetic phonetics and morphology remain largely unknown, not least because a vast amount of onomastic materials attested in Latin epigraphy has been neglected. This work aims to disclose some aspects of Venetic phonetics and word formation and show that Venetic is in all likelihood the closest relative of Latino-Faliscan.

### 2. AGENT NOUNS IN -TŌR AND ROOTS ENDING IN A DENTAL: DENTAL SOUNDS IN CONTACT AGAIN

As is well known, the western Indo-European dialects share a phonetic evolution by virtue of which two contiguous dental sounds yield *-ss-. Though this sound change has occasionally been challenged in the case of the cluster *-dht-, I assume it has essentially no exceptions in Italic, and deviant results are due to analogy. Cf. for instance U. Fisouie (< *bhidh-teu̯ -) vs. L. aestus (< *ai̯dh-tu-?; see EDLIL 28). Sequences of two dental sounds are often said not to be attested in Venetic (LV 146), but, as we are immediately going to see, several agent nouns exemplify the PIt. change *-d(h)t-, *-tt- > *-tst- > -ss-. An appellative a.n.śore.s. in a fragmentary context (Calalzo, LV 203) is compared by LV 152, 274-5 (with references to earlier suggestions) to Goth. a nsts (-i-stem) ‘favour’, and would constitute the only hypothetical example of a metathesis -st- > -ts- which is nowhere else to be found (cf. fag.s.to, dona.s.to, .e.ges.t.s). I find it more compelling to reconstruct an Italic noun *antstor-, ultimately (as if) from *h2n̥ - + dheh1-tor- ‘constructors; officials?’ with zero-grade of the root by the ‘Italic rule’. This presupposes a pp. *antsto- (< *h2n̥ - + dhh1-to-) with early contextbound laryngeal loss and the same prefix as in U. ampentu ‘touch’, anzeriatu, etc. ‘to watch birds’, O. αναfακετ ‘erected’ (WOU 94-5). While most compounded verb forms of this root, like the L. cognates con-ditor, con-ditus, etc., have restored the vowel in analogy to the simplex, *antstōr- must have been dissociated from the paradigm early on, like the L. DN Cōnsus (<*kom-dhh1-tu-). It has passed unnoticed that a number of PNN of the same origin, some of them perhaps originally designating names of professions, are exclusively attested in the Latin epigraphy of Venetia et Histria: The second name of sextiliae tatsoriae (dat., Emona/Igg, 1st C. AD) is likely to go back to *tn̥ d-tōr- ‘shaver’. The sequence <ts> is rendering the product of Italic /s:/. While the primitive form was probably *tend-tor-, fem. *tend-tr-ī, Latin shows a secondary o-grade, ultimately belonging to the present stem tondeō: cf. the masc. tōnsor, fem. tōnstrix, pp. tōnsus. In Venetic, the allomorph *tn̥ d- was probably taken from the original pp. *tn̥ d-to- obeying the ‘Italic rule’ and therefore reflects an older state of affairs than Latin does. The masculine variant of the suffix has been generalized and a further, still synchronically patronymic suffix -(i)i̯o- has been added. Accordingly, the underlying form is *tanssōr-(i)i̯ā, containing a fortis or long /s/ perceived or realized in this context as an affricate [ts] (as in Hittite, Basque or Romanesco), which attests to the phonetic difficulties of the nasal-fricative transition.2

A ps.-gentilic cavsorio felicissimo (Pula, VH), cavsoria valentina (Tergeste, VH) may go back to *keh2u-dhh1- ‘to strike’, and then is a cognate of L. cūdō and possibly cauda, caudex, caussa. One may add further connections like the Lusitanian PN corocavdi, corocavdivs ‘striker/smith of the army’ (see Redentor 2008; LIV 345). The expected Latin change *kau̯ ð- > *kau̯ β- may have been blocked when the fricative follows a diphthong; in our case, the actual realization was probably [aʊ]3. The cognomen in p(vblivs) oppivs vvlsor (CIL V 206, Pula, VH) can be traced back to *u̯ (e)ldh-tor- ‘destroyer’, undergirding the assumption that L. vellō goes back to *u̯ (e)lH-d(h)- (Schrijver 1991, 181), to be reanalysed in modern terms as a neo-root from *u̯ (e)l(H)-dhh1- with early laryngeal loss. The corresponding Latin form is identical but only attested to my knowledge in compounds. A ps.-gentilic Lastorius, attested twice as l(vcio) lastorio (Aquileia, VH) is definitely indicative of the stability of the cluster -VstV-. Another Lastorius was a tribune according to Livy. The root is given as *las- ‘to desire’ by LIV 397, and its existence in Italic and Celtic is guaranteed by L. lascīvus, OIr. lainn ‘voracious’ (< *las-ni-).

Now some words on the problem of the proposed evolution in the indigenous texts are in order. As we have seen, there is some reason to believe that /k/ became a glide in coda position. The intermediate stage preceding vocalization must have been a fricative sound. But inscriptions conducted in the Venetic alphabet never use the symbol <h> in codas. <h> is used in initial position, including the second member of compounds, until some time after punctuation was introduced, as in the PN ho.s.tihavo.s or horaiio.i. The handbooks (e.g. LV, 28) hold the view that <h> and <.i.> somehow underwent a stylistic convergence and became indistinguishable, which is strange: one would expect the Etruscan symbol going back to the Phoenician heth, which we transliterate as <h>, to be streamlined into <h>, as in Latin, not to turn the three horizontal lines into a vertical one and reduce the vertical ones to puncts, for which no intermediate stages are even conceivable. Since /h/ was lost some time after punctuation was introduced, what is actually meant is that the digraph <.i.v> (Lejeune’s <f3>) replaced <hv>, <vh> (Lejeune’s <f1>, <f2>) to render /f/, and that the symbol <.i.> is otherwise used to render the second part of a diphthong /ei̯ oi̯ ai̯/. In other words, the possibility of understanding any instance of <.i.> in codas as the late variant of <h> is avoided, since a sequence <ht> is implicitly regarded as impossible: the meager evidence seems to point to IE -kt- being rendered <.g.t>. For this reason, Lejeune’s classification of the three variants of <.i.> as <h2>, <h3> and <h4> is unfortunate and misguiding: what we know for certain is that <h> fell out of use and punctuated <.i.> was accorded two roles: the rendition of palatal glides in closing diphthongs and that of /f/ when used in combination with <v>. Only the second role needs to be clarified. There was no longer a symbol <h> of any shape, because there was no (post-)velar spirant phoneme in any context (see below for the deviant usage of <h1> in Idria).

Finally, the correct transliteration of two forms for ‘god’ in Gurina (Carinthia, with Lejeune’s <h4>) is .a..i.su.s. and .a..i.su.n. (LV nrs. 243-4) with etymological /i/, not .a.hsu.s. and .a.hsu.n.17 The question is settled by the discovery of a dedication to aeso in the same area (Schürr 2011, 100). <h4> is the only variant of <.i.> in which the two puncts look like hastae. If this could be interpreted as a transitional shape of <h>, it would weakly support the hypothetical convergence of <h> and <.i.>. In fact, the correct reading actually falsifies the idea. Given the above pieces of evidence, I would favour the following scenario:

1. When the written tradition began, a glottal fricative /h/ was on the way to becoming a simple glide in the codas: the chosen symbol at this initial stage was, ex hypothesi, <i> for -i.C-, but <h> for -jh.C-. As observed above, no inscription shows <h> of any shape in coda position, and this is consequently speculative, but in any event not different from the situation in Sabellic (O. ehtrad, úhtavis, U. ahtim, hahtu, etc.).18 Later, /h/ was lost with compensatory lengthening and this symbol even came to be used to mark vowel length and was often unrelated to an original velar sound, as in Umbrian sehmeniar, kumnahkle, etc. (cf. Meiser 1986, 30). Proto-Italic may still have had a paradigm of the type *regō, *rextos. Archaic Venetic had an independent, phonemic glottal fricative in initial position, and the weakening of [x] into a glottal fricative may have entailed selection of the symbol <h>. The result would be a morphophonemic alternation regV- vs. rejhC-. If prevocalization had occurred, the resulting glide was subphonemic, and therefore ignored by orthography.

2. A writing reform took place, and punctuation was introduced. Thereupon, /h/ disappeared in onsets. Crucially, it had already disap peared in codas, where the glide had been (or was about to be) phonemicized and merged with the inherited diphthongs. As a result, the symbol <.i.> was generalized in coda position at the expense of <h> to render the second part of closing diphthongs of various origins (IE -Vi̯- and -Vk-). The morphophonemic alternation, however, still bore witness to the existence of an underlying consonant (as in Ptg. faz ‘makes’ vs. feito ‘made’). And when the scribal decision was made it is possible that higher registers were still resistant to change.

An agent noun *di̯aritōr is attested at least twice in the mutilated bronze tablet of Este (c. 400 b.C., conducted in the Paduan alphabet) in the nom. pl. and the dat.-abl. pl. In her modellic edition, Marinetti 1998 contends that diaritores and diaritorbos are related to L. arātor. While this looks attractive at first sight, this agent noun would be based neither on the root, nor on the present stem, nor, contrary to expectations, on the past participle. This is likely to be a public text and *di̯aritōr must designate magistrates who were accorded more abstract administrative capacities than ploughing the fields. Attractive comparanda would be L. rītus ‘religious rites’, Gk. ἀριθμός ‘number’, Gk. νήριτος ‘countless’, from *h2r-(e)i- ‘to count, observe’ (a possibility already invoked by Magnin 2014, 123; see further OIr. renaid ‘to sell, exchange’, etc.), either with the full grade of the root or with a sequence of prefixes dē- and ad-. In the following lines I shall contend that at least two symmetric constructions can be discerned which allow a modest glimpse of the syntax and morphology of this text. In my view, its comprehension has been encumbered by the failure to recognize the graphic omission of the IE secondary verbal endings *-t and *-nt. According to Kümmel 2007, 96, OL. variants of 3rd p. perfect forms with voicing and loss of the dental (e.g. feced, dede, dedero/dedron) reflect the PIE aorist endings *-t and *-nt. Their voiced continuants *-ed (-e) and *-ond (> -on) survived into Latin, until they were ousted by the competing primary endings. Italian preterite forms like dièdero ‘they gave’ directly presuppose a survival of the ancient secondary ending. In my view, the nasal may additionally have undergone weakening in a complex coda -nd# early on. (Cf. the corresponding ending -οδ in the Palaeo-Italic cippus of Tortora, in which nasals in coda position are left out anyway, or SP. -úh from -od. Oscan and Umbrian -ns is a new ending probably modelled on the 1st and 2nd pl. forms in -mVs and -tVs, and may indirectly bear witness to an intolerable degree of phonetic erosion.) The process leading to the loss of the dental segment (and possibly the preceding nasal) must have begun prior to the loss of -i in the primary endings.

The application of these ideas to Venetic leads me to the conclusion that we shall find 3rd sg. pret. -e, 3rd pl. -o(n) and -a(n) (the only one attested thus far). It also follows that forms with preserved -(n)t have primary endings which have lost their final -i, as in Latin. There are thus far few clues on the descendants of the active secondary endings in Venetic: The form atisteit in the oldest Venetic text is unlikely to be a perfect: -ei-t cannot be compared to L. -ī-t. The Latin ending results from an internal process and has no parallels in Venetic. Some verbs, specifically those which had sigmatic preterites like vha.g.s.to ‘made’, put into service the medial endings (instead of using the primary ones, as in Latin) in order to counteract phonetic erosion and the subsequent loss of morphological distinctiveness, a problem that was not solved in the case of thematic root aorists or in the plural of sigmatic aorists (cf. donasa(n)).
