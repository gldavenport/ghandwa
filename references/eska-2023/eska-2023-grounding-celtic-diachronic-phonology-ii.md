# Grounding Celtic diachronic phonology II[^author-note]

Joseph F. Eska

## Abstract

This paper continues a series of treatments of sound changes in the Celtic languages that have not
been satisfactorily or fully explained to date. Sound changes that occurred in proto-Brittonic and
early Welsh are treated: (3) the shift of */j/ > /ð/ / ˈVr_V in proto-Brittonic; (4) the shift of
the group *-/ntʰL/- > -/θL/- in Old Welsh; (5) the evolution of the group */ltʰr/ in Welsh.

§1. This paper continues a series of treatments of non-straightforward sound changes attested in the
Celtic languages with the goal of motivating them in a non-stipulative way.[^1]


## 3. */j/ > /ð/ / ˈVr_V in proto-Brittonic

§2. It is now generally assumed that */j/ > */ð/ / r_ˈV in proto-Brittonic. The primary tokens
identified are MW 3.sg.pres. ard ‘ploughs’ (e.g., R 1345.6) < proto-Brit. */ar.ˈjV/- beside OIr.
airid ‘ploughs’; MW Iwerdon (e.g., PKM 29.16) < proto-Brit. */iː.wer.ˈjon/- beside MIr. Ériu
‘Ireland’; MW 3.sg.impf. gardei ‘groans, creaks’ (e.g., T 24.10) < proto-Brit. */kar.ˈje/- beside
OIr. gairid ‘shouts, wails’. OW casgoord gl. ‘satilites meos’ (Ox2 5.27), MW gwlat gord ‘land’s war
band’ (CA 36.905) beside Latinised Transalp. Celt. PETRVCORIO (CIL XII 275; dat. sg.)[^2] and MIr.
cuire ‘troop, host’ is also included, despite the fact that the form continues proto-Brit.
*/ˈkʰor.jo/- and the change occurs after the stressed vowel. Schrijver 1995, 282 states for the last
form that “the dd in W cordd ‘tribe, clan’” would have had to have “spread from the plural corddau”
< */kʰor.ˈjo.wes/,[^3] since */ˈkʰor.jo/- presumably would regularly be continued by **kyr.[^4]

§3. Pedersen 1909, 70 simply stipulates that this sound change took place. Those who have had more
to say are:[^5]

```text
(1) a. Morris-Jones 1913, 153 states that */j/ > /ð/ after both */l/ and */r/ in post-tonic
 position and that */lj/ > */lð/ > /ɬ/.[^6]
 b. Jackson 1953, 472 f. posits a change */j/ > */ʝ/ > /ð/ in pre-tonic position and
 says that it “is likely to be connected and co-eval with that of intervocalic i̯ >
 ð”. He also notes that “[t]he fact that after no other consonant does i̯ become ð
 may seem anomalous”.
 c. Schrijver 1995, 281 states that “[t]he development of *ri̯ to L[ate]P[roto]Br[itish] *rð was probably limited to the position before a (LPBr.) stressed vowel”.
 d. Russell 1990, 57 proposes that MW ard continues proto-Brit. */a.ˈri.jaːt/, hence
 */j/ > /ð/ not after */r/, but in the well-established environment ˈ{e i}._V (discussed in Eska 2018/2019 [2020], 21–24).[^7] He also posits that the /ð/ of
 Iwerdon is after proto-Brit. nom. sg. */iː.we.ˈri.juː/[^8] > Iweryd ‘IDIONYM’ (e.g.,
 MWG 364 §12.2.3) by the same rule. OW/MW kord < proto-Brit. */ˈkʰor.jo/-,
 instead, indicates that */j/ > /ð/ / r_ in post-tonic position.
```

§4. The difference between the positions of Morris-Jones and Russell vs. Jackson and Schrijver is
whether this sound change occurred prior to or after apocope. In view of the extreme rarity of the
sound change */j/ > /ð/ in the languages of the world,[^9] it is very likely that Jackson is correct
that this change after */r/ is “co-eval with that of intervocalic i̯ > ð”,[^10] which clearly occurred
prior to apocope upon the basis of MONEDORIGI (CIIC I 413 = CL Gn–12 = CEMIS III MR1) < proto-Brit.
*/mo.ˈni.jo/- and VEN͡EDOTIS (CIIC I 394 = CL Gn–32) < proto-Brit. */weː.ˈni.jaː/ (cf. Sims-Williams
2003, 23). Russell 1990, 52 f., 56 f. cites two forms that make it clear that *-/rj/- was continued
unchanged in pre-tonic position in proto-Brittonic:

```text
(2) a. MW kendeyryauc ‘furious’ (e.g., LlDW 120.11), OSWBrit. cunnaret boestol gl.
 ‘beluina rabies’ (DGVB 125) and OCorn. conerioc gl. ‘rabidus uel amens uel
 demens’ (VC 174 §392) < proto-Brit. */kʰu.no.tar.ˈjaː.kʰo/-.
 b. MW gorgoryawc ‘pertaining to a battle host’ (so GPC s.v. gorgoriog) (CT 5.28
 ≡ PT 6.28) < proto-Brit. ?*/wor.kʰor.ˈjaː.kʰo/-? (Transponat).[^11]
```

It seems, then, that OW/MW kord is the only incontrovertible evidence for this sound change. We must
take note that other forms of similar shape do not share in this change, however, e.g., proto-Celt.
*/ˈkʷʰar.jo/- > MIr. coire ‘cauldron’, but MW peir ‘cauldron’ and OCorn. per gl. ‘lebes’ (VC 394 §891), in which the entire final syllable appears to have been apocopated. This has led Jackson
1953, 472 and Russell 1990, 57 to the view that there was a double treatment of *-/rj/-. We will
turn to this seeming anomaly in §§14–15, but first will examine the evolution of */j/ > /ð/ / ˈVr_V in proto-Brit. */ˈkʰor.jo/-.

§5. Many languages avoid sequences of rhotic[^12] – whether denti-alveolar trill [r], flap [ɾ], or
approximant [ɹ]; retroflex flap [ɽ] or approximant [ɻ]; or uvular trill [ʀ] or fricative [ʁ][^13] –
plus high front vocoid, in either order, as discussed in detail in Hall & Hamann 2010. In the
Brittonic languages, the proto-Brittonic consonant group */rj/ is avoided in post-tonic position,
while it is continued unchanged in pre-tonic position.

§6. The gestural positioning of apical rhotics such as Brittonic /r/ = [r̺] is highly constrained,
requiring a lowered tongue blade and a lowered and retracted tongue dorsum, thus resulting in a
concave shape for the entirety of the tongue (Recasens 1991, 268; Recasens & Pallarès 1999; Solé
2002, 658).

§7. High front vocoids, similarly, require a high degree of articulatory precision. They, unlike
/r/, are produced with both the tongue blade and the tongue predorsum raised and fronted, thus
resulting in a convex shape for the entirety of the tongue (Recasens 1990; Recasens et al. 1993,
225).

§8. Ladefoged & Maddieson 1996, 221, thus, note that the articulatory gestures required to produce a
palatal consonant or secondary palatalisation make it difficult to maintain the conditions necessary
for trilling, to which we may add that the situation also applies in the reverse. Recasens &
Pallarès 1999, 144, indeed, discussing /r/ to /i/ coärticulation, describe their gestures as
“antagonistic” to each other.

§9. Hall & Hamann 2010, 1822 demonstrate that there are three avoidance strategies for the group
/Rj/:

```text
(3) a. /Rj/ does not occur, as in most varieties of English.
 b. /Rj/ is blocked from being derived,[^14] as in German, e.g., the sequence [VCiV]
 may optionally be realised as [VCjV] as in Familie [fa.ˈmiː.ljə] and Flexion
 [flɛ.ˈksjoːn] ‘inflexion’, but [j] cannot be derived after /ʁ/ as in Bakterie
 [pak.ˈteːʁɪ.ə] ‘bacterium’[^15] and Ferien [ˈfeː.ʁɪ.ən] ‘holidays’ (Hall & Hamann
 2010, 1826 f.).[^16]
 c. /Rj/ is repaired, as in Cypriot Greek, in which /j/ undergoes fortition to [k], e.g.,
 underlying /psarju/ surfaces as [psarku] ‘fish (gen.sg)’ (Hume & Odden 1996,
 366 f.).[^17]
```

The logical repair strategies that they outline are:

```text
(4) a. The rhotic may be deleted or changed.
 b. The high front vocoid may be deleted or changed.
 c. A vowel may be inserted between the rhotic and high front vocoid.
```

§10. In proto-Goidelic, *-/C.jV/- usually became -/Ci.jV/- (Pedersen 1909, 68; Thurneysen 1946, 121;
but cf. Uhlich 1993), so the resulting -/ri.jV/- did not require repair. Thus proto-Celt.
*/ˈkʷʰar.jo/- > proto-Goid. */ˈkʷʰa.ri.jo/- > MIr. coire ‘cauldron’. However, with the rise of
palatalisation, */rʲ/ developed. According to Hickey 2011, 220 f., palatalisation is retained in
the environments in (5) in Modern Irish:[^18]

```text
(5) a. In syllabic onset preceded by obstruent:
 príomh- /pʰʲrʲiːv/- [pʰʲɾʲiːv]- ‘chief, major’
 b. As the only intervocalical segment:
 aire /arʲə/ [æɾʲə] ‘care (f); minister (m)’
 c. As the only word-final segment:
 óir /oːrʲ/ [oːɾʲ] ‘gold (gen.sg)’
```

But in the environments in (6), /rʲ/ is depalatalised, thus following strategy (4b):

```text
(6) a. As the only word-initial segment:
 rian /rʲiənɣ/ [riənɣ] ‘trace’
 b. In syllabic coda followed by a plosive:
 páirt /pʰɑːrʲtʰʲ/ [pʰɑːrtʰʲ] ‘part’
```

The articulation of /rʲ/ varies across dialects, of course. For example, Ó Cuív 1944, 49 describes
the articulation of /rʲ/ as a palatalised voiced post-alveolar fricative – presumably [ʒʲ] –
intervocalically, in post-vocalic word-final position, and preceding palatalised bilabial and
palato-velar consonants and /h/ in the Irish of West Muskerry (Co. Cork), which is an example of
strategy (4a). In Scottish Gaelic dialects of the northern Hebrides, /rʲ/ is articulated as a
palatalised voiced interdental fricative [ðʲ] (Borgstrøm 1940, 71 f.; Oftedal 1956, 129),[^19] also an
example of strategy (4a). And in Late Spoken Manx, /rʲ/ was depalatalised and merged with /r/
(Jackson 1955, 116–121; Lewin 2019, 262–271), an example of strategy (4b), but, in intervocalic
position, could be reduced or deleted altogether (Broderick 1986, 17; Lewin 2019, 271–275), an
example of strategy (4a).[^20]

§11. It is strategy (4b) that occurred in proto-Brittonic. The first step is the fortition of */j/
to */ʝ/. Recasens 2013, 14–17 demonstrates that it is more anterior during gliding movement and that
it is frequently articulated at the alveolopalatal place, the tongue blade being alveolar and the
tongue dorsum palatal. “Thus,” as per Eska 2018/2019 [2020], 23, “it is not particularly surprising
to find that /j/ became articulated as the voiced post-alveolar fricative [ʒ] in Argentine Spanish
(Colantoni 2006; Kochetov & Colantoni 2011, esp. 329–331). Hence, calle ‘street’ is pronounced as
[kaʎe] in Castilian Spanish and [kaje] in Andalusian Spanish, but as [kaʝe]/[kaʤe]/[kaʒe]/[kaʃe] in
Argentine Spanish (Ohala & Solé 2010, 59 f.)” (cf. also Ohala 1983, 204).

§12. In Eska 2018/2019 [2020], 21–24, I propose that the very rare sound change */j/ > /ð/ (in the
environment ˈ{e i}._V) in proto-Brittonic is the result of post-alveolar fronting, a developmental
childhood speech sound disorder whereby a post-alveolar phone(me) in syllable-initial or
syllable-final position preserves its manner and voicing, but is articulated at a more anterior
place. Thus, after */j/ > *[ʝ] > *[ʒ] in proto-Brittonic, *[ʒ] > /ð/ via post-alveolar fronting, but,
instead of being naturally corrected in childhood, persisted and became intractable in the speech of
one or more elite members of society and spread through his/her social group as a prestige variant
which was subsequently adopted throughout the speech community.

§13. I posit, then, that after */ʝ/ > */ʒ/ following /r/ in post-tonic position, it became /ð/ in
proto-Brittonic via post-alveolar fronting, and, as Jackson surmised, was “connected and co-eval”
with the change of */j/ > /ð/ / ˈ{e i}._V, likewise in post-tonic position.

§14. We may turn now to the double treatment of */j/ / ˈVr_V in proto-Brittonic, whereby
proto-Brit. */ˈkʰor.jo/- > OW/MW kord, but proto-Brit. */ˈpʰar.jo/- > MW peir. I propose that, in
fact, they may both be considered as regular developments. It is crucial that we recognise that
“sound change is drawn from a pool of synchronic variation” (Ohala 1989), i.e., that there would
have been considerable phonetic variation across the proto-Brittonic speech area from which sound
changes would arise at various focal points and then disperse. A sound change could propagate
through the entire speech area, e.g., proto-IE */oː/ > proto-Celt. */aː/ in non-final syllables, but
could fail to do so, e.g., Vː > V / _[nasal]# spread through the most of the proto-Celtic speech
area, thus yielding gen.pl. -on -/on/ in, e.g., Transalp. Celt. neđđamon ‘of neighbours’ (RIG L–50),
but the change ceased to propagate before it reached the portion of the proto-Celtic speech area
that broke off to become Hispano-Celtic. Thus, one finds gen.pl. -um -/uːm/ in Celtib. aPuloKum ‘of
the *Apulokʰī’ (e.g., MLH K.16.1 = Hesp. IB.01.01), which evinces the change of proto-IE */oː/ >
proto-Celt. */uː/ / _C0# (Eska 2006).

§15. Let us presume, for the sake of argument, that there was a more northerly variety of
proto-Western Brittonic and one that was located somewhat to the south. It is not difficult to
outline a scenario in which both kord and peir developed regularly:

```text
(7) a. The first step is that */j/ > */ð/ / ˈVr_V begins in the more northerly area, thus
 yielding */ˈkʰor.ðo/- and */ˈpʰar.ðo/-, while */ˈkʰor.jo/- and */ˈpʰar.jo/- remained in the southern area. This change begins to propagate southward. It is
 possible that this change propogated via lexical diffusion, i.e., that it proceeded
 lexeme by lexeme, and that */ˈpʰar.jo/- was not affected.
 b. In the meantime, vowel affection takes place in the southern area, thus yielding
 */ˈkʰir.jo/- and */ˈpʰeɪr.jo/-. This change begins to propagate northward.
 c. At this stage, */j/ > / ˈV.C_V in the southern area. This change, reflected in
 southern Middle Welsh, has been studied by Thomas 1992, 1993. Willis 2005
 has argued that its distribution suggests that it spread via lexical diffusion. We
 now find */ˈkʰor.ðo/- and */ˈpʰar.ðo/- / */ˈpʰar.jo/- in the more northerly area,
 but */ˈkʰi.ro/- and */ˈpʰeɪ.ro/- in the southern area.[^21]
 d. In the northern area, vowel affection arrives, but does not affect */ˈkʰor.ðo/- and
 */ˈpʰar.ðo/- for lack of necessary phonological trigger. Had */j/ > */ð/ / ˈVr_V
 not affected */ˈpʰar.jo/-, it now becomes */ˈpʰeɪr.jo/-. The sound change */j/ >
 / ˈV.C_V ceases before it reaches the northern area. The necessary phonological
 environment for the sound change */j/ > */ð/ / ˈVr_V to occur no longer exists
 in the southern area owing to (7c).
 e. After apocope, one finds /ˈkʰorð/ and */ˈpʰarð/ / */ˈpʰeɪ.ri/ in the northern area,
 but */ˈkʰir/ and /ˈpʰeɪr/ in the southern area, which then mix across dialects. For
 whatever reason(s), the variants kord /ˈkʰorð/ and peir /ˈpʰeɪr/ win out.[^22]
```


## 4. *-/ntʰL/- > -/θL/- in Old Welsh[^23]

§16. Examples: MW kathil ‘song’ (e.g., LlDC 28.88) beside Bret. kentel ‘lesson’[^24] and MIr. cétal
‘song’ < proto-Celt. */kʰantʰlo/-; post-syncope MW cythlwng ‘hunger’ (e.g., IGE2 8.4) (Jones 1944)
beside OIr. cétlongad ‘fasting’ < proto-Celt. */kʰintʰu-lonk/-; MW ewythr ‘uncle’ beside Bret. eontr
‘uncle’ < */awon-tʰiːr/-;[^25] post-syncope OW ithr in the gloss íthr ír díu áil ‘between the two
eyebrows’ gl. ‘glabella medietas’ (MC 57 f. §59) beside MCorn. ynter, MBret. intre ‘between’, and
OIr. eter ‘between’ < proto-Celt. */entʰer/; cf. also Celtib. enTara /entʰraː/ ‘within’ (MLH K.1.1
A6 = Hesp. Z.09.01 A6).

§17. Sims-Williams 2008, 522 = 2007, 56 states that *-/ntʰL/- > -/θL/- is a “peculiar internal
change”. Those who have had more to say are:

```text
(8) a. Pedersen 1909, 149 f. argues that preceding nasals caused plosives to become
 fricatives in proto-Brittonic, but that they reverted back to plosives in Cornish
 and Breton. He is followed by McCone 1996b, 95 f.
 b. Morris-Jones 1913 argues that *-/ntʰL/- > *-/ttʰL/- > -/θL/- in Old Welsh. He is
 substantially followed by Jackson 1953, 500 and Isaac 2004, 66 f.
```

§18. Schrijver 1999, 13 is certainly correct that “there is no such thing as a spirant mutation
after nasals”. This is because the production of nasals requires the lowering of the velum, while
fricatives require a tightly closed velum in order to generate turbulence.

§19. Even were one to countenance that *-/ntʰ/- might develop to *-/ttʰ/-, it is well known that
geminates are blocked from being altered by any lenition process by Geminate Inalterability
(Kirchner 2000; 2001, 97–135) prior to being reduced to singletons. Thus, in languages in which /p
t k/ > /ɸ θ x/, /pp tt kk/ remain unaltered, i.e., they do not become /ɸ θ x/ nor /ɸɸ θθ xx/.
Kirchner 2001, 104 f. cites the example of Biblical Hebrew,[^26] which has a rule of frication of
post-vocalic labials, velars, and non-emphatic coronals:

```text
(9) Imperfect Basic perfect Causative perfect Gloss
 yix[p]or kaa[ɸ]ar ki[pp]eer ‘cover’
 yiɸ[t]aħ paa[θ]aħ pi[tt]eeaħ ‘open’
 yiz[k]or zaa[x]ar zi[kk]eer ‘remember’
```

Here one sees that post-consonantal radical /p t k/ are unchanged in the imperfect, but are fricated
to /ɸ θ x/ post-vocalically in the basic perfect, while geminate /pp tt kk/ remain unchanged
post-vocalically in the causative perfect.

§20. I propose that *-/ntʰL/- > -/θL/- occurred in two stages. The first is that the feature
[continuant] spread from the liquid to the preceding aspirated plosive, thus:

> Diagram note: the PDF shows [continuant] spreading from `L` to preceding `tʰ`.

```text
(10)  tʰ   L
       ↖---│
      [cont]
```

Russell 1984 posits a similar leftward spreading of the feature [continuant] in his discussion of
the variation in Welsh forms such as anadl ‘breath’ beside anaddl and gwadn ‘sole of the foot; boot;
sock’ beside gwaddn. He proposes that */tl tn/ *[t̬l t̬n] remained unchanged in intervocalic
position – hence they are heterosyllabic –,[^27] but that the spreading obtained in word-final position
– hence they are tautosyllabic –, after which levelling in either direction took place. Schrijver
1995, 360–365 argues, however, that the spreading occurred in both tautosyllabic and heterosyllabic
groups, after which /ð/ > [t̬] / _{l n} via [continuant]-dissimilation in heterosyllabic groups. I
take proto-Brit. *-/ntʰl/- and *-/ntʰr/- to have been syllabified as *-/n.tʰl/- and *-/n.tʰr/- upon
the basis of syllabic onsets as attested in MW tlws ‘jewel’ (e.g., HPE 9.30) and OW trum ‘heavy’ gl.
‘abrupta’ (Juv. 44r20), trumm gl. ‘aegrum’ (Juv. 47v13). Both *-/tʰl/- and *-/tʰr/-, thus, are
tautosyllabic and after being subject to [continuant]-spreading retained it. The resulting -/nθL/-
may be attested by OW cénthlíát gl. ‘Dauida canorum’ (Juv. 26r26)[^28] (> later ceithliad ‘singer’).[^29]

§21. The loss of /n/ is a matter of interarticulatory timing. Solé 2012, 124 explains that
“different outcomes of change may arise from small variations in the magnitude or coordination of
consecutive segments”. In the case of nasal + fricative groups, the required respective settings of
the velum are antagonistic to each other: it must be open to allow airflow through the nasal cavity
to enable nasal phonation, while it must be closed to allow the generation of sufficient turbulence
to enable frication. As Solé 2007, 47 f. notes, it is common for anticipatory closure of the velum
prior to the articulation of the fricative to result in the effacement of the nasal, e.gg., Lat.
institutum > Ital. istituto ‘institution’ and proto-Gmc. *fimfe > OE fīf ‘five’.[^30] Thus, we may
conclude that after proto-Brit. *-/ntʰL/- > -/nθL/- in Old Welsh via [continuant]-spreading, -/nθL/-
> -/θL/- via nasal effacement owing to articulatory constraints.


## 5. The evolution of */ltʰr/ in Welsh

§22. The consonantal group */ltʰr/ is continued as -/θr/- in intervocalic position in MW athro
‘teacher’ beside OSWBrit. altro (DGVB 59) ‘lord’, OCorn. altrou gl. ‘uictricus’ (sic; VC 81 §140),
and MIr. altra ‘foster-father’ < proto-Celt. */altʰro/- ‘nourisher; fosterer’ (cf. OIr. altram(m)
‘act of nourishing, fostering’) + */awon/- ‘maternal uncle’ (cf. MW ewythyr ‘uncle’ and Bret. eontr
< */awon-tʰiːr/) (Schumacher apud Stüber 1998, 109 f.). Pedersen 1909, 137 and Jackson 1953, 401
note, however, that one also finds -/ɬt/- in MW alldraỽon (pl.) ‘godfather; sponsor’,[^31] which is
sparsely attested in Middle Welsh (but a single medieval token is listed in GPC: LlA 158.15 [1346]).
The feminine form elltrewyn ‘stepmother; mother-in-law’ is, likewise, sparsely attested in Middle
Welsh (GPC, again, lists but a single medieval token: DMh17 3.48), to which compare OSWBrit.
eltroguen gl. ‘nouerca’ (DGVB 157) and OCorn. altruan gl. ‘nouerca’ (VC 81 §141). P s.v. elltrewen
in 1793 glosses the form as ‘tutoress; a sponsor; a gossip, a stepmother’. It appears, then, that we
are dealing with a lexical split. In word-final position, */ltʰr/ is continued as -/ɬtr/ in OW
cultir gl. ‘cultrum’ (Ox2 4.31), MW cỽlltẏr (e.g., WML 108.2),[^32] and MBret. coultr ‘coulter’ ⇐ Lat.
cultrum.

§23. There has been little comment upon the change of *-/ltʰr/- > -/θr/-:

```text
(11) a. Morris-Jones 1913, 151 states that *-/ltʰr/- > *-/ttʰr/- > -/θr/-.
 b. Jackson 1953, 401 stipulates that *-/ltʰr/- > -/θr/-, but notes that this occurs “apparently only internally, and not exclusively even then”.
 c. McCone 1996b, 95 argues that *-/ltʰr/- > *-/lθr/- > -/θr/-, but does not attempt to
 explain the -/ɬt/- variant in intervocalic position.
 d. Isaac 2004, 67 states that *-/ltʰr/- > *-/lttʰr/-.[^33]
```

Aside from Lewis’ 1943, 22 stipulation that Brit. Lat. /ltʰr/ > W -/ɬtr/ in final position, I am
unaware of any previous commentary on this sound change.

§24. As noted in §19, Geminate Inalterability does not allow for geminate plosives to change
directly to fricatives, so Morris-Jones’ and Isaac’s views cannot be correct, while Jackson and
McCone assume deletion of the *-/l/-, but without providing motivation.

§25. It is pertinent to examine the treatment of the group */ltʰ/ in Welsh. In intervocalic
position, it evolves to -/ɬ/-, while in final position, it first became -/lθ/, as demonstrated by
Schumacher 2019, by spreading of the feature [continuant] from */l/ to */tʰ/, leading to the
frication of /tʰ/ to /θ/, as in *-/lkʰ/ > -/lx/ in balch ‘proud’ < */palkʰ/.[^34] I have argued that
-/lθ/ > -/ɬt/ by metathesis of the feature [spread glottis], whereby it spreads from /θ/ to /l/,
after which it is delinked from /θ/ (Eska 2019), thus:

> Diagram note: the PDF shows [spread glottis] spreading between `l` and `θ`.

```text
(12)  l    θ
       ↖---│
       [sg]
```

This results in */θ/ being occluded to /t/, and devoices the */l/. The increased airflow through the
open glottis during the articulation of */l/ leads to turbulence, which results in /ɬ/ (Ohala 2005,
29; Ohala & Solé 2010, 43). This explanation also applies to the treatment of *-/ltʰr/ in final
position in OW cultir / MW cỽlltẏr.

§26. Upon the basis of MW porffor ‘purple’ ⇐ Lat. purpura and kylchet ‘blanket’ ⇐ Lat. culcita, it
is clear that intervocalic heterosyllabic *-/l.t/- would have become *-/l.θ/- in Welsh. In view of
the treatment of /lθ/ in syllable-final position as described in §25, I propose that the feature
[spread glottis] then spread from /θ/ to /l/, but did not delink from /θ/ across the syllable
boundary, thus yielding *-/ɬ.θ/-:

> Diagram note: the PDF shows [spread glottis] spreading between `l` and `θ`.

```text
(13)  l    θ
       ↖---│
       [sg]
```

But *-/ɬ.θ/-, as a sequence of two voiceless fricatives – a disfavoured sequence not otherwise known
in Welsh –, then dissimilated by deletion of */θ/[^35] so as to result in attested intervocalic
-/ɬ/-.[^36]

§27. In the case of the form athro, I propose that *-/l.tʰr/- first changed to *-/l.θr/- by
spreading of the feature [continuant] from */r/ to tautosyllabic */tʰ/ as described in §20.[^37] The
feature [spread glottis] then spread from */θ/ to heterosyllabic */l/ as described in §26, after
which the resulting disfavoured intervocalic consonant group *-/ɬ.θr/- dissimilated to attested
-/θr/- via deletion of /ɬ/.[^38]

§28. We now turn to the forms alldraỽon and elltrewyn, which appear to have the treatment of
proto-Brittonic */ltʰr/ as attested in tautosyllabic final position in OW cultir / MW cỽlltẏr.[^39] We
may note that proto-Brittonic */ltʰr/ is continued unchanged in Southwestern Brittonic, so it may be
that it was initially likewise continued unaltered in southeastern Western Brittonic, which likely
was a transition zone with Southwestern Brittonic.[^40] Under this view, */ltʰr/ subsequently underwent
metathesis of the feature [spread glottis] as described in §25 so as to yield attested -/ɬtr/-.
Alternatively, these forms could possibly have been borrowed from the plural of OCorn. altrou and
altruan, respectively – in which case the substitution of W -/ɬ/- for Corn. -/l/- / _t is required.
That these etyma underwent a lexical split between proto-Western Brittonic and proto-Southwestern
Brittonic perhaps suggests that the latter possibility may be more likely, though the paucity of
evidence hardly allows a definitive analysis.
