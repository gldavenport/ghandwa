## Abstract

Dieser Aufsatz behandelt eine Reihe von Problemen, die in der vermutlich keltischen Onomastik auf venetischen Inschriften auftreten. In diesem Zusammenhang werden hier neben der eigentlichen etymologischen Analyse eine Reihe von Fragen bezüglich des Verlusts von /p/ im Keltischen, des frühkeltischen Akzents, Anlautgruppen, Wortstellung, Schemata der Bildung komponierter Nomina agentis, Fehlwahrnehmungen oder unerwarteter phonemischer Interpretationen fremder Formen durch einheimische Schreiber sowie areallinguistischer Phänomene wie des Verlusts von intervokalischem -u̯- und -s- behandelt.

### 1. Introduction¹

As is well known, the Venetic record contains a number of unmistakably Gaulish names, both in the Venetic and the Latin alphabet. In this work, I shall set out to find some common traits that allow to distinguish them from the Italic names and give us a number of clues on the specific Gaulish dialect of the area and the way in which Gaulish words were adopted by Venetic and written down by Venetic scribes in both alphabets. Conversely, a number of phonetic and morphological traits of the Venetic language which have passed unseen will be isolated and compared to those of Celtic. Additionally, I shall enumerate the arguments that allow us to differentiate Gaulish and Venetic names occurring in the Venetic record when the similarity to names attested elsewhere does not suffice. Given the comparatively early age of the Venetic inscriptions, as well as the differences between the Latin and the Venetic alphabet, some hitherto overlooked facts about Eastern Gaulish will be brought to light. Finally, I shall try to cement the idea that Gaulish names have been treated in a rather predictable way, that is to say that by virtue of being filtered into the Venetic record they

### 3. IE /p/ in the Venetic onomastics

#### 3.1. The Gaulish and Venetic stop system

In a couple of previous works (cf. PRÓSPER 2018a and forthcoming), I have contended that the synchronic phonemic contrasts of (Eastern) Gaulish and Venetic can be described according to their interpretation by foreign scribes and in a foreign context. I have come to the conclusion that Gaulish distinguished the intervocalic lax voiceless stops [b̥ d̥ g̊]²⁰ from the voiced fricatives [β ð γ].²¹

By contrast, Venetic seems to have distinguished the intervocalic tense stops [p t k] (from PIE /p t k/, corresponding to equally voiceless tense stops in initial position) from the voiced stops [b d g] (from PIE /b d g/, corresponding to similar stops in initial position, which may have contrasted with their voiceless counterparts as lax, not as voiced stops, however).²² To this we may possibly add [β ð ɦ] (phonemically /f h/, albeit the status of [ð] is undetermined after the merger of /f/ and /θ/ in Anlaut) from the so-called IE voiced aspirates, but this is hypothetical.

As a consequence, PIE intervocalic voiceless stops in Gaulish names are often interpreted as voiced/lax stops by Venetic scribes, and, conversely, the voiced/lax stops of Venetic are integrated as their own lenited voiceless stops by Gaulish scribes. Initial plain voiced and voiceless stops do not seem to have been prone to confusion, which is consistent with the idea that their outcomes were not very different. ### 4. IE -st- in the Venetic record. Celtic or Venetic?

#### 4.1. Two Venetic numeral-based names and a Celtic one: How much Etruscan influence did Venetic really undergo?

While the name GAVIVS is comparatively well attested and there is a pseudo-gentilic RAVIS, dat. This is why an appellative must be taken into account, and the whole sense of the text reconsidered. In my view, FRIVI goes back to IE *bhriH-u̯o- (LIV 92–93), a deverbative adjective only attested in Italic, specifically in the Lat. diminutive frīvolus ‘insignificant’, re-frīvus ‘shredded’, and in MW. briw ‘wound, injury’. The use of this form is either designed to mean ‘small, baby’ or, perhaps more probably, that the son’s body had been badly injured. Contrary to Lejeune’s views, it is additionally more understandable that it is the deceased person’s PN (and his office) that is consigned. Gavis Raupatnis, the soldier, is in all likelihood the actual destinatary of the dedication, which alludes to his premature death in combat (during the social wars?). The father’s name is easily deducible since RAVPATNIS is a patronymic, and need not be repeated. The text consequently consists of two separate units, the first of which is only a heading: ‘G. R. miles infelicissimus. Vulnerati ossibus pater’. In sum, the fact that the deceased was poltos ‘unfortunate’ could be the direct consequence of his being *frīu̯os. On a more compelling view, however, POLTOS may belong to the second part of the text, and then agrees with PATER. The general sense would be: ‘G. R. miles. Afflictus vulnerati ossibus pater’. This new explanation would have some welcome consequences if we consider the usual word order of Venetic: Lejeune’s segmentation of MILES POLTOS and OSTINOBOS FRIVI as a unit comprised of determinatum + determinans stands in open contradiction with the usual Venetic phrase structure of adjective + noun and genitive + noun (see PRÓSPER 2018a). But as implied above, there was never a MILES POLTOS, but a POLTOS ... PATER ‘the afflicted father’ and not OSTINOBOS FRIVI ‘to the bones of Frivus’ but FRIVI PATER ‘the father of the ill-fated one’. This leads me to some general considerations about the original nature of the proper names prefixed by are- (*pr̥h₂-i-) in Continental Celtic. Most of the forms commonly mentioned to this effect are hypostatic compounds in which are- is a preposition governing a noun, and the resulting form means ‘living/located by/close to + place name or noun designating a place to be or extension of water or land’. But many others are, as in the veneticised case above, intensive compounds, in which the prefix modifies an adjective. The difference between both types lies in semantics, inner syntax, and prosody. On the one hand, the syncopated type in ar- is universally adjectival and endocentric, and states that the participation of the referent in the quality designated by the second member of the compound ranks high on an axiological scale. It only requires a trivial metaphorical shift from the concrete value ‘before, in front of’ of the prefix to the abstract ‘most, especially, very’. The type in are-, by contrast, is exocentric and places the referent in purely spatial terms, that is to say in front of, or along a more or less circumscribed territory, expressed by the second member. Accordingly, the fact that this prefix may surface as are- or ar- may constitute a comparatively reliable diagnostic tool to sort Continental Celtic compounds according to their internal structure. The reason for the syncopation vs. preservation of -e- from original -i- may be prosodic: In prepositional compounds, the stress is expected to fall on the governed noun and the preposition is proclitic, as in the phrase that gives rise to the compound. Additionally, most of the attested compounds are late and perhaps productive formations, whose second member is a proper name or a Celtic appellative, and the resulting compound stands no chance of being inherited. In contrast, compounds in which the prefix syntactically modifies a participle have borne the accent on the prefix. This is the case in ancient Vedic endocentric compounds like puró-hita- ‘placed in front’, sú-hita- ‘well placed’, Gk. ἀπόβλητος ‘to be thrown away’ and, as observed, for instance, by LÜHR 2004, it probably continues the Indo-European state of affairs. The Greek alternation of the simplex αἱρετός vs. the compounded -αἴρετος points in the same direction. This definitely speaks against the hypothesis that Gaulish

While ar- is mostly attested in personal names, usually conveying qualities attributed to its bearer, are- is more frequent in ethnic names and in place names. Ethnic names are so to speak an intermediate category in that they show characteristics of place names and of personal names. This makes them highly unpredictable in semantic terms. This is why it is methodologically advisable to make use of further criteria to interpret compounded ethnic names. For instance, there is something inherently strange in interpreting the EN Arnemetici (CIL XII 2820, Beaucaire) as ‘those living by a sacred grove, sanctuary’, since in most cases the governed noun is an entity whose existence logically precedes the population named after it (or at least the action of naming). Yet, under such a premise, the Arnemetici are named after a precinct that owes its nature to an act of consecration effected by the very population that is said to live close to it. While ancient sanctuaries are known to have played an aggregative role in the making of urban complexes, it does not look likely that this applies to ethnic names. This would be understandable if Arnemetici were an exoethnic name and if possessing a sanctuary or natural temple were an extraordinary circumstance for a Celtic population. ### 9. Conclusion: What Celtic and what Venetic at the northern Italian interface?
