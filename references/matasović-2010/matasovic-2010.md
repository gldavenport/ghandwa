# The etymology of Latin *focus* and the devoicing of final stops before `*s` in Proto-Indo-European

<!-- p. 212 -->

Latin *focus* “hearth, fireplace” represents an etymological crux.[^1] Despite many attempts to find the origin of this word, there is, at present, no commonly accepted etymology. The connection with Arm. *bocᶜ* “fire” (Walde-Hoffmann’s 1930-1950, I: 521, IEW 162) is untenable, since Arm. `-cᶜ-` points to the PIE cluster `*-sk-`. The Armenian word might or might not be related to Arm. *bosor* “red”, but this connection would not help us understand the origin of Lat. *focus*. A PIE root of the shape `DheT-` would contradict the phonotactic rules of PIE root-formation. The connection of *focus* with Lat. *fax* “torch”, Lith. *žvãkė* “candle” is impossible because of the diverging vocalism. Hamp’s (1992) attempt to explain *focus* as a back-formation from *foculus* “small stove” is extremely difficult. Hamp assumes that *foculus* is derived from `*dʰgʷʰe-tlo-`, from the root `*dʰegʷʰ-` “to burn” (Lat. *foveo* “to warm”), and that Proto-Italic `*xwetlo-` developed in Latin first to `*xwotlo-`, and only subsequently to `*fotlo-` > Lat. *foculu-*. However, as Michiel de Vaan (2008: 228) points out, the change of `*we-` to `*wo-` is datable to the fifth century BC, and the development of `*xw` to `*f` is almost certainly earlier (it is probably common Italic). After briefly reviewing the work of his predecessors, de Vaan follows Ernout and Meillet (1979: 243) in leaving the question of the etymology of *focus* unresolved.

Derivationally, *focus* is quite isolated in Latin; although it is attested since Plautus, it has only a handful of derivatives, including the mentioned *foculus*. This could point to borrowing from some unknown source, but to make such an assumption is nothing short of acknowledging ignorance. The other possibility is that *focus* represents a derivative from some well-known PIE root, but that the shape of the root was changed beyond recognition.

I would like to propose a new solution to this old problem. I believe that Lat. *focus* developed from the PIE root-noun `*dʰōgʷʰs` “flame, fire” from the root `*dʰegʷʰ-` “to burn” (Ved. *dáhati*, Lith. *degù*, *dègti*, OCS *žegǫ*, LIV 115f.). The root is attested in Latin in the derivative *febris* “fever” (< `*dʰegʷʰri-`) and in the causative *foveo* “to warm” (< `*dʰogʷʰ-eye-`).[^2] The root-final `*-gʷʰ-` would have been devoiced and delabialized before the nominative `*-s`, and the result was the cluster `*-ks-`, as in Lat. *vox* “voice” < `*wōkʷ-s`. Another reflex of the same PIE root noun is probably preserved in PCelt. `*degʷi-` “flame, blaze” (OIr. *daig*, cf. Matasović 2009: 93), with the transfer to the i-stems. If PCelt. `*degʷi-` generalized the stem of the oblique cases, we may reconstruct to PIE a static paradigm with the Nom. sg. `*dʰōgʷʰ-s`, Acc. sg. `*dʰogʷʰ-m`, Gen. sg. `*dʰegʷʰ-s`. This would have been parallel to, e.g., the inflection of the word for “night”, PIE Nom. sg. `*nōkʷt-s` (> Ved. *nák*), Acc. sg. `*nokʷt-m` (> Lat. *noct-em*), Gen. sg. `*nekʷt-s` (> Hitt. *nekuz*), cf. Schindler 1972. Of course, it is also possible that in PCelt. `*degʷi-` the e-grade was introduced under the analogical influence of the verb (PIE `*dʰegʷʰ-o-` > Skt. *dáhatí*).

The evolution of meaning from “flame, fire” to “hearth” might appear problematic, but these meanings are quite often related. It is true that the attested developments are usually in the reverse direction, from “hearth” to “fire”, as in the history of Lat. *focus* in the Romance languages, where the original Lat. *ignis* was generally replaced by the reflexes of *focus* in the meaning “fire” (e.g. It. *fuoco*, Sp. *fuego*, Fr. *feu*). Similarly, Croatian *vȁtra* (of unknown origin, but certainly connected with Alb. *vatër*, *votër* “fireplace, hearth”) changed the meaning from “hearth” to “fire” in the contemporary language, replacing the inherited reflex of Proto-Slavic `*ognь` (Russ. *ogónʹ* etc.). However, the extension of meaning from “fire” to “fireplace, hearth” is attested in Celtic; the older meaning of PCelt. `*teφnet-` is found in W *tân* “fire”, while OIr. *teine* (Gen. sg. *teined*) has both meanings, “fire” and “fireplace, hearth” (DIL 583). We may assume that the same change of meaning occurred in the history of *focus*.

The etymology of Lat. *focus* presented here would certainly be more convincing if we could find parallel examples, in which the apparently irregular correspondences of voiceless stops in one language, with voiced stops in others, are due to devoicing before final `*-s`, and the subsequent generalization of the voiceless stop in the whole paradigm. And indeed, there are similar cases. Latin *hortus* “garden”, Gr. *khórtos* “enclosed place, feeding place”, OIr. *gort* “field” < “*enclosed field” and W *garth* “pen, enclosure” (< PCelt. `*gorto-`, Matasović 2009: 164-5) all have the voiceless `-t-` because these forms developed by thematicization of a root noun, PIE `*gʰōrdʰ-s`, Acc. `*gʰordʰ-m`, Gen. `*gʰrdʰ-os` “enclosure”. The reflexes of the aspirated root-final `*dʰ` are preserved in OCS *gradъ* “town”, Lith. *gardas* “pen”, Skt. *gṛhá-* “house” (with the zero-grade of the root from the oblique cases, cf. Matasović 2004: 99). Here too we may start from a PIE root-noun in which the voiced or aspirated root-final consonant became voiceless before the word-final `*-s`. The voiceless root-final consonant was subsequently generalized throughout the paradigm. To posit both `*gʰor-to-` and `*gʰor-dʰ-o-` in PIE, as de Vaan does (2008: 290-291), is possible in principle, but it is rather unmotivated, since both forms would have had exactly the same meaning (“enclosure”) in PIE. Clearly a simpler explanation, deriving all the attested forms from a single PIE etymon is preferable. If `*gʰor-to-` is indeed a verbal adjective to the root `*gʰer-` “enclose”, the o-vocalism in Celtic and Greek is unexpected (Lat. *hortus* may, of course, be from both `*gʰrto-` and `*gʰorto-`). Moreover, the very existence of a root `*gʰer-` “enclose” is uncertain. LIV (157) and IEW posit `*gʰer-`, but the connection of the words for “enclosure” with Skt. *hárati* “takes, brings” is doubtful, both formally (OCS *gradъ* and Lith. *gardas* point to the plain velar in PIE)[^3] and semantically (the evolution of meaning from “enclose” to “take, bring”, or vice versa, is possible, but far from obvious).

Likewise, PCelt. `*druko-` “bad” (OIr. *droch-*, MW *drwg-*, etc., cf. Matasović 2009: 105) represents a thematicization of the stem `*druk-`, which is the regular outcome of the PIE root-noun `*dʰrōwgʰ-s` “lie, deceit” (cf. Skt. *drúhyate* “deceives”, *drúh-* “deceit”, OHG *gi-trog* “deceit”, IEW 276) before word-final `*-s` (with `*gʰs` > `*ks`). The zero-grade of the root was generalized, apparently, from the oblique cases (Gen. sg. `*dʰrugʰ-os`, etc.).

Next, Lat. *falx* (Gen. sg. *falcis*) “hook, scythe, sickle” is most likely related to OIr. *delg* “thorn, pin”, W *dala* “sting”, OIc. *dálkr* “pin, spine, dagger”, Lith. *dìlgė* “nettle”, but both the vocalism (`-al-` rather than `*-ol-`) and final `-c-` (rather than `*g`) are unexplained (de Vaan 2008: 200). Even if the word is borrowed in Latin from some IE language of Italy, as de Vaan thinks, its root-final `-c-` may be due to devoicing before final `*-s` in the Nom. sg. of the PIE root noun (`*dʰōlg-s`, Gen. `*dʰlg-os`).

Finally, PCelt. `*meko-` “sack, bushel (of corn)” (OIr. *míach*) can be plausibly connected to OPr. *moasis* “barley”, Latv. *mìeži* (pl.), Lith. *miẽžiai* (pl.) “barley”, if one starts from PIE root-noun `*meygʰs` [`meyks`] from which the root form with `*k` could have been extended analogically (Matasović 2009: 162). However, in the absence of cognates from other languages,[^4] this last example is clearly less persuasive than the others.

It is worth mentioning that the same process of the generalization of devoiced root-final stops in root nouns can be observed in the history of Latin. Lat. *pāx*, *pācis* “peace” owes its root-final `-c-` to the generalization of the root form which is expected before the final `-s`. The comparison with *pango* “to fix”, Gr. *pēgnymi* “to attach”, Skt. *pajrá-* “solid, firm” shows that the PIE root was `*peh₂g-`, and that the voiceless stop was generalized in Latin on the analogy with the Nom. sg., where `*g` was regularly devoiced before word-final `*-s`. The root form with `-c-` was extended to all formations from this root not only in Latin (cf. also *pācātus* “peaceful, settled”, *pacīscere* “to arrange by negotiation, betroth”), but also in other Italic languages, cf. Umbrian *paca* (postposition) “because of”, Paelignian *pacris* “merciful, auspicious”.

<!-- p. 215 -->

To conclude, then, there are both formal and semantic parallels to the scenario proposed here for the development of Lat. *focus* from the PIE root noun `*dʰōgʷʰ-s` “fire”. Latin either generalized the o-grade of the root from the stem of the Acc. sg. (`*dʰogʷʰ-m`), or introduced the o-grade analogically with the other thematic stems (cf. the same o-vocalism in *hortus* above).

[^1]: I would like to thank Michiel de Vaan for useful comments on the earlier version of this paper.

[^2]: The etymological connection of *focus* and *foveo* is alluded to in Servius’ commentary (*Commentary on the Aeneid*, 12.118): *quidquid ignem fovet, focus vocatur*. This also shows that *focus* is not a sacrificial altar in Latin (*āra*), but rather a common fireplace, so that the semantic evolution from the noun meaning “fire” is even more probable. On the semantic opposition between *āra* and *focus* see Nagy 1974: 88.

[^3]: Russ. *zoród* “hay-stack, enclosure for a hay-stack” and Lith. *žárdas* “rack for drying flax” are probably unrelated to OCS *gradъ* and Lith. *gardas*, because the accentuation of the two sets of forms disagree: the former words point to Balto-Slavic acute, and the latter to Balto-Slavic circumflex (Derksen 2008: 548). Hitt. *gurta-* “citadel” is also often connected with these words, but the vocalism (`-ur-` rather than expected `-ar-`) is quite irregular.

[^4]: The comparison with Khotanese *massa* “field” in Mallory and Adams (eds.) 1997: 51 is extremely doubtful.
