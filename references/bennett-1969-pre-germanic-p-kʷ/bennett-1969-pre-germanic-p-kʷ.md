# Pre-Germanic /p/ for Indo-European /kʷ/

William H. Bennett  
University of Michigan

For no apparent reason, Germanic may reflect a pre-Germanic /p/ where a reflex of IE /kʷ/ might be expected. The appearance of the /p/-reflexes is sporadic, inconsistent, and wholly unpredictable. All attempts to explain them in terms of phonologically definable processes are flatly contradicted by the reflexes themselves. It is suggested that the problem raised by their apparently capricious behavior is not a matter of phonology per se. Rather, they are due partly to borrowing (which is by nature sporadic, inconsistent, and unpredictable), and partly to contamination.

It has long been recognized that in sporadic instances Germanic shows reflexes of a pre-Germanic /p/ in forms that might be expected to reflect IE /kʷ/. The most interesting (and troublesome) feature of these forms is their unpredictability. Thus f < pre-Gmc. /p/ appears in OIcel. ulfr ‘wolf’, but g < IE /kʷ/ in OIcel. ylgr ‘she-wolf’. Similarly, a reflex of /p/ occurs in OIcel. ofn, OE ofen, and OHG ofan, but a reflex of /kʷ/ in Go. auhns and ONorw. ogn, all meaning ‘oven’. It is not surprising that attempts have been made to explain such apparent discrepancies on purely phonological grounds. Today, indeed, very few scholars regard the p- and kʷ-forms as being phonologically irreconcilable. Rather, as Prokosch (1939:74) says, ‘The fact [of their relationship] is certain, but the exact conditions are not known.’ The most commonly cited explanation would have IE /kʷ/ become pre-Gmc. /p/ in forms that contained more than one labiovelar articulation, as in IE *wḷkʷos > pre-Gmc. *wḷpos > PGmc. *wulfaz ‘wolf’ and IE *ukʷnos > pre-Gmc. *upnos > PGmc. *ufnaz ‘oven’. This labialization theory, however plausible it has seemed to some investigators, leaves a number of embarrassing questions unanswered. First, why would the suggested change be so erratic, appearing in some forms (OIcel. ulfr) but not in others (OIcel. ylgr), or occurring in one co-allomorph (OE ofen) but not in another (Go. auhns, ONorw. ogn)? Second, although /p/ in Gk. ipnós ‘oven’ is to be expected in the classical form of the language, why does /p/ occur also in the related word i-po-no ‘cooking bowl’, which appears in Mycenaean (Linear B), a kʷ-dialect? Third, if we accept the labialization theory, how can the assumed change of /kʷ/ to /p/ be explained in forms that never contained more than one labiovelar articulation? Noreen (1894:147) would answer that IE /kʷ/ produced pre-Gmc. /p/ > PGmc. /f/ (or /ƀ/) when in direct contact with /l/, /r/, or /n/. But even if we accept such an equation as IE /kʷ/ in *ljekʷṛt = pre-Gmc. /p/ > PGmc. /ƀ/ in OFris. livere, OHG lebara ‘liver’ (see Pokorny 1959:504), still another troublesome question arises: why would this change not occur also in forms like IE *sekʷnís > Go. siuns ‘form, appearance’? Noreen (149–50) assumes a parallel development of IE /gʷ/ to pre-Gmc. /b/ > PGmc. /p/, but most of his etymologies are now regarded as very uncertain (Prokosch, 74), and many of the forms he cites have other explanations that are more believable. Noreen says (147) that he can find no examples of a second parallel development, namely one that would convert IE /gʷʰ/ to PGmc. /ƀ/.[^1]

In a monograph published two years after Noreen’s treatment of the subject, Ernst Zupitza (1896:1–47) flatly rejects the labialization theory. Though his opinion is supported by some highly destructive arguments, they have not received the amount of attention that might be expected. It would be difficult to decide precisely why this is so, but there are two possible reasons. First, though Zupitza clearly disposes of the labialization theory, he offers no substitute. Second, the temptation to accept even a faulty explanation may be very strong when no simple alternative theory is available. So far as I have been able to determine, the only twentieth-century support for Zupitza’s rejection of the theory comes from Lane (1939:184), who remarks, ‘As I go through his material again, I marvel how so many great Germanists could later ignore, even deny, the probity of his evidence.’ (For a succinct history of the problem, see Lane, 184–92).

The object of the present study is not merely to re-assert Zupitza’s and Lane’s rejection of the labialization theory, but also to consider the ways in which pre-Gmc. /p/ replaced IE /kʷ/. Inasmuch as the problem under consideration has so consistently eluded a purely phonologic solution, it is pertinent to ask a further question: is the problem a matter of phonology per se? I would suggest that it is not. In examining a language like Latin, we are not perturbed at finding p in lupus ‘wolf’, although Latin is not a p-language. If we wish, we may conclude that lupus has been borrowed from Sabine, a p-dialect; or we may suppose that the /p/ has been introduced through contamination with the word for ‘jackal, fox’ (Skt. lopāśa- etc.; see Pokorny, 1178–9). But, regardless of how /p/ in lupus is to be explained, the problem is not simply a matter of Latin phonology, nor is Osc.-Umbr. b in Lat. bōs ‘ox’ for expected *vōs. If we insist nevertheless that Lat. lupus and bōs are somehow explicable in terms of Latin phonology, we must conclude that it needs additional phonologic formulas to explain them. It would not be difficult to concoct such additional formulas, to be sure, but they would work no better in Latin than the labialization theory works in Germanic.

Accordingly, it may be advisable to look at the problem from another point of view. As already observed, p-forms like Lat. lupus (or Go. wulfs) and Gk. Mycen. i-po-no may appear in q- (that is, /kʷ/-) dialects, but their occurrence is sporadic and phonologically unpredictable. They may be reflected in one form of a morpheme but not in another, even within a single dialect; or some dialects may employ the p-form and others the q-form. This apparent chaos, I would suggest, had nothing to do with special phonologic developments. In such instances, rather, the pre-Germanic language had two types of allomorphs. One type was of native origin and contained /kʷ/. The other type was either borrowed and contained /p/ < IE /kʷ/, or had been subject to contamination with inter-associated forms containing IE /p/. In short, the q-forms came lineally from Indo-European, whereas the p-forms were due partly to borrowing and partly to contamination.

As a matter of discipline, we expect phonologic changes to represent strictly definable processes, not caprices of chance. Borrowing, on the other hand, is by its very nature fortuitous, phonologically unpredictable, and very often sporadic. We know, for example, that the co-allomorphs of NE fox and vixen indicate that vixen has been borrowed from an English dialect in which [f] had become voiced in initial position. As might be expected, this borrowing is typically inconsistent: only the feminine form of the noun is affected. Similarly, NE beseech reflects a midland and southern palatalization of pre-OE /k/; NE seek does not. If we wish, we may speculate at length about why a language would borrow one co-allomorph and not another, but in any event the element of chance is not reducible to a phonologic formula.

Some of the forms borrowed in pre-Germanic times were nomads, which are ipso facto troublesome for the reason that their provenience may be either difficult or impossible to identify with certainty. The earlier the borrowing, the fewer distinctive non-Germanic features it shows, if indeed it shows any at all. In some instances a nomad has been so thoroughly naturalized by Germanic developments that its foreign origin can be detected only through external comparative evidence. Thus a form designating a kind of cloak, viz. *bajtā (baité in Herodotus) came into the pre-Germanic language and subsequently underwent the Proto-Germanic and Old High German consonant shifts, appearing as Go. paida ‘cloak’, OE pād ‘coat’, OS pēda ‘garment’, and OHG pfeit ‘cloak, shirt’, with each Germanic form showing the characteristic vowel developments of its respective language. As the phonologic evidence demonstrates, the fact of the borrowing is clear. Yet the source of the word has been variously given (Feist 1939:381–2) as an unidentified language of Asia Minor, Greek, Indo-European, Illyrian, Thracian, and even Letto-Slavic!

Fortunately, not all pre-Germanic borrowings are so troublesome. In some instances it is at least possible to form tentative conclusions regarding the source from which a p-form may have come. Classical Greek, Osco-Umbrian, and the Britannic subgroup of Celtic were p-languages. Among these, Britannic Celtic remained in close, prolonged contact with Germanic. The early home of the Celts, who were for many centuries neighbors of the Germanic tribes, lay in southern and southwestern Germany on both sides of the Rhine, which still retains its Celtic name. It was from this Celtic homeland that the La Tène iron age, variously estimated to have begun between 700 and 500 B.C., penetrated into the adjoining Germanic territory. Tacitus, in the forty-third chapter of his Germania, relates that the Cotini, a Gaulish-speaking people, mined iron ore for the Germani from deposits in the vicinity of the Carpathians. As might be expected, the vocabularies of Celtic and Germanic show mutual borrowing, some Germanic borrowings from Celtic antedating the Proto-Germanic consonant shift, e.g. *ojto- (OIr. oeth) > PGmc. *ajþa- > Go. aiþs ‘oath’, OIr. dūn ‘fortress’ = OE tūn ‘enclosed area, town’, Welsh gwyllt = Go. wilþeis ‘wild’.

More important for the immediate purpose is the Britannic reflex of IE /kʷ/, namely /p/: Lat. quattuor, OIr. cethir cethair but Old Welsh petguar ‘four’, Gal.-Lat. petorritum ‘four-wheeled chariot’. If IE *kʷet-wóres (and -wóres) had retained *kʷet- in pre-Germanic times and had undergone the Proto-Germanic consonant shift, pre-Gmc. *kʷet-wóres would have become PGmc. *xʷeðwōrez, and the Gothic form of the word for ‘four’ would have been *hvidwōr, not fidwōr. On the other hand, if *kʷet- was replaced by Brit. *pet- as in OW petguar and Gal.-Lat. petorritum, above, pre-Gmc. *pet-wóres became PGmc. *feðwōrez and Go. fidwōr. Similarly, IE *pénkʷe ‘five’ would have produced PGmc. *fiŋxʷe > *fīxʷe > Go. *feilv; but if the pre-shifted form was replaced by Brit. *pempe > *pimpe (OW pimp), it shifted regularly in Proto-Germanic and became Go. fimf. Here, as in the instances of NE fox vixen and beseech seek, the borrowing was sporadic and inconsistent. The same replacement of /kʷ/ by /p/ might have taken place in the word for ‘finger’, which is related to ‘five’; but in this instance *pimp- was not introduced, so that the consonant shift in ‘finger’, though equally regular, had a very different result: IE *peŋkʷrós > PGmc. *fiŋg(w)raz > Go. figgrs. Otherwise, pre-shifted *pimprós would have yielded Go. *fimbrs.

In other instances it is easier to correlate pre-Germanic with non-Germanic p-forms than to explain exactly how and from where they were transmitted, e.g. Gk. ipnós = OE ofen ‘oven’, though even here answers are possible. In studying a problem that is not immediately related to the present topic, Szemerényi (1960:26, n. 2) says, ‘For reasons that cannot be discussed here, I believe that Gk. ipnós is itself borrowed from Asia Minor. If that is true, it becomes even more likely that the term came to the Germanic tribes via the Greek form, or rather a derivative Balkan-form *upna or *upuna.’ As this example illustrates, the source of each pre-Germanic p-form constitutes a separate problem; there is no facile, generally applicable solution. In some instances borrowing provides a wholly credible explanation; in others, the rival p- and q-forms are not related, and they are inter-associated only through contamination. Thus, as Lane (194) suggests, the pre-Germanic forms for ‘wolf’ and ‘fox’ (respectively *wḷkʷ- and *wḷp-) may have been subject to contamination at a very early period, perhaps as far back as Proto-Indo-European times.

If we continue to believe that phonologic changes represent strictly definable processes, not caprices of chance, then we cannot accept the labialization theory, which contradicts itself in word after word and fails to explain the ascertainable facts. The sporadic, unpredictable occurrence of p-forms in the pre-Germanic language was not simply a matter of phonology. Rather, it was a characteristic feature of borrowing, which is still as capricious as ever, or of contamination, which can occur only in forms (or phrases) that are inter-associated. The Germanic development of the labiovelars is a problem that has remained dormant for far too many years; Seebold’s re-investigation of IE /gʷʰ/ in Germanic is especially welcome for this reason.[^2]

[^1]: A recent study by Seebold (1967:111–5) suggests three or possibly four instances of IE /gʷʰ/ producing PGmc. /ƀ/, but the context in which he examines them is very different from the present one.
