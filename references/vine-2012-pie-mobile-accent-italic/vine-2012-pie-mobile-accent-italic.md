# PIE mobile accent in Italic: Further evidence

**Brent Vine**  
University of California, Los Angeles

## Abstract

Several recent proposals have challenged the handbook assumption that the Proto-Indo-European mobile accent was lost in Italic, such as Rix’s suggestion (1996, updating a theory of Hirt) that Latin apocope was sensitive to the PIE accent (cf. et < *éti vs. pede < *ped-í), or that Thurneysen-Havet’s Law (*-ou̯(H)V- > Ital. /-au̯V-/) operated on unaccented *o in terms of the PIE accent (e.g. U. sauitu < *ksou̯-éi̯e/o-, Lat. cavus < *ḱou̯H-ó-; Vine 2004, 2006). Here I suggest that additional evidence of this kind may be seen in the problematic ī-conjugation of two Italic morphological categories, namely the acrostatic iterative-causative (seen in Lat. sōpīre) and o-stem denominatives (of the type servus → servīre). Both can be explained by a prehistoric raising of *e to /i/ before *i̯ (similar to the “pius-Gesetz”), which occurred in unaccented position in terms of the PIE accent (*su̯ṓpei̯e/o- > /su̯ōpii̯e-/, *seru̯-e-i̯é/ó- > /seru̯ii̯e-/). Accented *e in this position is not affected (as in standard iterative-causative *-éi̯e/o-, the “material adjective” suffix *-éi̯o-, and originally proterokinetic or hysterokinetic i-stem nom. pl. *-éi̯-es), whereas acrostatic i-stem nom. pl. *-ei̯-es (with root accent) would undergo the rule (> /-iyes/), providing an attractive explanation for the Italic i-stem nom. pl. -īs.

## 1 Italic accent: background

### 1.1

According to standard handbook-style assumptions, the Proto-Indo-European mobile accent is said to have been lost without a trace in Italic, having been converted (whether directly or indirectly) to an initial-stress accent, as seen in the so-called Common Italic “period of initial

<!-- source-page: 546 -->

stress”.1 This, in turn, is supported with reference to well-documented phonological features like widespread syncope in Italic, Middle and Late Etruscan syncope, vowel weakening in Latin (and to a lesser extent in Sabellic), as well as certain orthographic features (such as the “geminatio vocalium” so prominent in Oscan and early Latin, largely restricted to initial syllables).2 The Classical Latin penultimate stress system thus has no connection with pre-Italic accent, and is all but universally regarded as a still more recent development.

### 1.2

In recent years, however, this picture has been changing. Some handbooks now offer a more nuanced perspective that allows for possible traces of an early period of mobile accent in Italic. The main phenomena in question can be listed briefly as follows:

1 According to a widespread view,3 there is a double reflex of syllabic resonant followed by laryngeal when these are interconsonantal, depending on whether or not the resonant was accented: unaccented CR̥HC > Ital. /CRāC/ (e.g. *pl̥h₂-nó- > Lat. plānus ‘flat’), but accented CŔ̥HC (typically arising from a substantivizing accent retraction) > Ital. /CaRaC/ (e.g. *pl̥h₂-meh₂ > *palamā > Lat. palma ‘palm (of the hand)’, i.e. ‘flat part (of the hand)’, to the same root as plānus).4

2 According to another proposal,5 final unaccented short vowels were apocopated (e.g. *éti [cf. Ved. áti, Gk. ἔτι] > Lat. et ‘and’), whereas accented final vowels remain (e.g. loc. sg. *ped-í [cf. Gk. dat. ποδί] > Lat. abl. pede ‘foot’).

<!-- source-page: 547 -->

3 I have proposed another such hypothesis (2006a), according to which “Thurneysen-Havet’s Law”6 converts prevocalic *ou̯ to Ital. /au̯/ in unaccented position (in terms of PIE accentual patterns), as in iterative-causatives (e.g. *gʷʰou̯-éi̯e/o- > Lat. favēre ‘be favorable’), or “τομός forms” (e.g. *ḱou̯H-ó- ‘swelling’ > Lat. cavus ‘hollow’), but not otherwise (cf. acrostatic *h₂óu̯-i- ‘sheep’ > Lat. ovis).7

4 Note finally the conception of Latin medial vowel weakening by Pultrová (2006), who claims that medial vocalism reflects phonological treatments of PIE unaccented zero grades in certain consonantal configurations, and analogical extensions thereof.8

### 1.3

The preceding proposals may or may not be correct, and there is no space (and no need) to argue these matters here.9 They testify, however, to a growing openness toward a conception of Italic as a branch of Proto-Indo-European that – like Germanic – may have retained indirect traces of the old PIE mobile accent. The remainder of this paper offers an additional proposal that may point in the same direction, based on two problematic morphological categories in Italic, both displaying anomalous or unexpected ī-conjugation: acrostatic iterative-causatives, as seen in Lat. sōpīre ‘put to sleep’ (2); and o-stem denominative verbs (3).

## 2 Acrostatic iterative-causatives and Lat. sōpīre

### 2.1

The standard theory of acrostatic iterative-causatives – i.e., non-canonical iterative-causatives that correlate with acrostatic (or “Narten”) root athematic presents – is based on Klingenschmitt’s celebrated study of Lat. sōpīre ‘put to sleep’ (1978). According to this theory, which is promulgated as doctrine in LIV and otherwise very widely accepted,10 Lat. sōpīre – with its isolated lengthened ō-grade root vocalism – is a deverbative relic form, reconstructible as *su̯ṓp-(i)i̯e/o-. The accented long ō-grade of the root, together with the plain *-i̯e/o- suffix (as opposed to canonical iterative-causatives with the shape R(o)-éi̯e/o-), suggest a special type of iterative-causative in R(ṓ)-i̯e/o-, comparable to and morphologically associated with acrostatic presents, which show R(ḗ) in their active strong forms.11

### 2.2

The two pillars of Klingenschmitt’s analysis were, first of all, an attractive comparison with ON sœfa ‘kill’ (originally *‘put to sleep’) < Gmc. *su̯ōfii̯a-; and second, possible evidence (mainly from Indo-Iranian) that the root *su̯ep- ‘sleep’ formed a PIE acrostatic present *su̯ḗp-ti/*su̯ép-n̥ti. Initially, skepticism was voiced on both of these points: thus Bammesberger (1980) offered an inner-Germanic analogical account of the vocalism of ON sœfa; and Jamison (1982/3), reviewing the Indo-Iranian material, showed that the unexpected full grades there, instead of pointing to original acrostatic inflection, could be secondary, and she suggested that the root *su̯ep- itself might not even have made a present formation of any kind in the parent language. Current opinion remains somewhat unsettled. Thus Bammesberger’s conception is followed by García García (2005: 18 n. 8), as against Malzahn (2007: 237 n. 1), who asserts that “sœfa must undoubtedly be an archaic formation, i.e., derived from a Proto-Gmc. preform with root vowel *-ō-”. On the second point, concerning *su̯ep-: LIV

<!-- source-page: 549 -->

(s.v. *su̯ep-, n. 6) considers the acrostatic present to be a possibility, but admits (citing Jamison) that it cannot be confirmed by the Vedic material. Note also that, while the equation between Lat. sōpīre and ON sœfa remains attractive, the “Klingenschmitt iterative-causative” is very rarely attested: LIV tallies twenty-four possible instances,12 of which only half are deemed “sicher” – and only half of these have corresponding acrostatic presents.13 V. Martzloff (2006: 677) has usefully raised several additional points concerning ON sœfa. He observes, first, that the voiced -f- of this form has little significance, as Gmc. *-f- in Old Norse can be secondarily voiced intervocalically. Further, as is well known, PIE *-éi̯e/o- is indistinguishable in Germanic from plain *-(i)i̯e/o-. Thus ON sœfa cannot unambiguously show whether Verner’s Law has applied, or whether the suffix is actually plain *-(i)i̯e/o-; therefore, the form can in principle reflect any of the preforms *su̯ṓp-ii̯e/o-, *su̯ṓp-ei̯e/o-, *su̯ōp-ii̯é/ó-, *su̯ōp-éi̯e/o-.

### 2.3

I suggest further that the Klingenschmitt/LIV doctrine of acrostatic causatives is vulnerable to still other criticisms, beyond the initial reservations expressed by Bammesberger and Jamison, and in addition to the indeterminacies noted by Martzloff.14 On purely theoretical grounds, Jasanoff (2009: 48f.), commenting on ablauting suffixes in the verbal system, points out that the athematic optative suffix *-i̯eh₁-/*-ih₁- “was the only suffixed tense-aspect sign or mood sign in the entire PIE verbal system to show ablaut” (emphasis Jasanoff), and states that “the lesson to be learned from the special status of the optative is cautionary”: there is, as he notes, a whole series of problematic categories for which scholars have posited a single verbal morpheme with apophonic variants, such as LIV’s “essive” in *-h₁-i̯e/o- vs. “fientive” in *-eh₁-,15 or a series of Leiden School i-present theories.16 Indeed, another such in-

stance, as Jasanoff notes, is Klingenschmitt’s zero-grade causative suffix *-i̯e/o- beside the ordinary full grade *-éi̯e/o-. In Jasanoff’s words: “Even if there were no straightforward alternatives to these proposals, the near-absence of ablauting suffixes elsewhere in the verbal system would justify a position of skepticism” (2009: 49). More concretely, underlying problems inherent in the theory emerge from consideration of the LIV presentation of the material. Prime among these is a conspicuous prevalence of suffix “substitutions” or “normalizations”, of which the following examples (drawn from LIV’s tally of “certain” cases) are typical:

1 *g(ʷ)eĝʰ- ‘eindringen, hineinwaten’, caus. *gṓĝʰ-i̯e-: [SCr. gbziti ‘treten, waten’, “Mit Suffixersatz *-i̯e- → *-ei̯e- (> -i-)”;

2 *kʷeḱ- ‘sehen, erblicken’, caus. *kʷṓḱ-i̯e-: [AV sáṃ kāśáyati ‘macht sichtbar’, “Suffix nach dem produktiven Kausativtyp normalisiert”;17

3 *sed- ‘sich setzen’, caus.-it. *sṓd-i̯e-: [OIr. sádid ‘steckt’, “Wohl aus *sōd-ii̯e- zu *sādī- umgebildet”; and [OCS saditi ‘setzen’, “Mit sekundärer *ei̯e-Flexion, falls nicht erst innerslav. Bildung”;

4 *u̯edʰh₁- ‘stoßen’, iterat. *u̯ṓdʰh₁-i̯e-: ὠϑέω ‘stoße, dränge’, and ?[OCS vaditi ‘anklagen etc.’, “Zu *u̯ōdʰ(h₁)-éi̯e- umgebildet”.

Gk. ὠϑέω in (4) is particularly instructive. The root may in fact be aniṭ;18 and in that case, the LIV reconstruction with *-h₁- and zero-grade suffix is essentially a “trick”: there is little reason (other than a wish to squeeze the form into the R(ṓ)-i̯e/o- mold) to interpret ὠϑέω as anything other than what it appears to be – i.e., an iterative with the ordinary *-éi̯e/o- suffix (though, to be sure, with lengthened ō-grade in the root). Another case of this kind:

5 Lat. cōnārī ‘attempt’ < *kōnai̯e-, the result of an alleged “iterat. *kṓnh₁-i̯e-” (s.v. *kenh₁- ‘sich bemühen, sich anstrengen’); but there is no other evidence that the root was seṭ (or that the laryngeal was *h₁, as LIV admits, n. 1). Further, the root not only lacks an acrostat-

ic present, but appears not to have made a root present of any kind. Other explanations of Lat. cōnārī are certainly available, including a R(ō)-eh₂i̯e/o- iterative *kōn-āi̯e- (of the type στρωφάω ‘twirl about’, νωμάω ‘wield’, etc.),19 or a denominative to a R(ṓ)-eh₂ noun *kṓn-eh₂.20 Nor is the case for a *kṓnh₁-i̯e- strengthened by the other forms placed here (from Celtic and Slavic), as these involve “Suffixersatz”.

In a general way, of course, “Suffixersatz” is a necessary part of the theory, since in some branches – not only Germanic (2.2, ad fin.), but also Celtic, Albanian – *-éi̯e/o- and plain *-i̯e/o- fall together. Items like the following therefore cannot prove the existence of LIV’s type “caus.-it. R(ṓ)-i̯e/o-”:

6 caus. *i̯ṓs-i̯e- (: *i̯es- ‘sieden, schäumen’): here only ON œsa ‘in heftige Bewegung setzen’;

7 iterat. *kṓlh₁-i̯e- (: *kelh₁- ‘verlocken etc.’): here only ON hœla ‘rühmen, preisen’, OHG huolen ‘betrügen’ etc.;

8 caus. *(s)gʷṓsh₂-i̯e- (: *(s)gʷesh₂- ‘erlöschen’): here only OCS u-gasiti ‘auslöschen’.21

9 iterat. *kʷṓlh₁-i̯e/o- (: *kʷelh₁- ‘eine Drehung machen’): here only Gk. πωλέομαι ‘come and go’ [Hom.], beside well-attested *kʷolh₁-éi̯e/o-.

Most such forms, then, are indistinguishable from those with lengthened ō-grade and *-éi̯e/o- suffix (like Gk. ὠϑέω), or, in theory, lengthened and accented ō-grade with *-ei̯e/o- suffix. Indeed, the -s- of ON œsa ((6) above), without Verner’s Law, suggests *i̯ṓs-ei̯e-, with root accent. In *su̯ṓp-i̯e- itself and the alleged R(ṓ)-i̯e/o- forms (1) through (9) above, we have seen all but two of the dozen acrostatic causative forms that LIV registers as “certain”:

10 iterat. *pṓth₂-i̯e-, in πωτάομαι ‘fly’ (LIV s.v. 2. *peth₂-);

11 caus. *kʷṓi̯-i̯e- (LIV 1. *kʷei̯- ‘wahrnehmen, bemerken’) in Ru. káju, kájat’ ‘blame’ (kájat’sja ‘repent’, cf. OCS kajǫ sę, kajati sę).

Alternative accounts are available for both of these. A full discussion would take us too far afield, but in brief: for (10), πωτάομαι is equally plausible as a lengthened-grade iterative in *-eh₂i̯e/o- (cf. (5) above on the type στρωφάω); and for (11), the assignment to 1. *kʷei̯- (on morphological grounds, since 1. *kʷei̯- makes an acrostatic present) is unconvincing, as opposed to the traditional (and semantically more straightforward) assignment to 3. *kʷei̯- ‘blame, punish etc.’, which opens the door to other options, such as a lengthened-grade present of the type πλώω ‘swim’, OE flōwan ‘flow’ (cf. Jasanoff 2003: 224 n. 2). We may note, finally, that the attempt by Isebaert (1992) to add additional Latin “sōpīre forms” (mōlīrī ‘strive’, etc.) has been convincingly refuted by Martzloff (2006: 679ff.).22 The interim conclusion to be drawn from this survey is that the best example of a Klingenschmitt/LIV-style lengthened ō-grade iterative-causative – and possibly the only truly unambiguous-looking example – may be Lat. sōpīre itself. This is, for one thing, too slender a basis on which to establish an entire PIE category of the type hypothesized by Klingenschmitt and codified by LIV, even if (as seems likely) some type of special acrostatic iterative-causative may indeed have existed. At the very least, we are authorized to consider alternative accounts of Lat. sōpīre.

### 2.4

Some older ideas23 are no longer acceptable: for example, Brugmann’s conception of sōpīre as denominative to an i-stem noun *su̯ōp-icannot stand, since there is no such noun, and no evidence that such an istem type ever existed. Considerably more attractive, and nowadays rather widely accepted, is Nussbaum’s conception of sōpīre as denominative to a root noun *su̯ṓp-s.24 In more detail: although Lat. sopor ‘(deep) sleep’ is morphologically ambiguous,25 Nussbaum suggests that the widespread thematic no-stem (with fluctuating root vocalism) *su̯op-no- ~ *su̯ep-no- ~ *sup-no- could point to an old root noun. For the semantics: we would have an agentive root noun

<!-- source-page: 553 -->

(formally of the type κλώψ ‘thief’), with a meaning ‘sleep’ – not as an activity, but as a force that induces sleep; thus the causative (or rather “pseudocausative”) meaning of the denominative would be an automatic effect of this particular agentive value. Nussbaum compares, finally, Lat. custōdīre ‘to guard’, a denominative in -īre to the agentive consonant stem custōs ‘guard’. As attractive as this seems, it is not without problems. As for the alleged root noun *su̯ṓp-s: the thematic no-stem forms (*su̯op-no- etc.) could equally well derive from an r/n-stem, as Schindler thought (1966), and as others still think (e.g. Pinault 2009: 243f.);26 and in any case, such no-stems with fluctuating ablaut (also e.g. *u̯es-no- ~ *u̯os-no- ~ *u̯ēs-no- to *u̯es- ‘sell’, among others) constitute a general problem, with no clear basis in root nouns (as opposed to other formations, such as n-stems or r/n-stems). Nor is a nominal *su̯ōp- unambiguously attested, in Italic or anywhere else. The proposed semantic interpretation is exceedingly clever, and seems possible; but one would like to have parallels for agentive root nouns of this type, where a notional starting point ‘sleeper’ amounts to ‘sleepinducer’ or ‘sleep-effectuator’, with something like factitive value.27 As for the parallel with Lat. custōdīre: this may provide only limited support, since the rare consonant-stem denominatives in -īre seem mainly to be analogical creations (see Leumann 1977: 556), and thus need not reflect an old derivational type as such. Finally, this account leaves ON sœfa out of the picture; yet, as we have said, the formal and semantic convergence between this form and Lat. sōpīre is striking, and potentially important for the morphological background of both forms.

### 2.5

In pursuing an alternative analysis of Lat. sōpīre, it is reasonable to operate with the same basic assumption that drove Klingenschmitt’s pioneering study, namely that the verb does indeed reflect an old present formation – indeed, a causative with lengthened-grade root, which may even be a lengthened-grade causative associated with an original acrostatic root

<!-- source-page: 554 -->

present. Since, however, at least some lengthened-grade causatives seem to have been formed beside other types of present formations (cf. the LIV tallies in 2.2.), this last point is non-essential. More important is the idea that lengthened-grade causatives of the Klingenschmitt/LIV R(ṓ)-i̯e/otype, with zero-grade suffix as compared with canonic iterative-causatives in *-ei̯e/o-, may never have existed as such, as I argued above. Or if they did (and resulted, for example, from a very early reduction process), no clear trace of such forms actually survives, other than the explanandum sōpīre itself. Instead, one can posit the existence (or the early development) of acrostatic iterative-causatives with lengthened ō-grade root and root accent (similar to acrostatic root presents in *-ē-), but with the ordinary iterative-causative suffix *-ei̯e/o- (i.e. R(ṓ)-ei̯e/o-). Inevitably, most forms of this type would have been remodeled with the suffix accent of ordinary iterative-causatives: hence forms like Gk. ὠϑέω, πωλέομαι (2.3(4), (9)) or ON nœra ‘revive’ (< *nōs-éi̯e/o-28) with “Akzentersatz” – a less drastic and more credible adjustment than the pervasive “Suffixersatz” postulated by followers of the Klingenschmitt/LIV theory. The final assumption that must be made is that (as in the various formulations surveyed in 1.2 above) Italic may have inherited at least some traces of PIE mobile accent.

### 2.6

It is clear, first of all, that an original *su̯ōp-éi̯e/o- – that is, the remodeled acrostatic type with reaccentuation (as in forms like ὠϑέω) – would have ended up as Lat. ×sōpeō, ×sōpēre, like monēre ‘advise’ and other ordinary causatives. Yet sōpīre is functionally causative, has an isolated lengthened ō-grade, and stands a good chance of being a relic form, possibly the only relic of a lengthened-grade causative that is certifiably distinct from R(ō)-éi̯e/o- causatives. We may hypothesize, then, that sōpīre continues the more original acrostatic causative, i.e. a root-accented *su̯ṓp-ei̯e/o- – accentually distinct from both ordinary causatives like *mon-éi̯e/o- and reaccented acrostatic causatives of the type ὠϑέω.29 We can thus rephrase the problem as follows: an original *su̯ṓp-ei̯e/o- develops not to ×sōpēre, but rather to sōpīre, exactly as if it were *su̯ṓp-ii̯e/o-. As an initial step, I propose (in a way that will at first seem completely ad hoc) the following purely phonological solution, i.e. an early Italic rule

<!-- source-page: 555 -->

×́-e.i̯V > ×́-i.i̯V (in informal notation), which would state that unaccented /e/, in the syllable after an initial accented syllable, is raised to /i/ when it precedes /i̯/. Such a rule – a typologically natural assimilatory neutralization for unstressed syllables – would directly convert an original *su̯ṓp-ei̯e/o- to *su̯ṓp-ii̯e/o-.30 Note further that this rule must be assigned to a very early stage of Proto-Italic, as it precedes the loss of intervocalic *-i̯- (itself normally assigned to Proto-Italic: so e.g. Meiser 1998: 91). Within Latin itself, one can also point to some similar processes involving vowel assimilation before /i̯/. At an early stage, the so-called “pius-Gesetz”31 fronts /ū/ to /ī/ before immediately-following syllable-initial /i̯/ (although accent is not a factor). At a later stage, an even closer match (depending on how it is conceived phonetically) might be seen in the process that accounts for pronominal forms like mieis ‘meis’, ivm ‘eum’, iei ‘eī’, and more generally the common late Republican and Imperial variants iī, iīs etc. (for eī, eīs etc.).32 Here, in fact, accentual conditioning may be involved, given the proclitic and enclitic behavior of possessives like meus,33 and the frequent “Wackernagel” behavior of unstressed pronominal forms like eum.34 Having now proposed a phonological treatment of the sequence *-ei̯V-, under particular accentual conditions, designed to account for Lat. sōpīre, it behooves us to consider other instances of inherited *-ei̯V- in Latin (and elsewhere in Italic), so as to evaluate the behavior of our rule in those cases as well. Two such cases come to mind, namely so-called “material adjectives” in Lat. -eus < PIE *-ei̯o- (Lat. aureus ‘golden’, etc.), and the nom. pl. of i-stems (< PIE *-ei̯-es), also generalized in Latin as the nom. pl. of consonant stems. But it will be most convenient to return to this material after treating the second problematic morphological category of interest, namely o-stem denominatives in -īre/-īrī.

## 3 Italic o-stem denominatives

### 3.1

The formation of standard denominatives in Indo-European is clear and well-known: the nominal stem is suffixed with accented *-i̯é/ó-, as in a familiar example like Ved. pŕ̥tanā- ‘battle’ → pr̥tanāyánt- ‘doing battle, fighting’.35 There are, to be sure, various patterns of secondary development; but these tend to fall into certain well-documented subtypes, such as the generalization of resegmented sequences as productive denominative morphemes,36 or reaccentuation (in particular based on iterative-causatives in *-éi̯e/o-).37 For denominatives made to o-stems specifically: these follow the usual pattern; and where the stem vowel can be determined, it shows *-e- preceding the suffix, and not *-o- (R-e/o- → R-e-i̯é/ó-), as clearly in Greek: κόσμος ‘order’ → κοσμέω ‘arrange’, etc. So also in Armenian, as in a form like gorcem ‘work, do’ (cf. gorc ‘work’), where Arm. -em is in principle ambiguous between denominative *-e-i̯é/ó- and iterative *-éi̯e/o-, but in any case shows *-e- and not *-o-. Denominative *-e-i̯e/o- is also likely for Luvian and Lycian (though with reaccentuation based on iterative-causatives, as in Sanskrit; see Melchert 1997: 134ff. for details). It is evident, finally, that no information about the quality of the thematic vowel is recoverable from patterns in which the vowel itself is “submerged”, so to speak, as a result of phonological or morphophonemic processes, as in Germanic Weak II denominatives (Go. hails ‘healthy’ → hailjan ‘heal’, etc.); cf. the parallel “submerged” *-e- of Germanic causatives, already mentioned above in connection with ON sœfa (2.2).

### 3.2

There is a range of Latin material that one might loosely refer to as “o-stem denominatives”, including the following:

<!-- source-page: 557 -->

1 o-stem denominatives formed with productive resegmented *-āi̯e/o- (n. 36 above), on which see in extenso Steinbauer 1989;38

2 *-āi̯e/o- verbs built to adjectival bases of the type novus ‘new’ → renovāre ‘make new again’ – but these factitives are only superficially similar to denominatives stricto sensu, and have a different morphological background (see Jasanoff 2003: 139ff.);

3 *-ēi̯e/o- verbs built to o-stem adjectives of the type albus ‘white’ → albēre ‘be white’ – but these are “denominative statives” in *-eh₁-i̯e/o- (built with the stative morpheme *-eh₁-) and thus not “standard denominatives” of the type surveyed in 3.1 above.39

In contrast to all of these stands the following category:

4 o-stem noun and adjective bases with pendant denominative in -īre/ -īrī: e.g. servus ‘slave’ → servīre ‘be a slave’, saevus ‘cruel’ → saevīre ‘be cruel’. This is the category of interest here, as it is acknowledged to be the repository of “true” o-stem denominatives, comparable to Gk. κοσμέω etc. (3.1).40 The formation is also attested with certainty in Sabellic, and thus old within Italic: corresponding formally to Lat. servīre is U. seritu/seritu ‘observato; servato’, i.e. /serritu/ < *seru̯ītōd.41 As for the semantic discrepancy: as Rix has argued,42 Lat. servīre reflects a semantic renewal based on the late specialized meaning (‘servant, slave’) of servus, originally ‘guardian’ vel sim., whence U. seritu ‘servato’ (→ ‘observato’). Nor is this the only instance of a Sabellic o-stem denominative in -ī-: leaving aside less clear possibilities, a highly probable case appears in U. stahmito ‘established’, denominative to (dat. or loc. sg.) stahmei ‘establishment,

<!-- source-page: 558 -->

agreement’, best taken as an o-stem.43 The longstanding problem at issue is the descriptive fact that denominatives of this type behave as if they were based on i-stems: (servus →) servīre, like (sitis ‘thirst’ →) sitīre ‘be thirsty’. This puzzling behavior has never been satisfactorily explained, as the following survey of previous approaches should make clear.

### 3.3

There are essentially three analytical gambits that have been proposed:

1 According to Nussbaum (1975: 149f.), the o-stem denominatives in -īre/-īrī contain an inherited long *-ī-, which is historically identical to the o-stem gen. sg. formant -ī (< *-iH) that also appears in derivatives like (servo- ~) servī-lis (an archaic o-stem pattern: also vir ‘man’ ~ adj. virīlis, erus ‘master’ ~ adj. erīlis, and several others), beside servī-re. The denominative *-ī-i̯e/o- formation – originally proper to nouns – would then have been extended, rather early, to thematic adjective bases, whence forms of the type (saevus →) saevīre, (blandus ‘flattering’ →) blandīrī ‘caress, flatter’, etc.

There is some semantic attractiveness to this theory, insofar as most of the forms in question have “essive” meaning: i.e., servīre ~ servus esse, servīlis esse (like saevīre = saevus esse, blandīrī = blandus esse). Yet there are disquieting features as well. In formal terms, there is no trace of such a pattern of verbal derivation elsewhere, with the marginal exception of the Vedic type putrá- ‘son’ ~ RV putrīyánt- ‘desiring offspring’ (also AV putriyánt-), which has long been compared to a small subset of Latin denominatives with somewhat similar semantics, like equīre ‘be in heat’ (as if ‘desire the stallion’), catulīre ‘id.’ (cf. equus ‘horse’, catulus ‘puppy, whelp’). Nevertheless, as I discuss elsewhere (2009), the Vedic type (which, among other earmarks of late formations, has no parallel in Avestan, and may

<!-- source-page: 559 -->

even go back to *-i-i̯e/o-) is likely to reflect an inner-Indic development. Nor is it apparent why this “genitival” pattern in *-ī-i̯e/o- would have replaced inherited *-e-i̯e/o-, while the inherited ā-stem, i-stem, and u-stem patterns remain intact (i.e., *-ā- → *-ā-i̯e/o-, *-i- → *-i-i̯e/o-, *-u- → *-u-i̯e/o-), with no additional layer of derivation. Moreover, as Nussbaum admits, there is at least one old-looking item that is not essive semantically, namely artīre ‘tighten, fit (s.th.) tightly (onto s.th.)’ [Cato+], cf. artus ‘tight’; and it may be possible to add other forms of this type. Note, for example, Eichner’s analysis of Lat. (red)hostīre ‘requite, compensate’ [Plt., Naev.+] beside hostus ‘yield from olive-pressing’ [Cato, Varro] < *‘substitution, compensation’ (2002, cf. Martzloff 2006: 690). Further, although the problem cannot be examined here in full, another such non-essive instance may be Lat. polīre ‘polish’ [XII Tab.+], if Vendryes (1929) was correct in taking this as derivationally related to interpolis ‘refurbished’ [Plt.+] and if the thematic variant interpolus [glosses, Pliny] is actually the older form, as de Vaan argues (2008: 477).44 Finally on this point, the essive (or “stative”) meaning of servīre itself may even be secondary (cf. 3.2(4) above on Rix’s account of the semantics of U. seritu/seritu vis-à-vis Lat. servīre); and in any case, PIE denominative formations regularly incorporated both essive and factitive options (cf. A more precise formulation of just such an approach has recently been offered by Martzloff (2006: 688ff.), explicitly based on the theory of thematic morphological derivation articulated by Schindler (1976: 351, 1980) and developed since by many others.51 Martzloff’s version operates with the following three assumptions. First, it is taken as given (following Schindler) that o-stem adjectives can regularly serve as a derivational basis for i-stem abstracts (with replacement of *-o- by *-i-). It is these i-stem abstracts, in turn, that serve as the basis for denominative verbs in *-i-i̯e/o- (ultimately based on thematic adjectives). Finally, this derivational chain (“adj. *-o- → abstr. *-i- → denom. *-i-i̯e/o-”) can eventually be reinterpreted, by suppression of the intermediate stage, as a simpler process, whereby o-stem adjectives (and, secondarily, o-stem nouns) can directly develop denominatives in *-i-i̯e/o-, without the actual existence of intermediate i-stem formations.52 This theory is not credible, for a number of reasons. To begin with the data: Latin does indeed have several i-stem abstracts of the requisite type – i.e., forms ultimately interpretable as pendants to original thematic stems – and these include both nouns and adjectives. For the latter, we have already seen Lat. fortis (and, insofar as it may be relevant, sācri-), and I have discussed several other such i-stem cases elsewhere (2006b), including cīvis ‘citizen’ (originally *‘[societal] closeness’), collis ‘hill’ (< *‘elevation’), and others. <!-- source-page: 562 -->

apart from trivial or apparent exceptions.53 Nor, conversely, is there a single i-stem form (whether an original abstract or an adjectivized version) that is found beside any attested o-stem denominative verb: i.e., for nouns, there is no i-stem ×serv-i- (: servus) corresponding to servīre, no ×proci- (: procus ‘suitor’ [Plt.+]) corresponding to procīre* ‘be a suitor, woo’ (supine procītum [Liv. Andr.]), and for adjectives, no ×saevi- (: saevus) corresponding to saevīre, no ×bland-i- (: blandus) corresponding to blandīrī, etc.54 Nor, despite Martzloff, is there any indication that the adjective type is older than the noun type: in addition to servīre and procīre* (both evidently old), note also fastīdīre ‘feel disgust at, scorn’ [Plt.+] (: fastīdium ‘disgust’ [Plt.+]). Indeed, it is the better-represented adjective type that evidently became semi-productive, whereas the clearly residual servīre type has the appearance of an archaism. To put the entire matter in perspective: not only is the crucial “intermediate i-stem stage” bypassed, but it is in fact nowhere to be found (including, it should be noted, for the supposedly more basic adjective forms): we are asked to believe, in other words, that the entire substitutive morphological process occurred prehistorically, without a single surviving model. And at the same time, there is not a particle of evidence, in Italic or elsewhere, for a parallel denominative process; nor is any substantive reason provided as to why the expected formation of thematic denominatives (*-e/o- → *-e-i̯e/o-, like κοσμέω etc.) should have been replaced by the more complex substitutive process envisaged (*-o- → *-i- → *-i-i̯e/o-), when other vowel-final stems in Italic regularly follow the standard IE denominative pattern, as with ā-stems (→ *-ā-i̯e/o-), i-stems (→ *-i-i̯e/o-), and u-stems (→ *-u-i̯e/o-). At best, one might venture the claim that “homonymie fâcheuse” could have blocked the simple pattern *-e/o- → *-e-i̯e/o-, as this would have resulted in Italic “second-conjugation” ē-verbs. But this fails to convince: why should thematic denomina-

<!-- source-page: 563 -->

tives have constituted a critical mass for avoidance of the second conjugation, when so many other formations fell together as ē-verbs in just the same way (i.e., stative *-eh₁-i̯e/o-, iterative-causative *-éi̯e/o-, athematic *Ceh₁-(i̯e/o-))? There is, then, room for further consideration of the problem of Italic o-stem denominatives.

### 3.4

Clearly the simplest and most appealing approach would be one according to which the residual o-stem denominative type servīre, saevīre, etc. could be explained directly in terms of the predicted PIE pattern *-e-i̯é/ó- (comparable to forms like Gk. κοσμέω). This, I suggest, can be accomplished quite simply by a minimal reformulation – indeed, a generalization – of the phonological rule posited above (2.6) to account for Lat. sōpīre: if one generalizes the raising of /ĕ/ to /i/ before /y/ so as to apply to any unaccented /ĕ/, this will explain not only the post-tonic raising in *su̯ṓp-ei̯e/o- (> *su̯ṓp-ii̯e/o-), but also pre-tonic raising in thematic denominatives: (Ital.) *seru̯-e-i̯é/ó- > *seru̯-ii̯e/o- (> servīre), *prok-e-i̯é/ó- > *prok-ii̯e/o- (> procīre*), etc.55 As a consequence, the Italic residual o-stem denominatives of the fourth-conjugation servīre type would directly recover the predicted PIE denominative pattern (exactly as in denominatives to other vowel-final stems: ā-stems → *-ā-i̯e/o-, etc.; 3.3), without a need for the suspiciously elaborate (and in any case entirely unsupported) derivational mechanism involving “intermediate i-stems”. We would then, in ad-

<!-- source-page: 564 -->

dition, have further evidence for the maintenance, in early Italic, of at least some mobile accent contrasts with PIE pedigrees.

## 4 Concluding remarks

I have suggested that two morphologically unrelated instances of anomalous “ī-conjugation” in Italic (Lat. sōpīre and the category of Italic o-stem denominatives) can both be accounted for by a typologically natural reduction rule, which raises /e/ to /i/ in unaccented position before /i̯/. The rule is not only sensitive to accent, but correlates more specifically with accent patterns that are predictable on the basis of probable (in the case of Lat. sōpīre) or well-established (for thematic denominatives) configurations of PIE accent. This conception, if correct, would provide further evidence, to be considered together with other such hypotheses (1.2), for the maintenance in early Italic of at least some form of PIE mobile accent, preceding the period of initial stress.66 More specifically, early Italic would be seen to preserve a series of accentual contrasts in both verbal and nominal morphology:

1 root-accented acrostatic causatives in *-ei̯e/o- (with Lat. sōpīre as an isolated instance of this type) vs. standard iterative-causatives in *-éi̯e/o- and thematic denominatives in *-e-i̯é/ó-;

2 material adjectives in *-éi̯o- and i-stem nom. pl. *-éi̯-es with generalized (proterokinetic/hysterokinetic) suffix accent, vs. marginallypreserved i-stem (acrostatic) nom. pl. *-ei̯-es, with root accent and unaccented suffix.

In a broader perspective, one may thus conceive of Proto-Italic as a stage of Indo-European similar to Proto-Germanic, in terms of the behavior of in-

<!-- source-page: 568 -->

herited accent and the development of accentual innovations: in both branches, the old mobile accent appears nowhere on the surface, having been replaced at a very early period by an initial-stress accent; yet traces of its existence at an earlier stage are visible on the basis of segmental phonological alternations that are sensitive to accent, as with Verner’s Law (in Germanic), and – granting at least some of the material surveyed and proposed above – Italic phenomena such as the double treatment of CR̥HC sequences, Thurneysen-Havet’s Law, and the /e/ to /i/ raising that may account for both Lat. sōpīre and Italic o-stem denominatives.67
