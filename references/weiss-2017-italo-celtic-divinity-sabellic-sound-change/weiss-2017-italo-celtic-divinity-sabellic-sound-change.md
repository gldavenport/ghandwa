# An Italo-Celtic Divinity and a Common Sabellic Sound Change

Michael Weiss

## Abstract

The shadowy Roman god Sēmō and the plural group Sēmōnēs have long been associated with sēmen ‘seed.’ But the evidence that Sēmō or the Sēmōnēs have anything to do with seeds is lacking. The Sēmōnēs first appear in the Carmen Arvale: here they constitute Mars’s retinue. The Sabellic evidence also puts Semo firmly in the Martial sphere. The form Semo appears, in addition, as part of the Semo Sancus Dius Fidius complex. These divinities are connected with the sanctity (sancīre) of treaties (foedus, fidēs) and oaths. In “Dumézilian” terms Semo is a god of the first (priestly) and second (warrior) function, but not a god of the third (agricultural) function, precisely the opposite of what the standard etymology predicts. New evidence from Oscan allows us to reject conclusively the connection between sēmen and Sēmō. In an inscription from Pietrabbondante the god’s name is spelled seemuneí (dat. sg.) and this spelling with ee is not the expected one. If the Oscan form were a derivative of the root seen in sēmen, the spelling would have to have been †síímuneí. The spelling ee shows that the Oscan form, and its Latin cognate, must have a different origin. The only plausible source is *seγVmōn-. A form that matches reconstructed *seγVmōn- exactly is Gaulish Segomoni and Ogham Irish SEGAMANAS. The Gaulish god is identified with Mars. The Celtic and Italic forms continue a Proto-Italo-Celtic *seĝʰomōn- ‘strong one,’ ‘strongman,’ which is a derivative of a noun *seĝʰom ‘strength.’ The root *seĝʰ- (Gk. ἔχω etc.) had the original meaning ‘hold firmly’ and this developed to ‘be strong,’ ‘conquer’ in Indo-Iranian and Western Indo-European. The god *seĝʰomōn- is the sole example of a divine name that perhaps can be considered a unique and innovative feature of the ancient Proto-Italo-Celtic speech community.

## 1

On p. 282 of my Outline of the Historical and Comparative Grammar of Latin, I give as an example of internal derivation the pair (proterokinetic[^1]) *séh₁-mn̥ ‘seed’ (Lat. sēmen) → (amphikinetic) *séh₁-mō(n) ‘having seed’ (Lat. Sēmō ‘seed-god’).[^2] This example is supposed to illustrate the potentially possessive semantics of the amphikinetic internal derivative. But although the principle is correct and can be illustrated with other examples, there is a major problem with this particular example. Is Lat. Sēmō the name of a seed god? Despite the apparently confident assertion of the author of the Outline of the Historical and Comparative Grammar of Latin, this is far from certain. Let us begin with an investigation of the dossier of Sēmō. The earliest attestation of this name occurs in the Carmen Arvale, the ancient cult hymn of the Arval Brethren, preserved in an inscription dating to 218 CE:

(1) CIL 6.32388 (=Scheid 1998: 293)[^3]

ENOS LASES IVVATE

NEVE LVE RVE MARMAR SINS INCVRRERE IN PLEORES

SATVR FV, FERE MARS, LIMEN SALI, STA BERBER

SEMVNIS ALTERNEI ADVOCAPIT CONCTOS

ENOS MARMOR IVVATO

TRIVMPE TRIUMPE TRIVMPE TRIVMPE TRIVMPE

Help us, o Lares! Marmar, let not destruction or ruin fall on the people.

<!-- source page 372 -->

Be sated, fierce Mars! Leap on the threshold. Stand at every door(?)[^4]

semunis alternei aduocapit conctos Marmor, help us then! triumpe triumpe triumpe triumpe triumpe

The context focuses on Mars or Marmar, as he is also called, the Roman god of war. As far as we can understand the hymn, there is no agricultural or seedy content. The fourth line of this hymn includes the name of our divinities in the accusative plural.[^5]

The form ADVOCAPIT is traditionally interpreted as future, i.e., aduocabit, but the <p> for <b> is inexplicable, because this text was clearly originally composed in an alphabet that employed <b> (cf. BERBER in line 3). The future too is extremely odd and has been interpreted as a stage direction that has slipped into the hymn: “The magister (or “you all” reading an unparalleled aduocapite) will invoke....”[^6] It is certainly out of place in context between imperatives. I continue to find the reading aduoca=pit with a variant of the focus particle -pte < *pete attached to the verb—or really to the zero pronoun inherent in the verb—a better solution.[^7] Mars himself is being encouraged to summon all the Semunis, in their turn. Cf. the use of aduocare in Seneca’s description of the summoning of the council of the gods:

(2) Sen. Nat. 2.41

secundam (manubiam) mittit quidem Iuppiter, sed ex consilii sententia duodecim enim deos aduocat.

The second one (a subtype of manubia-lightning) is sent by Jupiter, but on the advice of his council: for he summons the twelve gods. Trans. Hine 2010.

One could compare the way Indra commands the Maruts to attend to the inspired poet in the Rig Veda:

(3) RV 1.165.14c

ó ṣú vartta maruto vípram áchā.

Turn here to the inspired poet, o Maruts. Trans. Jamison and Brereton 2014.

<!-- source page 373 -->

If this is correct, then the Semunis might be conceived of as Mars’s retinue and in any case are closely associated with that god.[^8]

## 2

The second apparent occurrence of the plural group is found not in Latin, but in the famous Paelignian Herentas epigram in which the inhabitant of the tomb is called cerfum sacaracirix semunu. The usual interpretation of this is “priestess of the Ceres (i.e., Demeter and Persephone) (and) the Semones.” Thus we would appear to have at least some evidence for the cereal appurtenance of the Semones. But the usual interpretation is wrong. To demonstrate this requires a slight excursus on the fate of -rs- clusters in Sabellic. As so often in Sabellic, we must recognize two different treatments depending on whether the cluster is original or arises by medial syllable syncope. Original *-rs- clusters are retained in Umbrian: tursitu < *torsētōd ‘frighten,’ çersiaru (name of a month) < *k̑ersii̯o- ‘pertaining to cutting,’ i.e., the month of harvest (cf. Hom. Gk. ἀ-κερσε-κόμης ‘with unshorn hair’).[^9] But in Oscan the *s is lost with compensatory lengthening of the preceding vowel: teer[úm] ‘earth’ (CA A 12) < *tersom. N.b. the spelling of the long vowel with geminatio in an inscription in the reformed Oscan alphabet, which shows that the long vowel is secondary and not to be directly compared with OIr. tír ‘earth.’ Secondary *-rs- clusters (rectius *-rz- clusters) arising by medial syllable syncope become -rf- in Umbrian but assimilate to -rr- in Oscan: e.g., Umb. parfa (type of bird) < *parasa- (cf. Lat. parra,[^10]

but Osc. kerrí ‘Cereri’ (TA A 3) < *keresēi̯). Generally Paelignian is a kind of north Oscan (with a South Picene admixture) and thus we should expect it to behave like Oscan in regard to its treatment of -rs- clusters. And in fact the outcome of secondary *-rs- is well attested in Paelignian in the adjective Cerria ‘of Ceres’ (Pg 18) < *keresii̯o-.[^11] The unavoidable conclusion is that cerfum cannot be related to Ceres since neither an original nor a secondary *-rs- cluster will yield Paelignian -rf-.[^12] The form cerfum, on the other hand, cannot be disassociated

<!-- source page 374 -->

from the Umbrian god Çerf(o)- and this means that this name too cannot be related to Ceres. The only possible sources that will give a Paelignian and Umbrian -rf- are sequences of r plus a labial or dental voiced aspirate. If we examine the abundant evidence for the god(s) Çerf(o)- in the Tabulae Iguvinae, we quickly see that this divinity has no points of contact with Lat. Ceres or Cerus. Çerf(o)- belongs primarily to the circle of Mars and is called Çerfo- Marti(o)- ‘Martian Çerfo-.’ This same Çerfo- Marti(o)- is, in turn, the superordinate deity for the gods Prestota ‘the one stationed in front’ (called Prestota Çerfia Çerfier Martier) and Tursa ‘terror’ (called Tursa Çerfia Çerfier Martier). This little circle of gods is invoked in the great prayer of the purification of the ocar ‘city’: they are asked to destroy the enemy and to guard the Iguvines. If not for the “obvious” etymology there would be no textual support for connecting this god with Ceres or Cerus. Çerfo- Marti(o)- is in essence a martial god in charge of warding off enemies and protecting the homeland. A potential proto-form *k̑erdʰo- (as already suggested by Giacomo Devoto)[^13] would match Ved. śárdha- ‘troop, force,’ especially of the Maruts, and the meaning ‘god of (military?) troop’ would be consistent with the profile of the Iguvine divine circle.[^14] To get back to the main point, the collocation of cerfum and semunu in the Herentas inscription does not support the connection of semunu with sēmen, but instead confirms the picture of the Carmen Arvale where the Semunes are close associates of the Martial sphere.[^15] One might object to the idea of a priestess of male divinities,

<!-- source page 375 -->

but this was already the case on the traditional reading of semunu. In fact, priestesses of male divinities are attested in ancient Italy.[^16]

## 3

Returning to Rome we find the singular Semo as part of a complicated and much disputed complex of divine names usually and erroneously given as Semo Sancus Dius Fidius. This lemmatization of the god’s name in this form is erroneous because precisely that combination never occurs. An examination of the attestations of the various forms shows that there are three levels to be distinguished.[^17]

A shrine or temple on the Quirinal, specifically on the Collis Mucialis, vowed according to legend by Tarquinius Superbus and dedicated by Sp. Postumius in 466 BCE, is known in the most ancient sources we have as the shrine of Dius Fidius. Thus the 3rd-century BCE list of sacraria argeorum given by Varro (L. 5.52) mentions the aedem dei Fidi. The Fasti Venusini and Fasti Antiates, conservative cult calendars, mention June 5th as the anniversary of the aedes dii Fidii in colle.[^18] Next, the god Sancus was added to the name and we find the same shrine referred to as the shrine of Sancus. Already in Cato as preserved by Dionysius of Halicarnassus (2.49) we find an explicit identification of Dius Fidius and Sancus; this identification is echoed by Aelius Stilo, the first Roman philologist (Var. L. 5.66). The association of Sancus and Dius Fidius is quite old because Festus tells us of a Porta Sanqualis in the old “Servian” wall (early 4th century BCE). In fact, the name Sancus is preferentially used as the name for this god in the classical period; it is only in the late 1st century BCE that the form Semo is added to the mix. In Livy 8.20.8 Semo Sancus is used and Ovid wonders whether the god should be called Sancus, (Dius) Fidius, or Semo pater.[^19] Finally, in a number of inscriptions of imperial date we find Semo, Sancus, and Deus (sic) Fidius.[^20]

<!-- source page 376 -->

Semo and Sancus are often treated as an indivisible unit in the secondary literature, yet this is evidently not the case, as the Ovid passage shows and as is confirmed by the inscriptions which give not only the order Semo Sancus, but also Sancus Sanctus Semo. Thus we can observe the gradual accretion of Dius Fidius with Sancus and finally with Semo. Let us say a few words about the other parts of this complex. The etymology and function of Dīus Fidius is clear. The first part is identical with the adjective dīus ‘radiant, divine’ which is the exact match for Oscan diíviaí and Gaulish diIiuion ‘heavenly’ of the Chamalières inscription.[^21] These forms all go back to a proto-form *dii̯eu̯ii̯o- ‘heavenly.’ The epithet fidius is no doubt related to fidēs and cōnfīdō ‘I trust in’ etc. This etymology is entirely consistent with the function of this god, who is the god of oath taking (cf. the well-attested oath formula medius fidius) and of the special bond of social relations and responsibilities that the Romans called fidēs. Sancus too is relatively clear, but here some observations are called for. The paradigm of Sancus is often said to vary between and o-stem Sancus, -ī and a u-stem Sancus, -ūs, but if one examines the forms, an interesting pattern emerges.[^22]

Paradigm of Sancus

nom. Sancus (Ov. Fast. 6.214; Tertull. ad Nat. 2, 9, Mart. Capell. 1. p. 17) gen. Sancūs (Cic. Pro Rab. 20.7, Liv. 32.1.10, Plin. Nat. 8.194, Fest. p. 276 2x, Paul. ex. Fest. p. 515) dat. Sancō (Ov. Fast. 6.213, Fest. p. 229, Paul. ex. Fest. p. 255, CIL 6.3544, 6.30795, 6.568, 6.30994, 6.30995, CIL 14.2458) acc. Sancum(Var.L. 5.66, Prop. 4.9.74, Sil.8.420;Aug. C.D.18.19. Lact. Div. Inst. 1.15.8, Isid. de Natura Rerum 4.5.32, AE 1989.182c)

From this one can see that there is no variation between forms: the dat. sg. is always Sancō and the gen. sg. is always Sancūs. The other forms are of course ambiguous. The u-stem forms may be of some antiquity because the derivatives Sanqualis (Porta Fest. p. 343 L, Paul. ex Fest. p. 465); (avis Fest. p. 197 L, p. 317 L; Paul. ex Fest. p. 3, p. 421; Liv. 41.13.1, Plin. 1.1.29, 10.20 3x; Serv. Auct. ad Aen. 1.394) and the gentilic Sanquinius. Variation between o- and u-stems famously does occur in other divine names, but here the patterns of attestation are different. Contrast Ianus and Consus where the genitive sg. Ianī (Var., Cic., Verg.) and Consī (Tac., Fest.) are the only forms attested. The dative is normally an o-stem (Ianō Cat.; Consō Var., Ov.) with one exception, Ianui (Fest. p. 204, maybe CIL 4.4436). How can we explain the specific pattern of Sancus? Many ancient authors considered Sancus to be a Sabine

<!-- source page 377 -->

importation.[^23] Of course, Varro, in particular, was well known for his fondness of claiming Sabine ancestry for many perfectly good Latin names. Still, it is noteworthy how insistent authors who are not Varro are about claiming Sabine origin for Sancus. Furthermore, the worship of the god is attested by Livy (32.1.10) in Volscian Velitrae in 199 BCE and the name cannot be separated from the Umbrian divine epithet Sa(n) çio-used of Jupiter and Fiso-. The Sabellic people clearly knew this god. The old derivative Sanqualis points to an old u-stem; if we take the idea of Sabine (i.e., Umbroid) origin seriously, what sort of paradigm might the Romans have taken over? I suggest that an old u-stem would have developed by regular sound change to look something like this:[^24]

nom. *sanks gen. *sankous dat. *sankou > *sankō acc. *sankom

Such a paradigm could easily have been nativized to Sancus, Sancūs, Sancō, Sancum, exactly the paradigm we find.[^25]

The etymology of Sancus is also clear. It cannot be separated from the verb sancīre which means among other things to ‘ratify’ (a binding agreement, a treaty, a law).[^26] Cf. Liv. 1.1.8: dextra data fidem futurae amicitiae sanxisse (“to have ratified with the right hand an agreement of future friendship”) and 23.8.11: sanguine Hannibalis sanciam Romanum foedus (“I will ratify the Roman treaty with the blood of Hannibal”). We are informed (D. H. 4.58) that the ancient treaty between Rome and Gabii written on an oxhide shield was preserved in the temple of Sancus. Livy recounts that a certain M. Vitruvius Vaccus of Fundi led a revolt of the Fundani and Privernates against Rome. His revolt was put down and he was killed, but the interesting detail is that the house he had owned on the Palatine was torn down and all the bronze salvaged from the house was melted down and formed into bronze orbs that were deposited in the shrine of Sancus.

(4) Liv. 8.20.8–9

aedes eius quae essent in Palatio diruenda, bona Semoni Sanco censuerunt consecranda; quodque aeris ex eis redactum est, ex eo aenei orbes facti positi in sacello Sancus aduersus aedem Quirini.

<!-- source page 378 -->

His house on the Palatine was to be pulled down, and his goods dedicated to Semo Sancus.[^27] Out of the bronze which his chattels realized were fashioned bronze disks, which were placed in the shrine of Sancus, over against the temple of Quirinus. Trans. Benjamin Oliver Foster.

It is plausible that Vaccus’s punishment was meant to highlight the perfidious nature of his revolt since both Fundi and Privernum had already been conquered and given civitas sine suffragio, and Vaccus himself must have been intimate with members of Roman society, given the fact that he had a house on the Palatine. The connection with sacrosanct agreements for Sancus is strong and the connection with the native Roman Dius Fidius makes good sense. What does not make good sense is the idea that the Semo who is eventually accreted to Sancus and Dius Fidius is a seed god.[^28] To sum up: none of the evidence we have looked at so far supplies the slightest support to the traditional connection of Semo and semen. Instead we find two apparently distinct entities. On the one hand, the plural Semones are a divine group associated with Mars or the likewise Martial circle of Cerfus and Çerfo-. On the other hand, we find the singular Semo associated with the gods of sacrosanct agreements, the Sabine Sancus or the Roman Dius Fidius. In Dumézilian terms, which I am not endorsing, but only using as a convenient shorthand, we find a god of the first function in Semo Sancus and gods of the second function in the Semones, but no evidence for the god of the third function that the standard etymology would lead us to expect.

## 4

At this point we must turn to another part of the Sabellic world, to the important Samnite site in the territory of the Pentri known by its modern name Pietrabbondante (Bovianum?). Here we find on the remains of the base of a podium or altar two fragments of an inscription (dated c. 125 BCE according to Crawford Te 9) that record the form seemuneí[s?], undoubtedly a dative or genitive of the Oscan cognate of Semo. The inscription is not that informative: it seems to be the record of some public works performed by the keenztur, but there are two crucial facts that can be gleaned from it. Two lines below the occurrence of seemuneí we find near the right edge [-?-.]anuseís which Crawford emends to anus(i)eís and interprets as gentilic Ansius, But a new discovery from Pietrabbondante has allowed Paolo Poccetti to come up with a better solution.[^29] The new text reads Hanuseís and suggests the restoration Hanuseís in our Seemuneís text. This name is the Oscan form of

<!-- source page 379 -->

Honos, which is confirmed by the form hanustu in the Herentas inscription. Now Honos was honored as a god in Rome in close conjunction with Virtus; they shared the same temple, the Aedes Honoris et Virtutis. Honos and Virtus “personified the honor and status acquired through successful military action.”[^30] Poccetti suggests that the Samnite pair of Hanus and Seemu may have been modeled on the Roman pair Honos and Virtus. If this is correct, we may infer that Seemu was seen by the Samnites as the rough equivalent of Virtus. Poccetti’s idea is not secure—the text is fragmentary and we cannot be certain that the two gods mentioned necessarily were functionally connected. Still, let us explore the hypothesis that the meaning of Semo was in the ballpark of virtus ‘(having) manly spirit, steadfastness.’ This meaning evidently works quite well for the plural Semones in both Latin and Paelignian. But perhaps Semo Sancus is ‘the compact which is steadfast,’ or the ‘steadfast spirit of the compact.’ One can compare the καρτερὸς ὅρκος ‘strong oath’ of Homer and Pindar, but closer to home are the well established Latin idioms foedus firmum and foedus or fidem firmare.

(5) Enn. Ann. Sk 32

Accipe daque fidem foedusque bene firmum.

Give and take your plighted troth and make a truly firm treaty. Trans. E. H. Warmington, Loeb edition.

(6) Ter. Hec. 749–51

BA. Si scirem aliud qui firmare meam apud uos possem fidem sanctius quam ius iurandum id pollicerer tibi, Laches.

If I knew a more solemn way to convince you of my truthfulness than by swearing an oath I would use it, Laches, to assure you. Trans. John Barsby, Loeb edition.

(7) Verg. Aen. 12.212

Talibus inter se firmabant foedera dictis.

With such words they sealed the treaty between them. Trans. G. P. Goold, Loeb edition.

(8) Verg. Aen. 12.313–14

——ego foedera faxo / firma manu.[^31]

This hand shall prove the treaty true. Trans. G. P. Goold, Loeb edition.

<!-- source page 380 -->

If Semo meant something like ‘having strength,’ ‘strong one,’ ‘hero,’ it makes the association with Hercules, which we find in Varro and Propertius, easier to understand. For Hercules has little to do with oaths or compacts but he was the strong man par excellence.[^32]

## 5

At this point we must turn our attention to orthography. As is well known, Proto-Sabellic long *ē, inherited from Proto-Italic *ē, was realized as a high-mid vowel. This is clear from its preferential spelling with i in the Latin alphabet and in the pre-reform Oscan alphabet and with í and íí in the reformed Oscan script used after 250 BCE. Umbrian in the Latin alphabet also frequently uses i for the reflex of *ē. Thus in the very inscription we have just been discussing we find the form fíís[nam] ‘shrine’ < *fēsnā < *dʰeh₁sneh₂ and líís[t ‘?’ The spelling of Seemuneí[s] is strikingly divergent. It cannot be explained as a defective spelling and the geminate can only be interpreted as spelling a long vowel. It cannot be explained as an attempt to spell a Latin *ē on the unsupported and unlikely hypothesis that the form is a loanword from Latin. Latin *ē is in all certain cases assimilated to Oscan *ē and spelled í(í), e.g., lígatuís ‘lēgātīs,’ ‘envoys’ (CA A 6, 7) where the semantic development guarantees a Latin to Oscan loan. On the other hand, there are other examples of the spelling ee in Oscan and in all clear cases they result from secondarily lengthened vowels. Again, this very inscription has a second example of the ee spelling in keenztur from *kenstōr with lengthening before ns, comparable to NS-lengthening in Latin, but a parallel, independent process. Another example is the Oscan word for ‘earth’ teer[um] from *tersom. What these examples show is that Oscan created new long vowels by various processes and, in the case of short e, the lengthening product did not merge with the old *ē but instead produced a new low-mid vowel [ɛː]. Thus the form seemuneí[s] conclusively eliminates the possibility of connecting Sēmō with sēmen < *sē-mn̥ < *seh₁mn̥. For in that case the spelling could only have been †sí(í)muneí[s]. Neither *-rs- nor *-ns- lengthening is a possible source for the secondary low-mid vowel of seemun-. But there are other possible sources. The preposition, which in Latin is ex, occurs in an s-less variant cognate with OLith. iž.[^33] The reflexes of Proto-Sabellic *eĝʰ show the following forms in Oscan: before a voiceless stop

<!-- source page 381 -->

we find the spelling eh in Oscan (eh preivíd, ehpeílatas). Before m we find the spelling e (emanafed).[^34] This latter form could be taken as an underspecified spelling for ee, but in fact there is no clear evidence in Oscan that h was lost with compensatory lengthening in this environment. Therefore emanafed might just as easily be explained as an error of omission for *ehmanafed. But there is a compensatory lengthening process operating specifically before m that could be invoked. Warren Cowgill long ago in his famous article on Italo-Celtic superlatives suggested that the Oscan and Umbrian superlatives in -aimo- (maimas ‘greatest’ gen. sg. f. TB 3, 7) and -imo- (Osc. nessimas (Cp 24 etc.), Umb. nesimei (VI a 9) ‘nearest’) should go back to earlier *-aisVmo- and *-isVmo-.[^35] Cowgill was hesitant about this account because *-sm- sequences were apparently retained in Sabellic: SPic. esmín ‘in this’ (AP 1 etc.), Umb. esmik ‘on this’ (Ia 28, 31), cf. Ved. ásmin; pusme ‘for whom’ (II a 40) etc.; cf. Ved. kásmai. But the discovery of an archaic Presamnite superlative ϝολαισυμος ‘best’ on the Tortora cippus (Ps 20) has made Cowgill’s hunch a certainty.[^36] What we must assume is that the sibilant was retained in original *-sm- but that *-sm- that arose by syncope lost the sibilant with compensatory lengthening. This hypothesis can only work if the secondary sequence differed phonetically from the original sequence. As a first approximation one might suggest that the most plausible phonetic difference would be the voicing of the original intervocalic s, for which we have abundant independent evidence.[^37] But this idea is problematic for two reasons. As Cowgill pointed out, it is unparalleled—to his knowledge and to mine as well—for intervocalic voicing of s not to extend also to post-vocalic pre-sonorant position. Thus one would suspect that s before m or another sonorant consonant was also voiced. This is evident in the spelling Osc. αιζνιω ‘brazen’ neut. pl. (Lu 5) < *aisnii̯ā. Instead we must suppose that intervocalic z was in some way more “reduced” than preconsonantal z. Perhaps the z in this environment was shorter or more approximant-like. At some point there was a phonetic difference between *z in intervocalic position and *z in preconsonantal position. This is shown for Umbrian at least by the fact that intervocalic *z became r whereas preconsonantal z remained. The rule then is that *-Vz̞Vm-[^38] >*Vz̞m- > -V:m-. In the case of the superlatives the vowel must have been lengthened in the first instance since it is always written with i and never with e. A second surprising long vowel allows us to confirm and expand this hypothesis. In Pompeii we find multiple instances of a one-word inscription on several columns in the Forum: vaamunim. This has long been explained as the equivalent of Lat. uadimōnium ‘a promise to appear in court at a certain date.’ The columns

<!-- source page 382 -->

marked the spots where the defendant was required to present himself. The Latin word is derived from uas, uadis ‘surety,’ which in turn is cognate with Go. wadi ‘earnest money’ and continues a West Indo-European *u̯adʰ-. This stem, if inherited into Sabellic, would have yielded *uaf- and a cognate or adaptation of Latin uadimōnium would have been in the first instance *u̯afemōnii̯om. In this form the -f- would have been voiced since the reflexes of the voiced aspirates in Sabellic were uncontroversially voiced in intervocalic position.[^39] The regular developments then would have been from *u̯aβemōnii̯om to *u̯aβemōnim to *u̯aβmunim and from here apparently to u̯aːmunim. If we want to write one rule covering the superlative development and the vaamunim case, we could suggest that a voiced approximant before m was deleted with compensatory lengthening. This long vowel was retained in initial, i.e., stressed, syllables. If this formulation is correct, we would predict that the third voiced approximant ɣ̞ would be subject to the same law. If we return to our starting point, we may entertain three proto-forms for Seemun-: *sezVmōn-, *seβVmōn-, or *seɣVmōn-. The first two forms may be quickly eliminated from further consideration. A root *sebʰ- is unsupported. *sezVmōn- could be interpreted as a reduplicated form of the root *seh₁- ‘sow,’ but, as we have seen, there is no support for connection with that root. The final proto-form, on the other hand, reminds us of the root *seĝʰ- ‘hold steadfast’ (Ved. sáhate, Gk. ἔχω, PGmc. *sigiz, Gaul. sego-). The root is continued in Italic in Lat. sevērus ‘tough, steadfast’ < *seĝʰ-u̯ēro-.[^40] A Proto-Italic form *seɣVmōn- ‘strong one’ would be phonologically and semantically perfect for Osc. Seemun-. The same form could easily yield Latin Sēmō as well, cf. *ne-homō or *ne-hemō > nēmō ‘no one,’ although a loan from some form of Sabellic is not impossible too.

## 6

Our *seɣVmōn- ‘strong one’ can be reconstructed for Proto-Italic, but can we go beyond that? In five Latin inscriptions from Gaul we find evidence for a god *Segomū, *Segomonos. These inscriptions cluster in the territories of the Sequani (Lyon, Arinthod), the Haedui (Nuits-Saint-Georges 2x), and the Ambarri (Culoz), i.e., in what is now Southwestern France. In one inscription the god is simply called Deo Segomoni (CIL 13.2846), but in the others he is identified with Mars:

Now this divinity clearly fits the bill for a potential cognate of PItal. *seɣVmō and in fact he can be shown to be quite an archaic and widespread divinity.[^41] For at the other end of the Celtic world, in Ireland, we find an Ogam inscription from Ardmore in County Waterford:

(14) CIIC 263

Of Lugud, son of X, the tribe of Nía Segamon

The sept of Nía Segaman or the “Champion of Segamu” occurs in one, possibly two, other inscriptions from the same region: Old Island Co. Waterford (CIIC 300: CUN[A]NETAS MAQ/I MMUC/[OI] NETA-SEGAMONAS) and possibly CIIC 292 Knockboy. The eponymous founder of this sept is recorded in later works as a mythical king of Cashel in the form Nía Segamain. The connection with nía ‘warrior’ (cf. Nettaslogi ‘warrior of the army’) confirms the warlike connection of the name Segamonas, and makes the identity with *Segomū very probable.[^42] The one difference between the two forms, the a vs. o of the second

syllable, can be explained in a number of plausible ways. Most probably, an inherited *Segomū was remade to *Segamū on the basis of the innovative *-amū suffix extracted from seṭ roots and extended in Irish in forms like medam ‘judge’ and legam ‘moth’ and also found in Gaul. casamo ‘assectator’ (Quint. 1.5.8). This *-amū must have been quite productive at one point since it is part of the relatively more productive agent suffix -em < *-iamū, e.g., brithem ‘judge.’[^43] The Celtic and Italic evidence converge on a unique proto-form *seĝʰomōn-. To my knowledge, this is the only divine name that can be reconstructed exclusively for the Italo-Celtic intermediate unity.

## 7

What is the best morphological analysis of a Proto-Italo-Celtic *seĝʰomōn-? It is evident that we are dealing with a secondary formation here, but what is the base and how and when did primary *-mōn get extended to secondary function? The base is a thematic stem *seĝʰo- that has direct reflexes in the Celtic languages. The facts about these forms are somewhat obscure and require some discussion. Middle Irish, Classical Modern Irish, and Modern Irish have a noun MIr. sed or seg, i.e., [ʃeɣ], Class. Mod. Ir. seadh, Mod. Ir. sea. To my knowledge, the form is not attested directly in any Old Irish work. The oldest meaning is ‘vital strength’ or ‘vigor.’ For example, in the Middle Irish composition Aislinge Meic Conglinne (55.4; LBr.) we find:

(15) conā bí súg nō seag innte. so that there was neither sap nor strength in it.

The same alliterative collocation is found in the MIr. version of the Gospel of Nicodemus (13.1; LBr. 163 β 2 2967):

(16) Bamar-ni for a ecla cin seg cin súg amal bemís marb. We were afraid without strength or sap as if we were dead.

The contextual meaning is confirmed by a legal glossary where segh is a gloss on trácht ‘strength’ and is given as an alternative to nert ‘strength’ (H. 4.22, 1460 p. 67c = CIH v 1569.25):

(17) tracht.i. segh no nert, ut est rith tar tracht. i.e., trácht i.e., strength or strength as is running beyond strength.[^44]

<!-- source page 385 -->

Several other Middle Irish passages confirm this meaning.[^45] From this meaning derives the meaning ‘essence’ ‘heart of the matter,’ ‘object of attention,’ which predominates in CMIr. and survives in MIr. The DIL and Matasović and LEIA all give the stem as masculine and this is correct as far as Middle and Classical Modern Irish go because it certainly is not feminine. On the other hand, this does not answer the question of the gender in Old Irish because Middle Irish eliminated the neuter by merger predominantly with the masculine. The word occurs in Middle Irish only in the nominative or accusative and without a following adjective. In the Irish Grammatical Tract on Nouns the item seadh belongs to what Armstrong has classified as the Ub class, i.e., those nouns that have a broad stem-final consonant and have a genitive and nom. pl. in stem plus -a.[^46] This class thus partly continues Old Irish u-stems, but seg cannot have been a u-stem since it shows no trace of u infection (contrast MIr. deog < OIr. deug < *degu- ‘thirst’) or raising. The nouns of the Ub class are a mixed bag and continue, in addition to actual Old Irish u-stems, o-stems, both masculine and neuter, (e.g., neuters: mían ‘desire,’ míadh, ‘honor,’ díall ‘swerving’; masculines: rían ‘sea,’ gíall ‘submission’) and feminine a-stems (e.g., ríar ‘will,’ íc ‘healing (if = ícc), rím ‘counting’). Thus an Old Irish *seg could just as well have been a neuter o-stem and not a masculine.[^47]

One form may point to the existence of an adjective in Irish. O’Clery’s glossary gives the entry seadh.i. laidir, [i.e., laitir] no laidirecht ‘strong or strength,’ where the first definition glosses an adjective. O’Clery’s glossary dates to the 17th century but draws on much old material and what makes this first meaning plausible is the fact that it exactly matches MW hy < *segos ‘bold.’ Cf. MW Mi nyt adwaen y gur hy “I don’t recognize the bold man,” OW PN Gurhi < *u̯irosegos. The same e-grade simple thematic is abundantly attested in the first member of various continental Celtic personal and place names: Celtib. sekobirikez /segobrigez/ abl. of Segobris, Sego-briga (‘strong fortress,’ Strabo, CIL 2.4191, cf. Nertobriga), Sego-dunum (Ptol. 2.11.14 Σεγόδουνον), Sego-marus (= OIr. segmar) Sego-latus (CIL 12.5127 ‘strong fury’?, cf. OIr. láth) Sego-uellauni (Plin. 3.34 ‘strong rulers’) Sego-uesus (‘strong and good’? Liv. 5.34, Secoveso CIL 2.2871 Hispania Citerior), Segouax (BG 5.22), Segorix spelled Secorix (Bagacum, Belgica, cf. Secorigienses).[^48]

<!-- source page 386 -->

The Celtic forms show no clear evidence of the meaning ‘victory,’ so familiar from the Germanic *sigiz, nor is there any reason to suppose these forms represent transformations of the neuter s-stem. Instead, all the forms taken together point to an e-grade thematic adjective *seĝʰó- ‘strong,’ a “*temós” adjective to the verbal root *seĝʰ- ‘hold firm.’[^49] The neuter of this adjective is the most probable source for the Middle Irish substantive seg. Given that the *temós type is not productive and that the verbal base *seĝʰ- does not otherwise survive in Western Indo-European, the formation must be very old. In fact, *seĝʰo- is exactly matched by the Rig-Vedic hapax sahá-, which is used as an epithet of none other than the Maruts:

(18) RV 7.34.24

ánu tád urvī́ ródasī jihātām ánu dyukṣó váruṇa índrasakhā ánu víśve marúto yé sahā́so rāyáḥ siyāma dharúṇaṃ dhiyádhyai

Then let the two broad world-halves follow suit, let heaven-ruling Varuna, whose comrade is Indra, follow; let the Maruts, who are victorious, follow. Might we be (fit) to found the buttress of wealth. Trans. Jamison and Brereton 2014.

## 8

The suffix *-mn̥ and its internal derivative *-mōn[^50] are normally regarded as a primary suffixes added to verbal roots, but this system of derivatives has made the leap to secondary denominal derivation in many Indo-European traditions. In Latin Tellumo (Aug. C. D. 7.23 based on Varro) appears to be a masculine derivative of Tellus.[^51]

Greek offers ἀκρεμών ‘branch’ (Sim. +) ← ἄκρος ‘extreme, point,’ δαιτύμων ‘dinner’ (Hom. +) ← δαιτύς ‘meal’ etc.[^52] Even Anatolian has clear cases of *-mn̥ and *-mōn in

<!-- source page 387 -->

denominal function.[^53] One might hypothesize that if a *-mn̥ ~ *-mōn complex happened to be derived from a root which also made a root noun it would be possible to reinterpret the *-mn̥ ~ *-mōn as deriving not directly from the root but from the nominal instantiation of the root and from there denominal derivation could spread. For example, the root *h₁reu̯dʰ- ‘red’ made a root noun *h₁reu̯dʰ-s continued by OIr. rú. This root also made a primary *-mn̥ and *-mōn stem continued in a number of traditions (Ice. rođmi, Lith. raumuõ ‘muscle’). Just such pairs could have provided the model for learners to extend the suffixes to nominal bases.

## 9

To sum up: none of the textual evidence from Ancient Italy supports a connection of Sēmō with sēmen. The Romans themselves, unbounded as they were by rules of historical phonology, never considered this possibility. The orthography of the Oscan form Seemuneí[s] positively excludes this relationship. The only plausible etymological source for the Italic forms is *seɣVmōn-, which in fact is perfectly superimposable on the Proto-Celtic divine name *Segomū. These two forms converge on a Proto-Italo-Celtic proto-form *seĝʰomō ‘the strong one.’ As far as I know, this is the only divine name reconstructable solely for the intermediate Italo-Celtic unity and is quite possibly an innovation of this fleeting period.[^54]

Anticlimactically, I discovered that the 22-year-old Hermann Osthoff saw all the essentials, deriving Semo from *seĝʰe- and connecting the Italic and Celtic forms in his 1869 dissertation Quaestiones Mythologicae.[^55] Like other insights of the brilliant and underappreciated linguist, it has taken almost a century and a half for someone to suggest again what he saw in the murky dawn of our discipline.
