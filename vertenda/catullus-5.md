# Catullus 5 (*Vivamus mea Lesbia*)

**Source:** Gaius Valerius Catullus, *Carmina* 5. c. 60 BCE. Public domain.

**Why this text:** The only current vertenda in the Life register. Lyric, metrically constrained (hendecasyllabic), and emotionally direct. Tests intimate/personal vocabulary, imperative constructions, and the numerals in an extended sequence. The contrast between *soles* (suns, days) and the finality of *nox perpetua* is a natural bridge between the Life and Sacred registers — relevant to the Ghandwa mythological framework around Agnis and time.

---

## Source Text (Latin)

> Vivamus, mea Lesbia, atque amemus,
> rumoresque senum severiorum
> omnes unius aestimemus assis!
> soles occidere et redire possunt:
> nobis cum semel occidit brevis lux,
> nox est perpetua una dormienda.
> da mi basia mille, deinde centum,
> dein mille altera, dein secunda centum,
> deinde usque altera mille, deinde centum.
> dein, cum milia multa fecerimus,
> conturbabimus illa, ne sciamus,
> aut ne quis malus inuidere possit,
> cum tantum sciat esse basiorum.

---

## English Translation (after Lee)

> Let us live, my Lesbia, and let us love,
> and all the rumors of stern old men
> let us value at a single penny!
> Suns can set and rise again:
> for us, when once the brief light has set,
> there is one perpetual night to be slept.
> Give me a thousand kisses, then a hundred,
> then another thousand, then a second hundred,
> then yet another thousand, then a hundred.
> Then, when we have made many thousands,
> we shall confuse them, so that we may not know,
> or lest any evil person be able to envy us
> when he knows there are so many kisses.
